Agent Orange

Cuprins
  • Use during the war
  • Harmful effects and protests
  • Legal consequences

Use during the war

Agent Orange was the most widely used defoliant during the Vietnam War. Chemical defoliants are military herbicides used to clear forests to prevent enemy troops from hiding and to make bombing targets more visible. Agent Orange is one of the Rainbow Herbicides, a group of herbicides named after the coloured stripes painted on the barrels they were stored into.

The Rainbow Herbicides had a tactical use in the attrition strategy the United States employed against North Vietnam and the Viet Cong. They were mainly used in Operation Ranch Hand, a massive chemical warfare campaign that went on from 1962 until 1971 and was meant to take away the enemy's advantage in the terrain and to deprive them of food resources. Aircrafts, helicopters, trucks, boats, and hand sprayers were used to spread the chemicals. It is estimated that during this operation, US and South Vietnamese forces sprayed more than 20.2 million gallons of herbicides over Vietnam, out of which 13 million gallons were of Agent Orange. Consequently, between 2 to 4 million people were exposed to these substances during the war.

Harmful effects and protests

The herbicides were contaminated with dioxin, a persistent toxic chemical. This chemical lingers in the water and in the local animals and plants that gets exposed to it. However, at the time, the extent of the toxicity of dioxin was not widely known.   

Agent Orange and the other Rainbow Herbicides caused a considerable number of deaths and injuries. Reports of possible health problems caused by defoliants have surfaced in the press since 1970, but few studies involving herbicide exposure were conducted at first. Negative health effects began to be more closely observed only after 1980 when a study was conducted on US veterans who served in sprayed areas and were at elevated risk of developing health problems such as different types of cancer. Studies on South Korean troops who served in Vietnam have revealed increased risks of diabetes and other disorders. After the end of the war, an elevated rate of birth defects in the Vietnamese population was linked to the herbicide exposure.

The defoliants also had a disastrous impact on the environment. Almost a quarter of the total forested area of Vietnam was sprayed during the war. This led to the disappearance of several species of plants and animals from these areas and encouraged the spread of invasive species that affect the local natural balance.

As the use of defoliants in warfare was previously done by the British forces in the 1950s during a guerrilla war, the United States used this precedent to motivate their choice. The Kennedy Administration argued that the use of defoliants does not violate any rule of international law and that it is an accepted tactic of war. However, the use of herbicides in the Vietnam War was controversial from the beginning. It was widely condemned and it stirred international outrage and protests. The scientific community was first to object, and press coverage of the topic began to increase in the late 1960s. This stirred a public health debate originally focused on the effects herbicides had on the environment.

Legal consequences

The aftermath of the use of Agent Orange in Vietnam resulted in various legal consequences. After 1970, extensive environmental damage began to be observed and. As a result, in December 1976, the United Nations passed Resolution 31/72, a resolution that was meant to ban military use of environmental modification techniques.

In 1980, lawsuits were filed on behalf of millions of veterans who were exposed to Agent Orange during their service in Vietnam. The lawsuits were filed against the companies which produced Agent Orange. At first, the companies blamed the US Government and denied any link between the health problems experienced by the veterans and Agent Orange. However, years later, several companies settled instead of going to court by agreeing to pay compensation to the veterans. The settlement drew negative reactions from some veterans, who felt it was an unjust decision.

Another lawsuit was initiated in 2004 by a group of Vietnamese activists against several chemical companies, including the same ones accused by the US veterans. The lawsuit was motivated by the lasting poisonous effects and health problems caused by Agent Orange, and it sought billions of dollars worth of damages. However, the lawsuit was dismissed on account that there was no legal basis for the claims; it was argued that, at the time when it was used, Agent Orange was not considered a poison under international law, and therefore, its use was not prohibited. The US Government also refused to take responsibility, invoking once more the precedent established by the British and stating that the US was not guilty of war crimes since the use of defoliants was not intended to harm humans, and thus it could not be classified as a chemical weapon.

In 2002, Vietnam and the United States held a joint conference on Human Health and Environmental Impacts of Agent Orange that had in view a joint research project but negotiations broke down. However, beginning with 2005, the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency began to work with the Vietnamese Government to measure the level of dioxin in affected areas. In 2007, the United States offered funding for programs for the remediation of dioxin risk areas and for public health programs. Special areas were also designed by the Vietnamese Government where medical and psychological help is given to those affected by Agent Orange.
