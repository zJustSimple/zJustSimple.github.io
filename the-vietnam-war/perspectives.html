Putting the Vietnam War into perspective

Cuprins
  • Immediate effects on Vietnam
  • Long-term effects on Vietnam
  • Effects on US soldiers

Immediate effects on Vietnam

The war had dramatic consequences for Vietnam as many lives were lost. Between 1 to 3 million Vietnamese civilians and soldiers are estimated to have died in the war. The conflict also significantly changed the nation’s relationship with other countries.

The involvement of the United States and its withdrawal played a major role in how the country later developed. During US military presence in South Vietnam the economy and society were profoundly transformed.

The end of the war was followed by a refugee crisis, as over 3 million people left Vietnam and the other neighbouring countries that were affected by the War. Vietnam remained impoverished and politically isolated until the 1980s when the government of Vietnam initiated a series of economic and political reforms, trying to integrate the country into the world economy. Since 2000, Vietnam's economic growth rate has been among the highest in the world.

Long-term effects on Vietnam

Despite the country’s recovery, the negative effects of the Vietnam War can still be witnessed today. The American bombing campaigns during the Vietnam War amounted to the longest and heaviest aerial bombardment in history, with more than 7,000,000 tons of bombs dropped in Southeast Asia. Many bombs failed to explode and still remain scattered throughout the country. This makes large areas of land impossible to cultivate and still causes many accidents and deaths. The landscape was also affected by the use of defoliants. These toxic chemical substances were used to defoliate forests and mangroves and destroy crops that supplied the enemy forces. The massive defoliation led to ecocide and residual contamination of soils in many areas of Vietnam.

Agent Orange and similar chemical substances used by the United States have also caused a considerable number of deaths and injuries. Between 2 to 4 million people were exposed to these substances during the war. Reports of possible health problems caused by defoliants have surfaced in the press since 1970, but few studies involving herbicide exposure were conducted. Negative health effects were not closely tested until after 1980 when a study was conducted on US veterans who served in sprayed areas and were found to be at elevated risk of developing health problems such as leukemia, lymphoma, sarcoma, and various other types of cancer. Studies on South Korean troops who served in Vietnam have revealed increased risks of diabetes and other disorders. After the end of the war, an elevated rate of birth defects in the Vietnamese population was attributed to the herbicide exposure. People who have been exposed to defoliants also experienced prolonged pain around the eyes and skin, fatigue, and gastrointestinal disorders.

Effects on US soldiers

The war had negative effects on both the physical and mental health of US soldiers. The soldiers were exposed to traumatic events and dangerous chemicals, and often had horrific injuries on top of this.

As a result, the soldiers experienced severe physical and psychological deterioration, and an increase in cases of rebellions during military service in Vietnam were reported.

After the war, rates of divorce, suicide, alcoholism, and drug use were high among veterans of the Vietnam War. Also, many suffered from Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD), a mental disorder that was officially recognized after the evaluation of a large number of American veterans serving during the Vietnam War. Consequently, the negative effect on US soldiers also affected their families and communities.
