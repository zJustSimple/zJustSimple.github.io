Romeo’s development throughout the play

Romeo is the male protagonist of Shakespeare’s Romeo and Juliet. His outer characterization is fairly brief: He is the only son of Lord Montague of Verona, which means he is a nobleman. We never learn his age, but he could be anything from 13 (like Juliet) to his early 20’s. He appears to have several friends, Mercutio and Benvolio being the most important ones.

As regards his inner characterization, Romeo is a complex character. This is also why it is difficult to establish whether he truly develops or not over the course of the play. We hear of him before meeting him: In Act 1, Scene 1, his parents and Benvolio talk about his mental state. He is depressed and cries constantly because he believes himself in love with a young noblewoman named Rosaline who does not return his feelings. This paints a picture of a young man who has extreme emotions.

Romeo then appears and complains about Rosaline saying that she will not “ope her lap to saint-seducing gold.” (1.1.207) He is essentially saying that she refuses to spread her legs even for gold. If we interpret this line as Romeo being angry that he could not bribe Rosaline to have sex with him, his talk of love does not seem very convincing. And as soon as he meets Juliet, he forgets all about Rosaline. At best, he comes across as immature and ignorant of what true love is, and at worst, he comes across as a selfish womanizer.

However, Lord Capulet – Romeo’s enemy – speaks highly of him in Act 1, Scene 5 where he describes him as “a virtuous and well-governed youth.” (1.5.67) And Romeo gets to demonstrate his qualities himself once he meets Juliet. His conversations with her sparkle with originality and passion, and this time he is truly in love. As he rhetorically asks upon seeing her for the first time: “Did my heart love till now?” (1.5.51)

Romeo agrees to marry Juliet right away and has his friend, Friar Lawrence, perform the wedding ceremony. This demonstrates Romeo’s impulsive nature, but it also shows his respect for Juliet’s wishes since he is willing to marry her and does not simply try to have sex with her. He therefore seems to be more mature than at the start.

After their secret wedding, Romeo does his best to avoid fighting Tybalt, Juliet’s hotheaded cousin, who is now also Romeo’s relative. Romeo acts like his sensible friend, Benvolio, and ignores Tybalt’s insults. Unfortunately, fate seems to overrule his decision as Tybalt kills Romeo’s friend Mercutio, which finally makes Romeo lose his temper and kill Tybalt. He is banished from Verona – and thereby Juliet – as punishment. These sad events mark a turning point in the play.

From now on, Romeo’s flair for extreme emotions takes over again. In Act 3, Scene 3, the sensitive Romeo reacts to the news of banishment by throwing himself on the ground and threatening to commit suicide. Almost ironically, it is a celibate priest, the Friar, who tells him to man up: “Art thou a man?” (3.3.108) This question echoes something Romeo said right after Mercutio’s murder where Romeo did not intervene: “O sweet Juliet,/ Thy beauty hath made me effeminate.” (3.1.109-110) We often talk of the power of love, but Romeo seems to think that his deep love for Juliet has made him less of a man. Possibly this is Shakespeare saying that love is a power that may also be destructive.

Just as Romeo was quick to marry Juliet, he immediately decides to kill himself with poison when he receives news of her death in Act 5, Scene 1. We might argue that he chooses the easy option, using poison which offers a quick death, whereas Juliet decides to use his dagger and stab herself. In any case, Romeo demonstrates his devotion to Juliet by choosing death over a life without her, just as he is kind enough to allow his rival in love, Paris, to be buried next to Juliet like himself.

Romeo: a tragic hero with a tragic flaw

It is relevant to discuss whether Romeo brought his tragic death on himself or whether the blame may be placed elsewhere. The answer seems to be a combination of the two. As outlined above, he is generally overly emotional and impulsive. If he had simply restrained himself from killing Tybalt or waited a day before committing suicide, the story might have had a happy ending!

However, other factors play a part as well. Society, the law, and their parents all prevent the two lovers from being together. And then there is the question of fate. Like Juliet, Romeo fatally embodies the tragic flaw of being very impulsive and controlled by his passions. Because of this defect within his character, he is doomed to fail. As the first prologue of the play says, the two lovers are “death-marked” (l. 9). The Elizabethans believed in fate, and this is why we see this character who cannot escape his destiny – which is to fall in love with Juliet and die.

Today, we tend to see the question of free will versus fate differently, just as parents’ power over their children has changed, too. If you are writing an essay or preparing for class, you might want to discuss how you see this and where you would place the responsibility for the sad outcome?
