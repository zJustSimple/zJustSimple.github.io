Juliet’s development throughout the play

Juliet is the female protagonist of Shakespeare’s Romeo and Juliet. As regards her outer characterization, she is the only daughter of Lord Capulet of Verona, which means she is a noblewoman. According to Romeo, she is very beautiful. Like him, we hear of Juliet before meeting her ourselves. In Act 1, Scene 2, her father says that “she hath not seen the change of fourteen years” (1.2.9), which means that she is just 13. This may seem extremely young to modern readers, but during the Elizabethan era the legal age of marriage for women was 12, so Juliet is old enough to marry. 

Juliet’s inner characterization is very interesting since she goes through a great development throughout the play. We first meet her in Act 1, Scene 3 when her mother informs her that she may be married to the good-looking Count Paris. Juliet shows no enthusiasm but replies like a dutiful daughter: “I’ll look to like, if looking liking move./ But no more deep will I endart mine eye/ Than your consent gives strength to make it fly.” (1.3.90-92) She is saying that she will do her best to like Paris but only to the extent her mother wishes. If her mother were to change her mind about Paris tomorrow, so would Juliet. Thus, our first impression of Juliet is of a very dutiful girl who has no will of her own.

In the Elizabethan era, women were expected to be virgins when marrying, especially if they were noblewomen, and we sense Juliet’s inexperience with sex in the play. In Act 1, Scene 3, her vulgar Nurse goes on about a sexual joke her late husband once made at Juliet’s expense (1.3.34-40), and the Nurse generally seems to think that sex and love is the same. Juliet is uncomfortable at this talk (1.3.52).

In Act 3, Scene 2, after Juliet has married Romeo, she awaits their wedding night with both anticipation and fear. In a lengthy soliloquy she asks the night to come and “hood my unmanned blood, bating in my cheeks,/ With thy black mantle, till strange love grow bold.” (3.2.14-15) She wants the night to conceal her blushing and shyness until she grows used to this strange new world.

Falling in love with Romeo and spending her wedding night with him helps Juliet to mature. Gradually, she starts thinking for herself and putting her own wishes over those of her parents. At first, she still relies on her Nurse for help; it is the Nurse who delivers messages between Juliet and Romeo and gets a rope ladder so Romeo can climb into Juliet’s room. However, the relationship between Juliet and the Nurse changes in Act 3, Scene 5 when the Nurse sides with Juliet’s parents and thinks that she should marry Paris instead. Juliet cannot forgive the Nurse for letting her down and angrily swears to herself that from now on they will go separate ways: “Go, counsellor,/ Thou and my bosom henceforth shall be twain.” (3.5.240-241)

Unlike Romeo, Juliet does not appear to have any friends her own age. She only has her parents – with whom she has a distant relationship – and her Nurse. Now she is more isolated than ever and seeks advice from Friar Lawrence, who talks her out of committing suicide. The fact that she chooses to distance herself from the Nurse, who has been her surrogate mother, shows us that she is growing up. Juliet is no longer an innocent, ignorant child but a determined young woman who no longer wants to compromise.

Compared to her lover, Juliet comes across as a more mature character whose emotions are more constant. While Romeo is ready to kill himself because he has to leave Verona, Juliet is prepared to endure the loss of her cousin Tybalt at Romeo’s hand, separation from Romeo while he is banished, drinking a frightening sleeping potion, and eventually stabbing herself to avoid a life without him.

Right before Juliet takes her own life in the end, Friar Lawrence offers her an escape by suggesting she become a nun. This would mean a peaceful life where her parents cannot marry her off. But her determination never wavers, and she has the bravery to stab herself to death, which is no easy solution. Thus, it is an important point that Juliet does not kill herself out of feminine weakness but because her feelings for Romeo are so strong that she refuses to compromise and live on without him.

Juliet: a tragic heroine with a tragic flaw

We could also discuss whether Juliet is to blame for her own tragic end or whether it is possible to place the blame elsewhere. Like with Romeo, the answer is probably a combination of the two. As described above, she turns into a very determined young lady who refuses to let others dictate her life. She could have chosen to marry Paris, who is both wealthy and good-looking, or she might even have chosen life as a nun. Instead, we could argue that she uses her free will to follow Romeo - even in death.

However, we must consider the question of fate. Like Romeo, Juliet fatally embodies the tragic flaw of being impulsive, uncompromising, and controlled by her passions. Because of this defect within her character, she seems doomed to fail. Just as the first prologue of the play says, the two lovers are “death-marked” (l. 9).

Other factors influence Juliet’s actions and tragic end, too. Society, the law, and their parents all prevent the young lovers from being together. During the Elizabethan era, daughters were expected to do as their fathers wanted, and it was illegal to marry without your parents’ consent. Today, many things have changed, just as we tend to believe in free will rather than fate. If you are preparing an essay or for class, you may want to discuss your thoughts on this and where you would place the responsibility for the tragic outcome?
