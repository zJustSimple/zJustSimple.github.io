Granger and his group

The living books

Granger and his group do not appear until the end of Ray Bradbury’s novel Fahrenheit 451. They live outside the entertainment society, near the railroad track and in the mountains south of town (Part 3, 75%). Guy Montag first sees the group at night from a distance, as they warm themselves by a fire (Part 3, 63%).

Granger seems to be the leader of the group, at least he is their spokesman. He explains to Guy the idea behind the group of academics. The men have made it their mission to memorize books, because books are burned and destroyed in this society. Granger himself has written a book called The Fingers in the Glove; the Proper Relationship between the Individual and Society.

The group also includes: Fred Clement, the former holder of the Thomas Hardy Chair at Harvard University; Dr. Simmons, a specialist in Ortega y Gasset at UCLA; Prof. West, a scholar of ethics at Columbia University; and the clergyman Father Padover (Part 3, 71%). They all have bearded faces. Their beards are neat and they have clean hands (Part 3, 63%).

The men around Granger carry a "special silence" (Part 3, 63%), as they take time to think and reflect on things. When they speak to each other, their voices express curiosity and wonder, from which Guy concludes that they can talk about anything (Part 3, 63%).

Hospitality and helpfulness

The group of academics watched Guy's chase on a portable TV. Although Guy has escaped, the TV talks about being on his trail. Granger knows this is a lie, and he can foresee that soon a random man will be sacrificed for a "show" (Part 3, 67%) - he will be passed off as Guy and killed (p. 170). Granger describes the state's approach as "Hell" (Part 3, 71%).

Granger is very hospitable to Guy. He invites the escaped man to join the group quietly. The men stand up out of courtesy to the guest. Granger immediately welcomes Guy, offers him a seat, and pours him coffee. He is very humorous and direct. He openly tells Guy that he looks "like hell" and that's how the group knows he must be the runaway fireman (Part 3, 78%).

Granger also gives Guy a potion that changes his body odor so that he no longer has to fear the tracking dog (Part 3, 65%). He makes a very trusting impression, putting his hand on Guy's arm and saying, " ‘Welcome back from the dead’ ." (Part 3, 71%).

From anger to pacifism

One day, Granger knocked down a fireman to protect his library. Such dissidents, like Granger and the academics, number in the thousands, all wandering like vagrants along the network of tracks around the city. For the last twenty years or so, they have been building a network (Part 3, 75%).

Granger says that the resisters used to be all alone with their anger (Part 3, 71%). But now they are completely pacifist (Part 3, 75%). They are left alone by the townspeople (p. 174). People know that they pose no threat and are therefore not hunted down, because "a few crackpots with verses in their heads can't touch them" (Part 3, 78%).

Granger is particularly impressed when people help shape, change or mold the world. He greatly admires his grandfather, who was a sculptor. When the grandfather died, Granger realized that he was saddened mostly because of the things the grandfather had designed and now would no longer make (part 3, 82%). For Granger, then, what a person does in the world is more important than the person themselves.

A life for literature

The academics think that the works of world literature are safely stored in their heads (Part 3, 75%). They want to save the books from extinction, so to speak. They regard themselves as "libraries inside" (Part 3, 76%) and "dust-jackets for books" (Part 3, 76%). Granger says about himself: " ‘I am Plato's Republic.’ " (Part 3, 73%). Simmons is Marcus Aurelius, another of the academics has taken on the book Gulliver's Travels (Part 3, 73%). They also know by heart other works by the most famous philosophers and writers.

Selflessness for humanity and peace

Granger makes sure that the men in the group don't see themselves as anything special. They are simply supposed to serve their cause. There is a selflessness, even a self-sacrifice, in the academics' behavior. Granger urges his followers not to forget:  " ‘You're not important. You're not anything. Some day the load we're carrying with us may help someone.’ " (Part 3, 96%).

The academics characterize themselves as an "odd minority crying in the wilderness." (Part 3, 76%). They simply hope to be of use to the world once the approaching war is over. They want to pass the books on to their children, but they also know that you cannot force people to take an interest in literature. They hope that one day people will realize on their own that this is why they have to read again, because ignorance always means war in the long run (Part 3, 76%). 

Granger is confident that after the war there will be a chance to educate people again with the help of the organization. He believes that, fortunately, the human species has the ability to start over again and again (Part 3, 76%). He believes that people are capable of learning and will perhaps one day learn to finally stop waging war (Part 3, 96%).

When the war is over, the city lies in ruins: the group sets off north, towards the bombed-out city . Granger hopes to be able to help the people to develop again a memory and a new culture.
