Captain Beatty

Cuprins
  • The friendly spy
  • The unscrupulous protectors of peace of mind
  • The unsatisfied intellectual

The friendly spy

Captain Beatty is Guy Montag's supervisor in the fire department. His job is to lead a group of firemen whose mission is to burn all the books in the city. The houses where books are hidden are also burned. Beatty accepts the fact that sometimes people die in the process.

Captain Beatty has black hair, soot-colored eyebrows, and bluish-looking skin where his beard has been shaved off (Part 1, 45%). He has a burned and sparkling face as a result of the many fires he has experienced (Part 1,54%). He smokes his brass pipe all the time, but he has bright red gums and white teeth (Part 1, 75%). His voice is very soft and he can make convincing speeches with it (Part 1, 82%).

The captain is always emphatically nonchalant, confident and self-assured. He laughs to himself when he notices that others are unsettled (Part 1, 37%). When he pays Guy a visit while Guy is sick, he strolls in and has a peaceful expression on his face (Part 1, 75%). He acts quite understanding and says it is quite normal for Guy to want to take a few days off sick.

When Guy suggests that he may have hidden a book, Beatty initially responds sympathetically: " ‘A natural error’ ", he says, pointing out only that the book must be delivered to the fire department for burning within 24 hours (Part 1, 89%). In reality, Beatty is out to spy on Guy, because he has strong suspicions about him.

The unscrupulous protectors of peace of mind

Beatty knows a lot and seems to have the system figured out. He says you have to understand how everything is connected, and it's important to know the history of the fire department if you're in this profession. Beatty enlightens Guy about this in detail, showing that he is a talented speaker (Part 1, 71%He summarizes the spiritual history of mankind as follows: " ‘Out of the nursery into the college and back to the nursery’ " (Part 1, 78%). Therefore, he speaks quite openly about the fact that people in today's society are becoming dumbed down. 

Instead of doing something against this system of stupidity, Beatty adapts completely and acts as an executive state power. He calls himself and his colleagues the "Happiness Boys" (Part 1, 89%) and seems to have no scruples about his profession. This is evident, for example, when Beatty interrogates a woman who has hidden books at home and who would rather burn herself with her books than be arrested (Part 1, 54%). Beatty slaps the woman "in the face with amazing objectivity " (Part 1, 41%).

The unsatisfied intellectual

Beatty is himself very well-read, although he fights precisely against this book knowledge. He believes that as a captain he should have such knowledge, but that he himself is sometimes amazed at how much he knows (Part 1, 57%). However, he classifies books as something very negative. " ‘A book is a loaded gun’ " (Part 1, 85%), is Beatty's opinion. He expresses that those " ‘who want to make everyone unhappy with conflicting theory and thought’ " must be fought (Part 1, 89%).

In his view, fictional literary works disturb the peace because the freely invented characters often make some minority in society feel attacked or offended (Part 1, 82%). He calls the specialized literatures of sociology and philosophy "slippery stuff" (Part 1, 89%) because they raise questions and one does not find clear answers in them to the important questions of life, which would disturb the peace of mind.

During his visit to the Montags, Beatty tells the real story of the society in great detail. There was less and less education offered over time, people became more and more occupied with superficial entertainment. At some point, books existed only in the form of summaries, then they were banned completely.

It is particularly evident that Beatty has read a great many books when he wants to show Guy Montag that he knows about Montag hiding some books at home. He quotes from various works and uses these quotes to create an invented dialogue that could have taken place between him and Montag (Part 2, 90% f.). In this way, he wants to provoke Montag into a confession. He finally forces Guy to burn down his own house (Part 3, 4% ff.).

Beatty, true to the system and conforming, defends book burning. He is like a kind of executive arm of what the majority of people want. Faber describes him as a very problematic figure: " ‘But remember that the Captain belongs to the most dangerous enemy of truth and freedom, the solid unmoving cattle of the majority. Oh, God, the terrible tyranny of the majority’ " (Part 2, 95%).

Beatty is a complex and contradictory character. In reality, Beatty, who always presents himself as cool and confident, is probably very dissatisfied and unhappy with his own life and with his job. Guy only notices this after Beatty is already dead: Beatty kept provoking Guy and threatening him when Guy confronted him with the flamethrower. When Guy kills him, Beatty makes no effort to fight back or save himself. He probably wished for his death (Part 3, 18%).
