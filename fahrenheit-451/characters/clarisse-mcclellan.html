Clarisse McClellan

Cuprins

The young, curious, and empathic neighbor

Clarisse McClellan is the new neighbor of the main character Guy Montag in Ray Bradbury’s novel Fahrenheit 451 (Part 1, 5%). She is seventeen years old (Part 1, 6%), but Guy thinks she looks much older (Part 1, 31%). She lives with her parents and uncle in a house on the same street as Guy. Before, the family lived in Chicago (Part 1, 86%).

When Guy sees her for the first time, Clarisse is wearing a white dress that rustles when she moves (Part 1, 3%). She has dark, sparkling eyes with a lot of charisma (Part 1, 3%). Guy thinks she is " beautiful(…) astonishing, in fact" (Part 1, 11%). Her face is narrow, pale, and has an expression of wonder and thirst for knowledge (Part 1, 3%). This is because Clarisse wants to get to the bottom of the things that concern her, not just indulge in factual knowledge (Part 1, 86%). 

For Guy, it seems as if Clarisse's face reflects the faces of the people she is looking at (Part 1, 6%). This impression is probably due to the fact that she feels a lot of empathy for other people (Part 1, 6%). 

Clarisse has a childlike imagination and sometimes behaves in a naïve manner. She tells Guy that there is a man in the moon(Part 1, 9%), and that you can find out if someone is in love by holding a dandelion flower under your chin: If the color rubs off, one is in love. She does the trick for Guy and it turns out that she is in love (Part 1, 28%). When it rains, she catches the drops with her mouth open (Part 1, 29%). She is very close to nature, walks in the woods and collects butterflies (Part 1, 29%). 

Clarisse becomes very important to Guy very quickly. They see each other every day for a week and walk to the street corner together (Part 1, 37%f.). Guy has the feeling that he has known her for years. She openly tells him that she likes him and leaves small gifts from nature for him on his doorstep as a sign of affection: a bouquet of roses she has picked herself, a few chestnuts she has collected, or autumn leaves she has attached to paper (Part 1, 38%).

She speaks very honestly and directly to Guy. Although she has just met him, she asks him if he is happy (Part 1, 9%). She talks quite a bit and tells Guy her views and what she is up to. Clarisse plays an important role in Guy's transformation in that her interest in him and her questions about his life act as a kind of catalyst for him: he begins to question the world.

Clarisse as an outsider

All of Clarisse's family members are outsiders. From her uncle, Clarisse has learned a lot about how life used to be (Part 1, 40%) when her grandfather a child was. As a result, she knows more than most other people who have forgotten the past. There is a lot of talking and laughing in their home (Part 1, 20%). The family is heavily supervised (Part 1, 86%).

Clarisse is non-conformist and able to think for herself. She says about herself that she is "crazy" (Part 1, 5%). She skips school and thinks they don't miss her there. Clarisse criticizes the methods at school, where questions are not allowed and independent thinking is nipped in the bud. Students are constantly kept mindlessly occupied with television, sports, and dictation (Part 1, 39%).

She likes to walk alone at night, sometimes until sunrise (Part 1, 6%). She also likes to observe people (Part 1, 7%) and listen to them when she rides the subway (Part 1, 42%). Sometimes she does nothing but think. These behaviors are not considered normal in the society Clarisse lives in and should be prevented and fought against. That is why she is sent to a psychiatrist (Part 1, 29%).

Clarisse is said to be unsociable. However, she herself believes she is sociable, because she likes the company of people with whom she can have real conversations. Her peers, however, occupy themselves differently: they race around in cars, do dares, harass people, riot, fight, and even kill each other (Part 1, 40%f.). Clarisse has no interest in this, and she is afraid of the other teenagers. That is why she has no friends (Part 1, 42%). She used to have friends, but six of them have already been shot and ten others have lost their lives in car accidents (Part 1, 42%).

Clarisse disappears from the scene again soon after meeting Guy. She was probably run over (Part 1, 66%). Her family moves out of the house after her daughter's death (ibid.).

Guy's boss, Captain Beatty, may be Clarisse's killer. He comments on her death in a way that could raise suspicions that he deliberately staged the car accident: He says that it is good that she died, because she was unhappy (Part 1, 88%). Later, he makes very condescending remarks about the late teenager: she had used her influence on Guy and was to blame for his change (Part 3, 1% f.).
