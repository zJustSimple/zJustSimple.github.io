The book-loving professor

Faber is an important character in the novel Fahrenheit 451 by Ray Bradbury. He is an old man whom Guy Montag met by chance in the park. He was a professor of English literature forty years ago, but was then dismissed because hardly anyone was still studying at the faculty (Part 2, 8%). He almost always stays at home and looks accordingly: His skin and hair are white as a wall and even his blue eyes have faded (Part 2, 23%).

When he meets Guy the fireman in the park, Faber has hidden books with him and at first he is afraid that Guy will hurt him. But then he gains trust in Guy, shows him the books, and tells him about himself. He even gives Guy his address, with the comment " ‘...in case you decide to be angry with me.’ " (Part 2, 10%).

Currently, Faber lives alone, secretly tinkering with inventions (Part 2, 20%) and thinking about what has gone wrong in society. He is opposing the system, but feels alone and is afraid of what would happen if he rebelled (Part 2, 41%).

Faber is very shy until Guy contacts him again. When Guy calls him, Faber ends the conversation as quickly as possible (cf. Part 2, 10%). When Guy visits him, Faber at first assures him that he has done nothing, and makes Guy swear that he really came alone (Part 2, 20%). He is very afraid of being arrested.

Faber says about himself that he likes to talk about the "meaning of things," that is, he likes to philosophize (Part 2, 10%). He is not religious (Part 2, 23%). He isolates himself from the propaganda in the state by not having TV screens, and when he rides the subway, he puts plugs in his ears to avoid hearing the propaganda broadcasts (Part 2, 41%).

Books have a very high value for Faber. He feels something for books. He believes that they play an important role in terms of mankind's ability to self-reflect: " ‘The books are to remind us what asses and fools we are.’ " (Part 2, 38%). Nevertheless, he finds that it is not only the books that are missing in society. He is concerned with the content of the books. He thinks that people no longer experience the quality that books have: They give important details of the truth (Part 2, 33% f.). People also lack, on the one hand, the leisure to reflect on what they have read and, on the other hand, the right to act in accordance with what they have learned (Part 2, 33% f.).

Guy's careful teacher

Guy wants Faber to become his mentor. When Faber refuses out of fear, Guy begins tearing the Bible that he brought him page by page. Faber cannot stand this and so he agrees to become Guy's teacher (Part 2, 44%). Faber is very cautious. When Guy says he can get books, Faber warns, " ‘You're running a risk.’ " (Part 2, 36%).

Faber regrets very much that he did not do something about the developments in society earlier (Part 2, 31%). He considers himself a coward (Part 2, 28%). Now, together with Guy, he recognizes a new chance to make a difference. The men conspire: Faber plans to contact an unemployed book printer so that together they could reproduce the books he and Guy have (Part 2, 26%).

In reality, Faber would like to help start a revolution, only so far he has felt quite alone in doing so. He jokingly suggests that books could be hidden in the houses of firemen and then denounced in order to destroy the fire department piece by piece. Guy likes the idea and this in turn scares Faber, but at the same time he is a little inspired by the replaced to avoid repetition of infiltrating the fire department (Part 2, 86%f.). He agrees to help Guy.

Faber has secretly built radio capsules with the help of which he can maintain contact with the carrier as with a radio (Part 2, 51%). He gives Guy a capsule and now tries to help Guy from home by speaking advice into his ears while Guy is talking to Captain Beatty or is with his wife. He mostly advises Guy to be careful and encourages him not to be intimidated by Beatty (Part 2, 92%).

Faber also reads book passages to Guy, who hears them through the radio capsule as he falls asleep. In this way, Guy is supposed to memorize the passages unconsciously (Part 2, 82%). Faber wants to help Guy make independent decisions. He hopes, however, that Guy will not rejoin Beatty, for he is a dangerous enemy of truth and freedom. He represents "the terrible tyranny of the majority," Faber sighs (Part 2, 85%). Faber himself is an individualist.

After Guy kills Captain Beatty, Faber helps Guy escape and saves his life. He lets Guy come to him and hands him old clothes and whiskey, which Guy uses to cover up his smell so that the mechanical tracking dog will not find him (Part 3, 43%). He gives Guy the tip that groups of like-minded academics can be found in the country along the railroad tracks (Part 3, 35%).

He himself now also plans to flee immediately and to go to St. Louis before the war breaks out. There, Guy is to make contact with him again one day in the future (Part 3, 35%). Faber has become quite lively again as a result of his conspiracy with Guy. He has gained new energy and found a new drive. He says himself, " ‘I feel alive for the first time in years. (...) For a little while I'm not afraid’ " (Part 3, 35%).
