Happiness and compliance

Cuprins
  • Consumer Society
  • Not quite happy
  • The tyranny of the majority
  • The other side of the coin

Consumer Society

The society Bradbury describes is characterized by very strong capitalism. The "happiness" of the citizens is mainly of a material nature: strong consumption is what people desire. People distract themselves from reality (that a war is threatening, for example) through wild parties and lots of alcohol (Mildred thinks she is just hungover when she wakes up the morning after her suicide attempt). People are "well-fed" and enjoy themselves all the time (p. 89), for which there are amusement parks where young people spend all their time (Part 2, 8%). Families live in houses and have fast cars. Banks are open around the clock. 

The billboards are seventy meters high (p. 23), and even when riding the subway you don't have a quiet minute because loud advertisements are playing from the loudspeakers all the time.

Even religion is used for marketing purposes: "Christ is one of the 'family' now.(...) He's a regular peppermint stick now,(...) when he isn't making veiled references to certain commercial products that every worshiper absolutely needs." (Part 2, 26%), says the former literature professor Faber, who conspires with Guy Montag against the system.

Not quite happy

Guy changes in the course of the story. In the beginning, he behaves according to the system and enjoys his job as a fireman. His new neighbor, seventeen-year-old Clarisse, has seeded doubt in his soul by simply asking Guy if he was happy during a conversation on the street (Part 1, 9%). He finally has to admit to himself that he is not happy (Part 1, 11%). Guy subsequently recognizes the cruelty of society and develops a thirst for knowledge, which he tries to quiet with books, but this is illegal.

"Happiness is important. Fun is everything. And yet I kept sitting there saying to myself, I'm not happy, I'm not happy." (Part 1, 94%) - this is how Guy Montag communicates his feelings to his wife Mildred. She replies, "I am. (...) And proud of it" (Part 1, 94%). 

Mildred does not seem to be really happy at all, although she loudly affirms it. Deep down, she is highly depressed, which is made clear by her suicide attempt, which she immediately forgets or would like to forget.

Mildred is well- adjusted and conforming, and her hobbies are shaped by norms. At one point, Guy asks her why she actually goes to see a movie at a friend's house and not just at home, and she replies, "it's nice visiting" (Part 1, 71%). She does not say "I", but "it". She does not express her opinion, but the one that is considered correct and normal by the general public.

Beatty, who points out that with the books all the unhappiness in the world is also burned, is not happy either, although he does his job conscientiously and defends and justifies the system. He never explains his dissatisfaction and he presents his life as if he is completely satisfied with it: "I just like solid entertainment." (Part 1, 88%). However, he seems to be secretly desperate and tired of life and deliberately provokes Guy until Guy kills him (see characterization of Beatty).

The tyranny of the majority

In a utopian society, there should be no differences between people. Captain Beatty describes it this way: "We must all be alike. (...) made equal. Each man the image of every other; then all are happy, for there are no mountains to make them cower, to judge themselves against." (Part 1, 85%). The highest goal is the happiness of the individual and it looks in Fahrenheit 451 as if the majority of the population is satisfied.

The fact that no one is supposed to be different from the others naturally brings with it certain disadvantages. For example, the word "intellectual" has become a dirty word. All people who stand out from others because of a special talent are now despised, discriminated against, or eliminated.

It is the same with those people who show a different behavior. For example, the very intelligent and fun-loving seventeen-year-old Clarisse, who can think for herself, is mysteriously run over by a car. Afterwards, Captain Beatty presents it as if she had been unhappy anyway and her death was therefore positive (Part 1, 87%). The dissidents must live in fear and fear for their lives. They are arbitrarily killed by the police or the fire department so that they cannot cause trouble (see section "Democracy and dictatorship").

Faber calls the conformity of the people and its threatening and oppressive power a "terrible tyranny of the majority. He speaks of a "solid unmoving cattle" (Part 2, 95%) to refer to the conformist majority of citizens, whom he characterizes as will-less animals.

In a way, the political system in Fahrenheit 451 is brings to mind real totalitarian regimes, be it Stalinist or Nazi, under which the conformist masses silently accepted the persecution and killing of dissidents and intellectuals.

The other side of the coin

The conformist, materially oriented people in Fahrenheit 451 are numbed all the time by the constant overstimulation of the mass media. Their lives consist of work and a meaningless series of rapid, superficial entertainment. Intense feelings are systematically suppressed: they treat television as their family (see section "Television and Manipulation"). People are always kept in a state of "mediocrity": Satisfied, dumbed down, uncritical.

The majority of citizens have no desire to deal with real life, with all the difficulties that go with it. That is why they have stopped reading, because books "show the pores in the face of life. The comfortable people want only wax moon faces, poreless, hairless, expressionless." (Part 2, 31%), according to Faber.

However, no human being can be permanently happy. Doubts, fears and the desire for change are part of human life. In order to be able to recognize happiness at all, people must experience both highs and lows - without the lows, he would not even recognize happiness as such.

The desired permanent state of happiness comes at a high price. Clarisse says that the students are exhausted in the evening: They have no choice "but go to bed or head for a Fun Park to bully people around, break windowpanes in the Window Smasher place (...) Or go out in the cars and race on the streets, trying to see how close you can get to lamp-posts, playing >chicken< and >knock hub-caps<." (Part 1, 45%). The brutalized and frustrated teenagers kill themselves, for example, by running each other over with a car or shooting each other for "fun" (Part 1, 45%).

The strong use of force by the police and the fire department is also seen more as an entertainment by the general population. Therefore, Guy’s helicopter chase and his (faked) execution are made into an exciting live broadcast on television (see section Television and Manipulation).

Burning the houses of those people who own banned books does not scare most people, but is a perverse amusement for them, a "small sideshow indeed" (Part 2, 41%), as Faber calls it. The fire department and the book burnings were not actually necessary, because people chose conformism and the rejection of intelligence for themselves in Fahrenheit 451.
