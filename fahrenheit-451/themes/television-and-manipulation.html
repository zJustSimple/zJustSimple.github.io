The TV as a friend and family

Television and its potential use for manipulation are important aspects of Ray Bradbury’s novel Fahrenheit 451. Both the dissident Faber and Granger own only small, traditional televisions, whose programs they watch critically in moderation.

For Mildred Montag, on the other hand, as for the average citizen, television takes the most important place in life. Mildred, the wife of the main character Guy Montag, represents the average citizen of the supposedly utopian society in Fahrenheit 451.

She spends most of her time in the "parlor" (Part 1, 25%), a room in which three walls already consist of TV screens. However, Mildred would like the fourth wall to be converted into a TV screen as soon as possible (Part 1, 29%).

Mildred finds herself in the midst of television broadcasting, surrounded by moving images. She can take on a small role herself in interactive broadcasts (Part 1, 27%). Through what appears to be a personal television host, Mildred is made to feel that the people on TV care about her: "The converter attachment (...) automatically supplied her name whenever the announcer addressed his anonymous audience (...). He was a friend, no doubt of it, a good friend." (Part 1, 92%).

Mildred builds an emotional bond with her television program. She even refers to the people on TV as her family (Part 1, 69%). She also does not want children. With her husband, she maintains only a careless, loveless relationship. Guy and Mildred also sleep in separate beds, which are once again separated by a glass wall (Part 1, 67%). Guy is haunted by the feeling when he lies in his bed that he is "on a winter island" (Part 1, 58%), so cold is his married life.

When Guy is very unwell and feels ill, it can be seen that Mildred's television program is more important than caring for her ill husband: he asks her to turn off the television, but she only wants to turn it down (Part 1, 69%). She does not grant him this wish. Instead, she coolly and mercilessly tells her husband that he is not sick at all. She then talks about important things: yesterday's television program (Part 1, 71%).

Harmful side effects

The television program, as presented in the novel, is like fireworks or, as Guy perceives it, it is like a "concussion" (Part 1, 63%): the loud music and the many colors and movements result in a complete sensory overload: the viewer is "sucked up in a gigantic vacuum" and drowns " in music and pure cacophony" (Part 1, 63%).

Due to this constant exposure to sound and the many visual impressions, the ability to think of the viewers, who often expose themselves to the TV program, suffers massively. Mildred, for example, has completely lost her imagination. That's why she can't do anything with books: "Books aren't people. You read and I look around, but there isn't anybody" (Part 2, 5%), she complains when Guy reads to her.

Both Guy's and Mildred's memories seem to have been greatly damaged by the enormous media consumption. Neither Mildred nor Guy can remember when and where they met the other. But instead of reacting with consternation, Mildred makes "an odd little laugh that went up and up" (Part 1, 60%) when she realizes that she can't remember. To her, the problem does not seem to be a serious one.

The empty content

The television program does not offer anything for discerning viewers. It consists of fast, flashy entertainment, commercials and lots of action. For example, Mildred is talking to her friends about the "five-minute romance" (Part 2, 62%) they saw yesterday. Not only are books only available as summaries in this society, but films must also be kept as short as possible, because viewers have only a short attention span.

For example, Mildred, who has spent her day in the TV room, talks to her husband about what she has seen in this dialogue:

‘I had a nice evening’, she said (...).

 ‘What doing?’

‘The parlor.’

 ‘What was on?’

 ‘Programs.’

 ‘What programs?’

 ‘Some of the best ever.’

 ‘Who?’

 ‘Oh, you know, the bunch.’

 ‘Yes, the bunch, the bunch, the bunch...’" (Part 1, 69%)

Here it is clear that Mildred cannot give any concrete answers to Guy's questions. She does not tell him what the programs were called, why they were particularly good, or who was in them. She also describes her evening as "nice," indicating that the program does not evoke any particular feelings in her: it only manages to make her feel pleasant, but not particularly joyful, not angry or sad.

As continuous entertainment, television acts like a narcotic on Mildred and the other viewers. The empty content of the programs prevents them from ever thinking too closely about anything. In Fahrenheit 451, the distribution of this medium among the people has contributed to the displacement of reading books .

Critical thinking

Trivial television programming robs viewers of any critical thinking. Through the constant sprinkling of programs that never address real problems or even real issues, viewers unlearn to think on their own.

Here is an example of an "argument" from a television program:

 ‘Well, everything will be all right now.’(...)

‘Oh, don't be too sure’ (...)

‘Now, don't get angry!’

‘Who's angry?’

 ‘You are!’

 ‘I am?’

 ‘You're mad!’

‘Why whould I be mad?’

 ‘Because!’(Part 1, 65%)

Here, the audience is supposed to be captivated by the dramatic conversation of an apparent conflict, but without ever having to think about a concrete problem, because this is not addressed. The situation seems absurd, but is not questioned by Mildred.

The loss of critical thinking, which is promoted by television, then manifests itself in the fact that viewers only think superficially in real life as well. This can also be seen, for example, when Mildred and her friends "talk politics" after a TV debate: The only thing they discuss are the appearances of the two presidential candidates. Not a word is said about the important aspects of policy in which the two candidates differ

The Fake Execution of Guy Montag

The chase for Guy Montag becomes exciting television entertainment as the manhunt is broadcast live for viewers. They are even called upon during the course of the show to help in the search and look outside to see if they spot Guy Montag. Guy eventually escapes from the police. But this is not communicated to the viewers.

Granger explains it to Guy: 

'They're faking. You threw them off at the river. They can't admit it. They know they can hold their audience only so long. The show's got to have a snap ending, quick! (…) So they're sniffing for a scape-goat to end things with a bang. Watch. They'll catch Montag in the next five minutes!' (Part 3, 67%)

Since Guy cannot be found, an innocent man is passed off as him and killed. " ‘They didn't show the man's face in focus. Did you notice? (...) They scrambled it just enough to let the imagination take over." (Part 3, 71%), Granger comments. The whole process seems very rehearsed and it is clear that this is not the first time that an innocent person has had to pay for the actions of another - the TV makers know exactly what they are doing.

Indeed, the live television broadcast of Guy Montag's assassination is a fake in addition to the already perverse media spectacle that is being made of it. It is a good example of the manipulation of the viewers.
