Jack

Cuprins
  • Outer characterization
  • Inner characterization

    • He disregards the rules
    • He is a bully and becomes increasingly violent

Outer characterization

Jack is the second main character in William Golding’s Lord of the Flies. He is the head of a choir and already has authority over the other choirboys. Initially, he wears a formal outfit including a black square cap and a long black cloak (p. 15): “Inside the floating cloak he was tall, thin, and bony: and his hair was red beneath the black cap. His face was crumpled and freckled, and ugly without silliness.” (p. 16)

Jack becomes increasingly dirty over the course of the novel and applies war-paint to himself. This allows him to act without self-consciousness as he becomes increasingly powerful and cruel. When he declares himself chief of a new tribe, he turns up “stark naked save for paint and a belt”, but “he was safe from shame or self-consciousness behind the mask of his paint” (p. 154).

Like Ralph, at the end of the novel his appearance is described from the naval officer’s perspective: “A little boy who wore the remains of an extraordinary black cap on his red hair” (p. 224). Here, readers are reminded that Jack is in fact just a child.

Inner characterization

Jack is a natural leader

Like Ralph, Jack asserts his personality and his authority early on. Unlike Ralph, Jack’s form of leadership involves making the boys excited and giving them orders. From the beginning, Jack has authority over the choir; he is described as “the boy who controlled them” (p. 15). He immediately claims leadership over all the other boys as well: “ ‘I ought to be chief,’ said Jack with simple arrogance, ‘because I’m chapter chorister and head boy. I can sing C sharp.’ ” (p. 18)

When the other boys vote for Ralph instead, Jack is embarrassed, but he soon accepts Ralph’s offer that he should lead the choir as hunters (p. 20). However, he repeatedly undermines Ralph’s authority by following his own desires and attracting the others’ attention away from the ideas Ralph has set out. For example, when Ralph suggests that they should build a signal fire, Jack takes matters into his own hands:

‘A fire! Make a fire!’

At once half the boys were on their feet. Jack clamoured among them, the conch forgotten. 

‘Come on! Follow me!’

[…] All at once the crowd swayed towards the island and were gone – following Jack. (p. 37)

He disregards the rules

As the previous quotation suggests, Jack does not believe the island’s rules should apply to him. When Ralph establishes the rule that only the person holding the conch can speak, Jack ironically immediately breaks it: 

‘I’ll give the conch to the next person to speak. He can hold it when he’s speaking. […] And he won’t be interrupted. Except by me.’

Jack was on his feet. 

‘We’ll have rules!’ he cried excitedly. ‘Lots of rules! Then when anyone breaks ’em -’ (pp. 31-32) 

This suggests that Jack wants the others to follow the rules, and he is more interested in punishing people who break the rules than in enforcing them through fairness and morality. Later, Jack takes his view even further:

‘The rules!’ shouted Ralph, ‘you’re breaking the rules!’

‘Who cares?’

Ralph summoned his wits. 

‘Because the rules are the only thing we’ve got!’

But Jack was shouting against him.

‘Bollocks to the rules! We’re strong – we hunt! If there’s a beast, we’ll hunt it down!’ (p. 99)

Jack’s attitude destroys the system established by Ralph and Piggy. 

He is a bully and becomes increasingly violent

Jack is also a bully, finding power and authority in his abuse of others. At first, he abuses Piggy as a stand-in for Ralph. When Piggy criticizes him for letting the fire out, Jack gives in to his violent nature and hits him: “This from Piggy, and the wails of agreement from some of the hunters drove Jack to violence. […] He took a step, and able at last to hit someone, stuck his fist into Piggy’s stomach.” (p. 75)

Jack continues to bully the other boys (particularly Piggy). His attitude takes a particularly sinister turn when he leads the others in play-acting killing a pig and encourages them to get carried away: “Robert was screaming and struggling with the strength of frenzy. Jack had him by the hair and was brandishing his knife.” (p. 125). He becomes increasingly violent and savage, especially when he breaks away from Ralph to form his own “tribe”. His cruelty reaches a point of no return when he ties up and tortures a boy without even giving a reason (p. 176).

Jack sets up a dictatorship

Jack establishes his own tribe as a dictatorship, where he rules by decree rather than allowing the boys to vote or using a system like the conch shell for having meetings. He controls the boys by manipulating their emotions and encouraging them to lose control, appealing to the darker and wilder sides of their characters. 

His power is cemented when he makes the boys do the ritualistic dance he invented until they become hysterical and collectively murder Simon (pp. 167-169). It is notable that after this point he is referred to in the narrative as “the Chief” rather than as “Jack”, suggesting that he has lost his humanity. Eventually, he has so much power that he can order the others to hunt and kill their former chief Ralph. 

However, at the end of the novel his authority fades in comparison with a real adult: 

‘Who’s the boss here?’

‘I am,’ said Ralph loudly.

A little boy who wore the remains of an extraordinary black cap on his red hair […] started forward, then changed his mind and stood still. (p. 224)
