Piggy

Piggy is the third main character in William Golding’s Lord of the Flies. He is an intelligent, overweight boy who has asthma and wears thick glasses (p. 1). The way he speaks suggests that he might come from a lower social class than the other boys: “ ‘I’m sorry I been such a time. Them fruit -’ ” (p. 5).

Cuprins
  • Piggy is an outsider and a victim
  • He is intelligent
  • Piggy is loyal to Ralph

Piggy is an outsider and a victim

Because of his appearance and his lower-class status, Piggy seems like an outsider from the beginning, and the other boys treat him as such. We never find out Piggy’s real name, and the other boys thoughtlessly use this cruel nickname. The other boys are often united by laughing at him: “A storm of laughter arose and even the tiniest child joined in. For the moment the boys were a closed circuit of sympathy with Piggy on the outside.” (pp. 17-18)

In particular, he becomes the victim of Jack’s hatred, who initially attacks Piggy instead of openly challenging Ralph: “He took a step, and able at last to hit someone, stuck his fist into Piggy’s stomach.” (p. 75)

He is intelligent

Piggy understands the motivations of the others, including Jack: “ ‘He hates you too, Ralph- […] I know about people. I know about me. And him. He can’t hurt you: but if you stand out of the way he’d hurt the next thing. And that’s me.’ ” (p. 101)

He is the cleverest of the boys and almost the only one who can think logically. From the beginning, he recognizes that they need to “do something” and not just play (p. 10). In some ways, he is more mature than the others and criticizes them for acting “ ‘like a pack of kids’ ” and failing to “ ‘act proper’ ” (p. 45). However, in other ways, he acts more childishly than the other older boys, whining when they don’t listen to him: “ ‘I got the conch!’ said Piggy indignantly. ‘Ralph – they ought to shut up, oughtn’t they? You shut up, you littluns!’ ” (p. 89)

Piggy is loyal to Ralph

Piggy sticks by Ralph from the very beginning of the novel, recognizing Ralph both as someone who might protect him and someone who will make a good, fair leader. Although in general the other boys dislike Piggy, Ralph eventually realizes Piggy’s worth and begins to see him as a friend: 

If you were a chief you had to think, you had to be wise. […] Only, decided Ralph as he faced the chief’s seat, I can’t think. Not like Piggy. 

 Once more that evening Ralph had to adjust his values. Piggy could think. He could go step by step inside that fat head of his, only Piggy was no chief. But Piggy, for all his ludicrous body, had brains. (p. 83)

Piggy ultimately becomes a victim to the savage side of the boys. First Jack steals his glasses and then Roger violently murders him (p. 201). Eventually, Ralph is left to cry over the loss of “the true, wise friend called Piggy” (p. 225).
