Ralph

Cuprins
  • Outer characterization
  • Inner characterization
    • Ralph is a natural leader
    • He develops from innocence to experience

Outer characterization

Ralph is the main character in William Golding’s Lord of the Flies. In the opening lines of the novel, he is described as having “fair hair” and wearing a “school sweater” (p. 1). He is apparently a typical British schoolboy: “The fair boy stopped and jerked his stockings with an automatic gesture that made the jungle seem for a moment like the Home Counties.” (p. 1). He is “twelve years and a few months” (p. 5) and he is somewhere between childhood and adolescence: “he might make a boxer, as far as width and heaviness of shoulders went, but there was a mildness about his mouth and eyes that proclaimed no devil.” (p. 5) 

By the end of the novel, however, Ralph’s outer characterization has changed: after weeks on the island, his clothes are torn, and his hair is long and dirty. The naval officer who finds him sees him as a “little scarecrow” who needs “a bath, a hair-cut, a nose-wipe and a good deal of ointment.” (p. 223)

Inner characterization

Ralph is a natural leader

Ralph seems like a natural leader. At the beginning of the novel, he blows through the conch shell to call the other boys to a meeting, who are drawn to his calm confidence:

What intelligence had been shown was traceable to Piggy while the most obvious leader was Jack. But there was a stillness about Ralph as he sat that marked him out: there was his size, and attractive appearance, and most obscurely, yet most powerfully, there was the conch. The being that had blown that, had sat waiting for them on the platform with the delicate thing balanced on his knees, was set apart. (p. 19)

Ralph recognizes that Jack wants to be leader. He shows himself to be fair and diplomatic when he offers Jack a role as leader of the choir, suggesting they should be hunters if they want. Ralph similarly recognizes the good qualities in Simon and Piggy, even when the others do not.

Ralph quickly establishes the boys’ most important goals while on the island: “ ‘We want to have fun. And we want to be rescued.’ ” (p. 36). He tells the boys they need to make a fire – but he can’t control the excitable boys, who instead follow Jack and end up burning down a huge part of the jungle: “Ralph was on his feet too, shouting for quiet, but no one heard him. All at once the crowd swayed towards the island and were gone – following Jack.” (p. 37)

He develops from innocence to experience

Ralph changes over the course of the novel, following a path from innocence to experience. When he first arrives on the island, he is excited to be in a place without any adults, seeing it as “the imagined but never fully realized place” of his dreams (p. 10). However, he begins to resent his responsibility and the moral obligation he feels to maintain order. 

A crisis comes when he spots a ship, but Jack has let the fire go out. Afterwards, he becomes cynical and finds himself thinking like an adult, but without an adult’s ability to decide what to do: 

He found himself understanding the wearisomeness of this life, where every path was an improvisation and a considerable part of one’s waking life was spent watching one’s feet. He stopped, facing the strip; and remembering that first enthusiastic exploration as though it were part of a brighter childhood, he smiled jeeringly. (p. 81)

Increasingly, Ralph despairs that things among the boys are “going wrong” and that they will never be rescued. Eventually he finds himself a complete outcast, hunted by Jack, Roger, and the others, who want to murder him. When he finds the naval officer at the end, he suddenly seems to become a child again as the presence of an adult means he no longer has to take responsibility: “with filthy body, matted hair, and unwiped nose, Ralph wept for the end of innocence, the darkness of man’s heart, and the fall through the air of the true, wise friend called Piggy.” (p. 225)
