Glossary of the key terms

On this page you can find words that are particularly useful to know when studying feminism and the #MeToo movement. Click each word for a full definition in English, including information about how the word is specifically relevant in the context of the topic.

affirmative action (s)
 

Action favouring those who have been excluded, discriminated against, or underrepresented. In the US, it typically refers to procedures and policies that seek to offer equal access to women and minorities in terms of employment and education opportunities.

enfranchisement (s)
 

The giving of a right or privilege, especially the right to vote. Disenfranchisement is the opposite - taking away voting rights from specific groups in society.

feminism (s)
 

A range of political and social movements which share the common goal of achieving social, economic, political, and personal equality of genders.

gender equality (s)
 

Gender equality is achieved when people have equal access to resources and opportunities and when their aspirations, needs, and behaviours are equally valued, regardless of gender.

gender pay gap (s)
 

Also called the gender wage gap, the term refers to the average difference between how much men and women are paid, often for doing the same job. In general, women are paid less than men.

gender stereotypes (s)
 

Preconceived ideas in which certain characteristics, behaviours, social roles, etc. are associated with a certain gender.

intersectionality (s)
 

Coined in 1989 by American professor Kimberlé Crenshaw, the term refers to an approach which analyses the way in which different social categories (such as a particular ethnicity, class, gender, ability, etc.) create a complex system of discrimination or disadvantage. Intersectional feminism observes the ways in which women experience discrimination not only as a result of their gender, but also as a result of belonging to one or more minorities.

misogyny
 

The term refers to a range of negative attitudes against women, which can be manifested in many different ways including discrimination, social exclusion, sexual objectification, or harassment and violence.

patriarchy (s)
 

Refers to a social system in which men hold primary power. In a patriarchal society, women are typically underrepresented in or excluded from positions of power and authority.

sexism (s)
 

Discrimination based on sex or gender.

sexual harassment
 

Harassment which takes on sexual undertones or is overtly sexual. It can take place in any professional or social situation, and it includes a wide range of actions, from unwanted obscene remarks, to sexual abuse or assault. It is generally observed that women are exposed to sexual harassment more often than men. 

suffragette (s)
 

A woman who advocated for women's suffrage, and who believed that militancy and violent action was justified in the fight for voting rights.

suffragist (s)
 

A woman who advocated for women's suffrage, and who believed the fight for voting rights should be fought with peaceful means such as protests and demonstrations.

victim-blaming (s)
 

Refers to holding a victim partially or wholly responsible for the crime committed against them. In the context of the feminist movement, it often refers to situations of sexual assault, where assaulted women receive negative reactions that either imply or directly propose that the victim’s attitude, clothing, behaviour, etc. attracted the assault. The act of victim-blaming is traumatic and can discourage the victim from seeking moral support as well as medical and legal help.

women's rights movement (s)
 

The movement, which originated in women’s fight for suffrage and property rights in the 19th century, aims to secure legal, economic, and social equality for women. Many different countries have had their own women's rights movements, though they often cooperated with each other.

women's suffrage (s)
 

Refers to women’s right to vote in elections.
