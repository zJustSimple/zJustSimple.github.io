Feminism in the 20th century

World War II and the women’s rights movement

In 1920, women gained the right to vote in the US. Although many women already participated in the industrial economy, they still lacked the same legal rights as men in many parts of society, including the workforce. The leader of the National Women’s Party, Alice Paul, wanted an Equal Rights Amendment (ERA) under which women would be completely equal to men under the law. However, only some protective legislation for women was passed in terms of physical labour and working hours.

During World War II, women proved their ability to participate in the economy and achieved greater economic independence by taking men’s places in factories and other jobs. Then, with the end of the war, millions of women quit their jobs and saw themselves returning to their old domestic roles of wife and mother.

Although women had more access to higher education and employment in the 1940s and the 1950s, the new media of television presented an idealised image of women as homemakers, and the marriage and birth rates increased, while the divorce rate dropped. As a result, traditional roles for women were largely supported by post-war American society, and many were still opposed to married women holding jobs. This largely isolated women from economics, politics, and lawmaking.

The story explores feminism in the 1950s by looking at women’s frustrations with only being housewives and mothers and at their desire to also find fulfilment in other ways, such as through financial independence and glamour. The story also looks at later generations of women and how the aims and ideals of feminism have moved on.

Civil rights and feminism

Books such as Simone de Beauvoir’s The Second Sex, published in the US in 1953, and Betty Friedan’s The Feminine Mystique, published in 1963, are said to have played a major role in women beginning to challenge traditional gender roles on a larger scale.

The women's rights movement that began in the 1960s addressed issues such as women’s employment, legal and social inequalities, as well as women’s sexuality and reproductive rights. It drew attention to issues such as sexual assault and domestic violence and sought changes in divorce law and custody laws. The general aim of feminist activists was not only political reform but also to challenge the gender-biased structure of power in society and highlight the more subtle ways in which women were oppressed.

The women’s rights movement used several tactics which proved successful in the civil rights movement. They used non-violent protest, civil disobedience, and symbolic actions to communicate the importance of women’s rights and highlight the oppression they suffered.

Progress for women’s civil rights

The progress concerning women’s civil rights was marked by several important events. In 1960, for example, the Food and Drug Administration approved birth control pills for women. This meant that women could build careers more easily, since they could avoid unwanted pregnancies and the need to remain at home caring for small children.

In 1961, President Kennedy formed the Presidential Commission on the Status of Women, which was tasked with researching women’s issues in areas such as work and education and advising the President on them.

The Equal Pay Act, passed in 1963, and the Civil Rights Act, passed in 1964, technically provided women with equal rights, as the first promised equal pay regardless of gender, while the second outlawed discrimination based on gender. However, many women felt that there was little actual change in society after the acts passed.

The National Organisation for Women (NOW)

In 1966, the National Organisation for Women (NOW) was created by Betty Friedan and other activists, as they felt society needed a civil rights organisation that specifically addressed women’s rights issues.

The NOW soon issued a “Statement of Purpose”, which addressed key feminist issues such as employment, education, political participation, and discrimination against African-American women.

In the following year, the NOW organised its first convention, where members chose to focus on the repeal of abortion laws, obtaining public funding for child care, and on the Equal Rights Amendment (ERA), which remained a major focus in the women’s rights movement for years to come.

In the following years, the NOW organised marches and boycotts in the states which had not ratified the ERA. It also lobbied for the extension of the final deadline for ERA’s ratification, but the deadline was only extended to 1982 and stalled because it was not ratified by the minimum required 38 states.

In the 1970s, the NOW also helped conceive and pass law regarding pregnancy and abortion rights. The organisation continued its activity on economic and reproductive rights throughout the decades. In 1972, it became illegal to exclude women from educational programs, which resulted in a large number of women enrolling in higher education. In 1973, the Supreme Court ruling Roe vs. Wade made abortion legal in all states. However, another ruling from 1989 affirmed the states’ right to prohibit public hospitals from performing abortions and to deny public funding for such procedures.

Feminism in the early 1990s  

Historians have noticed a change in focus in American feminism beginning with the early 1990s. Feminists, especially younger generations, focused more on racial diversity, individualism, and sexuality.

Another notable difference from the previous feminist period is marked by a different stance on pornography. The common anti-pornography stance of 1980s’ feminism described pornography as another way to oppress women and perpetuate violence against them. In contrast, 1990s’ feminism generally highlighted the importance of women choosing how they express themselves sexually, which might include the production of pornography and engaging in sex work.

The term “intersectionality”, first introduced in 1989, became more popular, as it was used to describe the different “layers” of oppression that women were exposed to. Feminists began to focus more on the idea that a person belonging to more than one marginalised group – a disabled gay woman, for instance – experiences more intense discrimination as a result of being at the intersection of these groups.

Intersectionality is important in the context of feminism as it challenged one of the ideas that stood at the base of women’s rights movement – that women have the same life experiences since they belong to the same oppressed gender. By including intersectionality in feminist theory, feminists also moved past the idea that women are socially defined only through their gender.

Two events are credited with sparking this shift in focus – the emergence of a feminist punk subculture in the early 199os in Washington and the publicity created around the televised testimony of Anita Hill, an African-American woman who accused Clarence Thomas, a nominee for the Supreme Court of the United States, of sexual harassment.

During this period, feminism gained more personal value as it focused on women’s individual stories. Sharing personal stories became a way for women to affirm that others, and themselves, are not alone in the experience of oppression.

A new understanding of the issue of gender also emerged in this period. The male-female binary began to be seen as an artificial social construct used to maintain power in dominant groups.

Overall, the feminist activity at the end of the 20th century focused on reproductive rights, sex positivity, as well as intersectionality. The aim was not only to challenge and propose laws related to women’s rights but also to challenge definitions and perceptions of women’s issues and highlight their complexity.
