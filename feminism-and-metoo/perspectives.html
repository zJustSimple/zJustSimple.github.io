Fight for women’s rights in India

Just like in other parts of the world, feminism in India pursues gender equality in the workforce, in education, and in politics. Indian feminists have challenged culture-specific issues such as inheritance laws.

Feminist movements in India have also been criticised for focusing too much on middle-class women and neglecting poorer or lower caste women. This has led to the creation of feminist organisations and movements which fight for issues specific to poorer women.

The patriarchal culture of India is an important factor in the discrimination of women. The idea of women’s inferiority to men is deeply ingrained in Indian culture, leaving women vulnerable to abuse. Women’s servitude and dependency on men are seen as virtues in many households and families, and independence from domestic, traditional roles as mothers, daughters, and wives is frowned upon.

Women’s sexuality is also largely seen as taboo, and the public discussion around sexual abuse often leans towards victim-blaming. This leads to many women modifying their behaviour to ensure some safety – they do not go out after dark, they avoid walking alone even during the day, and they censor their clothing, speech, and body posture to avoid unwanted male attention.

Although women are largely protected and given equality under India’s constitution and laws, India is often seen as a dangerous country for women.

Stories of sexual abuse of toddlers, girls, and women have emerged in the media in recent years, causing national and international outrage. Perhaps the most famous of such cases is the 2012 Delhi gang rape, in which a 23-year-old woman was beaten, raped, and tortured by six men on a bus. The victim later died in hospital, with both the incident and its tragic outcome generating large public protests around India. The protesters condemned state and central governments for failing to protect women from such acts.

The incident, however, was not an isolated event in India and is seen as part of a much larger issue that India is facing: the systematic oppression and degradation of women, which leads to the normalisation of everyday violence against women.

As the sexual harassment and abuse of women and girls in India gained attention in the media, and protesters pressured governments for better protection of women, several improvements to the legal system were made. The Indian government passed several sexual assault laws, which established more severe punishments for rapists and fast-track courts (where cases are solved faster) specifically designed to deal with rape prosecutions.

The common occurrence of violent acts against women, and the disinterest or inability of authorities to provide them with protection, has also led to the creation of vigilante groups which seek to protect girls, women, and children. The Gulabi Gang in North India is such a group, with its members wearing pink saris and armed with bamboo sticks. The group looks out for cases of rape, child molestation, sexual harassment, domestic violence, etc. and exposes them, often invading police stations demanding that cases be investigated.

Fight for women’s rights in China

In contrast with Western feminism, which emerged at a community level seeking to create change at a national level, modern Chinese feminism was driven by state policy. The Communist government of the newly-formed People’s Republic of China passed the Marriage Reform Law of 1950, thus initiating the women’s rights movement. The law changed many of the traditional practices in Chinese families, such as forced marriage or the denial of property rights for married women.

Historically, Chinese women fulfilled traditional gender roles, being forced to adopt a domestic lifestyle. Moreover, practices such as foot binding, which mutilated women’s feet, also made movement difficult, thus physically binding women to their home.

However, the Communist government of the People’s Republic of China sought to strengthen families and advance the nation through women’s equal participation in society. Implicitly, the intention was not necessarily to give women human rights but to make the nation more productive.

Women were given other rights as well to be better integrated into the workforce – equal pay and equal education opportunities. Women also gained representation in the political sphere, which involved them in decision making on public matters.

However, the women’s rights movement had unforeseen consequences, which worried the Communist Party. Sexual freedom, for example, attracted the disapproval of more conservative of citizens, and so, the Marriage Reform Law was abandoned in 1953. Events such as this reinforced the idea that the state only supported women’s rights as long as they served the Party’s interests.

Market reforms in the late '70s then complicated women’s place in the workforce, as their jobs were no longer guaranteed in a state-controlled economy, and they had to compete with men. Discrimination became common-place, with many women having to return to domestic roles. Other social issues such as sexual harassment in the workplace, sex trafficking, and domestic violence worsened.

A new wave of Chinese feminism, more connected with Western feminism and its theories, emerged in the 1980s and the 1990s. Feminists felt encouraged to challenge gender inequality, despite state suppression.

In 2015, Chinese feminism received worldwide attention and encouragement despite Chinese social media control after five activists were arrested during a peaceful protest against domestic abuse.

In recent years, it has been argued that China will no longer be able to ignore its women’s rights issues – the prevalence of domestic violence, of sexual harassment in the workplace, of gender-biased abortions, etc. – and will eventually have to begin working with feminist organisations and not against them.

Textual perspective - "Women's Rights are Human Rights" by Hillary Clinton
 

The speech was delivered in 1995, at the United Nations Fourth World Conference on Women in Beijing, China. Clinton’s speech is directed at the female audience, advocating for women to unite and fight for their rights. The speaker claims that women’s rights are human rights and exposes some of the unjust treatment women are victims of worldwide.
