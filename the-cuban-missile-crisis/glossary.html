Glossary

On this page, you can find words that are particularly useful to know when studying the Cuban Missile Crisis. Click each word for a full definition in English, including information about how the word is specifically relevant in the context of the topic.   

agreement (s)
 

The Cuban Missile Crisis ended with an official agreement between the Soviet Union and the US. According to this agreement, Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev promised to return the missiles installed in Cuba back to the Soviet Union. In return, American President John F. Kennedy agreed never to invade Cuba and to remove US missiles from Turkey.

ballistic missile (s)
 

A partially guided missile which delivers one or more warheads directed at a previously established target. The Cuban Missile Crisis was triggered when the US learned that the Soviet Union were placing ballistic missile launch facilities in Cuba, within striking range of the US. 

capitalism (s)
 

An economic system in which private owners control resources and facilities that are used to produce economic value, such as natural resources like land and water, or facilities such as factories. In capitalism, the government plays a limited role in the economy. The Cuban Missile Crisis took place during the Cold War, in which an important component was the competition between the communist ideology of the ruling party in the Soviet Union and the capitalist ideology of the US.

Cold War
 

The Cold War (1947 – 1991) was a period of hostility between the Western world, led by the US, and the Soviet Union. The conflict was largely caused by strong ideological differences between the two superpowers, as the US was a democratic capitalist country, while the Soviet Union was a socialist totalitarian nation. Both the US and the Soviet Union intended to expand their influence in the world and tried to minimise the influence of their opponent through propaganda, proxy wars, and efforts to prove military superiority.

communism (s)
 

A social and economic system or theory in which the state is abolished and resources (the means of production) are owned by the community, while wealth is divided equally among citizens. The measures and policies put in place by Fidel Castro’s communist regime caused the tense relationship between the US and Cuba, which led to the Bay of Pigs invasion in the hopes that Castro would be removed from power.

However, it is still debated whether either the Soviet Union or Cuba ever managed to become truly communist societies, as it was envisioned by the founder of the ideology, Karl Marx.

DEFCON (s)
 

Short for “defence readiness condition”, DEFCON is a level-based alert state system used by the United States Armed Forces, indicating the likeliness of an imminent attack. The least severe alert is DEFCON 5, while DEFCON 1 is the most severe. The Cuban Missile Crisis situation as a whole was categorised as DEFCON 3, though the American Air Force was at DEFCON 2 during the height of the crisis.

DEFCON 2 has only been used twice (during the Cuban Missile Crisis and at the beginning of the Gulf War). DEFCON 1 - signalling imminent nuclear war - has (fortunately!) never been used. 

embargo (s)
 

An order by a government imposing a ban on trade or other commercial activities with another country. The US has a long history of imposing embargoes on Cuba, limiting trade with and travel to Cuba at various levels since the 1960s.

the Iron Curtain (s)
 

A term that refers to the physical and symbolic line dividing Western Europe, which had political freedom, from Eastern Europe, which was under Soviet control, and did not have open contact with the West. The Iron Curtain ceased to exist once the Soviet Union dissolved and the Cold War ended in 1991; this was symbolised by the tearing down of the Berlin Wall.

MAD (s)
 

Short for Mutual (or Mutually) Assured Destruction, a nuclear strategy doctrine which emerged during the Cold War era. The idea behind MAD was that the nation targeted by a nuclear attack would undoubtedly retaliate, resulting in wide-scale destruction for the aggressor as well as the victim of a nuclear attack. The awareness of MAD allegedly helped prevent nuclear war, as it made both sides realise that a nuclear conflict would always mean devastating losses on both sides.

masikrovka (s)
 

Refers to various measures of military deception practiced in the Soviet Union. These measures were particularly intended to deceive Western intelligence. During the deployment of missiles to Cuba, the Soviets took several deceptive measures, including camouflage and establishing strict channels of communication, to prevent the US from learning about their plan.

missile launch facility (s)
 

Refers to a structure, usually constructed underground, that stores and can launch ballistic missiles. The Cuban Missile Crisis began when American President John F. Kennedy learned about missiles installed by the Soviet Union in Cuba from photographs showing missile bases under construction in Cuba. 

Moscow-Washington hotline (s)
 

Following the Cuban Missile Crisis, a direct line of communication between the Kremlin  and the US White House  was installed. Popularly known as the “red telephone”, the hotline provided the superpowers’ leaders with an efficient and direct way to communicate in order to prevent future conflicts from escalating.  

Note that the hotline was never an actual telephone - it was designed to send and receive only text messages, as speech might be easier to mishear or misinterpret. Today, the hotline exists as a severely encrypted email link.  

nuclear war (s)
 

Nuclear war was an important concern during the Cold War, as both the US and the Soviet Union had nuclear weapons. The Cuban Missile Crisis is seen as the closest the Cold War came to turning into a full nuclear war.

Soviet Union (s)
 

Also known as the Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (USSR), the Soviet Union was a former socialist country established in 1922. The USSR, which included Russia and 14 other socialist countries, dissolved in 1991.

The Soviet Union had a state-controlled economy, which was a strong contrast to the capitalist economies of western nations such as the US. The ideological contrast between capitalism and communism was one of the driving forces behind the Cold War - especially when the US began to actively oppose the further spread of communism. 

tension (s)
 

In this context, a politically strained relationship between two countries with opposing ideologies and political systems. The Cold War tension between the US and the Soviet Union is said to have reached its peak in the Cuban Missile Crisis, when many feared that political tensions could turn into a full war.
