The crisis begins

The Cuban Missile Crisis is generally considered to have taken place over 13 days, between the 16th and 28th of October, 1962.

The crisis was triggered by the Soviet Union's establishment of secret missile launch facilities in Cuba - a nation within striking range of major US cities. The Soviet missiles’ arrival in Cuba was kept secret for a month, which was considered an achievement considering the large number of troops and the heavy, large equipment transported. However, the missile sites were eventually difficult to disguise – even though at ground level they could be hidden somewhat, they were recognisable from above.

One of the missile sites was discovered on 14th of October, 1962, when an American aircraft took photographs of the site, confirming the existence of offensive missiles in Cuba.

On 16th of October, President Kennedy was informed about the discovery. He immediately assembled a committee – known as the executive committee or ExCom – that would handle the diplomatic crisis. The situation was seen as dangerous because the missiles were so close to the US and improved the Soviet Union’s position in its nuclear rivalry with the US.

ExCom had a difficult task– the question was how to handle the situation without causing a bigger conflict or even a nuclear war. Several courses of action were considered, such as an invasion of Cuba or bombing the Soviet missile sites.

Kennedy’s and Khrushchev's approach to the crisis

As the Cuban Missile Crisis unfolded, US President Kennedy’s advisers thought the best course of action was to invade Cuba, believing that the Soviets would not oppose this.

Kennedy was sceptical of this plan, as he wanted the US’s response to be firm but not to provoke the Soviets, realising that there was a strong possibility of a retaliatory attack using nuclear weapons.

Kennedy also interpreted the Soviet presence in Cuba as a sign of a future Soviet move to take over control of West Berlin (which was under Western influence). Khrushchev had previously expressed his intention to take it over, and East German and Soviet troops surrounded Berlin. Kennedy knew that the US could provide little assistance to West Berliners in the event of an attack. The US could only threaten with nuclear war, which could have provoked the Soviets to use nuclear weapons against the US.

Moreover, if the US could not resolve the crisis peacefully and lost Berlin, this would have affected the US’s reputation as a superpower and its relationship with its allies.

Kennedy, therefore, settled on a moderate but firm approach to the crisis. His administration set up a quarantine around Cuba that would prevent more Soviets from coming to Cuba and from introducing more equipment to the island. Second, he requested that the Soviets remove the missiles from Cuba.

On 22nd of October, President Kennedy also made the discovery of missiles in Cuba public and explained that the US was ready for a military confrontation if needed. This created panic among some Americans who, fearing nuclear war, started to gather supplies such as gas and food.

The US blockade was not challenged by the Soviets, even though approaching Soviet ships headed for Cuba came very close to it. Khrushchev initially ordered ships in international waters to continue their trip to Cuba but soon ordered them to stop at the blockade. This was seen by the US as a good sign for the development of the conflict.

Furthermore, note that Khrushchev did not initially want to place missiles in Cuba but later did it to deter the US from invading Cuba, because it was important for the Soviets to keep Cuba under Soviet influence. After the US discovered the missiles, Khrushchev did not see the need for an agreement, as the Soviets stated the missiles were placed in Cuba for defence purposes. However, seeing the US considered the missiles to be assault weapons, he realised the need to call for an agreement to prevent a nuclear war.

Black Saturday

The 27th of October 1962, also named Black Saturday, was the height of the 13-day-long conflict of the Cuban Missile Crisis, as several events occurred which pushed the world close to nuclear war. Some were known at the time, while others did not come to light until much later. 

On this day, an American spy plane was shot down over Cuba. While on a reconnaissance mission over eastern Cuba, the plane was shot down by a Soviet missile. The decision to fire on the plane was made by Soviet generals in Cuba. Even though any attack was against Khrushchev’s orders, the generals feared that the plane would discover missiles closely placed to the US base at Guantánamo. Without any time to contact Moscow for directions, the generals decided to fire on the US plane.

Upon learning of the pilot’s death, some of Kennedy’s advisers recommended that the US Air Force attack the missile site the next day. Others argued that this called for an immediate invasion of Cuba. Kennedy, however, did not agree to either request. Later during the day, Kennedy agreed that the US would return fire if US reconnaissance planes were fired upon again by the Soviets.

In another part of the world, a Soviet submarine was attacked by a number of American ships, which dropped signalling depth charges on it in an attempt to force it to surface. However, the Americans were unaware that the submarine was armed with nuclear warheads - and the submarine commander was, in turn, unaware that it was not being attacked with actual depth charges. The submarine had orders to retaliate if it was attacked, so the crew prepared their nuclear torpedo for launch against one of the American battleships. However, launch of a nuclear weapon required agreement of all three commanding officers on board - and one man, Vasili Arkhipov, was against the launch. Despite an intense discussion, he remained firm, and the nuclear torpedo was not launched. This was arguably the closest the world ever came to nuclear war, with the actions of just one man standing against it. Interestingly, the full significance of this event did not come to light until 2002, when the Russians revealed details of the event at a press conference. 

Kennedy and Khrushchev reach an agreement

US policy meant that the US should have launched an attack on at least one Soviet missile site after the American spy plane was shot down over Cuba. However, the Kennedy Administration decided not to pursue this course of action, as this could easily have sparked a military confrontation and a nuclear war.

Despite rising tensions, the US accepted Khrushchev’s demand that the US would not invade Cuba. Unofficially, the US also agreed to withdraw American missiles from Turkey. The Cuban Missile Crisis saw its official end on 28th of October, 1962.

However, the US blockade on Cuba was only lifted a month later, after tense negotiations at the UN finally reached an end when Khrushchev agreed to withdraw nuclear bombers from Cuba.

Khrushchev himself reportedly miscalculated the consequences of moving missiles to Cuba, never intending to almost cause a nuclear war. He knew that the Soviet Union was inferior in nuclear power, and the communication between Moscow and Cuba was too weak to coordinate a successful attack.
