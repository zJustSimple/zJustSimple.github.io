The North Korean crisis

Today, specialists consider the nuclear threat less severe than it was during the Cold War. Since that era, several international treaties have been created to prevent the spread of nuclear weapons and the development of new technology in this field.

However, not all countries are on board with this goal. In 2003, North Korea, for example, announced its withdrawal from the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty, which seeks the peaceful use of nuclear energy, nuclear disarmament, and a limit on the spread of nuclear weapons. Since then, North Korea has conducted underground nuclear explosions (in 2006 and 2009) and threatened to use its weapons against the US.

The most recent rise in tensions over North Korea’s nuclear plans was the 2017-2018 North Korean crisis. The crisis began when North Korea conducted missile and nuclear tests that signalled both unexpected speed in building its nuclear arsenal and its ability to launch long-range ballistic missiles. North Korean leader Kim Jong-un subsequently sent an open letter to several nations, boasting about North Korea’s nuclear power and threatening a nuclear retaliation if the US tried to attack.

The crisis was resolved when North Korea signed a declaration for peace with South Korea, promising to work for nuclear disarmament in the Korean peninsula. The agreement was strengthened through an unprecedented meeting between Kim Jong-un and American President Donald Trump in June 2018, where both leaders committed to peaceful relations.

Internationally, however, the sophistication of North Korea’s nuclear program is seen as both a security threat and as a weakening of the international rules that ensure peace between nations with nuclear abilities.

US plans to leave the Iran nuclear deal

Despite repeated denial that it is building nuclear weapons, Iran has developed a nuclear program over the years, which caused both international concern and a deteriorating relationship with the West.

In response to Iran pursuing a nuclear program, the West imposed economic sanctions on Iran. Iran sought to get relief from these sanctions by signing the Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action (known as the Iran deal) in 2015 and, consequently, agreeing to limit its nuclear activities.

However, since then, Iran tested three ballistic missiles and its relationship with the US worsened when the US imposed new economic sanctions and President Donald Trump criticised both Iran and the deal.

In May 2018, President Donald Trump announced that the United States would withdraw from the Iran Deal and would impose new sanctions, despite protests from US officials and nations that support the deal such as France, Germany, and the UK.

The main concern is that, once the US leaves the agreement, Iran itself will withdraw and, therefore, resume its nuclear development at a much greater speed than before. While Trump criticised both the terms of the deal and Iran’s alleged violations of it (without presenting any firm evidence for these violations), supporters of the deal consider that even a faulty deal is better than no deal, as it reduces global nuclear risk.

US and Russia exit the nuclear arms treaty

In October 2018, US President Donald Trump threatened to break off a 1987 nuclear arms deal with Russia - The Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces Treaty (INF) - as a response to suspicions that Russia is building a system which would allow it to attack Europe with nuclear weapons.

Critics expressed their concern that abandoning the treaty could usher in a new Cold War. Russian lawmakers criticised the US for creating conditions for a new arms race, while Vladimir Putin expressed his confidence in Russia’s nuclear abilities but promised never to initiate an attack.

Trump’s position on this issue, however, was met with support in some circles. Supporters see a withdrawal from the treaty as an opportunity for the US to start developing its nuclear technology in an effort to match China. Because China never signed the INF, it was able to increase its military power by developing a missile arsenal, which is seen as a threat to the US.

On 1st of February 2019, President Donald Trump officially announced the US’ withdrawal from the INF. The Trump Administration accused Russia of not complying with the treaty’s terms, which bans missiles with a range of 500 to 5,000 kilometres.

The Kremlin was formally notified a day later, with the US’ decision taking effect in six months if Russia did not comply with the treaty. As a response to US’s withdrawal, Russia suspended the treaty, while also instructing Russian ministers not to initiate disarmament talks with the US.

Russian President Vladimir Putin also stated that Russia will start work on creating new nuclear-capable missiles previously banned by the treaty, as the US expressed its intentions to improve its nuclear arsenal as well. However, Putin also stated that he will not deploy banned weapons in Europe or anywhere else in the world, as long as he does not see a similar move by the US.

Texts about the Cuban Missile Crisis

Below are suggestions for texts or movies that may be relevant when working with the Cuban Missile Crisis. We do not currently have study guides for these texts, but maybe you can be inspired to look for some of these titles yourself.

  • De Niro, Robert - The Good Shepherd (2006 movie)
  • Dobbs, Michael - One Minute to Midnight (2008 book)
  • Donaldson, Roger - Thirteen Days (2000 movie)
  • Kennedy, Robert F. - Thirteen Days: A Memoir of the Cuban Missile Crisis (1969 memoir)
  • Khrushchev, Nikita - Memoirs of Nikita Khrushchev (2006 memoir)
  • Khrushchev, Sergei - How My Father And President Kennedy Saved The World (2002 article)
  • Murray,  John and Reynolds, Emer - Here Was Cuba (2013 documentary)
  • Page, Anthony - The Missiles of October (1974 movie)
  • Stern, Sheldon M. - The Week the World Stood Still: Inside the Secret Cuban Missile Crisis (2005 book)
