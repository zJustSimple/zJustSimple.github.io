Aftermath of the crisis

The Cuban Missile Crisis had mixed consequences. While the relationship between the US and the Soviet Union improved, there was also some dissatisfaction for all involved in the conflict. For the Soviet Union and for Castro’s Cuba, there were also negative long-term consequences. 

After the crisis, the relationship between the two superpowers saw an improvement. In 1963, US President John F. Kennedy delivered a speech in which he acknowledged the Soviet Union’s achievements and recognised its people’s suffering during WWII. The speech was well received by Soviet Union’s Premier Nikita Khrushchev and, in July 1963, he agreed to a test ban treaty (which would only allow nuclear tests to be performed underground) with the US and the UK.

Fidel Castro, whose demands were ignored during the crisis, was disappointed at what he perceived as the Soviet Union’s lack of support during the crisis. However, the relationship between Cuba and the Soviet Union did not worsen, with Fidel Castro visiting the Soviet Union only six months after the crisis.

Affected by the US embargo, Cuba became largely dependent on economic assistance from the Soviet Union. Through financial aid and the United States’ promise that it would not invade Cuba, the Soviet Union succeeded in preserving Castro’s regime and keeping Cuba’s economy from collapsing.

However, by the 1980s, Cuba had become a financial liability for the Soviets. Once the Soviet Union collapsed in 1991, Cuba entered a period of severe economic hardship. The situation improved in the late 1990s, when Cuba established a trading relationship with Venezuela and, later, when it improved its relations with Russia.

Some officials in the Kennedy Administration were also discontented with how the Cuban Missile Crisis was handled, as they believed the US should have been more aggressive by invading Cuba to stop Castro’s communist regime.

The crisis also created more awareness about the potential reality of a nuclear war. Therefore, a direct hotline – also called “the red telephone” (though it was actually a text-only link) – was set up directly between Moscow and Washington, in the hope that direct communication between the leaders of the Soviet Union and the US would help prevent future conflicts from escalating.

However, the crisis also had another effect on the Soviets. They became convinced that they needed to invest more in the development of nuclear weapons, which proved very costly.

Khrushchev’s willingness to remove the missiles from Cuba was largely seen as a sign that the Soviet Union would be submissive to the US, especially since the agreement that the US would withdraw from Turkey was unofficial and, therefore, unknown to the public. The crisis and its resolution played an important part in Khrushchev’s fall from power only two years later.
