General aspects

The American Dream refers to a set of ideals. They refer to the freedom of opportunity for people to achieve their own version of success and happiness provided they work hard enough.

The term American Dream was popularized by historian James Truslow Adams in 1931. Adams wrote in his book The Epic of America: “…life should be better and richer and fuller for everyone, with opportunity for each according to ability or achievement regardless of social class or circumstances of birth.”

Before James Truslow Adams, historian Frederick Jackson Turner published the Frontier Thesis in 1893, in which he argued that the American frontier is what shaped both American democracy and the American Dream. According to Turner, the process of constantly pushing the frontier line westward on the American continent led to people valuing egalitarianism as well as a lack of interest in class differences. This was a clear contrast to most European societies at the time.

African-American reformer and statesman Frederick Douglass (1818-1895) also defined the American Dream in his speeches. He was an abolitionist and former slave. In his writings and autobiographies, he advocated for equality for everyone, whether black, woman, Native American, or recent immigrant. His discourse drew attention to the fact that the American Dream was out of reach for many groups. He would later become an inspiration for Martin Luther King, Jr. and other leaders of the civil rights movement in the US.

American exceptionalism

American exceptionalism is an ideology closely linked to the idea of the American Dream. According to those who believe in this concept, the US is a unique country that creates a unique context which allowed for the American Dream to develop. Like the American Dream, American exceptionalism is rooted in the American Revolution which led to the creation of the United States that valued liberty, individualism, republican views, democracy, and a capitalist economy.

During the Civil War, a new dimension was added to American exceptionalism regarding the country’s role in international relations. The idea was that the US had an international responsibility to uphold freedom and democracy worldwide. This was suggested in Abraham Lincoln’s famous Gettysburg Address speech: “…government of the people, by the people, for the people, shall not perish from the earth”. However, this role can also suggest the US considers itself superior to other nations, something which has sometimes been criticized by other countries. More recently, this has been an issue in connection with President Donald Trump taking office. The official foreign policy of the Trump administration is “America First” which emphasizes American nationalism, restricts foreign interventions, and frequently leads to pulling out of international agreements.

Like the American Dream, American exceptionalism is also connected with the idea of the Frontier which allowed for the development of democracy. The Frontier was a unique feature of American culture. It was always a new place that people explored and settled in. The lack of strict rules and social hierarchies at the Frontier is believed to have encouraged the creation of a democratic society based on equal opportunity.

Last but not least, American exceptionalism is linked to the fact that America quickly built a reputation of being a land of opportunity and took pride in offering people the context where they could be free from class and family background in a new world.

Interpretations of the American Dream

The American Dream has different dimensions and interpretations, which suggests that the concept is complex and has developed and changed over time.

One interpretation of the Dream is synonymous with material wealth, with people pursing material comfort and taking pride in living in the wealthiest society in the world. This view is focused on the American Dream as obtaining material wealth and encourages consumerism. This version of the American Dream started to develop in the 20th century and was constantly promoted after World War II. In this version of the American Dream, people associate happiness and success with being rich and with material possessions such as big cars and houses.

However, the American Dream also has a more idealistic, socio-political dimension, as many people understand it as living free from overly restrictive laws. This political dimension was given from the start of the American nation, as the American Revolution was against oppressive laws imposed by the British Empire. This appealed to the many European immigrants who often came to America to escape religious oppression or strict laws which often made social mobility difficult.

Nowadays, the American Dream also refers to having laws that some believe are helping economic growth such as fewer taxes on business. But it also refers to the state not interfering with personal choices such as being able to marry a same-sex person, having an abortion, carrying firearms or adhering to a certain religion.

Finally, the American Dream is about realizing your full potential. In this dimension, access to education is seen as an important component in achieving the American Dream.
