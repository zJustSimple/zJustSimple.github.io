Consumerism and its effect on the environment

Another important criticism related to the American Dream is that it increasingly promotes materialism and consumerism, attitudes which end up damaging our environment. Recently, the focus on climate change and the environment has increased all across the Globe.

Consumerism developed as an economic theory which states that being able to buy more is better for consumers. Materialism refers to people wanting to acquire more material things as a means to happiness.

In the US, consumerism started developing in the early 20th century, significant in the motto spread by the General Motors factory in 1919, “Buy now, pay later”. The company used the slogan to advertise giving loans to people, so they could afford to buy cars. The popular phrase “Keeping up with the Joneses” also developed during that time, to reflect people wanting to have the latest of any item just like their neighbors or friends. A key component in consumerism is that we tend to compare ourselves to others.

The Depression and WWII frustrated many consumers because they were no longer able to access basic goods. This turned into a high consumer drive after the War. The introduction of credit cards and their boom in the 1970s further encouraged people to buy things.

Nowadays, the US is one of the biggest consumer societies in the world. 

Generally, Americans are considered some of the biggest contributors to environmental damage, partly because of their consumer habits but also because of their unsustainable exploitation of natural resources. Producing many goods also lead to increased pollution.

Reducing consumption would lead to improving environmental processes, but this would require a huge change of mentality both in companies and among the American public, including rethinking the American Dream. However, we are starting to see various movements of minimalism and anti-consumerism which argue for a more modest, sustainable lifestyle.

Note that the issue of consumerism is by no means limited to the United States. Many nations across the world struggle with similarly destructive consumer habits.
