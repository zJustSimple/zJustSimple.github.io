Narrator and point of view

The short story “The Tell-Tale Heart” by Edgar Allan Poe is narrated in the past tense by an unnamed narrator. 

The narrator is unreliable. This is made evident from the beginning of the story, as he tries to explain to the readers that he is not mad, even though he is anxious and describes himself as having supernatural senses: “True--nervous --very, very dreadfully nervous I had been and am; but why will you say that I am mad? The disease had sharpened my senses”. He admits to having committed a murder but again denies his mental disorder by arguing that he has planned the murder too carefully to be mad.

The story is told from a first-person point of view, presenting the narrator’s own experience and interpretation of events: “I had my head in, and was about to open the lantern, when my thumb slipped upon the tin fastening, and the old man sprang up in bed, crying out”. The narrator also uses the second-person form, seemingly addressing the readers or the police, and adopting a rather accusatory tone: “You fancy me mad. Madmen know nothing. But you should have seen me. You should have seen how wisely I proceeded”.

The narrator is subjective. The narrator seems to intervene in the narration, adding critical comments about some characters and their actions: “They heard! --they suspected! --they knew! --they were making a mockery of my horror!-this I thought, and this I think”. He also adds his own interpretation to some events, making it impossible for the reader to know the truth:

Presently I heard a slight groan, and I knew it was the groan of mortal terror. It was not a groan of pain or of grief --oh, no! --it was the low stifled sound that arises from the bottom of the soul when overcharged with awe. I knew the sound well. In this case, for example, the reader cannot know if this is what the old man truly felt or if it is just the narrator’s subjective interpretation.

The narrator presents the events only from his perspective and focuses on his own reactions to what he has experienced: “No doubt I now grew very pale; --but I talked more fluently, and with a heightened voice”. The reader cannot know what the other characters are feeling or thinking. The reader can receive insights about this only through the narrator’s observations about the other characters’ behaviour: “Meantime the hellish tattoo of the heart increased. It grew quicker and quicker, and louder and louder every instant. The old man's terror must have been extreme!”.

Moreover, due to the narrator’s unreliability caused by his troubled state of mind, the insights received from the narrator’s observations might be false. For example, the narrator observes the policemen who exhibit a calm behaviour despite the sound of the heartbeat: “And still the men chatted pleasantly, and smiled”. However, his suspicion makes him interpret this as a sign of mockery on their part: “Was it possible they heard not? Almighty God! --no, no! They heard! --they suspected! --they knew! --they were making a mockery of my horror!”.
