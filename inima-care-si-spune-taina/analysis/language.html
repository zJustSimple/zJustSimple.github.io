Style of language

The language of the story can appear fragmented, as comments, explanations, and repetitions are sometimes inserted in the middle of a sentence: “I undid the lantern cautiously – oh, so cautiously – cautiously (for the hinges creaked)”. This is done to draw attention to the line, bring clarifications, strengthen the message of the text, or show the unstable state of mind of the narrator.

Some words are written in italics, suggesting the narrator’s emphasis and desperation: “I then replaced the boards so cleverly, so cunningly, that no human eye – not even his – could have detected any thing wrong”; “It grew louder – louder – louder!”

The text features little dialogue and is mostly written as a monologue or a confession made in retrospective. The narrator reports the conversations between himself and other characters but only presents the full dialogue on a few, especially dramatic occasions: “ ‘Villains!’ I shrieked, ‘dissemble no more! I admit the deed! --tear up the planks! here, here! --It is the beating of his hideous heart!’ ”.

Similes

A simile is a figure of speech in which two things are explicitly compared to each other. For example, “His room was as black as pitch”. Here, the narrator gives a clear image of the atmosphere by making a direct comparison between the darkness of the room and pitch (a black tar-like substance). Similarly, he also creates the simile “still dark as midnight”.

Similes can also be used to convey the characters’ state of mind: “It increased my fury, as the beating of a drum stimulates the soldier into courage”. Here, the simile reflects the effect the heart beat has upon the narrator’s mood, as the sound fills him with feelings of rage.

Symbols

As in any gothic horror story, symbols play an important part in the whole narrative. In “The Tell-Tale Heart”, the first symbol that intrigues is the old man’s vulture eye. Vultures are scavenger birds of prey that are often spotted in areas where other animals have died. The fact that the narrator perceives the eyes of the old man like this may be a bad sign of the murder about to happen. Even though the old man does not know he is going to die, his eyes already reflect that. The eye can also suggest scrutiny. The narrator feels scrutinised by that eye and he cannot stand being judged by others or others having insight into his thoughts:

I think it was his eye! yes, it was this! He had the eye of a vulture -a pale blue eye, with a film over it. Whenever it fell upon me, my blood ran cold; and so by degrees -very gradually -I made up my mind to take the life of the old man, and thus rid myself of the eye forever.

The beating heart is a symbol of the narrator’s conscience. Though the narrator believes he hears the heart of the old man, it might have been in fact the sound of his own heart. It is a symbol of the conflict between mind and heart. Even if his mind does not perceive the killing of another man as something bad, his heart does, suggesting his remorse: “Yet the sound increased -and what could I do? It was a low, dull, quick sound -much such a sound as a watch makes when enveloped in cotton”.

The beating sound could also be attributed to a type of insect, commonly referred to as the deathwatch beetle. The insects feed off wood and can be encountered in walls, floorboards, furniture, etc. They make a distinctive thumping sound that can be compared to a beating heart. The narrator makes reference to these insects in the text: “He was still sitting up in the bed listening; --just as I have done, night after night, hearkening to the death watches in the wall”. The deathwatch beetles are sometimes perceived as symbolising death.

The lantern is another symbol. While the lantern suggests light which generally has positive connotations, in the story, it is used in the dark; as if the light of reason was not powerful enough for a darkened mind: “but I could see nothing else of the old man's face or person: for I had directed the ray as if by instinct, precisely upon the damned spot”.

The bedroom and the bed are symbols of intimacy, it is where one sleeps and is most vulnerable. In the story, they act as symbols of extreme violation of privacy, as the narrator sneaks into the old man’s bedroom to spy on him: “To think that there I was, opening the door, little by little, and he not even to dream of my secret deeds or thoughts”. Furthermore, the bed also becomes a weapon, as the man is killed with his own bed. This may suggest that our vulnerabilities are the cause of our demise.
