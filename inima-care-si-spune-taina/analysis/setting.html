Setting

Cuprins

  • Social setting

Time and physical setting

Published in the 1840s, the short story “The Tell-Tale Heart” by Edgar Allan Poe was probably intended to be read as a contemporary one. 

The fact that the story is not very precise regarding both the time and physical setting contributes to creating a sense of mystery which is typical of thriller and horror stories.

The main events span over eight days. The narrator spies on the old man “for seven long nights – every night just at midnight”. On the final day, the narrator kills and hides the body of the old man between midnight and 4 o’clock in the morning. The time setting of the story also includes the future day when the narrator is telling the story, but this date is not specified.

The events take place inside a house, with the main setting being the old man’s bedroom. It is typical of gothic stories to describe the physical setting as dark and confusing: “His room was as black as pitch with the thick darkness, (for the shutters were close fastened, through fear of robbers,) and so I knew that he could not see the opening of the door”.

In the old man’s bedroom, three elements represent importance for the physical setting of the story: the old man’s bed, which the narrator uses as a weapon; the floorboards where the narrator hides the body; and the chairs that the narrator puts out for himself and the policemen.

Social setting

The social setting of the story deals with perceptions of sanity and insanity during the 19th century. This period saw the creation of separate asylums for the criminally insane, therefore, a link between mental disorders and people who commit violent crimes like murder was established, and these types of patients were separated from the other mentally ill people who were otherwise non-violent. When you reflect on this concept, consider that the definitions of sanity and insanity have changed over time as psychiatry and psychology evolved. In the 1840s, the classification of mental disorders differed from the one used nowadays.

The narrator of the short story seems to believe that having a mental disorder always implies a low level of intelligence, of calm, and of perspicacity. He states that “Madmen know nothing”. He tries to prove that he is not mad by underlining the fact that he is wise and cautious: “would a madman have been so wise as this”. He emphasises the fact that he planned the murder to show his discernment.

However, the narrator is often confronted with intense irrational fear. His irrational fear of the old man’s eye makes the narrator have obsessive thoughts about the eye and be highly suspicious of it. Ultimately, these thoughts serve as a fuel to murder the old man and get rid of the source of his fears and obsessions: “Whenever it fell upon me, my blood ran cold; and so by degrees --very gradually --I made up my mind to take the life of the old man, and thus rid myself of the eye forever”. He may also be experiencing hallucinations (auditory in the form of the heartbeat, and visual in the form of the vulture eye) that affect his ability to discern rationally. In this way, the author suggests that killers can be people who suffer from mental disorders.

The narrator also appears to think that madness dulls the senses: “The disease had sharpened my senses --not destroyed --not dulled them”. However, in some cases of mental disorder, one’s senses can become more alert. The narrator prides himself with an acute sense of hearing: “Above all was the sense of hearing acute. I heard all things in the heaven and in the earth. I heard many things in hell”. This heightened sense allows him to hear the heartbeat of the old man even after he is dead. However, this sound could be interpreted as a sign of mental disorder, especially one that causes auditory hallucinations.
