Glossary of key terms

On this page, you can find words (and their explanation) that are particularly useful to know when studying the Cold War. Click each word for a full definition in English, including information about how the word is specifically relevant in the context of the topic.

arms race (s)
 

A competition between nations in which each nation strives to develop a superior military power, usually through the development of technologically advanced weapons. The Cold War was characterized by a nuclear arms race, with the US and the Soviet Union trying to develop more and more efficient nuclear weapons.

atomic bomb (s)
 

A nuclear bomb with a very high capacity for destruction through blast, heat, and radioactivity. The Cold War was to a large extent defined by the ever-present threat of the use of these weapons. 

ballistic missile (s)
 

A missile which is initially guided but falls under gravity, delivering one or more explosives on its established target.

capitalism (s)
 

An economic system in which private owners control the means of production and distribution, which they operate for profit. The means of production and distribution refer to any resource or facility that is used to produce economic value, such as natural resources like land and water, or facilities such as factories.

Also, in capitalism, the government plays a limited role in the economy.

The competition between the capitalist ideology of the US and the communist ideology of the Soviet Union was an important feature of the Cold War. 

CIA (s)
 

Short for Central Intelligence Agency, a US government organization created in 1947 that collects foreign intelligence and conducts secret operations. The CIA typically assists the US President and policymakers to make decisions related to national security. The CIA was involved in the Cold War in a number of ways, from gathering intelligence to the worldwide promotion of the US and Western culture.

communism (s)
 

An economic and social system or theory developed by Karl Marx in which the state is abolished and the means of production and distribution are controlled by the workers, while wealth is divided equally among citizens. What the US considered to be the Soviet Union’s efforts to expand communism to other countries was one of the main reasons behind the decades-long tension between it and the US. You should note that the Soviet Union did not practice communism as theorized by Karl Marx, because it had a government that controlled the economy, not leaving the means of production to be owned by the community. 

containment (s)
 

A United States policy which emerged in the Cold War as a response to the Soviet Union’s intent to expand communism to other countries. The containment policy was based on the idea that communist governments would eventually disappear if they were prevented from spreading their influence.

détente (s)
 

An easing of strained political relations. In this context, the term refers to a period of time during the Cold War in which the relationship between the US and the Soviet Union improved. The Cold War détente lasted from 1969 until 1980.

glasnost (s)
 

A Russian term made popular as a political slogan during the 198 0s by Mikhail Gorbachev, the General Secretary of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union. The term, which can be translated as “openness”, refers to a policy of transparency in government institutions and activities, which was a strong contrast to the way previous Soviet governments had worked.

ICBM (s)
 

Short for inter-continental ballistic missiles, which were developed by both the Soviet Union and the US during the Cold War. ICBMs were able to deliver nuclear weapons across the planet and were immune to defensive measures.

ideology (s)
 

A set of ideas and ideals which often serves as a basis for an organization, party, or political system. The Cold War was characterized by a competition between the capitalist ideology of the US and the socialist ideology of the Soviet Union.

KGB (s)
 

A state organization dealing with Soviet Union state security from 1954 to 1991. Its mission was to provide internal security, gather foreign intelligence, and maintain a secret police. During the Cold War, the KGB’s purpose was to monitor both private and public opinion for disruptive political ideas and to detect and prevent plots against the Soviet Bloc.

MAD (s)
 

Short for Mutual Assured Destruction. This phrase describes the fact that there are no real winners in a global nuclear war. If, for example, the US launched a nuclear strike against the Soviet Union, the Soviets would be forced to retaliate immediately before being destroyed, resulting in a nuclear exchange that would cause unimaginable death and devastation across the globe. The MAD-principle was an important factor that helped prevent the Cold War getting 'hot', as neither side wanted the destruction that nuclear war would certainly bring. 

NATO (s)
 

An acronym for the North Atlantic Treaty Organization, an alliance created in 1949 between the United States and 11 Western States, meant to protect Western Europe from a possible attack, including a Soviet invasion.

NATO still exists today and has grown to cover 29 nations, but its purpose has become more generally focused on peacekeeping and humanitarian interventions - especially since the collapse of the Soviet Union.  

perestroika (s)
 

A Russian term for the political and economic restructuring initiated by Mikhail Gorbachev, leader of the Soviet Union, in the 1980s. This large-scale reform is believed to have been an important factor in the end of the Cold War. Perestroika policies included introducing some democratic elections and allowing some private businesses to grow.

proxy war (s)
 

A war that has been supported by major powers which do not fight each other directly. There were many proxy wars during the Cold War, with the best known being the Vietnam War, the Korean War, and the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan.

satellite nations (s)
 

A political term that refers to countries that were allied with, but also under the influence of, the Soviet Union during the Cold War, such as: Hungary, Romania, Poland, Czechoslovakia, Germany, and Bulgaria.

Soviet bloc (s)
 

Also known as the Eastern Bloc, the Soviet bloc was a group of socialist nations spread across Eastern and Central Europe which were largely under the control of the Soviet Union during the Cold War. The member countries of the Soviet bloc were the Soviet Union, Albania, Poland, Bulgaria, East Germany, Romania, Czechoslovakia, and Hungary.

socialism (s)
 

In Karl Marx’s theory, socialism is an intermediary state in the transition from capitalism to communism. Although it was viewed as a communist state in the West (mostly because it was governed only by the Communist Party), the Soviet Union was, in fact, a socialist country, as the means of production were controlled by the government and not by the people.

the Iron Curtain (s)
 

The name of the military, political, and ideological barrier between the Soviet Union and its allies, and the West and other non-socialist countries. The boundary divided the two areas from 1945 until 1991. The best-known physical border was the Berlin Wall, a concrete wall that separated East Berlin from West Berlin.

the Domino Theory (s)
 

The theory is based on an analogy between communist influence and a falling row of dominoes. The theory stated that, like falling dominoes, if one country fell under communist control, its neighboring countries would soon be led by communist governments as well.

the Red Scare (s)
 

Refers to widespread fear about the dangers of communism and the infiltration of communists in the US. The color red was associated with communists because of their allegiance to the red Soviet flag. Communists were often referred to as “Reds”.

the Truman Doctrine (s)
 

Announced to US Congress in 1947 during the Harry S. Truman administration, the doctrine refers to a foreign policy meant to prevent the expansion of the Soviet Union’s influence during the Cold War.

USSR (s)
 

Short for Union of Soviet Socialist Republics, it is also an alternative name for the Soviet Union. A former socialist country established in 1922 and dissolved in 1991, the USSR included Russia and 14 other socialist countries. 

Although the Soviet Union aligned itself with Karl Marx’s stance against capitalism, it was a socialist (not a communist) country, as the government, and not the workers, controlled the means of production.

Warsaw Pact (s)
 

Formally known as the Warsaw Treaty of Friendship, Cooperation, and Mutual Assistance, the treaty established an alliance between the Soviet Union and seven other socialist countries, as a response to NATO, which was seen as a threat. The treaty established a unified military force under the command of the Soviet Union, calling on its members to defend any members attacked by other forces. Signed in 1955, the pact remained intact until 1991, when it was dissolved because non-socialist governments had risen to power in its member countries.
