General aspects

The Cold War was a decades-long period of tension between the democratic Western World, led by the US, and the communist countries led by the Soviet Union.

Both the US and the Soviet Union sought to maintain and expand their influence in the world while trying to minimize the influence of their opponent. However, they never engaged in actual military conflicts and, instead, fought indirectly through proxy wars, the arms race, and the space race.

The conflict was called the Cold War because it never turned into direct warfare between the US and the Soviet Union (and thus was not an actual war) - though it came dangerously close on several occasions. 

Ideological differences

The Cold War was not only fueled by President Truman’s anti-communist views and Stalin’s expansionist ambitions but also by ideological differences between the West and the Soviet Union.

The United States followed a capitalist ideology, which meant that people and companies could own land and were encouraged to run businesses for profits. The role of the government was secondary and democratic. This also meant that Americans enjoyed more freedoms than their Soviet counterparts, and saw the Soviet Union as a threat to these freedoms.

According to communist ideology, capitalism creates two main social classes, the poor and the rich, essentially maintaining poverty and inequality. In communism as theorized by Karl Marx, each person contributes in society according to their ability, and the goods are distributed equally or according to needs among the citizens - who control the means of production. Note, however, that the Soviet Union did not practice communism as theorized by Karl Marx but was a socialist country, especially because the means of production were owned by the state and not by the people. Furthermore, the Soviet Union was also totalitarian, meaning that it was a one-party state ruled by a leader who oversaw many aspects of the citizens' life.

While Americans feared for their freedoms, an anti-American sentiment also grew in the Soviet Union. Anti-American propaganda was constantly distributed during the Cold War, with the Soviet state media depicting the US as an exploitative, decaying society, which promoted a false image of prosperity.

The different interests that the Soviet Union and the US had in Europe also contributed to the start of the Cold War. The US wanted Germany to rebuild itself and become a reliable trading partner, while the Soviet Union wanted a demilitarized, deindustrialized Germany, which would compensate the Soviet Union financially for war damage. The Soviet Union’s other goal was to surround the country with communist states which could protect it from future invasion.

Proxy wars

The proxy wars were conflicts that happened in other countries, in which each side involved in the conflict was supported by either the West or by the Soviet Union.

The Greek Civil War (1944-1945) is believed to be the first proxy war of the Cold War era. With the support of the United Kingdom and the US, the non-communist faction won the war, and Greece avoided a communist regime. Alongside Greece, Turkey was also threatened by communism, a situation which prompted the creation of the Truman Doctrine in 1947.

Under the Truman Doctrine, the US set out to provide military and economic aid to countries which were under Soviet influence in the hopes to prevent the expansion of communism in those countries.

Other examples of proxy wars in the Cold War include the Korean War (1950 –1953), the Vietnam War (1955-1975), and the Soviet-Afghan War (1979-1989).

The arms race and changes in the American lifestyle

With both US and the Soviet Union in possession of nuclear weapons, the rivalry between them started an arms race, a competition for equal or superior military capabilities. In this way, the US and the Soviet Union fought the Cold War by showcasing their technological and military power. This functioned as proof of their superiority and of their ability to launch an attack on their opponents or defend themselves from a potential attack.

As both powers developed the ability to deliver a devastating attack, the MAD military strategy emerged in the 1960s. Under MAD, each country accepted the possibility that, in the event of initiating an attack, their opponent would deliver an equally violent attack, ensuring mutual destruction. This, in theory, would deter either side from attacking, as it would mean certain death not only for the defender but also for the attacker. 

American lifestyle and culture were also impacted by the arms race, especially by the nuclear threat. Attack drills were performed in schools and public places, and bomb shelters began to be built around the country. In the 1950s and 1960s, the ideas of nuclear devastation, radioactive mutation, or post-apocalyptic worlds were explored through film, literature, and TV programs, depicting people’s terror of the atomic bomb but also their hope for survival.

The Space Race

The Cold War was also fought through a competition over space exploration. The space race began in 1957 with the launch of Sputnik 1, the first artificial Earth satellite, into orbit by the Soviets. Because Sputnik was delivered into space with a powerful missile, this indicated that the Soviets could also have the ability to launch nuclear weapons across large distances.

Eager to catch up with the Soviets, in 1959 the US launched its own satellite, Explorer I, created the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) and funded several programs to research the military potential of space.

In 1961, the Soviets showed considerable progress in the space race by launching the first man into space, cosmonaut Yuri Gagarin. However, the US gained a decisive victory in 1969, when American astronaut Neil Armstrong became the first person to set foot on the Moon.
