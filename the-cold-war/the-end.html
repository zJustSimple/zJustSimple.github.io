The end of the Cold War

Cuprins
  • Eastern Europe’s 1989 revolutions

Eastern Europe’s 1989 revolutions

As part of his strategy to revitalize the communist regime, Gorbachev reduced some of the Soviet influence in Eastern Europe and abandoned the previous doctrine of military intervention to preserve communist regimes in satellite nations. Instead, he encouraged reforms in those countries, believing that their communist leaders should take their own measures to gain and maintain the sympathy of the people.

This led to a wave of reforms and changes in the Soviet bloc. In 1989, Hungary’s communist government passed reforms which led to a multiparty system and competitive elections. Poland followed suit, where the first non-communist government since 1948 rose to power.

Political reforms were also initiated in East Germany. East German protestors asked for the borders separating East Germany from both West Berlin and West Germany to be opened. Although the government’s leader, Erich Honecker, resisted this possibility and threatened the protesters with violence, he lost Gorbachev’s support and was replaced. The decision to open the Berlin border was finally made on 9th of November, 1989, when the communist government of East Germany announced that it would grant freedom of movement to West Berlin.

During that night, thousands of East Germans and West Germans gathered by the Wall and began to tear it down, celebrating the end of a divided Germany. The celebration lasted several days, with people tearing down the Wall using chisels and hammers while bulldozers and cranes removed larger sections of it. The fall of the Berlin Wall quickly led to the reunification of Germany on the 3rd of October, 1990 and became a symbol of the end of the Cold War era.

Democracy was achieved through protests in other communist countries as well. At the end of 1989, in Czechoslovakia, the communist party peacefully transferred power to Vaclav Havel, leader of demonstrators and co-founder of a reform group. This peaceful revolution became known as the “Velvet Revolution”.

In 1989, the communist regime in Romania led by Nicolae Ceaușescu was overthrown through protests which turned violent as the protestors confronted the military, sent in to establish order and stop the riots. The revolution ended with many casualties on both sides, and with the quick trial and execution of Ceaușescu and his wife. Free elections were held that year, and a democratic government was installed.

Soon after the revolution in Romania, the communist parties in Albania and Bulgaria also renounced their power.

The final collapse of the Soviet Union

Following the revolutions of 1989 in Eastern Europe, the Soviet Union was dissolved and politically separated into 15 independent countries, including Russia, in 1991. The dissolution was overseen by Boris Yeltsin, who became President after Gorbachev resigned as leader of the Soviet Union. Gorbachev’s resignation came as a direct result of the formation of the Commonwealth of Independent States (CIS) by 11 former Soviet republics, which meant that the Soviet Union could begin its dissolution.

However, Gorbachev was also disliked in the Soviet Union, largely because of his reforms, which caused economic instability and distrust in the communist regime. Although he had a positive image in the US, the US Administration had also begun to think that Gorbachev was losing his influence and feared a leader with strong anti-US views would replace him.

The collapse of the Soviet Union also marked the end of the Cold War as, for the West, it symbolized the fall of the communist threat and the superiority of democracy and capitalism.
