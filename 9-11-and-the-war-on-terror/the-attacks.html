Al-Qaeda and its origins

The 9/11 attacks were four terrorist attacks on US soil which took place on September 11, 2001. The attacks, which were attributed to the terrorist network Al-Qaeda, caused about 3,000 casualties and injured around 6,000 people. The United States reacted to the attacks by launching the ‘War on Terror’, which sought to destroy Al-Qaeda and its allies.

Al-Qaeda is an Islamic international terrorist organization formed at the end of the 1980s by a group of Arabs who took part in the Soviet invasion of Afghanistan. The main leader of the group was Osama bin Laden.

Al-Qaeda members are religious fundamentalists, upholding a strict, literal interpretation of religious texts. The members of the group believe that Christians and Jews are working together to destroy the true Islamic faith. They believe killing non-combatants is allowed by their religion. They are against man-made laws and want to replace them with the religious law, known as Sharia law.

The network uses violent attacks such as suicide bombings to frighten people and express their rejection of Western involvement in the Middle East. Their declared goal is to eliminate all external influences from Muslim countries and create a new caliphate (medieval religious state) in the Muslim world. However, their attacks do not only target foreigners but also mainstream Muslims.

They consider their violent attacks are religious duty, also known as jihad, a holy war against non-believers.

Al-Qaeda members usually receive military training in camps in countries like Afghanistan, Iraq, Pakistan, or Sudan.

Various attacks in different countries have been attributed to Al-Qaeda. Some of the most important ones are the 1998 US embassy bombings, the 9/11 attacks, and the 2002 Bali bombings.

The events of 9/11

On September 11, 2001, 19 militants connected with Al-Qaeda hijacked four US airplanes with civilians on board and directed them towards several strategic targets. The attacks were all meant to be suicide attacks with the militants dying in the process.

The first two airplanes hit the World Trade Center Twin Towers, which soon began to collapse in flames. Many people who were inside the buildings above the points of impact chose to jump from the windows rather than wait for the towers to fall. The third plane hit a part of the Pentagon (the headquarters of the US military). The fourth plane crashed in the countryside in Pennsylvania after the civilian passengers tried to overpower the hijackers.

The 9/11 attacks were planned by Al-Qaeda leader Osama bin Laden. However, he initially avoided claiming responsibility for the attacks. In 2002, Osama bin Laden sent a Letter to America, where he mentions “this Crusade Bush began”, to refer to the War on Terror as a religious war. Furthermore, Osama bin Laden admitted his involvement in the attacks in a 2004 video in which he claimed “we are free ... and want to regain freedom for our nation. As you undermine our security, we undermine yours.”

Another Al-Qaeda member who planed and organized the attacks was Khalid Sheikh Mohammed. He was also an active member of the Muslim Brotherhood, an international Islamic organization founded in Egypt and connected with various terrorist groups. Like Al-Qaeda, the Muslim Brotherhood wants to replace man-made laws with religious laws. Khalid Sheikh Mohammed was a firm believer in jihad, seeing the attacks as a rightful holy war against unbelievers, as per the literal interpretation of  Muslim religious texts.

Considered the deadliest terrorist attacks on US ground, the 9/11 events led to almost 3,000 deaths, including those of more than 400 police officers and firefighters involved in the rescue operations. The rescue operations started immediately after the planes struck and lasted for a few weeks.

At the time of the attacks, the US President, George W. Bush, was visiting a school and was almost immediately put on a military plane for protection. He addressed the country the same night through a televised speech from the White House. It became clear that the policies of the War on Terror were already starting to take shape as Bush announced: “We will make no distinction between the terrorists who committed these acts and those who harbor them.”

The President visited the World Trade Center – which came to be known as “Ground Zero” – on September 14. His strong response to the crisis increased his approval rating from 55% to 90%.

In the aftermath of the events, the policies of the War on Terror began to be implemented.
