Putting 9/11 and the War on Terror into perspective

Cuprins
  • Worldwide response to the US War on Terror
  • Terrorist attacks in other parts of the world
  • Immigration rhetoric in the US
  • Recent consequences of the War on Terror

Worldwide response to the US War on Terror

Most countries backed the US War on Terror initially. This was because the US invoked Article 5 of the NATO Charter, which states that when a member is attacked the others should defend that country. For example, the invasion of Afghanistan was supported by troops from countries like the UK, Australia, Canada, New Zealand, Denmark, France, Italy, Netherlands, and Norway.

However, support for the US and the War on Terror started to decrease in 2002, when the American government stated its intention to invade Iraq.

The Australian government was initially quick to denounce the 9/11 attacks. The Australian government backed the War on Terror with military support and intelligence analysis but also with domestic laws regarding terrorism.

Canada initially backed the war in Afghanistan with troops and ships. However, by 2006, Canadian public support for the war decreased. A 2007 scandal about Canadian soldiers using torture put pressure on the government to withdraw its troops. Even so, Canadian troops did not withdraw from Afghanistan until 2011.

The strongest supporter of the War on Terror was the UK, which backed the US throughout the campaigns, including in Iraq, although it faced strong internal disapproval. This was partly a result of the fact that the UK was also the target of several terrorist attacks during this period (see below).

Terrorist attacks in other parts of the world

The US was not the only target of terrorist attacks following 9/11. Other countries have been targeted, with some of the most important incidents taking place in the UK, Indonesia, Spain, and France.

During the War on Terror, the UK was the target of two major terrorist attacks: the 2005 London bombings and the 2007 Glasgow International Airport attack. In both cases, a solid link to terrorist group Al-Qaeda could not be established. The first attack was carried out by domestic terrorists. The second was carried out by suicide bombers. Such incidents triggered a strong response from the UK and probably acted as an encouragement for the UK to continue fighting the War on Terror together with the US. However, critics have argued that the attacks were actually triggered by the UK’s support of the War on Terror, especially the invasion of Iraq in 2003.

Bali was the target of bombings in 2002, leading to over 200 deaths and another 200 injured people. The attack was carried out by a suicide bomber influenced by Al-Qaeda and who admired Osama bin Laden.

Another terrorist attack linked to Al-Qaeda was the 2004 Madrid train bombing which led to the death of almost 200 people and injured another 2000. The attack produced an internal crisis because it was carried out before the Spanish elections. By 2007, the investigation could not prove that Al-Qaeda was directly linked with the attack.

More recently, in 2015, there was a terrorist attack on the French newspaper Charlie Hebdo in Paris. The attack was carried out by two brothers who were members of an Al-Qaeda branch in Yemen. It is believed they attacked the publication because of the newspaper’s satirical works on prophet Muhammad and other Islamic leaders.

These are only some of the most publicized terrorist attacks, but there have been many others since 2001 in different parts of the world, including in Muslim countries. However, many of them were claimed by ISIS rather than Al-Qaeda. Some examples are the Marseille attacks and the London Bridge attacks.

Immigration rhetoric in the US

The War on Terror has had a strong negative impact on immigration rhetoric in the US because it has encouraged people to associate Muslim and South Asian immigrants with terrorism. The fear inspired by the attacks has been exploited by politicians to gain votes and follow their political agenda.

For example, Donald Trump has won the US presidential elections using strong anti-immigration rhetoric. Furthermore, once he became president, he used the War on Terror and people’s fear of terrorists to implement a travel ban for people coming from certain Muslim countries. Although the ban was strongly criticized and had to be reformed, parts of the ban are still enforced today.

Similar anti-immigration discourses have been present in the British political landscape, partially contributing to UK citizens voting to leave the EU in 2017.

Recent consequences of the War on Terror

Even though the War on Terror started almost two decades ago, it still has consequences today.

Recently, an International Criminal Court prosecutor announced he wants to sue US soldiers and intelligence personnel for using torture on detainees in Afghanistan. The ICC is part of the United Nations and was formed in 2002. Its purpose is to bring to justice those who have committed war crimes, genocides, and crimes against humanity.

However, the US does not officially recognize this court, and the current threat of an ICC trial has resulted in a strong anti-court reaction in the US. American National Security Adviser John Bolton has recently declared the court illegitimate, argued the country will not accept the accusation, and threatened to prosecute the ICC judges in the US system.
