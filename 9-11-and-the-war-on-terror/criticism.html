Criticism of the War on Terror

Cuprins
  • Introduction
  • Civilian casualties and drone strikes
  • Human rights violations
  • Edward Snowden and the NSA scandal

Introduction

The War on Terror in response to the 9/11 terrorist attacks on the US has often been criticized by various countries, organizations, political analysts, and NGOs (non-governmental organizations).

The main aspects that fell under scrutiny were related to ethical issues, inefficiency, and waste of economic resources. The name of the campaign itself was criticized because many have argued that one cannot wage war against a concept.

Many have argued that the 9/11 events were exploited to pursue other long-term military and strategic objectives as well as to limit civil liberties and human rights.

Civilian casualties and drone strikes

The total number of civilians who have died in different countries where the US carried out military campaigns against terrorist groups is hard to estimate. Some of the available data suggests that over 1 million people died in Iraq, almost 250,000 people died in Afghanistan, and thousands of people died in other countries.

Such high numbers of causalities are a reason for moral concern, especially since many of the casualties were ordinary civilians (rather than active terrorists or militants) and were caused by drone strikes.

The first drone strike was ordered in Afghanistan in 2001. During Bush’s Administration, a total of 57 drones strikes were authorized in different countries. During Obama’s presidency, the total number of drone strikes in the War on Terror reached 563.

For example, the CIA (Central Intelligence Agency) used drones in Pakistan to target and strike supposed terrorist cells. As drones are controlled remotely, it is hard to tell the difference between civilians and real targets. 

The use of drones in the aerial space of another country can be perceived as an invasion without declaring war. This breaches the traditional laws of war.

US military drone strikes were later used to target ISIL in 2016 and 2017. The US estimated that the drone strikes against ISIL might have caused the unintended death of over 700 civilians, but the real numbers might be higher. Critics argue that while drone strikes might protect the lives of American soldiers, they cause too many civilian casualties in countries where they are used, making their use morally wrong.

At the same time, there are also voices which argue that drones are ineffective in fighting terrorism and might help expand terrorism because they make civilians resent their use by the US. This could lead to the radicalization of more civilians who might become terrorists.

Human rights violations

The War on Terror has also been criticized because of human rights violations. After 9/11, human rights and civil liberties activists have often expressed criticism regarding the way the US government and its allies have treated both terrorist suspects and US citizens.

Following the 9/11 attacks, American authorities detained hundreds of thousands of individuals, in the US, in Iraq, in Afghanistan, and at different black sites (places where unacknowledged activities of the US government take place) around the world such as Guantanamo Bay.

These people were often imprisoned without trial for an indefinite period and questioned using enhanced interrogation techniques – a euphemism for torturing techniques such as waterboarding. Additionally, critics argued that, when released, some prisoners were sent to countries where they further risked being tortured or having their human rights abused.

Such treatment of prisoners violates the Geneva Conventions, which guarantee the humane treatment of war prisoners and suspects.

At these black sites, American soldiers sometimes abused prisoners, which became well-known after the case of Abu Ghraib. At this military prison, American soldiers committed physical and sexual abuse, torture, and killings. This became known to the public in 2004 with the publication of some photos. At that time, Bush argued that these were isolated incidents.

The UN publicly criticized the violation of human rights by several states during the War on Terror arguing that: “Some States have engaged in torture and other ill-treatment to counter terrorism, while the legal and practical safeguards available to prevent torture, such as regular and independent monitoring of detention centers, have often been disregarded.” 

The UN has also warned states that they are obliged by international law to respect human rights, even in the context of the War on Terror.

Edward Snowden and the NSA scandal

A major criticism of the War on Terror was related to the US reducing civil liberties by surveilling and monitoring its population, as well as people in other countries. This was revealed in 2013 when Edward Snowden (who was an NSA contractor) leaked documents about the NSA’s activities. The documents showed that the NSA was not only spying on US citizens but also on British, Australian, and Canadian people. 

At that time, the US President Barack Obama strongly denied the leaks, while Snowden was charged in absence because he was granted asylum in Russia.

The whole scandal led to strong critical reactions against alleged global surveillance programs. Critics argued that population monitoring violates people’s right to privacy because the documents suggested that people’s phones and online activity could be easily monitored.

Following the leaks, the UN denounced unjustified digital surveillance and adopted a symbolic statement mentioning people’s right to online privacy. The leaks also opened a debate in the US regrading privacy and surveillance which led to the USA Freedom Act of 2015.

The Act changed some provisions of the Patriot Act adopted by the Bush Administration following the 9/11 attacks. Among others, it imposed limits on collecting telecommunication data of Americans by intelligence agencies. Many believed the changes were a result of Snowden’s leaks.

Edward Snowden himself remains a fugitive, as he violated a number of US laws when he leaked the famous documents. To this day, there is a lot of disagreement about his legacy - many consider him a hero, because he revealed the extent of illegal surveillance to the people, but others call him a traitor because he gave up US state secrets.
