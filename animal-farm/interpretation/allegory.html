Animal Farm and the Russian Revolution

George Orwell intended Animal Farm to be an allegory about the Russian Revolution and the following rise of the Soviet Union. In this section you can read a brief summary of the historical events. Afterwards we will give a detailed overview of how these events correspond to the story of Animal Farm.

In response to a long period of poverty and Russia’s disastrous involvement in WWI, the people rose up against Tsar Nicholas II, who was forced to step down during the February Revolution of 1917 (and was later executed along with his wife and daughters).

The February Revolution was followed by the October Revolution led by Vladimir Lenin, which eventually led to the Russian Civil War. The civil war ended in 1922 with Lenin’s establishment of the new Soviet Union. 

After Lenin’s death in 1924, Josef Stalin rose to power within the Communist Party and gained control of the Soviet Union. In the following years, he increasingly established himself as a dictator and pulled the government in the direction of more strict control with both the people and the economy.

Leon Trotsky had also participated in the revolution and played a central role during the Civil War. He led a political faction which tried to resist Stalin’s power and presented a more liberal vision for Soviet Russia's future, but he was eventually expelled from all his political positions and forced into exile, giving Stalin full control of the government.

Many landowners (the so-called ‘kulaks’) were executed when Stalin reformed the Soviet economy, and their land was transferred to state ownership and turned into collective farms. It was believed that the creation of collective farm would increase productivity, but in practice it resulted in mass starvation, and millions of Russians died during the first years of Stalin’s reign (particularly during the Great Famine of 1932-1933).

Stalin was extremely intolerant of political criticism – and often had critics executed or sent to brutal labour camps. This happened to an enormous extent during the Great Purge of 1936-1938, in which hundreds of thousands of people were executed – and even more died in prison or labour camps.  

During WWII, Stalin had a complex relationship towards the other major world powers. In 1939, Soviet Russia signed a 10-year non-aggression pact with Nazi Germany, in which the two nations pledged not to attack each other (though they were not allies either).

However, Hitler broke the pact when he invaded the Soviet Union in 1941, leading to Stalin joining WWII on the side of the Allied forces. In the Tehran conference of 1943, Stalin met with the leaders of the US and the UK to coordinate their war efforts against Hitler.

Although the Allied forces won the war through their combined efforts, the relations between particularly the US and the Soviet Union quickly grew tense again due to the massive ideological differences between the two superpowers. This development eventually led to the Cold War, which would continue to some extent all the way up to the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991.

In the following tables, we will provide a quick overview of the main events and characters in the book - and what part of Russian or Soviet history they are (probably) meant to represent. The list should not be viewed as final, as some events and characters in the story might be related to several different real-life events or people.

Events

In Animal Farm

In Russian history

The Rebellion against Jones

The Russian Revolution (1917)

The pigs taking the best food for themselves

The communist government and the Red Army taking food from Russian farmers (‘forced requisitioning’) (1918-1922)

Snowball’s defeat and exile

Trotsky’s defeat and exile (1927-1928)

The various attempts to build the windmill

Stalin’s various five-year plans for the Soviet economy (1928->)

The hens’ short-lived rebellion against Napoleon’s plan to sell their eggs (where they destroyed their own eggs in protest), which was punished by cutting food rations.

The peasants’ resistance to Stalin’s collectivisation of their farms (where they sometimes burned their own crops in protest), which was punished by cutting food rations. (~1929-1933)

The food shortage which Napoleon tries to hide from the outside world.

The Soviet famine, which Stalin tried to hide from the outside world (1932-1933).

Napoleon’s executions of ‘traitors’

Stalin’s Great Purge, in which he executed or imprisoned enormous numbers of people he viewed as political opponents (1936-1938)

Frederick’s attack on Animal Farm

Hitler’s attack on the Soviet Union (1941)

The final meeting between pigs and humans

The Tehran Conference of Allied leaders during WWII (1943).

Rising tension again between pigs and humans

Rising tensions between the West and the Soviet Union after WWII (which would eventually lead to the Cold War).

 

Characters

In Animal Farm

In Russian history

Mr. Jones

Tsar Nicholas II

Old Major

Karl Marx and/or Vladimir Lenin

Napoleon

Josef Stalin

Snowball

Leon Trotsky

Squealer

A minister in Stalin’s government, possibly Vyacheslav Molotov. Also government propaganda in general.

Boxer

Possibly meant to represent Alexei Stakhanov, a dedicated and efficient worker whom the Soviet government used as a shining example. May also represent the working class in general.

Mr. Frederick

Adolf Hitler

Mr. Pilkington and his friends

Leaders of the major capitalist powers during WWII, such as British Prime Minister Winston Churchill and US President Franklin D. Roosevelt.

Moses the raven

The Russian Orthodox Church: Frequently attacked and criticised in the early days of the Soviet Union, but eventually restored to official status by Stalin during WWII.

Mollie

The Russians who left the country during or after the revolution.

The pigs

Senior members of the Communist Party

The dogs

The military and the (secret) police

The sheep

The state-controlled media repeating government propaganda. May alternatively represent a segment of the population particularly vulnerable to propaganda.

The other animals

The general population of the Soviet Union.
