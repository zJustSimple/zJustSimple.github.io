Summary

Wuthering Heights is a novel written by Emily Bronte between 1845 and 1846 and published in 1847 under the pen name “Ellis Bel”. The author’s only novel tells us the story about the unfulfilled love between Catherine and Heathcliff.

The novel starts with the narrator - a citizen who just arrived in Thrushcross Grange all the way from London, wishful to escape all the noise associated with big cities. He goes by the name of Lockwood. The house’s owner, Heathcliff, was known for his sullen attitude and his lack of respect for the rules.

Lockwood goes to Wuthering Heights specifically to meet Heathcliff. The place has always been affected by cold and harsh winds, however the house itself was built exactly to withstand such threats. The interior was as unwelcoming as the exterior. Lockwood is greeted by Joseph, an old servant and Zillah, a strong and big woman who came to save him from the dogs. Eventually, Heathcliff shows himself and speaks to Lockwood. The former promises to visit Wuthering Heights again the next day.

However, when Lockwood comes back to the estate the next day, Heathcliff is nowhere to be found. While entering the hallway, he sees a woman, who he was sure was Heathcliff’s wife. Lo and behold, Heathcliff finally comes back, and he’s accompanied by a dirty and disrespectful young man.

Lockwood finds out that the woman’s name is Catherline Linton, and that the man was called Hareton Earnshaw. The narrator becomes more and more curious about this family’s strange story, but he hesitates asking any questions regarding the subject. The weather gets worse and worse, thus Lockwood decides to spend the night in Wuthering Heights.

On the bedsides in which he was sleeping, the narrator sees some writings: “Catherine Linton”, “Catherine Earnshaw”, and “Catherine Heathcliff”. He also discovers a handbook that seemed like a diary. While reading it, he discovers how Catherine was beaten and abused by her brother, Hindley and also by Joseph. Lockwood has nightmares about what he had just read. He could see Catherine holding on to the window while trying to find her former room. After waking up from the surreal nightmare, he decides to leave Wuthering Heights and go back to Thrushcross Grange, with his cold now aggravated.

While laying in bed and thinking about how many boring days are coming, he decides to find out more about Catherine. He asks Ellen Dean, one of the servants, if she could find out more info. She agrees and starts telling the story from the point when mister Earnshaw (Hindley’s father) left for Liverpool. Hindley and Catherine were hoping to receive gifts once their father returned, however he comes back with another child. That kid was Heathcliff. Dirty, with an unpleasant look, he’s not received well by the two brothers and neither by the servant who felt repulsed by him. Despite all this, mister Earnshaw favored him most and always stood up for him when he was bullied by others. As time passes, Catherine becomes attached to Heathcliff, but Hindley starts detesting him more and more.

Once mister Earnshaw dies, Hindley comes back to Wuthering Heights after returning from college. He married and started acting like a despot. He forces Heathcliff to work as a servant in his own home. Meanwhile, love starts bonding Catherine closer to Heathcliff. They take joy in each other’s company in the few hours when they were free.

During one of their strolls, the two stumble upon the Linton family’s residence. The comfort and civility found during this visit changes Catherine altogether, as she would soon return to Wuthering Heights almost unrecognizable. Her experience can be analyzed on two levels:

  • A strong, spiritual connection between her and Heathcliff
  • Vanity, superficiality and obsession with material things.

She confesses to Nelly Dean. Heathcliff hears the last part of her confession and runs away from Wuthering Heights completely disappointed. Although Catherine looks for him everywhere, she fails to find him and gets struck by a strong fever.

During the following six months, Catherine makes all sorts of wrong decisions. When her health goes back to normal, she marries Edgar Linton and starts living a dull life with him. She, at the very least, seems happy right up until the moment Heathcliff returns. Rich and fancy-looking, he arrives at Wuthering Height to take revenge on those who humiliated him. He married Isabella, Edgar’s sister. His plan was to become the owner of Earnshaw and Linton estates.

Meanwhile, Cathy (Catherine’s daughter) and Hareton (Hindley’s son) fall in love and unite against Heathcliff. Looking at the two, he is reminded of his own story and his past feelings. Realising that he had become exactly like the people he despised, he loses his interest in revenge and soon dies. Cathy and Hareton are now free to build a future together. Thus, through their love, the love between Catherine and Heathcliff also survives.

In conclusion, Wuthering Heights shows us an outstanding perspective about love. Despite Catherine and Heathcliff’s failures, the vicious cycle ends once Cathy and Hareton commit to one another. You could say that it’s a tragedy with a happy end. The novel was declared one of the most valuable stories in the world in 1954.
