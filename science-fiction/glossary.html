Glossary of key terms

On this page, you can find words that are particularly useful to know when studying the science fiction genre. Click each word for a full definition in English.

AI (s)
 

Short for ‘Artificial Intelligence’– meaning intelligence, such as problem solving or reacting to environmental factors, demonstrated by a machine rather than a human or animal. Many science fiction stories include advanced AI programs, who may even act as full characters in the story alongside humans and aliens.

alien (s/adj)
 

In a science fiction context, an alien is a creature who is not native to Earth. Science fiction stories vary widely in how they depict aliens - sometimes they are described as being very similar to human beings, while other stories present them as completely bizarre.

cautionary tale (s)
 

A story that is meant as a warning. Dystopian science fiction stories often include some kind of warning about contemporary society, and the political, social or technological directions it might be moving in. Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949) by George Orwell is a famous example of a cautionary tale, written as a direct response to the rise of totalitarian regimes within Orwell’s lifetime.

dystopia (s)
 

A term that describes a society or world that is terrible to live in. Many famous science fiction stories take place in a dystopian setting, often in an imaginary future.

You can read much more about dystopia here.

extraterrestrial (s)
 

A word that describes something or someone that does not come from Earth. The word entered popular culture with the release of the 1982 science fiction movie E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial.

faster-than-light travel (s)
 

In our current understanding of science, it is impossible to move faster than the speed of light. However, science fiction settings that feature interplanetary or intergalactic travel often include some kind of invention or discovery that makes it possible to travel faster than light.

first contact (s)
 

Refers to the first meeting(s) between human beings and an alien race. Many famous science fiction stories focus on the first contact - such as the novel War of the Worlds (1898) or the movie Arrival (2016).

Hugo Award (s)
 

The most prestigious award for science fiction literature, named after the famous science fiction publisher Hugo Gernsback. It was first awarded in 1953 and still exists today. The award covers a number of different categories, including novels, short stories and drama.

social commentary (s)
 

A story intended as a comment on current social or political trends in society. Sometimes science fiction writers create stories intended as social commentary, especially if they want to criticise specific elements of contemporary society. Related to cautionary tale (see above).

subgenre (s)
 

Something that shares the main characteristics of a wider genre (such as science fiction), but also has specific features that mark it as belonging to a subgenre.

We cover some of the most popular subgenres of science fiction here.

totalitarianism (s)
 

A political system in which the government control all or most aspects of its citizen's lives. Many dystopian stories feature a totalitarian government that keeps its citizens under strict control, often through threats of imprisonment, torture or death. 

Transhumanism (s)
 

A real-life philosophical movement focused on the idea of using technology to improve the human body and mind in ways that allow us to surpass our normal, human limits. Common transhumanist goals include boosting human intelligence and achieving much longer human lifespans (or even some form of immortality). Transhumanist ideas are frequently explored in science fiction stories.

utopia (s)
 

A term that describes a perfect society or perfect world. Some science fiction stories involve descriptions of utopian societies (or societies that seem utopian at first).

You can read much more about utopia here.
