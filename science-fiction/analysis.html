Analysing science fiction

Cuprins
  • Introduction
  • Science fiction as social commentary
  • Common themes in science fiction stories

Introduction

When you are asked to analyse science fiction texts in class - or choose to do so when writing an assignment - there are a few important things to keep in mind.

Of course, most of the general advice about fiction analysis will also be relevant for your work - for example, it is probably a good idea to talk about characters and themes - but there are also some things that are uniquely relevant for science fiction analysis.

On a basic level, it may be interesting to study the genre traits that define your chosen story as science fiction, such as the use of a future setting or the presence of advanced technology. On a more advanced level, it will often be especially relevant to focus your interpretation on the social commentary that the author is trying to make about contemporary society. In addition, there are a number of literary themes that tend to be particularly common within the science fiction genre, because it offers a unique perspective on them. 

Science fiction as social commentary

Many science fiction stories have messages that are intended as some sort of commentary on the current state of the world. When you analyse and interpret a science fiction story, it is therefore interesting to examine whether such a message is intended, and how it comes across.

If you are working with a dystopian science fiction story, it is highly likely that it is intended as some kind of cautionary tale, warning against developments in modern society that could potentially lead in a harmful direction. An obvious example of this might be climate change: it is generally agreed that climate change is damaging our planet and will continue to do so until major changes are made, making it a popular subject in science fiction stories.

If you are working with a piece of utopian science fiction, the message you are looking for is more likely to be an inspirational one. Instead of showing a potential disaster, utopian stories typically focus on the heights that humanity might reach if we overcome our divisions and limitations, and push our efforts into making a brighter future for everyone. Sometimes these stories still present some indirect criticism of modern society, but by showing the best-case scenarios of the future instead of the worst, they try to create a feeling of optimism that might move people to see solutions instead of problems.

Be mindful of the year that your story was released when you seek to make this kind of interpretation - historical science fiction authors naturally comment on the situations that they observe in their own time. However, it may still be an interesting perspective to consider whether historical science fiction stories can offer relevant insights about the current state of the world. For example, many have argued that the points George Orwell make about government surveillance in Nineteen Eighty-Four have only become more relevant since the novel was published in 1949, particularly due to the invention of increasingly sophisticated technological surveillance techniques.

Finally, note that there are also science fiction stories that are not directly aimed at social commentary, but instead focus on exploring other themes, or perhaps simply aim to entertain. 

Common themes in science fiction stories

Some literary themes are particularly common in science fiction stories, because the genre provides new and interesting ways to let authors explore them. Below we will consider some of the themes that you are very likely to find in science fiction stories.

Human nature

Science fiction stories have a lot of potential when it comes to exploring philosophical ideas about humanity, and what it is that makes us human. This is because science fiction offers many ways to describe the human condition in contrast to other, somewhat similar forms of existence - typically either robots/artificial intelligence or alien lifeforms. By using these contrasts in their stories, authors can offer their perspective on what it means to be human - and what separates us from other species.

Science fiction may also include transhumanist themes, where the story describes various attempts to take human existence to a different level - perhaps by means of technology, or through symbiosis with alien lifeforms.

Examples of stories with this theme:

Stranger in a Strange Land (1961) by Robert A. Heinlein

Blade Runner (1982) by Ridley Scott

The Hyperion Cantos (1989-1997) by Dan Simmons

Textual perspective - "Compassion Circuit" by John Wyndham
 

This short story describes a world in which human-like robots can be purchased as servants - the most advanced of them being equipped with a 'compassion circuit' that allows them to show some degree of human emotions, allowing them to become very considerate caregivers. However, the robotic technology soon starts to intrude in the main characters' lives in more disturbing ways.

Technology

Technology’s role in human lives is a very common theme in science fiction stories. A specific question that is often examined is whether technology genuinely improves human lives, or whether it should be regarded as a threatening force. Many science fiction stories are at least somewhat pessimistic about technology, though some have also highlighted its positive impact in making the world a better place. 

The Black Mirror TV show is often centered on this theme, usually with a pessimistic description of the way technology affects people. A number of science fiction stories also comment on the dangers of using technology to ‘play god’ – a tradition that can be traced back to Mary Shelley’s Frankenstein.

Examples of stories with this theme:

Frankenstein (1818) by Mary Shelley

The Foundation series (1942-1993) by Isaac Asimov

The Black Mirror TV show (2011-ongoing) by Charlie Brooker

Textual perspective - "iAnna" by Will Self
 

The short story describes a new medical condition known as 'iPhrenia' caused by extreme overuse of smartphones. People who suffer from the condition interact with the world as if it was a smartphone, using finger gestures and holding imagined messaging conversations with themselves.

Reality

Since many science fiction stories feature descriptions of different forms of existence - such as exceptionally realistic virtual worlds or aliens with a completely different perception of the world - they are also well-suited to explore philosophical ideas about the nature of reality.

The Matrix movies are perhaps the most famous examples of this theme in popular culture, raising the question about whether we can be certain that the world surrounding us is truly real, or just a result of input being fed to us by a computer somewhere.

Examples of stories with this theme:

Neuromancer (1984) by William Gibson

The Otherland series (1996-2001) by Tad Williams

The Matrix trilogy (1999-2003) by the Wachowski Brothers

Rebellion

Because they often feature evil totalitarian governments, science fiction stories tend to focus on protagonists that take on the roles of rebels, fighting against injustice. Sometimes the rebels emerge victorious, other times they become tragic heroes, as it turns out the dystopian government is not so easily defeated. Often the rebels start out as parts of the system, but eventually realise the error of their ways and join or start the rebellion.

This theme is particularly common in modern young adult science fiction stories - perhaps because the theme of rebellion is already a natural fit for stories that focus on teenagers in the process of growing up, even before you add an evil, oppressive government to the mix.

When analysing and interpreting stories with this theme, it may be useful to focus particularly on the main character’s development, and how this character responds to the dystopian setting of the story. It is also interesting to note whether the rebellion in the story is successful or not, and the reasons behind why the totalitarian government is weak or powerful.

Example of stories with this theme:

Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949) by George Orwell

Fahrenheit 451 (1953) by Ray Bradbury

The Hunger Games trilogy (2008-2010) by Suzanne Collins

The Divergent trilogy (2011-2013) by Veronica Roth

The end of the world

Many science fiction stories are focused on describing some kind of collapse of human civilisation - and/or the aftermath of such a collapse.

The stories may vary greatly in their descriptions of how the world comes to an end, however. If the story describes a situation where humanity brings the end upon itself (nuclear war, climate change or pollution), it will often be intended as a kind of cautionary tale. In this case, it will probably be useful to focus your interpretation on the story’s message.

Regardless of how the collapse is described, the plots of these stories are often driven by the main characters’ struggle for survival - either during the catastrophe itself or in the post-apocalyptic remnants of civilisation.

Examples of stories with this theme:

The Death of Grass (1956) by John Christopher

The Day After Tomorrow (2004) by Roland Emmerich

The Road (2006) by Cormac McCarthy
