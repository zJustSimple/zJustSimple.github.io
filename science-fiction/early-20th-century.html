Science fiction in the early 20th century

Introduction

The early 20th century provided many opportunities for the science fiction genre to grow and evolve. The first cinematic movies started to be produced, and some directors began to experiment with the genre early on, producing impressive works despite technical limitations. Meanwhile, the rise of science fiction magazines also served to bring stories and ideas to a wider audience. Finally, two of the most iconic works of science fiction in history were published during these five decades – Brave New World by Aldous Huxley and Nineteen Eighty-Four by George Orwell.  

The first science fiction movies

Movies were still a very recent invention at the turn of the century, usually enjoyed by only a small number of people at dedicated exhibitions. The first movies were both silent and black and white, but even at this point some movie makers were very creative with both themes and special effects.

The first science fiction movie is widely thought to be Le Voyage dans la Lune (A Trip to the Moon , 1902), by the French movie maker George Méliès. The movie shows a rocket travelling to the moon, through special effects that were remarkable at the time. The movie was only about 13 minutes long, but was still a huge hit with contemporary audiences. 

Another famous example of early science fiction in cinema is Metropolis (1927), by the German director Fritz Lang. It was one of the first feature-length science fiction movies, and just like A Trip to the Moon it introduced groundbreaking special effects. Metropolis received mixed reviews at the time (H.G. Wells notably hated it), but today it is recognised as an early science fiction classic.

Finally, some of H.G. Wells’ classic science fiction stories were adapted for the cinema in this period. Examples include The Invisible Man (1933) and Island of Lost Souls (1932).

Amazing Stories

In 1926, the American inventor Hugo Gernsback launched Amazing Stories, the first dedicated science fiction magazine. Science fiction stories had appeared in other magazines in previous years, but a dedicated platform was an important step in bringing science fiction to wider audiences, leading some to regard Gernsback as another “Father of Science Fiction”. Gernsback even invented the term ‘science fiction’, coining it in 1929, and his first name is also attached to the most famous literary prize in the science fiction world, the Hugo Award.

Amazing Stories at first focused on reprints of classic stories by authors such as Jules Verne and H.G. Wells, but also became a platform for newer writers. Despite its influence, the magazine continually struggled financially, and often changed owners and publishing strategies in the coming years. However, it persisted through these problems and is still being published today.

Aldous Huxley’s Brave New World

Aldous Huxley (1894-1963) was a British writer who wrote extensively and produced both fiction and non-fiction. 

The dystopian novel Brave New World (1931) is Huxley’s most famous work. Its title is highly ironic, as the world it depicts is not intended to be a positive vision of humanity’s future. Set in 2540 in a futuristic world state, the novel depicts a dystopian society in which the population is kept under control by mindless entertainment and hedonistic pleasures. However, some members of this society starts to question their lifestyle when they make contact with outsiders who live by different rules.

Brave New World can be read as a warning against consumerism and nihilism, as it depicts a society where people have no real values and simply pursue immediate pleasures. Some have argued that the story has renewed relevance today, in a digital world that offers endless entertainment with just a few clicks.

George Orwell’s Nineteen Eighty-Four

George Orwell was a British journalist and author who wrote extensively in many different genres. Orwell was a democratic socialist and much of his work has strong political overtones, often taking the form of direct criticism of political tendencies that Orwell observed in contemporary society.

Shortly before his death, Orwell completed Nineteen Eighty-Four (1949), perhaps the most famous dystopian science fiction novel of all time. Orwell chose to set the novel in the future: it is set in 1984 in Oceania, a future super-state in which the population is kept under strict surveillance, and is ruled by the Inner Party and the mysterious Big Brother. The main character of the story, Winston Smith, tries to rebel against the tyranny of the Party, but soon realises that their control is very hard to shake off.

Nineteen Eighty-Four has had enormous influence on both later science fiction stories and popular culture in general, to the point where the iconic phrase “BIG BROTHER IS WATCHING YOU!” is immediately recognisable to many people today, even if they have not read the book. In particular, the novel has inspired many later stories about surveillance, totalitarian governments and attempts at rebellious actions from within a dystopian society.

Orwell's novel is also brought up in connection with real life politics - for example, many newspapers have drawn parallels between Donald Trump’s presidency and the events described in Nineteen Eighty-Four, as the Trump administration sometimes seem to distort the truth and flat out deny basic facts about reality, in much the same way that the Inner Party does in Orwell’s novel. Nineteen Eighty-Four was also frequently referenced in connection with Edward Snowden’s revelations about the NSA in 2013, since they suggested that we in some senses live in a surveillance state similar to the one that Orwell described.
