The beginning of science fiction as a literary genre

Though a number of earlier stories had contained science fiction elements (fantastic inventions, aliens, time travel, etc…), the first stories that resembled modern science fiction started to emerge in the 19th century. However, because it is hard to find a definition of science fiction that everyone agrees on, it is also difficult to determine who started the genre. Below, we will consider three possible candidates.

Mary Shelley

The British author Mary Wollstonecraft Shelley (1797-1851) is best known for the novel Frankenstein; or, the Modern Prometheus (1818). The novel tells the story of Victor Frankenstein, a scientist whose experiments are focused on the creation of life. Victor eventually attempts to create human life, but the creature he produces is so hideous that he flees upon seeing it. The monster escapes and causes further trouble for Victor, but it is also revealed that the monster is suffering from loneliness and existential struggles, in some ways making the readers feel sorry for it.

Those who argue that Frankenstein should be considered the first science fiction novel focus on the fact that the main character is a scientist who uses technological means to carry out his plan, whereas earlier stories of human beings creating life tend to focus on magical or divine forces. Because Shelley attempts to create a scientific explanation for the supernatural event that occurs, this pulls the story in the direction of science fiction rather than fantasy. The story also suggests the dangers of ‘playing god’ through technology – something that later science fiction authors also frequently explore.

However, note that Frankenstein also has many characteristics of the gothic horror genre.

Jules Verne

The French author Jules Verne (1828-1905) was highly productive and wrote a huge number of adventure stories. Many of his most famous stories were centered around technology that did not yet exist, but seemed plausible as future technological developments, which is a classic feature of the science fiction genre.

One example of this is the two-part series De la Terre à la Lune (From the Earth to the Moon, 1865) and Autour de la Lune (Around the Moon, 1869), which tells the story of a manned spaceship which is launched towards the Moon and completes an orbit of it (a feat that would be matched in real life a century later in 1968, with the Apollo 8 mission).

However, the most famous example is probably Vingt Mille Lieues sous les mers (Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea, 1869-1870), which gives a detailed description of an advanced submarine - an invention which did exist in Verne’s time in primitive versions, but which had not yet become practical for use (submarines first started to be used widely during the First World War, 1914-1918).

Those who argue that Verne is the ‘Father of Science Fiction’ focus on these stories and their descriptions of the future capabilities of human technology, though critics argue that his predictions were rarely based on the scientific realities of his own time, and were intended to be more fantastical than realistic.

H.G. Wells

The British writer Herbert George Wells (1866-1946) is one of the most famous early science fiction writers - and those who do not believe Verne or Shelley pioneered the genre tend to focus on Wells, as there is little doubt that many of his novels belong in (and helped define!) the modern science fiction genre. His works have had enormous influence on later science fiction stories in all kinds of media, to the point where some have even called him “The Shakespeare of Science Fiction”.
