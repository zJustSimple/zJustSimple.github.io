Dystopia and utopia

Cuprins
  • Introduction
  • Utopia
  • Dystopia
  • Dystopian and utopian science fiction

Introduction

It is important to understand the terms utopia and dystopia when studying science fiction, as many science fiction works touch upon one or both of these concepts. Specifically, science fiction settings typically feature a society (or societies) that is either utopian or dystopian when compared to the contemporary society of the science fiction author.

Utopia

The word ‘utopia’ was invented by Sir Thomas Moore in 1516. It is formed from a combination of two Ancient Greek words, and literally translated it means ‘no-place’. It was originally used to refer to any kind of fictional society, but over time it gained a more specific, positive meaning.

Today, the word ‘utopia’ is used to refer to fictional societies that are significantly better than the world we currently live in, or even perfect. Typical utopian societies might feature elements such as complete social equality, plenty of resources for everyone, respect for the natural environment, a high standard of living, and people who are free to pursue happiness in whatever way they choose. However, note that utopias has been depicted in very different ways over the years, often reflecting some of the social values of the era in which they were written. A socialist utopia would look significantly different from a capitalist utopia, for example. 

Critics of the idea have pointed out that it may be difficult or impossible to achieve a perfect utopia in real life, because human beings want different things (sometimes directly contradictory things), and it would be hard to imagine a world that would leave every single person satisfied. As a result of this criticism, the term ‘utopia’ is also sometimes brought up in real-life politics, often used with negative connotations. A politician might criticise his opponents views as being utopian - usually meaning that they are unrealistic and could never work in the real world.

Dystopia

Dystopia is the opposite of utopia. The word is also constructed from elements of Ancient Greek, and its meaning can be directly translated as ‘bad place’.

The word is used to refer to fictional societies that are considerably worse than the world we currently live in. Typical dystopian societies might feature elements such as high levels of social inequality, scarcity of resources, an environment ravaged by pollution, horrible standards of living, and - very commonly - people who are oppressed by a totalitarian government.

The word ‘dystopia’ also shows up in real-life politics on occasion, especially when people are trying to criticise the current state of affairs of society, by noting that it has dystopian characteristics such as high levels of government surveillance, or heavy restrictions on individual freedom.

Dystopian and utopian science fiction

Science fiction stories are often categorised as being either utopian or dystopian, depending on whether they describe a pleasant or terrifying society.

Dystopian science fiction tends to be much more common than utopian science fiction. Some have speculated that this might reflect a general sense of pessimism about humanity’s future among science fiction writers, but there may also be a simpler explanation - after all, it is easier to create an interesting story in a setting that contains potential for conflict, and a purely utopian society might therefore be boring to read about.

Many science fiction stories are also written to seem like utopian stories at first, but gradually dark truths about the society are revealed, showing that the world is actually a dystopia after all. On the other hand, a few dystopian stories end with a society that has been transformed and turned in the direction of a utopia through the heroic efforts of the main characters who rebel against the system (though in other cases the dystopian society remains undefeated).

The message of utopian science fiction is often intended to be inspirational. By showing a beautiful image of humanity’s future, the author might try to create feelings of optimism, and inspire readers to work towards realising a better world.

On the contrary, dystopian science fiction stories are often meant as cautionary tales. By reflecting real elements of the readers’ society and taking them to extremes, the authors might attempt to warn readers that the world could be turning in the direction of a dystopia unless major political, social or technological changes are made.

Textual perspective - "A Place in the Sun" by Joanne Harris
 

This short story takes place in a dystopian future where the only social goal is to become as physically attractive as possible, and everybody goes to extreme lengths to achieve this. The society depicted in the story is heavily segregated, ensuring that only the most beautiful people of all are allowed into the most desirable areas.

"A Place in the Sun" clearly serves as a cautionary tale that warns against obsession with physical attraction and criticises the use of plastic surgery.
