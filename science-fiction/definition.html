What is science fiction?

Cuprins

  • Science fiction vs speculative fiction
  • Science fiction vs fantasy
  • Subgenres within science fiction

Science fiction characteristics

Science fiction is a difficult genre to define, because science fiction stories may cover a wide range of topics and touch upon a wide range of themes. It is hard to find complete agreement on the elements necessary to consider a story science fiction, as almost all narrow definitions end up excluding works that belong in the genre. For example, if one tries to define science fiction through its use of a future setting, it would be necessary to exclude the Star Wars franchise, since it is set in the distant past; however, most people would agree that Star Wars is science fiction.

Instead of attempting a narrow definition, it is therefore more useful to make a list of elements that are common in science fiction (but not essential to it). For example, most science fictions stories feature one or more of the following elements:

  • Advanced technology
  • Future setting
  • Utopian or dystopian societies
  • Non-human characters such as aliens or robots
  • Interplanetary or intergalactic travel

Science fiction vs speculative fiction

Sometimes the term speculative fiction is used to classify works of science fiction that are fairly realistic in terms of technological development and touch upon more serious themes. This is partly a result of the science fiction genre’s low reputation historically - because science fiction was associated with popular culture and not considered serious literature, people did not like to classify important works by established authors as science fiction.

For example, George Orwell’s Nineteen Eighty-Four is sometimes labelled ‘speculative fiction’ instead of ‘science fiction’, and Margaret Atwood (author of The Handmaid’s Tale) also defines herself as a writer of speculative fiction rather than science fiction.

Science fiction vs fantasy

In some cases it may be tricky to draw a direct line between science fiction and fantasy, but there are some important differences between them.

First of all, where science fiction typically features a future setting with advanced technology, the typical fantasy setting is often based on a society from the past, such as the medieval era. Examples of this include The Lord of the Rings or Game of Thrones.

Secondly, fantasy settings often contain magic and magical creatures, whereas science fiction tends to focus more on technological inventions and (possibly) aliens. In science fiction there is usually a stronger tendency to attempt to explain unrealistic elements in scientific ways, whereas unexplained supernatural elements are more commonplace in fantasy. Relevant examples include works by Ursula K. Le Guin and Neil Gaiman.

Some genre fiction deliberately combines fantasy and science fiction elements (see ‘science fantasy’ below).

Subgenres within science fiction

Since science fiction is such a diverse literary genre, a large number of sub-genres exist. The ones listed here are simply some of the most well-established ones.

Some science fiction stories may belong in several different subgenres. For example, there is often overlap between climate fiction and post-apocalyptic science fiction.

Climate fiction

A type of science fiction that is specifically focused on the future consequences of climate change. Climate fiction stories are often intended as cautionary tales, by attempting to show readers how terrible the world might become if we do not take the threat of climate change more seriously. An early example of climate fiction is The Drowned World (1962) by J.G. Ballard.

The subgenre has become very popular in the 21st century, as global awareness of the threat of climate change has grown. The genre is sometimes abbreviated to ‘cli-fi’.

Cyberpunk

A subgenre that is often focused on the use of cybernetic technology to augment human beings in various ways. The typical cyberpunk setting involves huge, sprawling cities with many people living at the edges of society. Most cyberpunk stories are dystopian.

One of the most famous cyberpunk novels is Neuromancer (1984) by William Gibson (a novel that also helped Gibson’s invented word ‘cyberspace’ enter mainstream vocabulary). In the world of cinema, Blade Runner (1982) and The Matrix (1999) are both examples of cyberpunk.

Feminist science fiction

A subgenre of science fiction that is especially focused on themes related to gender or gender discrimination. Typically, these works imagine a future in which the power balance between the genders has shifted in either a positive or negative direction.

Famous works of feminist science fiction include the utopian story Herland (1915) by Charlotte Perkins Gilman and the dystopian The Handmaid’s Tale (1985) by Margaret Atwood. Atwood’s novel has recently been adapted into a successful TV show. These works often use imaginary societies to highlight the injustices of gender relations in the author’s own society, and act either as cautionary tales, or suggest alternative social models.

Military science fiction

A subgenre that tends to focus on main characters who are members of a military organisation - typically undergoing military training, fighting wars that span several planets, and using advanced technology in their battles. Famous examples of military science fiction include Starship Troopers (1959) by Robert A. Heinlein and Ender’s Game (1985) by Orson Scott Card. This subgenre commonly overlaps with other subgenres.

Post-apocalyptic science fiction

An apocalypse is some kind of major disaster (nuclear war, alien invasion, climate change, disease, etc...) that wipes out most or all of humanity and destroys civilisation as we know it. Post-apocalyptic science fiction stories typically focus on a group of survivors who are trying to find their place in an almost destroyed world, perhaps still dealing with the threat of radiation, a hostile climate, or alien lifeforms. Examples include Blade Runner (1982), Mad Max (1979) and The Drowned World (1962) by J.G. Ballard.

Textual perspective - "Diary of an Interesting Year" by Helen Simpson
 

This short story takes place in a post-apocalyptic world in which resources have become incredibly scarce due to global warming. The main character is forced to leave her home, and goes through terrible hardships in her struggle for survival.

Science fantasy

As the name suggests, this genre is a blend of science fiction and fantasy. A science fantasy story might take place in a typical science fiction setting with advanced technology and interplanetary travel, but also feature fantasy elements such as magic and mythological creatures. The subgenre was never fully defined, and mostly refers to works published in the 1950s and 60s that used the techniques of science fiction and applied them to fantasy subjects.

Steampunk

A genre that combines science fiction elements with technology and architecture associated with the Industrial Revolution of the 19th century. These stories and films often involve some kind of alternative history of the Earth, where some advanced 20th century inventions were instead created during the age of steam.

Steampunk stories might therefore feature inventions such as steam-powered airships or robots, or even steam-powered space travel. Examples include Philip Reeve’s Mortal Engines (2001-2006), a series of novels set in an imaginary future where steam-powered moving cities fight each other for resources.

Steampunk can also be viewed as a version of science fantasy, as the genre reimagines the past as well as frequently combining different types of technology that could not realistically work together.
