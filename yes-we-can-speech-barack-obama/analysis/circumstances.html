Barack Obama's "Yes We Can" speech
  1. Engleză
  2. Barack Obama's "Yes We Can" speech
  3. Analysis

Circumstances

Cuprins
  • Democratic Party presidential primaries
  • American society

Democratic Party presidential primaries

Barack Obama delivered his “Yes We Can” speech in a gym hall in New Hampshire on 8th of January, 2008 during the primary elections. This famous speech is now known as “Yes We Can”.

The speech was given in the Nashua South High School Gym after Obama lost New Hampshire to his Democrat rival Hillary Clinton by a small margin. In the US, each of the two major parties (Democrats and Republicans) first holds primary elections to select their candidate for the US Presidency.

At this point, Obama was still Senator and his main rival candidate from the Democratic Party was Hillary Clinton. All of these aspects are suggested through a short line in which he congratulates her for winning: “Well, first of all, I want to congratulate Senator Clinton on a hard-fought victory here in New Hampshire” (ll. 5-6).

In the beginning of the electoral campaign, Obama was not considered likely to win. However, by the time of the speech, Obama had gained significantly more popularity among voters, and the polls showed that he was likely to win the primaries. This is why Obama makes the following statement in the speech: “For most of this campaign, we were far behind. We always knew our climb would be steep. But in record numbers, you came out, and you spoke up for change” (ll. 11-13).

American society

In the speech, you will also find many hints about the situation in American society at that time. Political division is suggested through the phrase “the division and distraction that has clouded Washington” (ll. 35-36). At that time, there was a lot of tension between Democrats and Republicans, particularly over the war in Iraq authorized by George W. Bush (who was the US President at the time). This way, Obama gives particular attention to this topic in his speech:

We will bring our troops home. We will finish the job -- we will finish the job against Al Qaida in Afghanistan, […] And we will never use 9/11 as a way to scare up votes, because it is not a tactic to win an election. (ll. 60-64)

Other issues in American society included economic challenges. People were frustrated about those who used “money and influence” (l. 38), by “unaffordable, unavailable health care” (l. 41) and by “the tax breaks for corporations that ship our jobs overseas” (ll. 46-47).

There was also dissatisfaction with the educational system: children often did not have access to good education and teachers were underpaid. Both these aspects are mentioned directly by Obama: “sending our children to schools with corridors of shame” (l. 49).

Concerns over environmental issues, terrorism, and poverty were also dominant themes both in the US and worldwide: “to free this nation from the tyranny of oil and save our planet from a point of no return” (ll. 55-56); “the common threats of the 21st century: terrorism and nuclear weapons, climate change and poverty, genocide and disease” (ll. 65-66).

In this context, Obama positioned himself as a game changer, as the person who would lead a fresh start in politics and society. When this speech was delivered, Obama needed to outline these aspects heavily as the campaign was ongoing, and he needed to mobilize voters in other states as well: “And so, tomorrow, as we take the campaign south and west, as we learn that the struggles of the textile workers in Spartanburg are not so different than the plight of the dishwasher in Las Vegas” (ll. 103-105).

The speech had a very powerful impact, both at the time and afterward. It even inspired a music video in support of Barack Obama. The video features many American celebrities and was produced by will.i.am, lead singer of the band Black Eyed Peas.

As we know today, Obama went to win both the primary elections and presidential elections. He became the 44th US President and served two terms in office.
