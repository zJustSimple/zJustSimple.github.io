Allusions and direct references

Allusions are indirect references to people and events. In his "Yes We Can" speech, Barack Obama combines allusions with direct references to people and events that are relevant to his arguments in favor of hope, change, and unity.

First of all, Obama uses two of his electoral campaign slogans, allusions that remind the audience of his campaign and what he stands for: “I am still fired up and ready to go” (l. 3); “Yes, we can. Yes, we can.” (l. 90). Obama’s message is that he stands for a fresh political change and hope in a better future.

Many of Obama’s allusions are historical. The most relevant example is:

It was the call of workers who organized, women who reached for the ballot, a president who chose the moon as our new frontier, and a king who took us to the mountaintop and pointed the way to the promised land… (ll. 105-107)

In a single sentence, Obama alludes to trade unions, the women’s suffrage movement, John F. Kennedy, and Martin Luther King Jr. Through these allusions, Obama wants to associate himself with pioneers in American history to encourage the audience to trust and vote for him.

The phrase “a creed written into the founding documents” (l. 99) alludes to the Declaration of Independence and the fact that the document guarantees certain rights and liberties. He uses the Declaration of Independence as an example of hope and change in American society, suggesting his own promises of hope and change are derived from founding American principles.

The phrase “from sea to shining sea” (l. 111) is an allusion to the patriotic song “America the Beautiful”. The allusion encourages patriotism and unity and subtly suggests that voting for Obama is a patriotic act.

You can also consider the allusion to current divisions in US politics: “Democrats, independents and Republicans who are tired of the division and distraction that has clouded Washington” (ll. 35-36). Obama builds on this division to present himself as the person who can create unity and change American politics.

Apart from allusions, Obama also uses numerous direct references. For example, he mentions 9/11 and the war in Iraq: “we will end this war in Iraq and bring our troops home.” (ll. 57-58); “And we will never use 9/11 as a way to scare up votes, because it is not a tactic to win an election” (ll. 63-66). Note that the direct reference to 9/11 also includes an allusion to George W. Bush, whom many believed won his second term because he exploited the population’s fear of terrorism after 9/11 in his campaign.

There are a few other allusions and direct references in the speech, so feel free to identify them and add them to your analysis.

Analogy and direct address

In one instance, Obama makes an analogy between different Americans and their hardships: 

…the struggles of the textile workers in Spartanburg are not so different than the plight of the dishwasher in Las Vegas, that the hopes of the little girl who goes to the crumbling school in Dillon are the same as the dreams of the boy who learns on the streets of L.A.,… (ll. 104-107)

The purpose of this analogy is to outline that all Americans share common challenges and experiences despite apparent differences.

Barack Obama frequently addresses his audience directly. Some examples are: “You, all of you who are here tonight, all who put so much heart and soul and work into this campaign, you can be the new majority…”(ll. 32-33); “It is also about what you, the people who love this country, the citizens of the United States of America, can do to change it.” (ll. 80-81).

Obama uses direct address to make the audience feel involved. It is also a way for the speaker to gain their sympathy and votes because he makes them feel that their voices matter.

Descriptive language and metaphors

Obama often uses descriptive words that create visual images for the audience. One such example is: “when men and women in Des Moines and Davenport, in Lebanon and Concord, come out in the snows of January to wait in lines that stretch block after block because they believe in what this country can be” (ll. 17-25). By describing people lining up, Obama presents voting as a challenge but also helps the audience empathize with the voters they imagine waiting in the cold, yet filled with hope. In the video, this description is further emphasized through video footage showing lines of voters standing in the snow.

Obama also describes his historical references vividly and metaphorically, which helps the audience relate to events in the distant past. Here are two such examples: “It was sung by immigrants as they struck out from distant shores and pioneers who pushed westward against an unforgiving wilderness” (ll. 95-96); “...a president who chose the moon as our new frontier, and a king who took us to the mountaintop and pointed the way to the promised land…” (ll. 97-99).

Metaphors help complete the descriptions, and give more literary emphasis to a speech that is supposed to be political. For example, Obama describes the current political scene in the US as “a long political darkness” (ll. 34). Instead of simply saying that oil is harmful for the environment, he uses the metaphorical description: “…free this nation from the tyranny of oil and save our planet from a point of no return.” (ll. 55-56).

In another instance, he mentions the “chorus of cynics” (l. 81) as a metaphor for those who do not support him and his campaign. In this way, he trivializes any arguments they might have against him.

Talking about slavery and its end, the speaker uses a metaphorical description that appeals to emotions: “…slaves and abolitionists as they blazed a trail towards freedom through the darkest of nights” (ll. 101-102).

You can always look for more examples in the speech to expand your analysis.

Repetition

Repetition is one of the most frequently used rhetorical devices in this speech. Its purpose is to make ideas more memorable, as well as to give structure and momentum to Obama’s arguments.

For example, the repetition of the phrase “there is something happening” (l. 17, l. 21, l. 25) creates suspense and builds up to the idea that people in the US want the change that Obama embodies and also that the people are part of that change.

A similar emphasis is constructed when Obama talks about the war in Iraq using repetition: “…we will end this war in Iraq and bring our troops home. (APPLAUSE) We will end this war in Iraq. We will bring our troops home” (ll. 57-60). The purpose of this repetition is to reassure the audience that the speaker is determined to end the war and that it is a priority for him.

One of the key examples of repetition in the text is that of Obama’s slogan “Yes, we can. Yes, we can. Yes, we can.” (ll. 97-98). The slogan is repeated after numerous sentences (ll. 90-112) in an almost hypnotizing manner. By the end of the speech, it becomes a powerful call to action, a symbol of the change Obama represents.

The repetition of “We can stop” (l. 49, l. 51) empowers the audience, making them an active part of the speaker’s proposed reforms.

To encourage his audience to be resilient in the face of challenges, he uses the following anaphora (repeating the same words at the beginning of different sentences): “there is no problem we cannot solve, there is no destiny that we cannot fulfill.” (ll. 39-40).

Note that, in the video, the audience takes up many of Obama’s phrases and repeats them, for example chanting “we want change” (around l. 30) and “yes we can” (around l. 90).
