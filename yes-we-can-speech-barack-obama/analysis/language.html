Language

Cuprins
  • Choice of words
  • Sentence structure
  • Tone

Choice of words

Barack Obama’s “Yes We Can” speech is presented using language that is filled with pathos. The language is typical of a political speech, focused on appealing to feelings and to ideas, rather than on giving details about how Obama will implement his platform if he is elected president.

The choice of words reveals that Obama chooses to focus on positive aspects, despite being defeated that day. Most of the positive words and phrases he uses target others, such as the audience and his political opponent: “outstanding job” (l. 6), “young in age and in spirit” (l. 22), “a fundamentally new direction” (l. 29), “great teachers”, etc.

When talking about his experience, Obama uses phrases that suggest he and the American people need to overcome a series of challenges: “improbable journey” (l. 78), “impossible odds” (l. 95) “a long political darkness” (l. 34), “outrage of unaffordable, unavailable health care” (l. 41), (l.51), “grow louder and more dissonant” (l. 90), “false hope” (l. 92) “the darkest of nights” (l. 102), etc.

Use of pronouns

Obama’s use of pronouns in this speech is particularly notable. The speaker constantly includes his audience by using the first-person plural (“our”, “we”) even when speaking about his own election results: “Our new American majority can end the outrage of unaffordable, unavailable health care in our time” (ll. 30-31); “We will end this war in Iraq. We will bring our troops home” (l. 60). This makes the audience feel like they are sharing both his achievements and challenges.

However, Obama also uses the second-person address (“you”): “you came out, and you spoke up for change.” (ll. 12-13); “It is also about what you, the people who love this country, the citizens of the United States of America, can do to change it” (ll. 80-81). In this way, the speaker passes on the responsibility of moving America in a new direction to voters. His message is that, as they will share the results of his victory, they should also share his challenges.

Occasionally, Obama refers to himself in the first-person (“I”): “Thank you, New Hampshire. I love you back. Thank you. Thank you. Well, thank you so much. I am still fired up and ready to go” (ll. 1-3); “And when I am president of the United States, we will end this war in Iraq and bring our troops home” (ll. 57-58). These instances help add more confidence to the speech and give Obama a certain authority, often highlighting his conviction that he will win the elections.

Sentence structure

The speech is constructed using sentences of varying lengths. Sometimes medium-length sentences are followed by a short sentence which gives emphasis to the ideas expressed before: “You know, a few weeks ago, no one imagined that we'd have accomplished what we did here tonight in New Hampshire. No one could have imagined it.” (ll. 9-10).

Other times, short sentences give rhythm to the speech and summarize an idea: “There is something happening.” (l. 21); “We will end this war in Iraq. We will bring our troops home.” (l. 60).

Long sentences tend to create powerful imagery and add details to the ideas presented by the speaker: “There's something happening when Americans who are young in age and in spirit, who've never participated in politics before, turn out in numbers we have never seen because they know in their hearts that this time must be different” (ll. 21-24). Obama builds suspense throughout this long sentence, helping the audience to imagine people lining up to vote and appealing to feelings of hope.

Tone

Although Barack Obama gave his speech after being defeated in New Hampshire, the overall tone of the speech is victorious and hopeful. This is suggested from the first paragraphs: “Thank you, New Hampshire. I love you back. Thank you. Thank you. Well, thank you so much. I am still fired up and ready to go.” (ll. 1-3); “You know, a few weeks ago, no one imagined that we'd have accomplished what we did here tonight in New Hampshire” (ll. 9-10). These two examples show that Obama feels like a victor and manages to remind the audience that he lost with a very small margin. His message is that his support among voters is increasing.

Sometimes, Obama takes on a more concerned and critical tone: “And we will never use 9/11 as a way to scare up votes, because it is not a tactic to win an election.” (ll. 63-64); “We have been told we cannot do this by a chorus of cynics” (l. 81). The first quote targets George W. Bush’s unpopularity (Bush was the US President when the 9/11 terrorist attacks on US soil happened). The second targets those who don’t approve of Obama’s candidacy and political platform. By criticizing these people, the speaker wants to encourage readers to vote for him.

Other instances in which the speaker is critical target industries that damage the environment and the healthcare system: “…free this nation from the tyranny of oil and save our planet from a point of no return” (ll. 55-56); “…and we can tell the drug and insurance industry that, while they get a seat at the table, they don't get to buy every chair, not this time, not now” (ll. 43-44). By rejecting aspects of American society and economy that many voters dislike, Obama attempts to demonstrate that he shares their views and encourage them to support his reforms.

Finally, throughout most of the speech, Obama’s tone is hopeful and resilient, thereby encouraging the audience to feel the same. Here are just a couple of relevant examples from the speech: “That's what's happening in America right now; change is what's happening in America.” (ll. 30-31); “Yes, we can, to opportunity and prosperity. Yes, we can heal this nation. Yes, we can repair this world. Yes, we can.” (ll. 101-102).

Consider also his tone and actions in the video. For example, sometimes Obama adds emphasis to his tone by leaning into the microphone: “That's what's happening in America right now; change is what's happening in America.” (ll. 30-31). Other times, he stresses each word in a sentence: “And, together, we will begin the next great chapter in the American story, with three words that will ring from coast to coast, from sea to shining sea: Yes, we can” (ll. 110-112). This suggests that Obama uses a convincing tone, trying to make voters believe in his vision and to show up and vote for him at the next primaries.
