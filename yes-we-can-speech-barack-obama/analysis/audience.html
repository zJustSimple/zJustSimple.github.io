Target audience

Barack Obama’s “Yes We Can” speech was delivered in front of a live audience and televised.

The live audience included many of Obama’s voters and supporters. If you watch the video of the speech, you will notice that the audience is constantly cheering Obama and that they wear campaign caps and carry placards with supportive slogans. You can also see that they are a diverse crowd and often interrupt the speaker with applause, sometimes chanting his name or his slogan “yes we can”, suggesting they support his candidacy.

In the speech, Obama often addresses his live audience directly: “Thank you, New Hampshire. I love you back.” (l. 1); “You, all of you who are here tonight” (l. 32). This shows that he wants to keep them engaged with his speech and give his voters the credit they deserve.

Given that the speech was televised and shared online, this suggests that the target audience is the whole of America and even the rest of the English-speaking world. Obama uses direct address to target a wider American audience as well as the people present in New Hampshire. Here are some relevant examples that show Obama targets all Americans with his speech: “your voices and your votes” (l. 14); “Americans who are young in age and in spirit” (l. 22).

The speaker also addresses his counterpart Hillary Clinton, who won the elections that day, as customary in the tradition of American elections: “…I want to congratulate Senator Clinton on a hard-fought victory here in New Hampshire.” (ll. 5-6).

Obama also targets the American citizens in general directly: "That's why tonight belongs to you" (l. 75). However, he only refers to different categories of Americans citizens without targeting them directly.

Potential audience

The first part of the speech is focused on different Americans as voters: “men and women in Des Moines and Davenport, in Lebanon and Concord, come out in the snows of January to wait in lines” (ll. 17-19). This is because the speaker aims to attract further votes in the electoral campaign, even though he lost New Hampshire.

The speech is filled with references to different categories of Americans, all of whom are potential voters: “…we are rich or poor, black or white, Latino or Asian, whether we hail from Iowa or New Hampshire, Nevada or South Carolina” (ll. 27-28). Obama’s purpose is to appeal to as many groups of voters as possible and encourage them to vote for him. He, therefore, targets a very varied audience. He also wants to unite voters concerned with the health system and mentions “doctors and patients, workers and businesses, Democrats and Republicans” (l. 42). An important part of Obama’s platform was giving Americans affordable health care, and he consequently hopes to appeal to voters who are dissatisfied with the American health care system.

The speaker goes as far as even trying to win votes from the other parties as he mentions “Democrats, independents and Republicans who are tired of the division” (l. 35).

Other categories of Americans mentioned in the speech are the “middle-class” (l. 47), “working Americans” (l. 47), “children” (l. 49), “teachers” (l. 51), “farmers and scientists, citizens and entrepreneurs” (ll. 54-55), “the people who love this country, the citizens of the United States of America” (ll. 80-81), and “organizers, and the volunteers, and the staff” (ll. 83-84). In these examples, Obama attempts to address his message widely and to thank both ordinary people and his closest supporters for their help.

There are also segments of the audience that Obama mentions with the aim of criticizing. These include: “the drug and insurance industry” (l. 43); “corporations that ship our jobs overseas” (ll. 46-47) and the “chorus of cynics” (l. 89) which refer to all those who do not support Obama’s candidacy.

Obama also praises all the candidates in the race: “All of the candidates in this race share these goals. All of the candidates in this race have good ideas and all are patriots who serve this country honorably.” (ll. 67-68).

The speaker also mentions the war with Iraq, along with soldiers and veterans:

We will end this war in Iraq. We will bring our troops home. We will finish the job -- we will finish the job against Al Qaida in Afghanistan. We will care for our veterans. We will restore our moral standing in the world. (ll. 60-62)

This indicates that Obama also targets Americans overseas as well as an international audience.

The historical references to “slaves and abolitionists” (l. 93), “immigrants” (l. 95), or “workers who organized, women who reached for the ballot” (l. 97) suggest that Obama targets people that identify with this past. These people might include African-Americans, trade unionists, immigrants and women.

A similar association is made when Obama give examples of hardships faced by many Americans:

…workers in Spartanburg are not so different than the plight of the dishwasher in Las Vegas, that the hopes of the little girl who goes to the crumbling school in Dillon are the same as the dreams of the boy who learns on the streets of L.A…. (ll. 104-107)

In this way, he targets potential voters who might identify with the situations he describes.

Overall, Barack Obama’s speech is very inclusive speech, targeting most categories of American voters. Obama emphasizes the role of his audience in the quest for change, and he, therefore, implies that it is the audience’s responsibility to vote for him to help make change happen.
