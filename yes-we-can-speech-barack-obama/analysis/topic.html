Political change

Barack Obama’s “New Hampshire Primary Speech”, also known as his “Yes We Can” speech, is a political speech delivered during the primary elections of 2008, when the speaker was competing against other Democrats to win the nomination of the Democratic Party for the US Presidency.

As a presidential candidate, Obama links his potential election with the idea of change in American society, particularly since he is African-American. This is suggested by the fact that the speaker admits he is the ‘underdog’ and many people didn’t believe he had a chance of winning: “For most of this campaign, we were far behind. We always knew our climb would be steep. But in record numbers, you came out, and you spoke up for change.” (ll. 11-13).

The idea of change is further explored in connection with the fact that more people have decided to vote in these elections than in previous years, and through their vote, they show the political class that they want a change:

There's something happening when Americans who are young in age and in spirit, who've never participated in politics before, turn out in numbers we have never seen because they know in their hearts that this time must be different. (ll. 21-23)

Obama emphasizes this idea through his repetition of the phrase “there’s something happening”. To further promote political change, Obama talks about taking “this country in a fundamentally new direction” (ll. 28-29).

The speaker does not only talk about change in general abstract terms. He also explores the topic by listing specific issues in the US society that need to change:

“Our new American majority can end the outrage of unaffordable, unavailable health care in our time.” (ll. 30-31);

“…end the tax breaks for corporations that ship our jobs overseas…” (ll. 46-47);

“…stop talking about how great teachers are and start rewarding them…” (l. 51);

“We will end this war in Iraq. We will bring our troops home.” (l. 60)

He involves the audience with the topic by making them an active part of change: “It is also about what you, the people who love this country, the citizens of the United States of America, can do to change it.” (ll. 72-73); “And, together, we will begin the next great chapter in the American story, with three words that will ring from coast to coast, from sea to shining sea: Yes, we can.” (ll. 110-111).

Through the repetition of his election slogan “yes we can”, Obama instills in his audience the conviction that change is possible and that it will come into being through the joint efforts of the whole nation.

Finally, to support the idea that the US can change, he gives historical examples of labor unions, women rights activists, and civil rights activists who changed American society:

It was the call of workers who organized, women who reached for the ballot, a president who chose the moon as our new frontier, and a king who took us to the mountaintop and pointed the way to the promised land: Yes, we can, to justice and equality. (ll. 97-100)

Other examples of changes in American society he uses are the American Revolution, the end of slavery, and the arrival of immigrants.

Hope and resilience

The theme of hope and resilience is also explored through voters’ attitudes and historical examples. For instance, Obama argues that people came out to vote in large numbers because they have hope that the country can change for the better: “…wait in lines that stretch block after block because they believe in what this country can be.” (ll. 19-20); “There's something happening when people vote not just for party that they belong to, but the hopes that they hold in common.” (ll. 25-26).

At the same time, Obama criticizes those who do not believe in the American hope: “We've been warned against offering the people of this nation false hope. But in the unlikely story that is America, there has never been anything false about hope.” (ll. 83-85).

To prove these critics wrong, he lists historical examples that show that hope is a natural American value that has attracted people to America for generations: “It was sung by immigrants as they struck out from distant shores and pioneers who pushed westward against an unforgiving wilderness: Yes, we can.” (ll. 95-96).

The other examples continue to show that change in American society was triggered by hope and resilience. Here, Obama refers to American revolutionaries, slaves, African Americans during the civil rights movement, women who fought for the right to vote, workers who formed unions, etc.

Argumentation

Barack Obama’s "Yes We Can" speech is constructed using both direct and hidden argumentation. When the speaker argues for his views directly, he states them explicitly, often giving rational arguments for them such as:

And we will never use 9/11 as a way to scare up votes, because it is not a tactic to win an election. It is a challenge that should unite America and the world against the common threats of the 21st century… (ll. 63-65)

Another example of direct argumentation is when Obama openly claims his success is due to his voters and supporters:

But the reason our campaign has always been different, the reason we began this improbable journey almost a year ago is because it's not just about what I will do as president. It is also about what you… (ll. 70-72)

He also mentions his critics directly and then refutes their arguments with historical examples: “We have been told we cannot do this by a chorus of cynics. And they will only grow louder and more dissonant in the weeks and months to come.” (ll. 81-82); “We've been warned against offering the people of this nation false hope. But in the unlikely story that is America, there has never been anything false about hope.” (ll. 83-85).

Hidden argumentation means the speaker uses the power of examples and facts to suggest his views indirectly. For instance, instead of saying directly that most polls indicated that he had a good chance of winning the primaries, he talks about the people lining up to vote: “come out in the snows of January to wait in lines that stretch block after block because they believe in what this country can be.” (ll. 18-20); “…turn out in numbers we have never seen because they know in their hearts that this time must be different.” (ll. 23-24).

In another instance, he indirectly argues for social unity by making an analogy between Americans from different places:

 …the struggles of the textile workers in Spartanburg are not so different than the plight of the dishwasher in Las Vegas, that the hopes of the little girl who goes to the crumbling school in Dillon are the same as the dreams of the boy who learns on the streets of L.A. (ll. 104-107)
