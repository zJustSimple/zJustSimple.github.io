Threats to privacy

Cuprins
  • Exposure of personal information
  • Social media data
  • Third parties
  • The Facebook-Cambridge Analytica scandal

Exposure of personal information

The greatest risk of social media is to one’s privacy. The use of social media raises the issue of information management such as personally identifiable information, security of government data, and transparency.

The initial risk is related to exposure of personal data on the Internet. Social media profiles often contain personal data about the users, including full name, place of birth, date of birth, and contact information. This can make a user more likely to become the target of identity fraud because others may be able to reconstruct the user’s key information such as their social security number by using the information found in their profile.

Cyberstalking is also a problem that can arise from making personal information public. For example, the frequent disclosure of private activities on social media, such as status updates, location data, pictures, and adding new friends, provide stalkers with material that helps them survey their targets.

Exposure of personal information can also have negative effects on the personal and professional life of social media users, especially young users. Websites are often considered an ongoing collective record of users’ activity. Therefore, even if a comment, picture, or video posted on a social media website is later deleted, the data is often retained by the site and it could be potentially recovered. Furthermore, once made public, the user has no control over the way other users interact with the material they have posted. Therefore, copies of a comment or photo can be created and distributed on other websites. If the content is inappropriate, the post may jeopardise the user’s future education or job opportunities.

As a result, many people advocate for “the right to be forgotten”, a concept that encourages, among other things, the removal of compromising material about one’s past from the Internet. The concept has been partly adopted in data protection regulations (especially in the European Union) that require data controllers such as search engines to remove data that is considered inadequate, irrelevant, or no longer relevant.

The exposure of personal information also happens when a person posts pictures of other people without their permission. This is especially interesting in the case of parents who post pictures of their children on social media. Some argue that the parents have the right to post pictures without the child’s permission, while others argue that the child should be in control of how their information is being used. Some children who had pictures of them posted on social media by a parent voiced concern over the fact that facial recognition algorithms might trace the pictures to their own account or that their parent’s profile was public and therefore meant that the picture could potentially be viewed by the entire world.

Social media data

When Internet users visit various websites, they normally leave behind evidence of which sites they have visited, unless they use advanced means to avoid it. This can be used to identify users as a target audience for advertisements or to turn them into victims of online scams such as phishing.

Similarly, social media platforms also collect data about their users’ activities. Social media data tracks how users engage with content, gathering information about their shares, likes, conversations, comments, etc. This type of information is usually gathered by platforms to form statistics that can be used to assess the performance of social media platforms and to create new business strategies. By analysing which type of content generates most clicks, likes, comments, or shares, and by analysing the demographic factors (age, gender, education, etc.) of the people who prefer this type of content, social media platforms can predict their users’ behaviour and, therefore, offer content that is more relevant to their interests.

Third parties

Social media sites have agreements with thousands of third-party organisations, such as those who publish games and surveys or the companies that purchase rights to advertise on these platforms. Most third-party applications ask permission to gain access to a user’s profile and contacts and claim to use this information to provide a better experience for the user. This way, third-party organisations are able to gather information about millions of users, and their use of this information can at times be dubious.

By using recommendation and personalisation algorithms, sites can make more relevant suggestions to users. Social media platforms also use this approach to assist users with ﬁnding preferred content and viewing more relevant advertisements. However, once the system registers the user’s preferences, the user has little choice over the recommendations he or she receives.

The Facebook-Cambridge Analytica scandal

The way third-party organisations handle personal data has stirred many concerns about privacy. It also sparked direct controversy, as in the case of the Facebook-Cambridge Analytica scandal.

In 2018, a scandal relating to social media data surfaced regarding the relationship between Facebook and Cambridge Analytica. Cambridge Analytica was a consulting firm which analysed data for political purposes. It has been linked with aiding politicians such as Ted Cruz and Donald Trump in their campaigns. The scandal began with the revelation that Cambridge Analytica had gathered the personal data of millions of Facebook users without their consent and used it for political purposes.

Cambridge Analytica acquired the information from an external research organisation that, through a survey application hosted on Facebook, was able to collect the personal information of not only the people who agreed to take the survey but also of all the people in those users' Friends List. Although the application was designed to gather data for academic purposes, the users’ information was sold to Cambridge Analytica, who obtained data about 50 to 90 million Facebook users.

The data contained information about users’ likes, birthday, current city, News Feed, timeline, and messages. Cambridge Analytica created statistics based on this data and used them to advertise political events and to persuade users to participate to them.

Newspapers discovered this breach of privacy from a former employee at Cambridge Analytica and published the story, stirring international outrage. Mark Zuckerberg, the creator of Facebook, claimed that Cambridge Analytica had lied to Facebook about the data they gathered and kept. As a result of the scandal, Mark Zuckerberg had to testify in front of the United States Congress where he publicly apologised for the incident and agreed to change Facebook’s data protection policies, aiming to give individuals more control over their personal data.
