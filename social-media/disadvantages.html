Addiction and alienation

As social media becomes an increasingly global phenomenon, it gets more ingrained into daily life. This can have serious negative effects on a social, physical, or psychological level.

Social media features a wide variety of activities towards which people might develop addiction by frequently engaging in them and devoting a considerable amount of time for these activities at the expense of other aspects of life. A typical American uses three social media platforms. In 2018, 74% of adult Facebook users in the United States said they visit the site daily, with around 51% of them saying they do so several times a day. Furthermore, 40% of all social media users in the United States said they would find it hard to give up social media, with 14% of them stating that it would be very hard for them to do this.

Once addicted to social media, users tend to spend more and more time browsing and socialising on social network sites, and often feel unable to stop. They also tend to feel unpleasant feelings when offline. This can affect the user’s productivity and focus in school or the workplace, which in turn can lead to negative consequences. Furthermore, some social media addicts have developed insomnia and exhibit symptoms of anxiety. However, social networking addiction is not officially recognised as a medical disorder.

Analysis indicates that highly outgoing people are more inclined to develop addictive tendencies and to spend time using social networking sites. This could be explained by the fact that using social media satisfies their need to socialise. However, social media addiction also causes alienation. Addicted users tend to have more conflicts with their friends and family while offline and stop engaging in other activities. This behaviour can affect their offline interpersonal relationships and lead to feelings of loneliness. Parents who use social media too frequently are also at risk of becoming disengaged in their parenting. While they may be physically present in their children’s lives, parents addicted to social media may be less emotionally engaged.

Addiction to social media can also influence other disorders. By presenting depression as a romantic and melancholic state or by promoting images of people with eating disorders, social media can influence vulnerable users to commit self-harm.

Textual perspective 1 - "iAnna" by Will Self
 

The short story follows the way two psychiatrists try to solve the case of a schizophrenic woman (Anna Richardson) who believes the world is an iPad. Anna’s case illustrates how digital technology can lead to extreme cases of alienation, as she is no longer able to perceive people as flesh and blood beings but as their digital versions. Also, the story suggest that we are all alienated by new technologies until they become something common.

Textual perspective 2 - "Why we need to slow down our lives" by Pico Iyer
 

In this essay, Pico Iyer says that people have lost their appreciation of stillness and meditation in a world of constant digital entertainment. He argues that we need to get better at stepping back from social media and other digital entertainment to help us find peace of mind in our stressful lives.

"How Not to Be Alone" by Jonathan Safran Foer
 

In this essay, Foer argues that developments in digital communication technology have taken a heavy toll on people's ability to truly communicate with each other, which means that modern society is becoming increasingly alienating. Foer does not recommend giving up social media completely, but argues that it is important to use it in a balanced way.

Cyberbullying and low self-esteem

Some researchers have speculated that young people with narcissistic tendencies are especially prone to engaging with social media in an addictive way. This is because the online environment empowers them to construct their ideal selves and to present themselves favourably to the world. Also, this exposure presents them with instant gratification as their content gains likes and favourable comments.  

Also, low self-esteem may drive people to use social media as an escape from their real identity or to make them feel better about themselves. For example, playing games on social media and winning may make people experience an increase in confidence. On the other hand, social media often put pressure people to present the best version of themselves, and for a person with low self-esteem, this may cause feelings of inadequacy or anxiety.

However, low self-esteem can also be cultivated through the use of social media, especially by encountering cyberbullying. Young people, especially high school students, are the most common victims but also the most common perpetrators of cyberbullying. This practice can have profoundly negative impacts on a person’s self-esteem and psyche, as they are faced with humiliation and threats. Negative psychological outcomes of cyberbullying include depression, anxiety, severe isolation, and in extreme cases it can lead to suicide.

Textual perspective - "Online Harassment and Cyber Mobs" by Anita Sarkeesian
 

Sarkeesian’s speech highlights the idea that online harassment of women can lead to real-life effects. Sarkeesian presents the way in which the “cyber bullies” on social media transformed into a “cyber mob” and the way in which her privacy and personal life were put at risk.

Fake news, propaganda, and inappropriate content

One of the main functions of social media is to gain and share information and knowledge. In 2016, 62% of the adults in the United States stated that they used social media platforms to receive news, compared to 2012 when only 49% looked at news on social media. However, much of the content available on social media is user-generated and users can manipulate information and create untruthful or misleading material. Therefore, social media is not always a reliable source, as it often hosts and facilitates the spread of fake news.  This occurrence is particularly noticeable during political events when various propaganda groups spread fake news in an attempt to alter the public opinion and the voter’s choice. For example, Russia was accused of hiring over 1000 agents during the United States presidential elections of 2016 to create and spread fake news about Donald Trump’s opponent, Hillary Clinton.  

Social media platforms are not directly regulated by law and the sites are responsible for moderating the content they host. However, this practice can prove faulty as some social media platforms have inconsistent guidelines, or their policies are too general to prevent the spread of inappropriate content. For example, some videos aimed at children have been reported to feature bizarre themes or to have dark messages. Without parental guidance, children who end up watching these videos may be negatively affected by them. For this reason, social media platforms have been urged to take more responsibility for the content they host, especially since it can be easily accessed by children.

Propaganda and hate speech are also often easily spread on social media. As social media allows connectivity between like-minded people, it facilitates the creation of communities that share the same set of beliefs or identities. While this function may have many positive aspects, it can also be used to form harmful groups with extremist views. These groups act like an echo chamber where people can share and receive a type of information that they would otherwise not be allowed to share with an offline community. This enables them to spread propaganda and hate speech, especially since they can hide behind an anonymous profile. Social media algorithms also facilitate the creation of echo chambers by showing users posts that appeal to the user’s interests and political views. This sometimes makes users unaware that they have access only to limited information that does not reflect a full overview and reinforces their own beliefs.

Influencers and targeted advertising

The rise of social media also gave rise to influencers. These people are usually celebrities with a large number of followers on various social media platforms. Influencers are so named because they are often able to influence the opinion and behaviour of their followers with their recommendations. For this reason, influencers are often employed by brands to advertise their products or services. Influencers will, therefore, advertise the products on their social media profiles, perhaps even if they themselves have not tried the product or do not personally like it. Many followers who look up to these influencers and trust their recommendations will likely become interested in the product. However, influencers themselves are not necessarily a negative aspect of social media, as many influencers use their large follower base to promote social and political involvement and raise awareness about current issues.

Many social media sites display advertisements that are customised based on a user’s browsing behaviour or on demographic factors such as age, gender, education, marital status, etc. These advertisements influence the buying tendencies of social media users. Therefore, they represent a risk, especially to children, because as they appeal to a user’s specific interest, they may cause shopping or gambling addictions. Furthermore, advertisements can normalise impulsive purchasing decisions.
