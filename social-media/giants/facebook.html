Launch and development

Facebook was launched in early 2004, and it was founded by Mark Zuckerberg and others while they were students at Harvard University. Initially, the site was only designed to support college networks and was intended to be a way to keep in touch with university peers after graduation. When it was initially launched, Facebook was restricted to Harvard students and users were required to have a Harvard email address to join the site. In 2005, the network was extended to include other colleges and high schools, but the users were still required to have email addresses connected to institutions to join. This requirement kept the site relatively closed and created the perception that the site offered a private community.

The network was later extended to include corporate networks and, after 2006, everyone above the age of 13 could sign up to Facebook. The access of new users to closed networks (like corporate or high school networks) was still rather limited, and users were unable to make their full profiles public to all. Nonetheless, the site quickly gained popularity. In 2008, it became the most visited site in the United States, surpassing MySpace, which was until then the most popular social networking site. In 2009, Facebook was ranked as the most-used social network worldwide. In 2018, 68% of the adult population of the United States used Facebook, and approximately three-quarters of those users reported accessing Facebook on a daily basis. Worldwide, the social media platform had over 2.2 billion active users in 2018.

Main features and services

Facebook is a social network form of social media, because it focuses on enabling communication between users. Reports note that users use Facebook mainly to maintain existing relationships rather than to meet new people. This is in contrast to Twitter, for example, where users often follow celebrities, politicians, and people they admire, as well as or instead of their real-life acquaintances.

Registered users can create profiles where they can list their interests, preferences, and contact and personal information. Users can communicate with both people added to their Friends List and with those outside of it through chat and video functions. They can also create and join groups and events, and create pages and fundraisers, etc.

Facebook’s main features include the News Feed, used to check for updates from people and pages followed by the user, and the Wall (or Timeline) where users can post photos, videos, information about their whereabouts, etc. Users can change their status to announce changes in their relationship, work place, studies, etc, and they can tag other users in comments or photos. They can like or react to posts, comments, and messages, and can share links and photos. The recently added Story feature lets users upload temporary photos or videos that become unavailable to view after a certain amount of time.

Features such as People You May Know help users find friends by suggesting other people the user may be interested in adding to their Friends List. The suggestions are based on algorithms that take into account the number of shared friends between users, shared groups, whether or not the users were tagged in the same photos, etc.

Facebook also hosts advertisements and games. Users can create and view advertisements, browse through advertised jobs, or join buy-and-sell groups. Companies purchase rights to place international adverts on Facebook and collect data about the users to provide them with information that is relevant to their interests. For this, Facebook employs recommendation and personalisation algorithms used to make relevant product suggestions. Similarly, companies also purchase rights to publish games on the social platform.

Controversy

Facebook has recently come under criticism for invading or compromising users’ privacy through data leaks. For example, the social platform has reported security flaws and bugs in its password management system several times, therefore exposing the passwords of millions of users. Facebook has also been the target of numerous cyberattacks that aimed at obtaining personal data from its users. The Cambridge Analytica scandal also brought many issues to light concerning the way Facebook handles the private information of its users. The inadequate safety regulations of Facebook allowed Cambridge Analytica to harvest data from millions of accounts. You can read more about the scandal in the “Threats to privacy” section of this analysis.

Facebook’s News Feed feature has also been critiqued for enabling the spread of fake news, as fake news stories generally see higher sharing rates than legitimate news. This is because fake news articles usually have eye-catching headlines and gripping or sensationalist content. According to analysis, 30% of all fake news traffic can be linked back to Facebook, as opposed to only 8% of legitimate news traffic. Facebook users play a major role in spreading fake news stories by turning them viral. The ability for users to spread fake news stories on Facebook  – either deliberately or accidentally – came under heavy criticism during both the 2016 US presidential election campaign and the Brexit campaign in the UK. In both instances, fake news spread on social media platforms such as Facebook has been linked to influencing the unexpected outcomes of these political events.

After the multiple data-sharing and fake news scandals, Mark Zuckerberg, the creator of Facebook, had to testify in front of the United States Congress. Zuckerberg answered questions addressed by Congress membersrelating to privacy regulations and Cambridge Analytica. He admitted that Facebook made a mistake by not taking action against Cambridge Analytica sooner, and he apologised. He also assured that while Facebook does store users’ data, it does not sell the data to advertisers. Zuckerberg also addressed Russia’s interference in spreading fake news with the purpose of altering election results, saying that Facebook is in an ongoing process to identify this type of interference.
