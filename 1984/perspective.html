Historical perspective

Nineteen Eighty-Four was published in 1949, and George Orwell often refers to major events that took place in the first half of the 20th century, particularly in relation to the totalitarian states that emerged at the time.

For example, the novel contains multiple references to Soviet Russia (which was still a major world power in 1949) and its version of Communism. We learn that the Party was originally founded on something resembling Socialist principles, and Oceania clearly has some form of planned economy in place, as the government has extremely tight control of the means of production and the flow of goods through the Ministry of Plenty (p. 215).

The reference to frequent “Three-Year Plans” (e.g. p. 4) is also an obvious parallel to Josef Stalin’s Five-Year Plans for Soviet Russia’s economy. There are also parallels between the rebel leader Emmanuel Goldstein and Leon Trotsky, who was a central figure in the early years of the Soviet Union. Just as Trotsky used to be a prominent member of the Communist party before he was expelled and exiled by Josef Stalin, Goldstein is said to have been one of the Party’s founding members, before he betrayed its principles and was cast out.

Finally, the novel mentions that the Party often carries out “[...] great purges of traitors and thought-criminals” (p. 47), which is a reference to Stalin’s Great Purge in 1936-1938, during which hundreds of thousands of people were executed for political reasons. The many references to the Party’s methods of torturing ‘traitors’ until they are willing to make all kinds of false confessions (e.g. p. 254) also mirrors the methods that were systematically employed by Stalin’s secret police.  

Written just a few years after the end of World War II, Orwell also uses the novel as a direct criticism of Nazi Germany.

For example, Goldstein, with his Jewish appearance and name, can also be seen as a reference to the Nazi regime’s strategy of using Jews as scapegoats for all the problems in society, and as symbols of everything evil and bad. The early, detailed description of the Two Minutes Hate forms a particularly clear parallel to the anti-Jewish propaganda spread by Nazi Germany (pp. 13-17), as it is noted that the audience is guided to direct their hatred towards Goldstein’s facial features in addition to his politics (just as the Nazi education system trained people to recognise and despise ‘characteristically Jewish’ faces). Also note that the refugee woman who is violently killed in one of the Party’s propaganda movies looks like she “might have been a jewess” (p. 10).

The specific focus on educating Oceania’s children to become single-mindedly loyal Party members through organisations such as the Youth League and the Junior Spies (p. 23) also mirrors the practice of Nazi organisations like the Hitlerjugend. The idea of using children as spies and informers against adults was realised in Nazi Germany, as children were encouraged to speak to authorities if their teachers or parents did not seem properly devoted to Nazi ideals.  

Big Brother’s physical appearance also makes him sound somewhat similar to both Adolf Hitler and Josef Stalin, particularly with the reference to his ‘black moustache’ (p. 3).

It is interesting to note that both Soviet Russia and Nazi Germany are also directly established as historical regimes within the book’s setting, as Goldstein’s book contains several references to them as earlier examples of totalitarian states (e.g p. 213). It is also implied that Eurasia - one of the setting’s three superstates - partially grew out of the Soviet Union and was initially founded on the political principles associated with Bolshevism (a particular form of Communism) (p. 211).

Orwell’s novel is partly intended as the imagined result of Russia, America and China (three of the biggest world powers in 1949) engaging in nuclear warfare and taking over their nearest neighbours before declaring a sort of stalemate and imposing similar totalitarian regimes over their citizens. This is indicated particularly through reference to “the atomic war of the nineteen-fifties” (p. 197). Nuclear war presented a very real threat in the late 1940s, when Orwell was writing. Although for several years only the US had the knowledge to make a nuclear bomb, but the Soviet Union succeeded in detonating one in August 1949, two months after the publication of Nineteen Eighty-Four.

Current issues

Within the past decade, several major events and developments have invited comparisons to Nineteen Eighty-Four - and often caused its popularity to go up at the same time.

Real-world surveillance

One of these events was the NSA scandal in 2013, in which whistleblowerEdward Snowden revealed that an American intelligence organization collected information about people to an extensive and even illegal extent, supposedly in its efforts to combat terrorism. Many commenters compared this situation to the situation Orwell described in Nineteen Eighty-Four, because it pointed to a society dominated by government surveillance.

Comparisons to the novel have also been made in connection to other, more recent cases of online surveillance, such as revelations about the way social media giants like Facebook handle private user data.  

As an interesting contrast to Orwell’s novel, it is notable that real-world surveillance is often enabled by populations who voluntarily hand over a large amount of data about themselves to - for example - social media sites, which may then be leaked to other corporations or even governments, and eventually (mis)used for various purposes. The recent Cambridge Analytica scandal revealed how widespread this problem is - and how dramatic its effects on society and political events can be.

Donald Trump’s presidency

The novel also gained new popularity in the first days of Donald Trump’s presidency. Though many had already made comparisons between Trump’s rhetoric during his presidential campaign and the Newspeak used in Nineteen Eighty-Four, the comparison gained new life following a 2017 interview with Kellyanne Conway, who was Donald Trump’s campaign manager at the time.

In this interview, Conway used the Orwellian phrase “alternative facts” to defend Trump’s press secretary Sean Spicer, who had been caught in outright lies about the size of the crowd during Trump’s inauguration. Many compared her statement to the Party’s rhetoric in Nineteen Eighty-Four - and in the days after the interview, sales of Orwell’s novel went up by more than 8000 percent.

Trump’s presidency has also invited more general comparisons to Nineteen Eighty-Four. For instance, the Trump administration’s ongoing tendency to deny basic facts mirrors the Party’s systematic manipulation of the truth, while Trump’s frequent use of hyperbole (overstatement) in political speeches and tweets is mirrored by the language used in the Party’s propaganda messages: “Vast strategic manoeuvre - perfect coordination [...] … greatest victory in human history - victory, victory, victory!” (p. 310).

Trump’s use of the term ‘fake news’ to describe any media stories that paint him and his administration in a negative light is also a propaganda technique meant to distort people’s understanding of the truth. By systematically undermining people’s trust in the media (even calling mainstream media the “enemy of the people”), it becomes easier for Trump to make people accept his own, manipulated version of the truth.

The strategy of dismissing any reports that disagree with the leader’s narrative as fake is often used by the Party in Nineteen Eighty-Four as well. The main difference between the novel and reality is that the Party also happens to be in full control of all the media in society, so it is much harder for opposing voices to be heard – whereas the media’s negative reports are a source of considerable frustration for Trump, because he is not in control of them.
