Setting

A note on genre

Because Nineteen Eighty-Four is a story in the science-fiction genre, it is even more important than usual to pay close attention the book’s setting. Science-fiction stories typically depict some kind of future scenario - often featuring a future version of our own world, but sometimes taking place in entirely fictional locations.

Important questions to ask when looking at science-fiction settings are typically related to the political, social and economic situation of future society, as well as its level of technological advancement.

Utopia vs dystopia

When analysing the of science-fiction stories, it is first of all useful to know the terms utopia and dystopia.

A utopia is a perfect world. A typical utopia may be a society free of suffering, pollution and disease, with freedom and equality enjoyed by all citizens. Literature which describes a utopian society is called utopian literature.

A dystopia is the opposite of a utopia. A typical dystopia may be a society plagued by war or famine, with people living in poverty or oppressed by a tyrannical government. Literature which describes a dystopian society is called dystopian literature.

Dystopian science-fiction is much more common than utopian science-fiction - most of the famous science-fiction classics paint a mainly negative picture of humanity’s future. This is especially true of Nineteen Eighty-Four, which presents an extremely dark and pessimistic vision, and became a highly influential example of dystopian writing for subsequent generations of authors.  

Dystopian science-fiction stories are often intended as cautionary tales. The story functions as a warning that our present society may be headed in the direction of the future dystopia, unless we somehow change our ways in the present. This clearly seems to be Orwell’s intention as well - you can read more about this in the section about the novel's message.
