Overview

Most of the plot takes place in the city of London. However, the middle section also zooms in on specific locations, such as a small bit of countryside near the city, or Winston and Julia’s secret hideout. The last section is almost entirely set within the Ministry of Love’s heavily guarded headquarters.  

London

London is part of Airstrip One (previously England or Britain, p. 34), a province within Oceania (p. 5). Oceania is one of three superpowers which are in control of the earth at this point in history, alongside Eurasia and Eastasia. We are told in an extract from ‘Goldstein’s book’ that “Oceania comprises the Americas, the Atlantic islands including the British Isles, Australasia, and the southern portion of Africa” (p. 193).

London is described in mostly negative terms. It is clearly a city dominated by poverty, with apartment complexes smelling of “boiled cabbage and old rag mats” (p.3) and electricity being strictly rationed (p. 3). As Winston looks out of his window in the opening scene of the book, we get a sense of how grey and lifeless the environment appears:

[...] the world looked cold. Down in the street little eddies of wind were whirling dust and torn paper into spirals, and though the sun was shining and the sky a harsh blue, there seemed to be no colour in anything, except the posters that were plastered everywhere. (p. 4)

Later on, we get a description of the Inner Party district, which is a strong contrast to the poverty of the rest of the city - suggesting that London is an area with significant class differences:

[...] the huge block of flats, the richness and spaciousness of everything, the unfamiliar smells of good food and good tobacco, the silent and incredibly rapid lifts sliding up and down, the white-jacketed servants hurrying to and fro…” (p. 175)

The book’s opening scenes reveal that the story takes place in a sort of police state, as we see numerous references to video surveillance (e.g. p. 3), police helicopters (e.g. p. 4) and something called “the Thought Police” (p. 4). We get an image of a city that is very unpleasant to live in, with citizens constantly having to consider their every move as they are always being watched and judged. The city of London is also full of gigantic posters of Big Brother (pp. 3-4), the mysterious ruler of Oceania that all its citizens are meant to be fanatically devoted to.

Oceania is continually at war with one of the other global superpowers, which means that London is periodically struck by bombs (p. 28). Most of the victims of these bombs are ‘proles’, the lower-class citizens of Oceania.  

Finally, it is important to note that London contains four gigantic buildings which house the four ministries which are in charge of various aspects of government: The Ministry of Truth (Minitrue), the Ministry of Peace (Minipax), the Ministry of Plenty (Miniplenty) and the Ministry of Love (Miniluv).

The countryside

The brief description of the countryside marks a very strong contrast to the description of London. It stands out from the rest of the book because it is described as a positive and happy place, comparatively free of government surveillance and control. The beauty of nature is also described in great detail:

They were standing in the shade of hazel bushes. The sunlight, filtered through innumerable leaves, was still hot on their faces. [...] An old, close-bitten pasture, with a footpath wandering across it and a molehill here and there. In the ragged hedge on the opposite side the boughs of the elm trees swayed just perceptively in the breeze, and their leaves stirred faintly in dense masses like women’s hair. (p. 129)

Winston associates this countryside scene with ‘the Golden Country’, a sort of paradise he sometimes sees in his dreams (pp. 32-33).

The room above the junk shop

Like the countryside, the room above the junk shop comes to represent a safe haven for Winston and Julia, a place where they can finally be themselves and enjoy their relationship without being disturbed by the outside world.

The room also has another level of significance, since its contents of old furniture and items makes it a representation of the wonderful past that existed before the Party took control of everything:

[...] the room had awakened in him a sort of nostalgia, a sort of ancestral memory. It seemed to him that he knew exactly what it felt like to sit in a room like this, in an armchair beside an open fire with your feet in the fender and a kettle on the hob: utterly alone, utterly secure, with nobody watching you, no voice pursuing you, no sound except the singing of the kettle and the friendly ticking of the clock. (p. 100)

Mr. Charrington the shopkeeper, seemingly a relic of old times himself, further strengthens this nostalgic image, and Winston enjoys talking to him to stay connected to the mysterious past.

However, the illusion of the room is shattered in the end. Far from being a safe haven, it turns out the Party has been spying on Winston and Julia for their entire stay there. Even kindly old Mr. Charrington is revealed to be a cunning agent of the Thought Police (pp. 230-234).

The Ministry of Love

The Ministry of Love is the headquarters of the police force (including the Thought Police), where both Winston and Julia are sent when they are caught at the end of Part II. At the beginning of the story, we get a brief description of what this heavily guarded complex looks like from the outside (which foreshadows the important role it will play in Part III):

The Ministry of Love was the really frightening one. There were no windows in it at all. [...] It was a place impossible to enter except on official business, and then only by penetrating through a maze of barbed-wire entanglements, steel doors and hidden machine-gun nests. Even the streets leading up to the outer barriers were roamed by gorilla-faced guards in black uniforms, armed with jointed truncheons. (p.6)

Despite its name, the Ministry of Love is an extremely unpleasant place, containing both uncomfortable prison cells and various kinds of torture rooms and devices.

At first, the description of the Ministry simply matches that of a regular prison in a tyrannical state, with overcrowded cells and violent prison guards (pp. 238-240). However, the image becomes even more disturbing when the book moves on to focus on the systematic use of both psychological and physical torture in an effort to completely break a person’s will and independence.

The building itself is also designed to be disorienting. There are no windows anywhere and bright electric lights are constantly shining, so it is impossible for prisoners to know either where they physically are or what time it is (p. 241). This set-up mirrors the structure of the entire state of Oceania, as all of its citizens are also kept strictly isolated from the outside world, to prevent them from learning about the realities of government control, or from realising that foreign enemies are not so different from themselves and therefore limiting their hunger for war (p. 207).

The most terrible place within the Ministry is Room 101. During Winston’s stay the existence of this room is repeatedly brought up, often in combination with prisoners who react with horror (e.g. pp. 248-249). When Winston fails to give up his love for Julia - the one thing he has left (p. 286) - he is finally sent there as well. We learn that Room 101 is designed to confront prisoners with their greatest fear, and that everyone breaks down in response to this treatment (pp. 295-300).    

A note on technology  

Compared to many other famous science-fiction stories, the level of technological advancement described in Nineteen Eighty-Four is surprisingly low, as Orwell’s main focus is to warn against the consequences of politicaldevelopments, not technological developments.  

Goldstein’s book also notes that there is little reason for further technological development in any fields, as Oceanian society is based on inefficiency. There is no longer any drive to improve the living conditions of humanity (in fact the Party strives to avoid such improvements), so there is no reason to put any focus on science and technology. The only two exceptions to this are the scientific fields related to human psychology (to detect thought criminals more efficiently) and warfare (in an attempt to gain the upper hand in the struggle against the other two superstates) (pp. 201-208).

The most important technological device in Oceania is the telescreen - a combined television and video camera. Even when Orwell wrote this book, the possibility of such a screen would not have seemed too far out of the ordinary, as both televisions and video cameras had existed for decades.

Today, telescreen-like devices are of course extremely common - in fact, you are almost certainly reading these words on a device that can both record and display video. In the words of Goldstein’s book, “with the development of television, and the technical advance which made it possible to receive and transmit simultaneously on the same instrument, private life came to an end” (p. 214).
