Themes

Cuprins
  • Totalitarianism

Totalitarianism

One of the main themes of Nineteen Eighty-Four is totalitarianism, as many sections of the book are focused on describing ‘the perfect totalitarian state’. Historically, most totalitarian states have eventually been destroyed by war, revolution or - less violently – by political reforms. However, the totalitarian state described in the novel is specifically designed to be immune to all of these threats (pp. 215-226), and it is implied that it may be able to endure forever, because it is more aware of the potential threats to its powers than previous totalitarian states such as Soviet Russia and Nazi Germany.

The Party's only goal is preserving power for its own sake, and this desire to remain in power makes it flexible enough to keep following its own ideals and crush any opposition.  

Surveillance

Nineteen Eighty-Four is heavily focused on the theme of government surveillance, as it takes place in a society where citizens are watched constantly.

In the novel, this is particularly seen through the government’s use of the telescreen - a device that is a combination of a television and a video camera (essentially making it an important tool of both surveillance and propaganda). Telescreens are everywhere in Oceania, especially in locations where Party members live and work, so each person has to stay constantly aware of his or her words, actions, movements and facial expressions:

You had to live - did live, from habit that became instinct – in the assumption that every sound you made was overheard, and, except in darkness, every movement scrutinised. (p. 5)

Even the slightest break from the norm can lead to suspicion, and the interval between suspicion and arrest is often short.

In addition to the telescreens, Orwell also describes how individual citizens are turned into government informers through indoctrination and propaganda. Even in situations where the telescreens fail, people are constantly watched by their co-workers, partners or even children.

Propaganda

Orwell’s novel is also focused on the power of government propaganda.

The Ministry of Truth is described as an extremely efficient propaganda machine, specialised in producing fake news reports and media designed to control the population’s thoughts. They even have strategies for altering existing propaganda - recalling books and altering records whenever it becomes necessary to reflect a new world view:

…it was known that the chiefs of the Department intended that within one week no reference to the war with Eurasia, or the alliance with Eastasia, should remain in existence anywhere.[…] A mighty deed, which could never be mentioned, had been achieved. It was now impossible for any human being to prove by documentary evidence that the war with Eurasia had ever happened. (pp. 190-191)

With very few exceptions, the people living in Oceania are also described as highly vulnerable to propaganda, often failing to question even those lies and alterations that should be very obvious. The most extreme example of this is seen in the middle of ‘Hate Week’, when a speech is interrupted by news that the identity of the enemy has changed. Both speaker and audience immediately accept the new reality (pp. 188-189), to Winston’s shock and horror. The novel indicates that this susceptibility to propaganda is partly created by the government’s constant control over people’s lives, leaving no possible evidence that anything they publish is untrue or contradictory. This means that people have nothing to trust in except what the government tells them and their own memories and even their memories are systematically modified.

However, the most frightening part of the book’s description of propaganda may be the fact that even the people involved in creating the propaganda and making up the appropriate facts often end up believing in their own lies, thanks to the principle of doublethink at the core of Party politics.

The nature of reality

The novel also contains a philosophical theme about the nature of reality itself. Winston clings to the idea that reality exists outside of what human beings might believe, but the Party’s rule is based on the belief that reality depends on people’s beliefs combined with the contents of the historical records. Since the Party has full control of both historical records and people’s beliefs and memories, they believe that they have full control of reality itself.

This theme is especially explored in Part III of the book, during the discussions between Winston and O’Brien, when Winston continually fails to convince O’Brien of his point of view:

‘[...] where does the past exist, if at all?’

‘In records. It is written down.’

‘In records. And ---- ?’

‘In the mind. In human memories.’

‘In memory. Very well, then. We, the Party, control all records, and we control all memories. Then we control the past, do we not?’ (p. 260)

Many of these arguments are inspired by various philosophical theories about how to view reality, and whether anything is real outside of the human mind. Note that Winston and O’Brien also reference solipsism in their discussion - the idea that it is impossible for any individual to truly know whether anything exists outside of his or her own mind and experiences.
