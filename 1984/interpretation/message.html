Warning against totalitarianism

George Orwell’s main intention with Nineteen Eighty-Four is to warn against totalitarianism. By describing the terrible reality that might result if we let politics move in a totalitarian direction, Orwell tries to show the readers that they must take an active stance and reject such developments.

This message is conveyed throughout the book, but it is perhaps made most clear in O’Brien’s disturbing monologue in Part III, when he talks about the future direction of the world:

‘A world of fear and treachery and torment, a world of trampling and being trampled upon, a world which will grow not less but more merciless at it refines itself. Progress in our world will be progress towards more pain. [...] There will be no loyalty, except loyalty towards the Party. There will be no love, except the love of Big Brother. There will be no laughter, except the laugh of triumph over a defeated enemy. [...] If you want a picture of the future, imagine a boot stamping on a human face - for ever.’ (p. 280)

This image of the future is so frightening that the message about not letting the real world develop in such a direction is made extremely clear to the readers.

The dark conclusion to the novel also adds an extra level of urgency to this message. Since a really efficient totalitarian system might be very difficult - or even impossible - to rebel against once it is in place, it becomes even more urgent not to let the world turn into such a system in the first place. In other words, Orwell is saying that we must always be quick to recognise signs of totalitarianism and turn away from it before it is too late.  

Note that Orwell himself stated that warning people about totalitarianism was a key part of his work, in his famous essay about writing:

Every line of serious work that I have written since 1936 has been written, directly or indirectly, against totalitarianism and for democratic socialism, as I understand it. It seems to me nonsense, in a period like our own, to think that one can avoid writing of such subjects. Everyone writes of them in one guise or another. (George Orwell, “Why I Write”, 1946)

It is also clear that his warning is to a large extent inspired by his observations of the totalitarian states that arose in the first half of the 20th century, such as Soviet Russia and Nazi Germany. You can read more about Orwell’s specific references to these states in the Perspectives section.

Warning against propaganda

Orwell also intends to show the power of state propaganda, which is meant as a warning about the importance of being able to recognise and reject such propaganda. Winston serves as the good example for most of the book, as he is one of the very few people in Oceanic society who somehow seems to have escaped its influence. He is focused on the importance of truth, so the amount of lies and manipulation that is involved in the Party’s propaganda machine seems particularly horrible to him, and he is usually able to see through it quite efficiently. Note that this is partly ironic, as Winston’s own job in the Ministry of Truth is to create such propaganda.

Orwell’s description of the general population in Oceania depicts the people as extremely vulnerable to propaganda, and quick to accept new versions of reality as the Party presents them. The clearest example of this happens during Hate Week, when the identity of the enemy state changes in the middle of an aggressive speech:

Nothing altered in [the speaker’s] voice or manner, or in the content of what he was saying, but suddenly the names were different. [...] Oceania was at war with Eastasia! [...] One minute more, and the feral roars of rage were again bursting from the crowd. The Hate continued exactly as before, except that the target had been changed. (pp. 188-189)

This image is intended to send a message about the importance of having a well-educated population, who are always prepared to question any suspicious statements that their leaders might deliver. The book makes several comments on the importance of education, and how a lack of education and lack of access to objective information can be a powerful tool for oppression. This is ironically conveyed through the Party slogan ‘Ignorance is strength’, as well as at various points in Goldstein’s book:

[…] since military and commercial rivalry are no longer important, the level of popular education is actually declining. What opinions the masses hold, or do not hold, is looked on as a matter of indifference. They can be granted intellectual liberty because they have no intellect. In a Party member, on the other hand, not even the smallest deviation of opinion on the most unimportant subject can be tolerated. (p. 219)

More generally, the warning against propaganda is also related to a general message about the importance of the truth. Until his mind is finally defeated, Winston acts as a sort of champion of the truth, clinging to the idea that there are facts about reality outside of what human beings might believe: “Being in a minority, even a minority of one, did not make you mad. There was truth and there was untruth, and if you clung to the truth even against the whole world, you were not mad” (p. 226). However, the novel also points to the ease with which the truth can be distorted, manipulated or hidden, creating a dangerous situation.

The importance of personal identity

Nineteen Eighty-Four also deals with the importance of personal identity. One of the most frightening aspects of the dystopian society Orwell describes is that it leaves extremely little room for people to ‘be themselves’. Because of constant surveillance and zero tolerance for people who do not follow the norms of the Party, it is very difficult to have personal goals, beliefs and desires.

This also extends to relationships between people. It is very difficult to have loving relationships with others, since everybody - even family members - must be viewed as potential spies for the Party. It may also be difficult to fall in love in the first place, because few individuals stand out because few have unique personalities. Even physical desire is suppressed by the Party’s systematic indoctrination.  

In the world of Nineteen Eighty-Four, it is clear that Orwell’s sympathies lie with the people who do manage to cling to their personal identities despite the system (at least for a while). Winston and Julia are the heroes of the story - not because they accomplish much in the struggle against the Party, but because they follow their own desires and dreams, acting like normal human beings. Therefore, it is doubly tragic to see both of them reduced to shadows of their former selves after the torture they have endured within the Ministry of Love.

Nineteen Eighty-Four may therefore also be seen as a defence of individual identity, and a warning against political systems which try to turn the individual into a faceless part of the machine of the state (e.g. Fascism and some forms of Communism). However, the novel also makes it clear that individuality and personal emotions can be erased in extreme circumstances by the system and through physical and psychological torture.
