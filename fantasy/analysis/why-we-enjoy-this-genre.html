Why we enjoy fantasy

When analyzing a work of fantasy, it might be useful to consider why we read or watch fantasy. We go into two potential reasons here.

Cuprins
  • Fantasy as escapism
  • Fantasy as a social commentary
    • Fantasy goes woke

Fantasy as escapism

Escapism means to seek distraction (= escape) from unpleasant realities. By losing yourself in a work of fantasy, you can forget about your own problems. For instance, if you are bullied in school, you might read Harry Potter and dream of being part of the warm Hogwarts community - or fantasize about casting a Stupefy spell on your bullies. It might not be real, but it can provide emotional and psychological relief all the same. 

In fact, many fantasy authors themselves seem to long for an escape from the complexities of modern life. This may be why the fantasy genre traditionally presents an unspoiled, pre-industrialized past which idealizes traditional values like heroism, loyalty, and friendship. This is for instance seen in The Lord of the Rings (1954-1955). Modern fantasy, however, is often darker and more realistic in nature. 

A curious fact is that many of the major fantasy authors lost a parent at a young age. This goes for Lewis Carroll, J. R. R. Tolkien, C. S. Lewis, Philip Pullman, and J. K. Rowling. This might explain why orphaned protagonists and death have become common elements in fantasy.

Fantasy as a social commentary

Fantasy is not just about turning your back to the real world. In fact, the fantasy genre frequently comments on the real world by critically pointing out social norms and stereotypes that seem unfair or discriminatory. This may be done directly or indirectly. When you analyze a fantasy text, it is therefore relevant to examine if such a message is intended, and how it comes across.

Among other things, social commentary may rists, authors, and readers, but a growing number of strong female protagonists are changing this. Having strong female characters enables authors to demonstrate that women can be just as brave and ambitious as men. 

Social commentary may also be about race. Many fantasy works deliberately feature non-white characters. A recent example of both tendencies is The Poppy War (2018-2020) by R. F. Kuang whose protagonist is an ambitious girl (= gender) and Asian (= race).

Fantasy goes woke

Social commentary is generally evident from the increased ‘wokeness’ of the genre. Recently, we have seen more works featuring gender fluid, trans, or homosexual characters, such as The Priory of the Orange Tree (2019) by Samantha Shannon, which gives a nuanced description of homosexual romances. This type of fantasy sends a strong message that all types of gender and sexuality are equal.

Interestingly, it is normally the magical secondary world which comments on our society, and not the primary world. In Harry Potter, for instance, the power structure of the wizarding world may be seen as a comment on our world. The same does not go for the “muggle” world, which mainly functions as a backdrop.
