Common fantasy themes

Some themes are particularly common in fantasy stories. Below we consider some of the themes that you are very likely to find in fantasy.

Cuprins
  • Good vs. evil
  • Finding your identity
  • Death and sacrifice
  • Justice
  • Love
  • Friendship

Good vs. evil

The struggle between good and evil is a typical fantasy element. Traditionally, evil is represented by the antagonist and his/her army, while good is represented by the protagonist and his/her loyal supporters. Many fantasy works culminate in an epic battle, such as the battle of Helm’s Deep in the 2002 movie Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers. The story should always conclude with a happy ending where good triumphs over evil, and world order is restored.

However, the battle between good and evil might not only play out at an external scale. It may also take place at an internal level within the characters themselves. One example is Jamie Lannister in Game of Thrones (2011-2019), who is torn between his loyalty to his cruel sister and his growing sense of morality.

Finding your identity

Another universal theme in fantasy is finding your identity. This might sometimes also be phrased as growing up, since discovering who you are is part of growing up. This theme dates all the way back to the fairy tale genre.

The identity theme is linked to the development of the - often young - protagonist. At first, the protagonist is unaware of his/her powers, but after being propelled into the action, he/she discovers something about his/her identity or heritage. As the story concludes, the protagonist has developed into a mature individual, changed by the dangers faced.

Using Philip Pullman’s His Dark Materials (1995-2000) as an example, Lyra starts out as an ignorant child and practically an orphan. Gradually, she discovers that she is destined to liberate her world from the grip of the Magisterium. She also learns more about her parents. As the series concludes, she is a lot wiser, and the Magisterium has been defeated. 

Death and sacrifice

The fantasy genre can be gloomy, and death is a common theme. This is most often expressed via the many dangers faced by the protagonist. These dangers are not the challenges of ordinary life but life-threatening situations, such as being burnt to a crisp by a dragon. These near-death experiences create tension in the story and help the protagonist develop.

Death is also felt in the large number of orphaned protagonists. Harry Potter is constantly troubled by the fact that his parents are dead, and his longing for a family is a key element in the series.

Sometimes there is no way around death. The protagonist may have to sacrifice himself/herself to save the world from the antagonist. Again, Harry Potter is a classic example, as he actually ends up allowing Voldemort to kill him. Because of their close connection, this is the only way to destroy the villain. However, Harry is rewarded for his ultimate sacrifice by being restored to life, much like Jesus. Thus, fantasy teaches us valuable lessons about overcoming death and sacrificing yourself for what you believe in.

Justice

Sometimes, justice is a theme in fantasy. Because the antagonist is extremely powerful, extreme injustices are carried out against the central characters. The protagonist might lose a parent, or an entire city might be burned down. Because the protagonist is ‘the chosen one’, he/she often becomes a champion of justice who must set things straight again. 

There is a fine line between justice and revenge, though. Sometimes the protagonist is faced with ethical dilemmas which may have serious consequences for others as well as the protagonist. One example is Rin in The Poppy War (2018) by R. F. Kuang. She is given the choice of destroying an island as revenge for something the island’s army did. It was many years ago, and the island is home to innocent people as well, but Rin still decides to use her magical powers to destroy the island. The question is whether this makes her as morally corrupt as her enemies?

Love

Love is a common element in many fantasy stories. Often, love is complicated: The love object might be an enemy, a monster, or simply belong to the ‘wrong’ sex. 

An example of a monster lover is seen in Stephenie Meyer’s Twilight Saga (2005-2008), where Bella - an ordinary girl - falls in love with Edward the vampire. There are many obstacles to their love. First, Edward must learn to control his urge for blood when around Bella, then they must face the disapproval of the union from their loved ones, and finally Bella must persuade Edward to kill her and turn her into a vampire. Thus, they must overcome death itself to be together forever.

The portrayal of love as a powerful force which may even overcome death is common in fantasy. In Harry Potter, Harry discovers that he is magically protected by his late mother’s love.

Friendship

Friendship is another popular theme. The protagonist may start out as a lonely outsider but always manages to gain friends along the way. One example is Harry Potter who makes friends with Ron and Hermione. The trio support one another in times of danger and learn from each other’s strengths. In a way, Ron and Hermione become the family Harry never had.

Friendship is often linked with loyalty, which is a virtue found in medieval romances (a genre which fantasy is inspired by). In The Lord of the Rings (1954-1955), Sam’s fierce loyalty is a crucial component in making Frodo go on so he can destroy the Ring of Power.
