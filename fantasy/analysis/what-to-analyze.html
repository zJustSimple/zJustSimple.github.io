What elements to analyze

There are a number of key elements or features in fantasy, which are central to the genre. Once you are able to recognize these, analyzing fantasy is pretty straightforward.

Setting: Worlds and portals

A work of fantasy is either set entirely in a fantasy world or in a mixture of fantasy and reality. Thus, worlds and how to move between them are characteristic of the genre.

Analytically speaking, our world is called the primary world. This one is characterized by realism and familiarity. In contrast, the fantasy world is called the secondary world. Some fantasy stories only feature the latter, such as Tolkien’s The Lord of the Rings (1954-1955) where the characters never leave the magical land of Middle-earth. Others feature both, such as J. K. Rowling’s Harry Potter (1997-2007), where Harry and his friends are able to move between the “muggle” world and the wizarding world. 

The moving between worlds typically happens through a portal. This could be practically anything. When Harry first goes to the wizarding world, it happens via a magical train. In C. S. Lewis’ The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe (1950), four siblings enter the magical kingdom of Narnia through a wardrobe. 

The worlds and portals may be symbolic

When analyzing worlds and portals, pay attention to potential symbolism. The fantasy stories that feature child or teenage protagonists are typically about growing up. In these, moving through a portal to a strange place can be seen as a symbol of the growing up process. Some portals even resemble a mother’s womb, like the wardrobe in Narnia or the train in Harry Potter. This underlines how the protagonists are ‘born’ into a new world. Here, they will face difficult choices and increased responsibility, just like adult life.

Plot structure

Certain plot elements almost always feature in a work of fantasy: 

  • A conflict sets the events in motion. This might be in the form of an evil villain who is threatening to destroy the world order.
  • A point of no return eventually occurs. This typically involves the young protagonist realizing that he/she is the chosen one who is meant to save the world.
  • This responsibility means that the protagonist must go on a quest and face many dangers. The protagonist typically meets friends or helpers along the way.
  • After great sacrifices, the protagonist succeeds in beating the antagonist and order is restored. The story concludes with a happy ending.

You can link your observations on plot with the personal development of the protagonist. The journey which he/she must take symbolizes his/her development into an adult.

Characters

Fantasy works feature a great number of characters. These might for instance be elves, witches, werewolves, or dragons, but they might also be human. The characters frequently have traditional roles known from fairy tales, such as kings, queens, warriors, or wizards. Also, the characters might be good, evil, or both. Particularly modern fantasy plays with our ideas of good and evil by having well-rounded, complex characters.

Below, we outline some of the most important characters to analyze.

Protagonist

The protagonist (or main character) is normally young - sometimes even a child. Initially, the protagonist is portrayed as relatively weak and a bit of an outsider. He/she might be an orphan, like Harry Potter, or have a troubled relationship with his/her parents. 

Despite being an unlikely hero, the protagonist reluctantly accepts the role as the chosen one, meaning the one who can fight the antagonist. An example of this is Frodo in The Lord of the Rings. As he is just a little hobbit, he is a very unlikely hero. However, because he has no lust for power, he is perfect for carrying the evil ring. 

Sometimes, the protagonist even has a physical mark that singles him/her out as the chosen one. The scar on Harry’s forehead is the perfect example.

The protagonist is typically fighting a war on two fronts: He/she battles an external enemy, such as Voldemort in Harry Potter, but he/she is also fighting himself/herself as an internal war. For instance, the protagonist might be afraid of dying, might be in love with the wrong person, or might be struggling with teenage life. 

The protagonist normally goes through a personal development throughout the story. He/she matures as an individual and finally finds his/her place in life. Often, the protagonist discovers something about his/her family background in the process.

Antagonist

The antagonist is the evil villain whom the protagonist must fight. Typically, the antagonist wants to take over the world or destroy the natural order of things. 

In many types of fantasy, the antagonist is purely evil, which makes it impossible to sympathize with him/her. One example is the White Witch in The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe (1950). Her evil and cold nature is even reflected in the eternal winter that has fallen on Narnia during her reign. 

In some modern fantasy stories, however, the antagonist is a bit more relatable. In A Song of Ice and Fire (1996-ongoing), Cersei Lannister is cruel and devious. However, we are able to understand why she became that way, as she grew up with a cold father and was married off to a man who did not care. This becomes clear when she is made a viewpoint character in volume 4, enabling readers to gain access to her thoughts and feelings. Thus, in such cases it may be relevant to analyze how the story plays with reader sympathy. 

Mentor

At first, the fantasy protagonist is young and/or unprepared for the dangers that lie ahead. In many cases, he/she gets a powerful and wise mentor whose role it is to prepare the protagonist. The mentor may even function as a sort of surrogate parent for the often orphaned protagonist. Traditionally, the mentor is an older individual - often a wise old wizard, which is a character already known from fairy tales. In The Lord of the Rings, Gandalf becomes a mentor to young Frodo, as he teaches him how to handle the dangerous ring. 

Often, the mentor dies at one point. This symbolizes that the protagonist must now stand on his/her own feet.

Helper

The protagonist normally meets several helpers on his/her way; beating the antagonist is rarely a one-man show. It is no coincidence that friendship is a common fantasy theme.

These helpers might take many forms. Quite often they become close friends of the protagonist, like Ron and Hermione in Harry Potter, who almost function as Harry’s family. Another example is Sam in The Lord of the Rings whose loyalty and support make Frodo go on. 

Love object

Many fantasy stories feature romance. This means that the protagonist often meets his/her love object and ends up in a relationship with that person in the ending. In Harry Potter, Harry ends up marrying Ginny, which underlines how the former orphan has now succeeded in creating his own family.

Sometimes romance becomes part of the main conflict in the plot. In Stephenie Meyer’s Twilight Saga (2005-2008), Bella falls in love with a vampire, which is quite problematic. Partly because he wants to drink her blood, and partly because being with him requires her to die and become a vampire herself.
