Fantasy in the early 20th century

In the first part of the 20th century, fantasy was still considered children’s literature. This was due to successful children’s books in the past, such as Alice in Wonderland (1865), and due to the imaginative content of the genre, such as fairies, dragons, and magic.

A popular work of the era was the 1904 play Peter Pan, or the Boy Who Wouldn’t Grow Up by Scottish author and playwright J. M. Barrie. Like so many fantasy heroes after him, Peter is an orphan and has magical abilities such as flying. 

Another influential work of the era was The Hobbit, or There and Back Again (1937) by J. R. R. Tolkien. In this popular children’s book, Tolkien outlines the imaginary world of Middle-earth in which his later series The Lord of the Rings would also be set.

Fantasy in the late 20th century

The fantasy genre really started to become popular halfway through the 20th century. Many of the novels we consider fantasy masterpieces today were written in this period. The increase in popularity also meant that it gradually became more acceptable for adults to read fantasy.

The Chronicles of Narnia by C. S. Lewis

C. S. Lewis’ The Chronicles of Narnia  (1950-1956) was the first fantasy series to gain major popularity. Through seven books it tells the story of the kingdom of Narnia. The series is mainly set in an imaginary secondary world resembling the Middle Ages. The child protagonists move from the primary world to the secondary one via portals , such as the iconic wardrobe in The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe. The idea of portals would later be picked up by authors such as Philip Pullman and J. K. Rowling.

Lewis was a firm believer in Christianity. The Narnia series can be read as an allegory of events from the Bible, such as the killing of Jesus Christ.

The Lord of the Rings by J. R. R. Tolkien

J. R. R. Tolkien’s trilogy The Lord of the Rings  (1954-1955) was also set in a secondary world with medieval features expressing the same longing for the past as Narnia. However, in Tolkien’s books there is no primary world; the series is set entirely in the imaginary world of Middle-earth. 

Along with Narnia, The Lord of the Rings helped establish certain fantasy elements as ‘the norm’, which many later works of fantasy would follow - such as the medieval-style setting and the protagonists’ quest. However, while The Chronicles of Narnia were children’s books, The Lord of the Rings were adult in nature and featured grown-up characters. Thanks to Tolkien’s series, fantasy was no longer considered a children’s genre.

Earthsea by Ursula K. Le Guin

American author Ursula K. Le Guin mainly wrote science fiction, but her fantasy series Earthsea (spanning 1968-2001) includes many of the elements already used by Lewis and Tolkien, such as the pre-industrialized setting, magical creatures, and the young hero as the chosen one. 

Le Guin is often credited for inventing the wizard school and the boy wizard which have become popular fantasy elements. In volume one, Ged, a young boy with magical abilities, joins a school of wizardry where he is given a scar by a powerful enemy. This idea would later be copied by J. K. Rowling in Harry Potter. 

Fantasy usually equals novels, but Le Guin also wrote short stories about the land of Earthsea. These helped explain the setting of the Earthsea novels more clearly. One example is the short story “The Rule of Names” (1964).

Le Guin frequently used her writing to turn stereotypical ideas of race, gender, and power upside down. For example, most characters in Earthsea have dark skin, whereas the Kargs, an enemy people, are white-skinned. Thus, political messages can be found in her writing. 

His Dark Materials by Philip Pullman

English author Philip Pullman’s trilogy His Dark Materials (1995-2000) is set in parallel worlds which often resemble our world in the past or present. The series is essentially the story of the Fall of Man. Pullman, who declares himself an atheist, says that he wanted to write a story of how the Fall was in fact the best thing that ever happened to us. To him, the Fall symbolizes the natural transformation from innocent child to knowing, sexually active adult. 

Pullman’s books are widely considered a critique of Christianity and the Catholic Church, which has led to criticism from religious groups. Like Le Guin’s writings, this series is an example of fantasy literature that actively comments on society.

Interestingly, Pullman has said that he strongly dislikes the Narnia series, which he considers religious indoctrination, as well as sexist and racist. Thus, Pullman’s series can be seen as a direct response to Narnia. 

A Song of Ice and Fire by George R. R. Martin

If you have not heard of American author George R. R. Martin’s fantasy series A Song of Ice and Fire (1996-ongoing), you have most likely heard of the TV series Game of Thrones which would later be based on the books.

A Song of Ice and Fire resembles traditional fantasy as it features a medieval-looking secondary world. However, Martin adds modern twists. One such twist is the impressive amount of (sometimes brutal) explicit sex. In traditional fantasy, sex was typically replaced by love and romance. This dated back to the medieval romances which the fantasy genre was inspired by. 

Another modern twist is the fact that Martin’s characters are complex. In traditional fantasy, the protagonist was usually purely good while the antagonist was purely evil. In A Song of Ice and Fire, however, many characters have both good and bad sides, making readers’ sympathies shift.

One example is the character of Jamie Lannister. He initially seems like a selfish, immoral traitor, but turns out to be both loyal and brave. The point is that he is all of those things at the same time, which makes him a complex and realistic character.

Finally, A Song of Ice and Fire includes relatively little magic compared to many other fantasy stories. The lack of supernatural elements makes the series seem rather realistic.

Harry Potter by J. K. Rowling

A massive breakthrough for fantasy literature came with J. K. Rowling’s  Harry Potter  series (1997-2007). The story of the orphan boy who discovers that he is a wizard destined to save the world captured the imagination of millions of readers worldwide. Many also found the idea of joining a school of magic fascinating. 

In terms of worlds, Harry Potter features both a primary world inhabited by normal, non-magical people and a secondary world inhabited by wizards and witches. The mixing of worlds is one of the fascinating elements of the series.
