The origin of fantasy 

The origin of the fantasy genre dates back to oral traditions, myths, and folklore: People have been telling stories of magical beasts and sorcery for thousands of years. Superstition has always been a way to explain upsetting events, such as losing a child or the harvest failing. By blaming such events on something supernatural, people were able to make sense of things and cope with hardships.

The belief in supernatural beings and events continued long after the invention of writing. For instance, William Shakespeare’s play Macbeth (1623) features three cunning witches which may be blamed for Macbeth’s downfall. The play was written in honor of King James I who believed firmly in witchcraft.

We do not know for sure when people stopped believing that the stories of magical beings were real. This makes it hard to tell when fantasy as we know it today really began. Fantasy is not fantasy until the author and readers alike realize that this is just a story. However, most people agree that modern fantasy literature originated in the 19th century.

Fantasy literature in the 19th century

One of the earliest fantasy novels ever was Phantastes (1858) by Scottish author George MacDonald. The story is about a young man who is drawn into a dream world. It is regarded as the first fantasy novel for adults. 

Another first mover within the genre was English author William Morris. He was inspired by the medieval romances about noble knights and beautiful maidens - a genre which had been dead for centuries. This medieval inspiration can be seen in his 1896 novel, The Well at the World’s End, which features a young prince who goes on an exciting quest to find a magical well. Like MacDonald, Morris wrote for adults. However, Morris was the first author to create a complete fantasy world, rather than just having his characters enter a dream.

Fantasy was being written for children, too. One of the most famous examples from the 19th century is Alice in Wonderland (1865) by Lewis Carroll. It is worth noting that people did not refer to such stories as “fantasy” yet. The genre was still in its early days, and it was not until the 20th century that fantasy truly started gaining popularity.
