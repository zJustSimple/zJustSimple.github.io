Summary of the series

The Lord of the Rings (1954-1955) is a fantasy trilogy by English author J. R. R. Tolkien. It is about the quest to destroy the One Ring to prevent evil from taking over the world. The story is mainly told through the eyes of four hobbits (short, human-like beings with hairy feet). 

The Fellowship of the Ring (1954)

Frodo, a hobbit living in the Shire, is given a magical ring by his older cousin Bilbo. Through the wizard Gandalf, Frodo learns that it is the One Ring - a powerful ring created by Sauron, with the potential to turn its bearer evil and destroy the free world. 

Frodo volunteers to take the Ring to Mount Doom to destroy it and eventually gets eight traveling fellows: his three hobbit friends Sam, Merry, and Pippin; the two men Boromir and Aragorn; the elf Legolas; the dwarf Gimli; and Gandalf. 

They are constantly pursued by the nine Black Riders as well as the chief wizard Saruman and his brutal orcs. Gandalf is killed. When Boromir tries to take the Ring of Power, Frodo leaves the fellowship with Sam, not trusting anyone else.

The Two Towers (1954)

Boromir is killed by orcs while trying to protect Merry and Pippin. Gandalf comes back from the dead as chief wizard and strips Saruman of his powers. Merry and Pippin have the tree-like giants called ents destroy Saruman’s headquarters. The fellowship then helps defeat Saruman’s orc army in the battle of Helm’s Deep.

Frodo and Sam meet Gollum, a tragic creature obsessed with the Ring. He becomes their guide, but his desire for the Ring makes him betray them: He leads them into the cave of a giant spider. As a wounded Frodo is found by orcs, Sam takes the Ring. 

The Return of the King (1955)

Wanting his Ring back, Sauron sends an army against Gondor, home of the late Boromir and his brother, Faramir. Aragorn and the rest of the fellowship help defeat the army, buying Frodo and Sam some time. 

Sam helps Frodo escape and give the Ring back to him. When they finally reach Mount Doom, Frodo gives in to the Ring’s power and puts it on. Gollum appears and takes it, but falls into the fires of the mountain. As the Ring is destroyed, so is Sauron’s power. 

Aragorn becomes King of Gondor and marries Arwen, an elf princess. The four hobbits return to the Shire, only to discover that they must first drive away Saruman and his followers who have taken over their home. Saruman is killed and harmony restored. Frodo, however, is still affected by the burden of the Ring, and he eventually sails off with Gandalf and Bilbo to find peace.

Tolkien and the creation of the series 

J. R. R. Tolkien (1892-1973) was very gifted at writing and reading at an early age, and he started creating his own languages in his teens. As an adult, Tolkien first worked at the Oxford English Dictionary before beginning an academic career at Oxford University where he taught English language and literature. 

He was close friends with another Oxford lecturer, C. S. Lewis, who was the author of The Chronicles of Narnia series. Both men were part of The Inklings, a literary group where the members would meet and discuss the fantasy novels they were currently working on. Tolkien, who was a Catholic, also inspired Lewis to convert to Christianity. 

Using his knowledge of history and languages, Tolkien published his first fantasy novel in 1937: The Hobbit. This children’s story takes place in the fictional world of Middle-earth. The later trilogy The Lord of the Rings (1954-1955) was more sinister in tone and took several years to finish. 

Studying The Lord of the Rings

The Lord of the Rings is set completely in a secondary world, Middle-earth, which resembles the Middle Ages. The series reflects Tolkien’s fascination with the past as a time when things were better. Due to the old-fashioned language of the books, they appear a bit hard to read today, which may be part of the reason why they are considered adult fantasy.

Comprehensive world building 

The Lord of the Rings is a great example of world building: the creation of an entire fictional world. This is a common feature in fantasy literature. Tolkien is widely admired (and analyzed) for creating one of the most comprehensive fantasy worlds ever. For all the books, he used his academic knowledge of mythology, philology, and languages (such as Old Norse, Old English, and Celtic) to create an entire world for his characters from scratch. He even invented languages.

Perhaps the most striking feature of the series is the fact that Tolkien went to such lengths to keep the illusion that this fictional world is real. This is seen by the fact that there is no primary world in the series; we never leave Middle-earth and are never reminded that this is just a fictional place. Bizarrely, Tolkien even claimed that his imaginary world was in fact the real world in the past. 

The illusion of reality is also supported by the maps, illustrations, and appendices with background information which the books contain. These help legitimize the fictional world to us.

Medieval nostalgia

Furthermore, The Lord of the Rings expresses a nostalgic longing for the pre-industrialized past when everything was perhaps simpler and purer. This is seen by Middle-earth’s likeness to the Middle Ages: People travel on horseback, some of the characters are kings or knights, and old-fashioned virtues like loyalty, sacrifice, and bravery are idealized. 

The nostalgia is also seen by the fact that the series seems to be inspired by old literary genres such as fairy tales and medieval romances. For example, the characters are often either good or evil. Frodo is good and humble while Sauron is evil and power-hungry. The ending of the series is also classic: Good overcomes evil, and Aragorn is crowned as king and marries a beautiful noblewoman. 

However, the ending also has sad elements. Frodo never recovers from carrying the Ring for so long, and his decision to leave the Shire may be read as a symbolic death. Thus, the hero has truly sacrificed himself for the greater good.

Archaic style

The Lord of the Rings is written in an archaic style, which means that the language is old-fashioned. One example is this passage from The Two Towers spoken by Aragorn: 

‘Alas!’ said Aragorn. [...] ‘Now the Company is all in ruin. It is I that have failed. Vain was Gandalf’s trust in me. What shall I do now?

Phrases like “alas” and “it is I” make it seem old-fashioned. The same goes for the word order in “vain was Gandalf’s trust…”. In his series, Tolkien deliberately mimics the language of medieval romances: A horse may be called “a steed”, the enemy may be “the foe”, and to die is “to perish”. 

The old-style words may put some modern readers off because this type of language can seem stiff and complicated. However, it is a classic fantasy feature to copy medieval language, especially in high fantasy, and in The Lord of the Rings it helps underline the setting. 

Old-fashioned (male) characters

The archaic style is also seen by the characters who resemble archetypes. An archetype is a character-type that is commonly seen in literature, such as the wizard or the evil stepmother. In The Lord of the Rings, Aragorn resembles the archetypal heroic knight or king, while Gandalf is the archetypal wise old wizard. This helps the readers work out who the characters are and whether they are good or evil. In turn, Tolkien’s series itself would go on to inspire future works of fantasy.

Another striking feature of Tolkien’s novels is that there are almost no female characters. For instance, the fellowship consists entirely of nine males. Women often seem to be a sort of prize for the heroes: In the ending, Sam marries Rosie from the Shire while Aragorn marries his elf princess. When the books were later made into movies, minor female characters like Arwen and Éowyn were given greater importance to appeal to modern viewers. 

Themes

Certain universal themes can be identified in The Lord of the Rings. One is that of glory and honor, which is closely linked to the series’ fascination with medieval romances. Tolkien’s series is basically a story about heroes who win glory and honor by fighting bravely against evil and sacrificing themselves for the greater cause. 

Contrary to fairy tales and some older fantasy stories, glory and honor is not gained by finding a treasure of some sort. The Lord of the Rings is about getting rid of a treasure (the Ring of Power).

Another universal theme is good vs. evil, which is common in fantasy. In the end, Sauron’s evil is overcome, but at great personal costs to the heroes.

A third theme is the danger of power. Each of Sauron’s magical Rings is so powerful that the wearer is constantly in danger of being corrupted. Even a humble anti-hero like Frodo cannot escape its magic: After resisting over the course of three books, the Ring tempts him into seeking its power instead of destroying it. 

Finally, war is also a potential theme. Tolkien took part in WW1 when he was young, and The Lord of the Rings was written around the time of WW2. Some argue that the series is inspired by these wars. For example, the Rings may be read as a symbol of world domination; the courage and friendship of ordinary hobbits may symbolize soldiers of war; and the mass mobilization of orcs may resemble German mobilization in the world wars.

Legacy and adaptations 

Tolkien’s trilogy became incredibly popular - especially among young people in the 1960s when paperback editions came out. Many works of fantasy, as well as role-playing games, have been inspired by Tolkien’s books, which is why Tolkien is often referred to as the father of modern fantasy.

The trilogy has been adapted into various media types such as radio and movies. The most successful adaptation so far has been Peter Jackson’s film trilogy: The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring (2001), The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers (2002), and The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King (2003). The movies won several Oscars. 

Amazon is currently preparing a TV series set in an age before the one which Tolkien’s trilogy takes place in, meaning that some of the settings and characters will be familiar. The title will be The Lord of the Rings: The Rings of Power.
