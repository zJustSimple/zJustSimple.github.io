Definition of fantasy

Fantasy can be defined as a fiction genre which involves magical elements, settings, and creatures. It is typically set in an imaginary world that may or may not resemble the Middle Ages. 

Fantasy is often inspired by ancient myths, cultures, or religions. Thus, the genre frequently features mythical or magical beings, such as elves, dragons, witches, and fairies.

In many ways, fantasy depends on the readers’ suspension of disbelief - meaning that we must accept all the unrealistic and impossible elements to enjoy it. We know that what we are reading or watching is impossible but that is part of the enjoyment. 

Today the fantasy genre spans many different types of media, such as novels, comic books, video games, TV series, and movies. It has also become mainstream and acceptable entertainment for adults, despite initially being labeled as a children’s genre.

Fantasy vs. fairy tales

To some extent, fairy tales can be considered early versions of fantasy. There are many similarities between the two genres. The protagonist is typically a young person who must overcome many obstacles on the way to adulthood. Thus, a central theme is growing up and finding your place in life. In both genres, the protagonist meets an antagonist. The plot features a conflict, a quest, and most often a happy ending. Magic and magical creatures are typical elements in both genres, too.

There are differences, though. Characters tend to be flat in fairy tales, which means that they are either good or bad. Particularly in modern fantasy, however, characters are rounded and display both positive and negative qualities. For instance, the TV characters in Game of Thrones (2011-2019) often challenge our ideas of good and evil.

Fairy tales also tend to follow a more fixed ‘pattern’ compared to fantasy, for instance by subjecting the protagonist to three challenges. Another difference is the variety of themes found in fantasy. Since fantasy is a much more modern genre than fairy tales, themes like LGBT rights, bullying, and feminism may be explored.

Fantasy vs. science fiction

Some people confuse fantasy and science fiction, but there are actually important differences between the two genres. 

Firstly, there is the time setting. Science fiction is typically set in the future with advanced technology or space travel playing a major role. One example is the TV series Westworld (2016-ongoing) which features androids - human-like robots. In contrast, fantasy is typically set in a world which resembles our past, such as the medieval era. Game of Thrones is a good example.

Secondly, science fiction focuses on technology whereas fantasy focuses on magic. This means that the science fiction genre usually explains unrealistic elements in scientific ways, whereas fantasy uses magic as an explanation - or does not explain it at all. 

Thirdly, science fiction often portrays a dystopian world, meaning a frightening future society which is typically characterized by natural catastrophes, totalitarian regimes, and the like. In contrast, fantasy typically attempts to present the ideals for a better society, such as justice, bravery, or sacrifice. There is plenty of evil in fantasy, but the good always prevails in the end. 

Subgenres of fantasy

Below, we list some of the most common fantasy subgenres. Note, however, that a work of fantasy might include elements from more than one subgenre. 

High fantasy 

High fantasy is also called epic fantasy. It is often told by an omniscient narrator and contains many of the characteristics that people most often associate with fantasy:

The setting is typically a fictional world, which in fantasy-terms is called the secondary world. The protagonist is most often a child or teenager, who is somehow an outsider in society and an unlikely hero. However, as an evil villain threatens to take over the world, the protagonist is forced to realize that he/she is the chosen one meant to save the world. This might be seen from a physical mark, such as the scar on Harry Potter’s forehead. 

The role as the chosen one usually results in a dangerous quest for the protagonist who must make great sacrifices to overcome the villain. A common theme in high fantasy is the battle between good and evil.

One example of high fantasy is J. R. R. Tolkien’s The Lord of the Rings series (1954-1955). Young Frodo becomes a reluctant hero in the battle between good and evil.

Contemporary fantasy 

As the name implies, contemporary fantasy takes place in the present day and in familiar settings. If it takes place in a contemporary city, the subgenre is also called urban fantasy. 

The genre combines the familiar and the strange: Magic is either part of the contemporary world or exists in an alternative world alongside the normal one, as in J. K. Rowling’s Harry Potter (1997-2007). Many of the characters are often mythological beings such as werewolves, vampires, or witches. The story is frequently told by a first-person narrator. 

A common theme is the relationship between humans and other beings (ranging from peaceful coexistence to deadly conflict). Works with a romantic angle typically focus on the romance between a human and a supernatural creature. One example is Stephenie Meyer’s Twilight Saga (2005-2008).

Another trend in the genre is the combination of fantasy and the detective story. This is often the case in stories set in modern big cities (urban fantasy). For example, The Dresden Files (2000-ongoing) by Jim Butcher features the wizard detective Harry Dresden who solves supernatural crime cases.

Dark fantasy

As hinted by the name, dark fantasy includes frightening or sinister elements. The subgenre frequently features ‘traditional monsters’ like vampires, werewolves, or the walking dead, while portraying them in a more positive light.

The story might even be told from the monster’s point of view to evoke sympathy in the readers. (This is a clear contrast to horror, which traditionally sympathizes with the victims). The protagonists may be fighting an external battle, but they might also be battling their own minds.

Examples include Stephenie Meyer’s Twilight Saga (2005-2008) and Glen Duncan’s The Last Werewolf (2011-2014). Note that both contain horror elements, too.

Sword and sorcery

Sword and sorcery is a stereotypically male type of fantasy, which features muscular heroes who battle all sorts of enemies. The story is action-packed. Unlike high fantasy, the protagonist does not sacrifice himself for a greater cause - he fights for himself and is no role model in terms of morality. 

Often, female characters are just ‘damsels in distress’, meaning that they must be rescued by the protagonist or function as his reward. This tendency might explain why the subgenre is no longer that popular. 

A famous sword and sorcery example is the “Conan the Barbarian” character, used for various types of media. In 2011, an adventure movie of the same name featured the ever-muscular Jason Momoa.
