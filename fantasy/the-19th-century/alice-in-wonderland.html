Summary of Alice in Wonderland

One of the earliest examples of fantasy literature is the English children’s book Alice in Wonderland (1865). The full title is Alice’s Adventures in Wonderland. It was written by Charles Lutwidge Dodgson under the pen name Lewis Carroll. 

As the story begins, Alice, a seven-year-old girl, is sitting on a riverbank, feeling tired. Suddenly a talking rabbit runs by, and Alice follows it down the rabbit hole. She ends up journeying through an underworld - Wonderland - where everything seems strange and illogical. 

Alice shrinks and grows in size throughout the story, always due to something she eats or drinks. She meets many magical creatures, such as the Cheshire Cat, the March Hare, the Hatter, and the Queen of Hearts. Most of the beings in Wonderland puzzle her with strange tales or riddles, and some of the beings even frighten or attack her. However, she gradually learns to stand up for herself. 

In the final chapter, Alice disrupts a courtroom, which makes the Queen of Hearts scream: “Off with her head!” As the Queen’s guards attack, Alice is woken up on the riverbank by her sister, making everything that happened to her seem like a dream.

Carroll and the creation of the book 

Lewis Carroll (1832-1898) was an extremely gifted child, with a flair for logic, mathematics, and word play. He went on to teach mathematics at Oxford University. He never married or had any children, but he became very close for a while with the three daughters of his friend, Henry Liddell. 

Alice in Wonderland started out as a story which Caroll told to Liddell’s daughters on a boat trip. Carroll was particularly close to Alice, the middle child. She is believed to be the inspiration behind Alice in the book. The real Alice liked the story so much that she asked Carroll to write it down, and a few years later it was published. 

In 1872, a sequel was published, titled Through the Looking-Glass, and What Alice Found There.

Studying Alice in Wonderland 

Alice in Wonderland is set almost entirely in a secondary world, which is apparently revealed to be nothing but a dream. Despite being labeled as children’s literature, the book challenges our ideas of logic and language. Therefore, the book’s portrayal of logic, sense, and mathematics is a typical point of analysis. 

Alice’s development as a character is another interesting element of the book. One of the reasons is that she represents a true Victorian while also representing the opposite, which we outline below.

Literary nonsense 

Alice in Wonderland belongs to the type of literature called literary nonsense. This basically means a type of literature that does not make sense. There are indeed a lot of situations and conversations in Alice in Wonderland that do not make sense. One example is Chapter 7, “A Mad Tea-Party”. 

One characteristic of literary nonsense is the creation of words that are completely new, combined in new ways, or make no sense at all. This is illustrated by the character of Humpty Dumpty who states: “When I use a word [...] it means just what I choose it to”. This attitude to language obviously leads to chaos. An example of a made-up word is “uglification”.  

Another characteristic is the use of wordplay or riddles. An example of wordplay lies in the remark that “flamingoes and mustard both bite”. Literally speaking, only the flamingo can bite; the mustard is merely spicy. An example of a nonsensical riddle comes when the Hatter asks Alice: “Why is a raven like a writing-desk?” When Alice cannot solve it, the Hatter answers that he does not know either. Without an answer to it, the riddle becomes pointless. This is part of what makes the book funny. It is funny because it makes no sense.

Alice’s development is related to the nonsense

Alice’s development is reflected in the way she responds to all the nonsense and illogical rules of Wonderland. In the beginning, she behaves like a typical Victorian girl: She is polite, has faith in authority, and dutifully tries to use what she has learned in school to deal with challenges. However, she frequently ends up in conflicts with the creatures of Wonderland because her rationality is at odds with the strange world she now finds herself in. 

Gradually, Alice starts standing up for herself by challenging the illogical rules presented by the fantasy characters. At the end of the book, she has developed a healthy skepticism towards authorities and nonsense. This is also part of the book’s message.

Themes

There are several themes to focus on in the book. Many of these reflect how Alice in Wonderland satirizes aspects of Victorian society. A major one is nonsense as the book seems to play with fixed ideas of what does and does not make sense. Thus, it challenges the way we see the world.

Another important theme is mathematics and logic. Carroll was a very skilled mathematician and taught at university, and Alice in Wonderland includes many references to mathematics and logic, such as the use of syllogisms. Carroll was a conservative mathematician and disliked abstract mathematics which was gaining popularity at the time. This is expressed in the book, for instance through absurd syllogisms. 

Alice in Wonderland might also be interpreted as a critique of Victorian child upbringing. During the 19th century, children were expected to behave like little adults. Education was strict, based on rote learning, and did not leave much room for thinking out of the box. Carroll seems to be arguing that Alice has been taught ‘nonsense’ in school, which means that she is ill-equipped for real life (symbolized by Wonderland). 

This can be seen in chapter 2 when Alice meets a mouse. She tries to talk to it by reciting the Latin inflections of the word ‘mouse’ because she has seen this in a school book. Unsurprisingly, it does not work. 

Legacy and adaptations

Alice in Wonderland was well received by both literary critics and the public in 1865, and it soon became a classic. The book also marked a turning point in children’s literature: Victorian literature had so far been quite didactic, but the focus gradually shifted. Now children’s books were expected to be more entertaining and have characters which the child readers could identify with. 

Carroll’s work has been adapted numerous times, for instance in the form of operas, plays,  movies, and even videogames. Most influential is perhaps the 1951 animation movie by Disney, which has inspired popular culture ever since. A recent example is Tim Burton’s dark fantasy movie Alice in Wonderland (2010), which starred Mia Wasikowska as Alice and Johnny Depp as the Mad Hatter. In 2016, a sequel entitled Alice Through the Looking Glass was made.
