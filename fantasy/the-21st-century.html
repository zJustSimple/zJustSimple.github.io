Fantasy in the 21st century

Fantasy as a mainstream, popular genre

Where the fantasy genre will go in the 21st century still remains to be seen. So far, however, it has become immensely popular. What used to be a niche genre has now become an established one, appealing to both children, adults, men, and women. 

Also, fantasy no longer just includes books; the genre has spread to other media types, such as movies, TV series, and video games. Below, we outline some of the tendencies of the period which have emerged so far.

Screen adaptations of the classics

In the first part of the 21st century, fantasy was especially made mainstream by the many film and TV series adaptations of popular works. These meant that the genre could reach a much wider audience. 

Two movie series in particular helped kickstart this trend: Harry Potter (2001-2011) and The Lord of the Rings (2001-2003). Both series set new standards for visual effects, such as the creation of Gollum in The Lord of the Rings. Other examples of movie adaptations included The Chronicles of Narnia (2005-2010) and The Golden Compass (2007). In 2012-2014, three movies based on Tolkien’s book The Hobbit were released. 

However, as streaming providers like Netflix and HBO emerged, the series format became the preferred type of adaptation for many fantasy works. Particularly Game of Thrones (2011-2019) was a monster hit, gaining a large, international fanbase. The series has been criticized, though, for its level of nudity and sexual violence, such as rape against women. Other examples of series include The Witcher (2019-ongoing), His Dark Materials (2019-ongoing), and Shadow and Bone (2021-ongoing). All three were based on popular book series.

Note that superhero stories like the Batman and Superman franchises can also be categorized as fantasy. We do not go into this in this webbook, though.

Fantasy video games

The fantasy genre has also been hugely influential on the world of video games. In fact, some of the longest running gaming franchises have been mainly or entirely set in fantasy settings, including The Legend of Zelda (1986-ongoing) and Final Fantasy (1987-ongoing). 

Many fantasy video games have been inspired by tabletop roleplaying games  such as Dungeons and Dragons, which was itself inspired by J. R. R. Tolkien and other fantasy authors. Notable computer role playing games (RPGs) with fantasy settings are Baldur’s Gate (1998) and Planescape Torment (1999), which are both directly based on the Dungeons and Dragons ruleset. More recent examples of computer RPGs are Dragon Age (2009-2014) and The Witcher (2007-2015).

As the internet became more widespread, a subgenre of RPGs emerged where thousands of players could play together in the same fantasy world. The best known example of these MMORPGs is World of Warcraft (2004), which is still played today. A reason behind the popularity of the MMORPGs may be that they offer even more escapism than fantasy novels, because they offer the closest thing possible to actually living within a fantasy world. 

Notably, the Game of Thrones author George R. R. Martin was involved in creating Elden Ring, making it another example of how fantasy literature may influence fantasy games. 

A more diverse genre commenting on current topics

Despite dealing with imaginary worlds, the fantasy genre often comments on society, for instance by indirectly criticizing social, moral, or religious norms. Recently, issues of race, sexuality, and gender identity have become topics of debate in our society. It is no surprise, then, that some of these topics are now finding their way into the fantasy genre. 

This has resulted in fantasy literature becoming more diverse and ‘woke’, with a greater focus on minorities. In many ways, this focus fits the genre; fantasy protagonists are traditionally outsiders in society who must fight for their right to be who they are.

One example of this new trend is Mask of Shadows (2017-2018) by Linsey Miller. The duology features a gender fluid protagonist, Sal, who falls in love with a woman while plotting to take revenge for the killing of their family. 

Another example is The Priory of the Orange Tree (2019) by Samantha Shannon. The book is feminist in many ways. This is for instance expressed by the strong female leads and the description of a world in which women can be rulers or dragon riders. The book also includes several LGBT love stories.

Note, however, that some older works have also commented on society. Ursula K. Le Guin’s Earthsea (spanning 1968-2001) addresses issues of race, while Philip Pullman’s His Dark Materials (1995-2000) criticizes Christianity. 

Fantasy literature based on non-western mythologies

Fantasy literature has traditionally been a very English genre, with its worlds often being based on Western European geography and mythology. E.g. The Chronicles of Narnia (1950-1956) is essentially about Christianity, and Game of Thrones takes place in the imaginary kingdom of Westeros which strongly resembles Britain. 

However, a growing trend in 21st century fantasy literature is the emergence of books based on non-western geography and mythology. The authors may for instance be of Asian, African, or Middle Eastern descent. One example is the novel Kaikeyi (2022) by Indian-American author Vaishnavi Patel, which mixes Indian mythology with a tale of a young woman fighting to prove herself in a world dominated by men and gods. 

Examples of Asian fantasy works include the The Poppy War trilogy (2018-2020) by R. F. Kuang and The Celestial Kingdom duology (2022-ongoing) by Malaysian author Sue Lynn Tan who lives in Hongkong. Both series are inspired by Chinese history, geography, and mythology.
