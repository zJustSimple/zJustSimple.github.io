Definitions and interpretations of fake news

The term “fake news” refers to false information spread through media channels such as print, broadcast, or online platforms. It can have many definitions and interpretations.

Fake news can be seen as a hoax or a fabricated story that has no factual basis but is presented as the truth. Sometimes, politicians use such fake news to gain political success. One example is US President Donald Trump, who sometimes makes false statements that represent his personal opinions and presents them as facts.

Fake news can also be defined by its purpose. Fake news is usually deliberately created to mislead the public, in which case its purpose is disinformation. Consequently, fake news could be considered propaganda. This is because fake news usually includes scandalous material, blame, misleading images, and quotes taken out of context. All these elements can be used to distort facts and truths for political gains.

However, fake news can also be the result of an honest mistake or negligence, in which case they lead to misinformation.

However, note that since the second half of 2016, some politicians have started to invoke the term “fake news” against critical press material. It is important to note the current President of the United States, Donald Trump, uses “fake news” to refer to true reports that he simply does not agree with or that paint a negative picture of his presidency. By doing this, he tries to bring an end to debates about the validity of his actions and he to discredit journalistic practices, encouraging people to distrust the mainstream media.

The term has also attracted some controversy. The criticism addressed toward the use of the term lies mostly in its negative effects on the public’s trust in the mainstream media. Some have drawn attention to the fact that the fake news phenomenon has created mass distrust, affecting analytic thinking and people’s ability to discern between real and false information.

Textual perspective 1 - Donald Trump's Super Tuesday Victory Speech
 

Here, Trump makes statements that represent his personal opinions and presents them as facts, making claims that are not very accurate.

Textual perspective 2 - Donald Trump's Inaugural Address
 

In his speech, Trump uses propaganda as he exaggerates the number of his supporters and people present at his inauguration, for example. In fact, the media compared the size of the audience at his inaugural address with the size of the audience during Barack Obama’s inaugural addresses and concluded that Trump’s audience was significantly smaller. Also note that many of Trump’s statements during the presidential campaign were considered to be inaccurate.

Types of content that can classify as fake news

There are different types of content that can classify as fake news and can be encountered in media at present. It is important to distinguish between them to get a better understanding of the phenomenon.

Satire or parody can often be interpreted as fake news. In mainstream media, satire employs comedy to blur the line between news and entertainment. For example, in television, political satire programs often include fake “evening news” shows. Satirical websites such as The Onion also contain articles that cover both real and fictional events. However, the intention of satire is not to spread disinformation among the audience, but to offer entertainment. Therefore, the stories are often presented in such an exaggerated way that it is easy to recognise as false - but even so, people are sometimes fooled by them. 

Impostor content is created when well-known news sources are being impersonated. This means that fake news articles are linked to or are passed off as coming from a reliable source. This can be done to cause harm and undermine the validity of the source. It can also have the purpose of using the reputation of the source to gain profit and media coverage for the impersonator.

Manipulated content such as edited images, video, audio, or text, is often used to create fake news. This kind of content deliberately modifies already-existing material to deceive the public and manipulate their opinion. It is usually employed to discredit or promote the influence of a person or political party, and as a result, it is commonly used in propaganda.

Clickbait material is used to spread fake news. Clickbait articles use eye-catching headlines that draw the attention or curiosity of the reader. However, this type of articles relies on false connections, meaning that the headlines, visuals, or captions do not reflect the content of the article or have very little connection to it. Therefore, clickbait techniques are used to mislead the public. Clickbait has also been used for political purposes and has been blamed for the rise of post-truth politics, since it focuses more on the reaction it receives than on portraying accurate facts.

False context is commonly used to create fake news. The practice involves taking content that is true and either removing it from its original context so that the meaning is altered, or sharing it alongside false contextual information. False context can happen due to poor journalistic skills, in which case it spreads misinformation, or because it has malicious intent and it is designed to spread disinformation. False context can also be used in propaganda.

Fabricated content is used to create fake news in the traditional sense of the term. It is new content that is created without being based on facts and that is, therefore, false. Fabricated content can be used to parody or provoke, but it is mainly used to gain profit or to manipulate the public for political purposes.
