Glossary for fake news

On this page you can find words that are particularly useful to know when studying the fake news phenomenon. Click each word for a full definition in English.

algorithm (s)
 

A set of steps that are followed to complete a computer process. In this context, it refers to the way a program processes user information to show the user relevant suggestions. For example, each individual Facebook user has a news feed which is controlled by a complex algorithm that takes both the user's Facebook activities and other gathered data into account. 

alt-right (s)
 

Short for “alternative right” - a political movement whose members reject mainstream politics and instead embrace extremist right-wing beliefs and policies typically centred on ideas of nationalism and white supremacy. The alt-right is also known for misogyny, LGBT+ discrimination and climate change denial.  

anti-Semitic (adj)
 

Feeling or showing hostility or discrimination towards Jewish culture or Jews as an ethnic group. Some fake news is heavily anti-Semitic, such as stories about imagined "Jewish conspiracies". 

bot (s)
 

A computer program that performs automatic repetitive tasks. In this context, it refers to programs that create or share fake news articles or social media posts related to fake news.

censorship (s)
 

The system or practice of examining writings or movies and taking out elements considered offensive or immoral. Censorship can be used in positive ways, but it is also a dangerous tool for dictators and authoritarian governments, who often like to suppress the media.

chain email (s)
 

An e-mail sent to several people, usually accompanied by the request for each person to forward the e-mail to others.

clickbait (s)
 

A headline designed to make readers want to click on a hyperlink, especially when the link leads to content of dubious value or interest. "10 things celebrities do to stay young. Number 7 will shock you!" is a typical example of a clickbait headline - providing little information, but promising some kind of sensational payoff when the link is followed. 

confirmation bias (s)
 

A tendency to search for or favour content and views that are similar to one’s own beliefs. In this context, it refers to users seeking out information they already agree with, without viewing contradicting points.

conspiracy theory (s)
 

A theory that attempts to explain an event or situation by suggesting it is the result of a secret plan usually committed by powerful people or groups. These theories are not backed up by genuine evidence and they rely on the idea that a secret is being kept from the public, as in the case of the Obama birther conspiracy.

debunk (v)
 

To show that something, such as a belief or theory, is not true. The rise of fake news and conspiracy theories have also led to an increase in sites dedicated to debunking fake stories. 

echo chamber (s)
 

A closed system where bias or personal beliefs are reinforced by the repetition of the same idea. Because of the way algorithms on pages like Facebook or YouTube work, users tend to get exposed to content that is similar to what they already like. This means that a user looking up a lot of conspiracy theories on YouTube will probably see even more conspiracy videos in their recommended video feed, rather than factual stories which debunk the theories.

fact check (s)
 

An analysis done to determine the validity and correctness of a statement in a text or speech. In this context, it refers to sites that check the truthfulness or credibility of news sources.

forgery (n)
 

A false version of something  (often an official document) that has been designed to look real.  

hashtag (s)
 

A word or phrase that starts with the symbol # and that classifies or explains the accompanying text. Hashtags are also used to organise or connect content on social media platforms. Many fake news stories get a dedicated hashtag, making it easy for users to share and view content related to them.

hoax (s)
 

A fake story that is deliberately passed off as being real.

internet troll (s)
 

A person who intentionally antagonises others online by posting offensive comments or other disruptive content. It may also refer to people that intentionally spread fake news. Some nations use professional trolls to influence popular opinions in other nations by sharing fake or disruptive stories.

journalistic ethics (s)
 

A set of moral duties and obligations that journalists should follow to respect work practice. In this context, it refers to rules journalists should follow to create accurate and truthful news.

leak (s)
 

A situation in which people learn about information that is supposed to be secret, perhaps because an insider in a government or organisation decide to illegally share the information with the public, or perhaps because hackers gained access to it from outside.  

mainstream media (s)
 

A term used to refer collectively to media that reaches, influences, and is consumed by a large number of people, reflecting and shaping current trends and popular attitudes.

polarisation (s)
 

Division into two opposites, especially in political terms. Many countries show tendencies to become more polarised, as people on different sides of the political spectrum become more extreme in their respective beliefs, and therefore disagree more and more violently with each other.

post-truth politics (s)
 

A political culture in which debates appeal to emotion rather than focus on facts. Donald Trump is regarded as one of the main symptoms of this problem, as he frequently repeats direct lies or distort statistics to suit his purposes, but still manages to retain his popularity.  

propaganda (s)
 

Ideas, information, or allegations spread deliberately to further a cause or to damage an opposing cause.

sensationalism (s)
 

The use of shocking details to cause a lot of excitement or interest. In this context, it mainly refers to stories that have a shocking or eye-catching topic or title.

source (s)
 

A point of origin, or someone or something that supplies information. In this context, it refers to the sites that create and distribute news articles. Many fake news stories have anonymous or questionable sources, but this can be hard to spot when they are shared through social media. 

spam (s)
 

Something unwanted, such as an e-mail, that is sent to large numbers of people and that consists mostly of advertising.

tabloid (s)
 

A newspaper that typically contains many photographs and stories about topics such as lifestyle or pop culture. Tabloid are often regarded as less serious newspapers, and tend to be more likely to have sensationalist stories that exaggerate or manipulate with facts.

user-generated (adj)
 

Any form of content, such as images, videos, text, and audio, that have been posted by users of online platforms. As more and more people get their news from user-generated content, it becomes easier to spread fake news stories. 

viral (adj)
 

Quickly and widely spread or popularised, especially through being shared on social media.

yellow journalism (s)
 

Term for journalism associated with little or no legitimate well-researched news. In this context, it refers to newspapers that present news of dubious value while using eye-catching headlines to attract attention.
