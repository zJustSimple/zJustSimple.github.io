Social media and fake news

Cuprins
  • General aspects
  • User-centred bias
  • Manipulation

General aspects

The 21st century saw a rise in popularity of fake news. This is due to the further spread of the Internet and the development of communication technologies. When the Internet became available to the public in the 1990s, its main purpose was accessing information. Over time, the Internet has grown and developed, and nowadays, it contains a huge amount of information, with more coming in every day. Users can manipulate this information by adding their own opinion or knowledge on the topic, by modifying texts on open-source sites, by creating their own content, etc. However, this also allows the Internet to provide platforms for false and misleading material.

User-centred bias

Today, the capacity to mislead is enhanced by the widespread use of social media. This is because social media facilitates the creation and sharing of information and ideas. The content made available on social media platforms is primarily user-generated, meaning that users post personal experiences, creations, preferences, etc. Since it is personal, information of this kind can become difficult to assess objectively, and therefore, the message conveyed by this type of content is often subjective and not entirely reliable.

The Internet is becoming increasingly personalised. Websites and social networking sites collect data about their users to provide them with information that is relevant to their interests. For example, online e-commerce websites include recommendation and personalisation algorithms used to make relevant product suggestions. Social media platforms also use this approach to assist users with ﬁnding preferred content and viewing more relevant advertisements. Similarly, news consumption has also become an extremely personalised experience, and newspapers and news websites adapt content to reader preferences. This is done as part of the intense competition for attention in the digital media environment.

The use of personalisation algorithms in news recommendation can also lead to biases. Once the system registers the user’s preferences, the user has little choice over the recommendations he or she receives. The algorithm shows only news articles that align with the user’s views or preferences. This impacts the diversity of news and isolates the user from specific news sources, possibly leading to the reinforcement of the user’s views on certain topics and, therefore, also leading to the creation of an echo chamber. The lack of explanation about how the content is selected for the user can lead to the user being unaware of the fact that he or she has access to only a small subset of news. Instead, the user may believe that he has access to all the available information, and this can create an involuntary bias.

Manipulation

Social media and other online information platforms are replacing television and newspapers as the primary news sources. In 2016, 62% of the adults in the United States stated that they used social media platforms to receive news, compared to 2012 when only 49% looked at news on social media. Facebook, Reddit, Twitter, and Tumblr were the most popular sites from which users received their news, with Facebook being the largest, despite the fact that it does not advertise itself as a news site or produce its own content. Most Facebook users stated that they are more likely to get their news online mostly by chance, when they are on the platform doing other things. Therefore, they assume a passive role when consuming news and are less likely to fact-check the information they receive.

The Facebook newsfeed is also notorious for enabling the spread of fake news, as fake news generally see higher sharing rates than legitimate news stories, due to their sensationalist content. According to analysis, 30% of all fake news traffic can be linked back to Facebook, as opposed to only 8% of legitimate news traffic. Facebook users play a major role in spreading fake news stories by promoting popular content to more users.

Therefore, social media plays a major factor in the spread of fake news. Internet trolls are also a problem because they facilitate the spread of disinformation. Because their purpose is to sow discord on online platforms and between online communities, internet trolls often create fabricated or misleading content and share fake news, provoking other people to start debates over them and, therefore, increasing the visibility of the false information. 

Social media users and malicious bots can create and spread targeted fake news content in different forms for political gains and to manipulate the public opinion on specific topics. Social media is preferred as a medium of spreading fake news because it allows a quick and easy spread of the information. The rapid consumption of media leads to the creation of trends, and viral sharing of content gives articles, images, and videos a wide reach.
