The political impact of fake news

Cuprins
  • Introduction
  • Brexit referendum
  • 2016 US presidential election

Introduction

Recent political events, such as the controversial Brexit referendum in the United Kingdom and the victory of Donald Trump in the US presidential election of 2016, have led to a wave of interest in the phenomenon of fake news. This is because fake news is widely believed to have played a significant role in shaping the outcome of both political events.

Brexit referendum

In June 2016, the Conservative government of the United Kingdom initiated a referendum on the withdrawal of the United Kingdom (UK) from the European Union (EU). The referendum passed by a narrow margin and the UK is due to leave the EU on 29th of March, 2019. The withdrawal is referred to as Brexit, short for “British exit”.

In the light of this event, research on fake news has increased and research methods were developed to better understand and capture the dynamic of online behaviour, especially the behaviour of voters.

Links between fake news and ideological polarisation were clearly observed during the Brexit referendum. Therefore, voters were polarised between those who wanted the UK to leave the EU and those who wanted the UK to remain. 

However, the spread of fake news was not facilitated only by the supporters of one side or the other. After analysing approximately 800,000 tweets associated with the United Kingdom referendum, media analysts uncovered a network of Twitter bots comprised of 13,493 accounts that tweeted about the referendum and used hashtags such as #voteleave, #voteremain, and #brexit. Some organisations have tried to trace the involvement of the Russian government in the creation and spread of these tweets, however, evidence remained scarce. Most of these accounts tweeted or retweeted content that supported the withdrawal of the United Kingdom from the European Union, therefore offering the Leave campaign a larger online reach. This factor could have had an important impact on the outcome of the referendum, as the widespread fake news amplified political communication related to Brexit. Furthermore, many voters who consumed this media content received confirmation bias from it because they viewed information that aligned with their political views.

2016 US presidential election

During the 2016 United States presidential election, the creation and coverage of fake news increased substantially. Fake news stories relating to the event consistently appeared in Google's top news search results and were widely shared on Facebook. As a result, they were taken seriously, and they were even shared by members of Trump’s presidential campaign.

Most fake news stories that people read before and during the election were pro-Donald Trump and were, therefore, extensively shared by his supporters. Many of them tried to discredit Hillary Clinton by directing completely wild accusations against her - such as suggesting she was selling weapons to terrorist organisations or that she was involved in human trafficking of children. After the victory of Donald Trump, several debates were started over the impact of fake news in the election. Analysts tried to establish whether or not the circulation of fake news aided the narrow win of Donald Trump. Debates were also started over alleged interference from the Russian government. Russian-backed organisations, sites, and hackers are thought to have increased the spread of pro-Trump fake news, spread disinformation about Hillary Clinton, and thereby to have altered the results of the election.

Fake news during elections can spread doubt among voters. They might further lead to polarised opinions in the political arena, as misleading claims are likely to give rise to false beliefs. Research also shows that most Americans suspect that fake news has a real impact on political outcomes. In a survey conducted after the 2016 US presidential election, 64% of American adults believed that fabricated news stories cause a great deal of confusion about the basic facts of current issues and events. Out of these, 23% said they themselves have shared fake news, with 14% saying they shared a story they knew was fake at the time and 16% having shared a story they later realised was fake. These statistics show that fake news can be shared both with the purpose of consciously spreading false information, and also due to a lack of proper knowledge about the topic or due to a mistake. It is important to note that the percentage in both cases is close in value.

Furthermore, besides influencing the outcome of elections, fake news can also have an impact on the daily lives of ordinary people. The Pizzagate scandal was spawned by fake news during the 2016 election. This led to the creation of a conspiracy theory that had many believers. One man took it upon himself to investigate the theory and brought a gun into a popular Washington, D.C. pizza restaurant. After reading a fake news article, he was under the impression that the pizzeria was hiding a child prostitution ring. Moreover, the owners and staff of the pizzeria were also harassed and threatened after fake news articles used manipulated content to portray them in incriminating circumstances.

The rise of fake news has also enabled politicians to misuse the term for their own purposes. Because the many fake stories that circulated during the 2016 presidential election served to undermine people's trust in the media, Donald Trump is able to take advantage of this lack of trust to simply label all news stories he does not like 'fake news', instead of actually addressing their concerns.
