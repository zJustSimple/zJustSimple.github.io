Macedonia

Several websites spreading fake news about the 2016 US presidential election were set up by teenagers based in the Republic of Macedonia.

Their task was to rapidly create and plagiarise sensationalist stories for various US-based companies and parties. Their main goal was to generate revenue from the online advertisements that were shown next to the fake news articles. Fake news articles were used as clickbait and the topic was chosen in the hope that it would catch the attention of a large audience and lead them to share the fake news on social media platforms, increasing the amount of internet traffic for the fake news websites and, consequently, also increasing the revenue of the creators.

Therefore, it seems like the main purpose of the fake news creators was not to influence the outcome of the election but to produce income.

India

Much of India's fake news is spread through the smartphone instant messenger application WhatsApp. Messages about natural disasters, conspiracy theories, and health hazards are circulated, sometimes causing mass hysteria among the population.

These stories sometimes lead to tensions between castes and religions. A video of a gang war in Brazil depicting two people being decapitated was spread around Indian social media, with those sharing it falsely claiming that it showed Indian soldiers being killed in Pakistan. In another instance, several fake images said to depict violent attacks against Hindus by Muslim Rohingya people were spread through a tweet. These examples of fake news were intended to instigate hatred and can lead to discrimination.

In 2017, false rumours about child abductors in a village in Jharkhand spread through WhatsApp messages and prompted villagers to attack strangers. Seven people were beaten to death as a result. Locals also clashed with policemen in multiple areas.

Another similar event happened in 2018 in the region of Tripura. Here, fake news about child-lifting and organ trafficking instilled hysteria in the population and led to the death of two people within 48 hours, while six others were injured. A governmental employee was also attacked and killed. As a result, the government decided to suspend all mobile internet services for two days in an attempt to stop the rumours from spreading.

Russia

Russia is believed to use fake news as a means of weaponising information and using it to run false information campaigns to manipulate the public opinion. These allegations have intensified especially after Russia’s annexation of Crimea in 2014. In the same year, Russia also passed two new laws to extend state control over the online sphere.

Russia is suspected to have been involved in the 2016 US presidential election, facilitating the victory of Donald Trump by employing bots and internet trolls to circulate fake news and disinformation about Hillary Clinton. Russian hackers are also rumoured to be behind the cyberattack that made the e-mails of Hillary Clinton's campaign manager public, which in turn triggered the Pizzagate conspiracy. Cyberattacks attributed to Russia were also reported during the 2017 French presidential election and the 2017 German elections. However, the Russian government has continuously denied its involvement in these events and in any other international disinformation campaign.

The news agency Sputnik and the television network Russia Today are widely perceived as facilitating the spread of fake news. They have been frequently described as a propaganda outlet for the Russian government. The government also extends its influence over Internet companies and online platforms through the Federal Service for Supervision of Communications, Information Technology and Mass Media. This is the Russian federal executive body responsible for censorship in media and telecommunications. Its role is to administer Internet censorship filters, supervise personal data processing requirements, monitor the radio frequency service, etc. However, this body is often criticised for its arbitrariness. Besides blacklisting and blocking harmful sites, the federal service has also temporarily blocked Wikipedia pages related to narcotics, an instant messenger application (Telegram), and articles related to radical feminist movements and the LGBT community. The body has also blocked a number of websites criticising Russian policy in Ukraine.

France

Since 2016, France has witnessed an increase in the circulation of fake news.

An increase in disinformation and propaganda was observed especially during the 2017 presidential election. Researchers analysed more than 840,000 tweets by using a combination of hashtags associated with the primary presidential candidates. They have discovered that fake news comprised 21% of all political links shared on Twitter in France. Most content shared on Twitter was about Emmanuel Macron, who ultimately won the election.

Macron’s campaign was targeted by a cyberattack that led to the leak of professional and private e-mails, memos, contracts, and accounting documents of campaign members. Most of these documents were genuine, but fabricated content was also shared alongside it to sow doubt and disinformation about the campaign. During this time, Facebook also suspended 30,000 suspected bot accounts in France.

Myanmar

In Myanmar, the repressive government has employed fake news to prosecute minorities. Investigations led to the conclusion that Facebook may have been a key element in helping incite government-backed lynchings against the Rohingya Muslim minority by facilitating the spread of fake news about them.

A series of posts on Facebook created by the Buddhist majority used derogatory terms about the Muslim minorities and accused them of wrongdoings while also inciting punishment and banishment of Rohingya people. The campaign led to discrimination and mass murder. Rohingya settlements were burned down and many people were killed, while over 100,000 had to flee abroad.

At the time, Facebook did not review the content posted, therefore facilitating its spread. However, Facebook has since deleted accounts of Myanmar military figures accused of inciting violence.
