Historical background

The term “fake news” is relatively new. However, the practice it describes has been frequently encountered over the course of history. Misinformation, rumours, and false records are common, and they can be traced back to antiquity.

One renowned case that can be considered an early example of the fake news phenomenon is that of Mark Antony, a Roman politician and general, and the Egyptian queen Cleopatra VII. During Antony’s Civil War (32 BC - 30 BC), his rival, Octavian, spread misinformation to discredit Antony. Octavian came across a document that he claimed was Antony’s will, naming his children by Cleopatra as Antony’s heirs even though they were not Roman citizens. It is unclear whether this document was genuine, but it created great controversy. Octavian read the incriminating document in front of the Roman Senate, and then, a decree was issued and made available to the common people. The Roman people were outraged by Antony’s will and Octavian spread further rumours about Antony being a puppet of Cleopatra. In this way, Octavian managed to convince the Romans to declare war against Cleopatra.

Fake news became even more common after the invention of the printing press. From the 15th century onwards, publications became widespread but were no journalistic ethics by which to monitor and analyse what was being printed. Therefore, the news was not verified and misinformation and sensationalism were frequently encountered in publications.

Censorship of printed materials was first instated by the Catholic Church who required pre-authorisation of texts for printing to maintain the consistency of church doctrines and to supervise the educational and moral values of the texts. During and after the Protestant Reformation (1517 - 1648), censorship began to be implemented by the owners of printing houses or by the government of both Protestant countries such as England and Catholic countries such as France or Spain. Texts were censored both before and after printing, and after the mid-16th century, printed works had to carry the name of the publisher and the place of publication, while the printing of false news could be punished by imprisonment.

In the 17th century, the press was increasingly used to exploit and manipulate public opinion, especially through the printing of pamphlets. Print was employed by the political class to spread their ideology by manipulating the public. In 1674, King Charles II of England issued A Proclamation to Restrain the Spreading of False News, and Licentious Talking of Matters of State and Government meant to prevent the spread of fake news about the state or the king.

Fake news was also used to spread racist and anti-Semitic messages. As an example, after the American Revolution (1765 - 1783), publications containing false derogatory information about the African-American population were published.

The 18th century saw an increase in freedom of the press. In 1766, Sweden was the first country to introduce a constitutional law where censorship was abolished and the freedom of the press was guaranteed.

During the late 19th century, a new journalistic technique called yellow journalism was developed. The defining characteristics of yellow journalism include the use of faked interviews, misleading headlines, and edited images. A great contributor to this practice was Joseph Pulitzer, an American journalist who owned the New York World newspaper. Pulitzer began to emphasise sensational stories that dealt with crime, scandal, or horror to attract the attention of the readers. His newspaper was one of the first mass-circulation newspapers that depended on advertising revenue, selling hundreds of thousands of copies each day for almost 50 years.

Propaganda fuelled by false information increasingly began to be used during the First World War (1914 - 1918). Anti-German propaganda circulated throughout England and featured hoax stories of “Tallow Factories” where Germans were rumoured to bring human corpses to turn them into fat and use them for creating items such as candles, lubricants, or soap. After the end of the war, the stories proved to be false. However, during the Second World War (1939 - 1945), the fabricated British stories from the First World War made it possible for German Nazi politicians to deny the ongoing massacre of Jewish people, describing it as another false British propaganda story.

After Hitler’s ascent to power in 1933, the Reich Ministry of Public Enlightenment and Propaganda was used to enforce Nazi ideology by taking control over both printed and broadcasted material. During this time, the German press circulated numerous examples of fake news with strong anti-Semitic messages. The purpose of Nazi propaganda was to manipulate the public, gain loyalty for their political party, and to induce race consciousness and a feeling of superiority among the population.

The Soviet Union also used various methods to spread fake news or to alter already existing information. One of the most notorious methods was modifying photographs, similar to the way people do today. Political opponents, disloyal members of the Communist Party, and even fired cosmonauts were erased from pictures as a way of also erasing them from history. Josef Stalin in particular was known to use this practice very often when dealing with political opponents, effectively erasing them from history by modifying both records and photographs. 

Journalistic ethics as a concept did not fully emerge until the 20th century. The first journalistic code of ethical practice was drafted in the United States during the early 1920s. Most European countries also adopted a journalistic code of conduct in the following years. After the end of the Second World War, emphasis was also put on the reestablishment of the freedom of the press.

Textual perspective - Animal Farm by George Orwell
 

Română
Animal Farm by George Orwell
Interpretare text
Animal Farm by George Orwell

George Orwell's novel is an allegory of Stalin's rise to power within the Soviet Union, and contains many references to Soviet propaganda. For example, the dictator Napoleon (who represents Stalin) is often shown to directly manipulate and distort historical facts to his own advantage.
