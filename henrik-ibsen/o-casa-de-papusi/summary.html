O casă de păpuși de Henrik Ibsen
  1. Alte materii
  2. O casă de păpuși de Henrik Ibsen

Summary

The play A Doll's House, written by the Norwegian Henrik Ibsen and published in 1879, is about Nora, the wife of a bank employee. Nora has been married to her husband for eight years, and they have three children together. The three-act play presents a family drama that takes place in the couple's apartment between December 24 and 26.

At the beginning of the plot, the Helmer family and their three children lead a seemingly completely harmonious, happy, middle-class life. After long and difficult years, the father Torvald is to take up a position as manager of a joint-stock bank in the new year, which will bring the family a good living. In view of this positive change, Nora is already in a very joyful mood and wants to spend an especially nice Christmas with her family. Even though her husband criticizes her for all her spending, he too wants it to be a special holiday.

During the preparations for December 24, Christine arrives. She is Nora’s childhood friend, and Nora has not seen her in ten years. Christine married a wealthy man for financial reasons eight years ago . She moved away from the city with him to care for her ill mother and her two younger brothers. 

However, the wealthy man soon died and left Christine nothing. Therefore, after the death of her mother and when her brothers grow up, Christine returns to the city to look for work. As part of this search, she turns to Torvald, who promises her a job as a secretary. To do so, he must force his old university colleague, Nils Krogstad to resign. Krogstad has been shunned by society after a scandal involving forged signatures.

After Christine has left the house, Krogstad appears and confronts Nora. Nora has borrowed from him at that time the high sum of two hundred and fifty pounds (1200 species) without her husband’s knowledge. For this she had to forge the signature of her already deceased father. On the advice of the doctors, she was able to use the money to finance a cure for her husband in Italy. A year later, Torvald, who was seriously ill, has fully recovered. For years, Nora lied to her husband about this and hid the loan from him, as he hates acquiring debts.

Carelessly and naively, Nora tells Krogstad about his approaching resignation. He then reveals to her that he knows she has forged the signature on the promissory note and has thus committed a criminal offense. He now wants to disclose everything to her husband if she does not stand up for him and prevent the approaching dismissal.

In the time that follows, this situation weighs heavily on Nora. She cannot convince her husband not to give notice, and Krogstad then writes a letter explaining Nora's crime. He knows that this leaves Torvald completely at his mercy and expresses his ambition to take over as manager in Torvald’s place in a year's time.

The widowed Christine learns of Krogstad's blackmail of Nora. She first tries to persuade her former lover Krogstad to reclaim the letter before it is read. In the course of the plot, however, she realizes that there must be an open discussion between the two spouses. Christine watches the messy situation with great concern. She also wants to live with Krogstad again and take care of his children, which makes him forget about any resentment towards the Helmer family.

When Torvald reads Krogstad's letter after a masked ball on the evening of December 26, he is horrified and fears for his social and professional position. He does not protect Nora, but is intimidated by the threats and denies her any ability to think and act independently. Likewise, he wants to forbid her from continuing to raise the children. Outwardly, he wants to maintain the appearance of an intact marriage, but he despises his wife at this moment and criticizes her harshly.

When another letter arrives from Krogstad shortly afterwards, in which the lawyer, now appeased by Christine's presence, apologizes to Nora for his behavior and gives her back the promissory note, Torvald's mood turns improves. Seeing himself and his social position saved again, he wants to forgive Nora everything and treats her even more like a little child than before. 

Nora, however, is no longer willing to be treated this way and has realized that her husband does not love her. His lack of commitment and self-centered behavior make it impossible for her to love him anymore. She leaves him that very evening to return to her birthplace and start a new, self-determined, and independent life.

Ibsen's play portrays the role patterns in society at the time in a very vivid way. Like many women at that time in the bourgeois circles, Nora, is nothing more than a mere doll. This attitude was started by her father, when she was a girl, and is continued by her husband now that she has reached adulthood. 

The men in Nora’s life do not allow her to think and act independently at all. For them, she represents a possession and a trophy with which they can do what they like. Nora understands through this conflict that her freedom has always been completely restricted, and she has been treated condescendingly. She breaks with the social conventions of the time and leaves her husband and children that very evening to start a new and self-determined life.
