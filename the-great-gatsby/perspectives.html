Historical perspective

The Great Gatsby is set in the early years of the 1920s. Since Fitzgerald wrote the book just a couple of years after the book’s time setting, the book is closely inspired by Fitzgerald's own time and experiences and so has a particular historical context.

Politics

To better understand the novel’s historical context, it is useful to know that the US experienced significant economic growth after the First World War had ended in 1918, with industry and trade doing well. This gave rise to an era of prosperity known as “The Jazz Age” (a term coined by Fitzgerald) or “The Roaring Twenties”, and many people became very wealthy. This included many former members of the lower and middle classes, and society was quickly changed by this shift in money. Many of the aristocratic families maintained their wealth, but increasingly people from non-traditional backgrounds (like Gatsby) were building large houses and changing the way society was structured.

Politically, there was also a large change in the position of women, who were granted the right to vote across the country in 1920. During the First World War, many women had enjoyed greater freedom and taken up many jobs traditionally done by men. Many continued to pursue this freedom in the 1920s, often seeking financial independence or employment. Notably, in The Great Gatsby, Jordan Baker makes her living as a professional golfer.

In 1929, the Wall Street crash marked an abrupt end to the Jazz Age, plunging the US into economic depression within a short span of time. In a way, The Great Gatsby is almost prophetic with regards to this event, with the novel’s focus on the fragile nature of dreams and the contrast between illusion and reality. In many ways, The Wall Street crash happened precisely because investors were increasingly out of touch with reality - caught up in the optimistic spirit of the times, they eagerly bought stocks and countless companies were eventually valued far beyond their actual value and the actual goods they produced.

Culture

During the 1920s, clothing, architecture and design became more daring. Women's dresses became much shorter than they had previously been, and waistlines were dropped to create a more boyish figure. This was a symbol of the sexual freedom aimed for by many people at this time. Similarly, cars had become a popular and more affordable form of transport, and many of them were created in bright colors and stylish designs. For example, Gatsby's car is a highly recognisable yellow Rolls Royce.

At this time, jazz had become a very popular form of music. This even gave the era its popular name, the Jazz Age. Jazz was an informal type of music originating in African-American communities, characterized by the use of improvisation and a strong sense of rhythm. It made particular use of instruments such as the trumpet, double bass, and piano. It was ideal for the popular dancing that took place at parties such as Gatsby's and night clubs or 'speakeasies'.

Lifestyle

The Jazz Age lifestyle present in The Great Gatsby is to some extent the one that F. Scott Fitzgerald himself lived. It is important to note that this was not the life lived by a majority of people, as it was mainly enjoyed by the very rich.

This lifestyle in The Great Gatsby and the “Jazz Age” was characterized by wild parties, where people danced to jazz music and enjoyed large amounts of extravagant food and drink. Private parties and secret clubs were important in the 1920s since the Prohibition Act was passed in 1919. This law prohibited the sale (but not the consumption) of alcohol, and this set the tone of many social gatherings.

Most of the very rich, especially aristocrats and women, did not work. Instead, they filled their time with social engagements. Frequently, however, their lifestyle meant that they did very little. When Nick enters the Buchanans' house, for example, he finds Daisy and Jordan sitting on the sofa, almost unable to get up after their long inactivity.

Literary perspective

F. Scott Fitzgerald wrote The Great Gatsby at a time when Modernism was the main literary movement. The 1920s was an era which produced many well-known American authors, including Fitzgerald himself, his wife Zelda (who was also a talented author), Sinclair Lewis,  Ezra Pound and Gertrude Stein.

Like authors such as Hemingway and Stein, Fitzgerald's form of modernism involved exploring the “modern condition” of the human being, or what it meant to be alive in the modern age. Like many other American modernists, Fitzgerald spent some time traveling in Europe, among other famous writers such as Ernest Hemingway. However, unlike many modernists such as Hemingway and Pound, Fitzgerald always returned to live in the United States. Because of this, Fitzgerald's novels capture a particularly American way of life.

The form of the novel was being explored by many authors at this time, pushing the boundaries of what could be produced. Modernism in the United States was frequently concerned with the American condition, the psychological problems created by the First World War, and how the self is created through a constructed identity or “mask” with which the individual faces society. All of these are themes explored by Fitzgerald in The Great Gatsby.

Textual perspective

Here you can find suggestions for other texts that deal with similar themes and may be useful to compare to The Great Gatsby. The most obvious choice may be comparison to other fictional texts, but depending on how you are working with the novel, it may also be relevant to bring up non-fiction works such as speeches and interviews. 

Fiction

The Grapes of Wrath by John Steinbeck is another iconic American novel. Despite the wildly different plotlines, it is may be useful to compare it to The Great Gatsby, since it deals with the decade immediately following the luxurious Jazz Age. The Grapes of Wrath thus zooms in on The Great Depression that followed the Wall Street crash of 1929, focusing on the struggles of a family desperately traveling the US in search of work. The vivid descriptions of starvation, disease and other hardships make a stark contrast to Gatsby’s luxurious parties in the previous decade. It is interesting to note that both Fitzgerald and Steinbeck intended their novels to be a strong criticism of the corruption of American society, although they achieved this in very different ways.    

For a more modern perspective, you might wish to take a look at American Psycho by Brett Easton Ellis. This modern-day American classic focuses on describing the emptiness of upper-class life in the US of the late 1980s, specifically the yuppie culture. Patrick Bateman, the main character and narrator, is so disillusioned by his life that he finds excitement through a series of psychotic murders, which are all described in great detail. This feature makes the book much more shocking and grotesque than The Great Gatsby, despite its somewhat similar message about the potential emptiness of upper-class life in a capitalistic consumer society. 

Non-fiction

The idea of the American Dream is still a popular reference point in American politics. Barack Obama typically put an unusually social spin on the idea, focusing on the view that American society ought to be structured in a way that makes it as easy as possible for citizens to achieve the dream. That is one of his main points in this early campaign speech, delivered before he was elected President.

In an 1980 interview, the successful American immigrant Arnold Schwarzenegger offers his extremely optimistic perspective on America and the American dream. He describes the pursuit of success, wealth and fame in very positive terms, suggesting that the Dream is perfectly achievable through hard work, thanks to the wonderful nature of American society.
