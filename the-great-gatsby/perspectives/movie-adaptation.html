Movie adaptation

The Great Gatsby by F. Scott Fitzgerald has been brought to the big screen several times since it was published in 1925. Each director and cast have brought a very different approach to the story and presented Gatsby in very different ways. These adaptations include a silent film, two blockbuster movies and a made-for-TV movie.

In the novel the story is told from the point of view of a first-person narrator. This fact makes the adaptation of the story difficult, as elements of Nick's storytelling are often lost. However, using a movie format can open up other possibilities for the film. For example, it is much easier to jump between times and places in movies, allowing for a more dynamic narrative structure.

The earliest movie version of F. Scott Fitzgerald's The Great Gatsby was made in 1926, only a year after the publication of the novel. It was a silent movie and was based on a stage version of the book. The director, Herbert Brenon, emphasized Gatsby's parties and made less use of character development than in later adaptations. Unfortunately, the film has been lost, but a short trailer for the movie remains. It was reported that when Fitzgerald and his wife Zelda went to see the movie, they left the cinema in protest, believing it did not capture the novel at all.

Later popular adaptations include the 1974 movie starring Robert Redford and Mia Farrow, which was a box-office success but received mixed reviews. This version stays fairly close to the original narrative by Fitzgerald. There was also a made-for-TV movie adaptation made in 2000, which aired both in Britain and in the United States, but which was less successful version.

Baz Luhrmann’s 2013 movie

In 2013, Baz Luhrmann directed Leonardo DiCaprio and Carey Mulligan in a big-budget adaptation of The Great Gatsby. For this movie, Luhrmann offered a 21st-century approach to Fitzgerald's original story, most evident in his choice of contemporary pop music as the film's soundtrack. This offers a unique perspective on the story, but it also obscures much of the subtlety that can be found in the novel. The Great Gatsby is a novel of its age, the 1920s Jazz Era, and to give it a modern soundtrack is to erase a huge part of the musical culture after which the era was named.

Luhrmann also rewrites the story slightly, so that Nick is telling his story to a psychologist in a sanatorium as a recovering alcoholic. This gives the narrative a very different tone, as in the book Nick is calm and collected, giving him a much more detached point of view. Luhrmann's change feels like an easy option to catch the modern audiences, and Nick's subtlety as a narrator is lost because of it.

However, despite these changes from the book, Luhrmann's film is highly colorful and captures some of the wildness of the 1920s. Leonardo DiCaprio gives a particularly good performance as Gatsby, managing to create a character who is both highly complex and childishly innocent at the same time.
