Physical setting

A dramatic setting at night

Shakespeare’s play Macbeth was written in the Elizabethan era around 1506 but is set in Scotland in the 11th century in the Middle Ages (make sure you do not confuse those two historical contexts). Scotland in the Middle Ages is depicted as being a rather wild and dangerous place. The bloody battle description we get in Act 1, Scene 2 of the play suggests that war was common and that to be successful men had to be strong, brave and well-respected in order to survive.

The setting and structure of the scenes in Macbeth contribute to the narrative technique of the play. The scenes take place either outdoors, typically on desolate heaths or battlefields, or indoors, typically within claustrophobic castle walls. In the first part of the final act, Act 5, Shakespeare cuts - almost like a modern action film - between the setting of the castle of Dunsinane, where a depressed Macbeth is preparing for his final battle, and the fields surrounding the castle, where Malcolm’s army is advancing. This technique creates a fastpaced struggle (which reflects that of the battlefield outside) and the heroic English army heading for Macbeth’s castle to restore order in Scotland.

Also, many of the scenes in the play are set at night, for instance the murder of Duncan, the murder of Banquo, and the banquet scene with Banquo’s ghost. This contributes to the gloomy atmosphere of evil which generally runs throughout the play. In Act 1, Scene 5, Lady Macbeth actively addresses the night, asking it to conceal the coming murder of Duncan:

Come, thick night,
And pall thee in the dunnest smoke of hell,
That my keen knife see not the would it makes.
Nor heaven peep through the blanket of the dark,
To cry, ‘Hold, hold.’ (1.5.48-52)

Thus, the night is personified as a force in itself. Macbeth does the same in Act 3, Scene 2 when he is preparing to have Banquo murdered (3.2.52-59). This suggests that the setting is crucial to understanding the play.

We sense this atmosphere of evil from the very first scene of the play where we meet the three witches. Their unappealing physical appearances, the strange way they speak, and the stormy weather which accompanies them are all indications that these are dangerous and unnatural creatures. This does not bode well for the story we are about to follow.

The state of the universe is reflected by the setting and unnatural bodies

After the murder of King Duncan in Act 2, Scene 2, the physical setting directly reflects the state of the world order. The Elizabethans believed in the Chain of Being - a strict hierarchy where everything in the universe had its place. (We explain this in our Background information). If you upset the balance of a kingdom, for instance by killing a king, you would upset the entire balance of the universe. Killing a king - regicide - was the worst possible crime to the Elizabethans. In Macbeth, we see nature itself reacting violently to Duncan’s murder. In Act 2, Scene 3, Lennox lists the many strange events of the night of the murder:

The night has been unruly: where we lay,
Our chimneys were blown down; and, as they say,
Lamentings heard i’ the air; strange screams of death, [...]
the obscure bird
Clamour’d the livelong night: some say, the earth
Was feverous and did shake. (2.23.64-71)

The noisy bird he is referring to here is the night owl, which was associated with death. In Act 2, Scene 4, we get another dramatic description of how daylight has not come and how Duncan’s horses are said to eat one another (2.4.7-21). These changes in the physical setting support the message that regicide is morally wrong and will have enormous consequences.

The Elizabethans believed that the consequences for the macrocosm of society are mirrored in the microcosm of the individual. For example, Macbeth is full of people who are not doing well and whose bodies are behaving unnaturally. Already in Act 1, Scene 2 we meet a bleeding sergeant whose blood is outside of his body although, naturally, it ought to be inside it. Duncan voices the wrongness of this by asking: “What bloody man is that?” (1.2.1). The play has other scenes where people have blood outside of their bodies such as Macbeth and his wife whose hands are stained with Duncan’s blood (Act 2, Scene 2) or the murderer with Banquo’s blood on his face (Act 2, Scene 4).

Other examples of unnatural bodies are the witches whose bodies ought to be female but “your beards forbid me to interpret/ That you are so,” as a confused Banquo puts it (1.3.47-48). The same goes for Lady Macbeth when she asks evil spirits to possess her body and remove female characteristics like remorse and babies’ milk (Act 1, Scene 5). When she proceeds to dominate her soldier husband into killing Duncan, she seems to be even more unnatural according to Elizabethan gender roles.

Yet another unnatural body in the play is Banquo’s ghost (the dead refuse to remain dead, it seems). Finally, we have Macbeth’s inability to sleep after the murder of Duncan (2.2.46-55) and Lady Macbeth’s sleepwalking (Act 5, Scene 1). In addition, we learn that Lady Macbeth has grown afraid of the dark as “she has light by her/ continually; ‘tis her command.” (5.1.20-21). It seems her guilty conscience has finally caught up with her. Just like the setting, the many unnatural bodies in the play reflect the idea of the Chain of Being: if you disturb the balance, chaos breaks out. There are even more examples of rebellious bodies in Macbeth that you may want to search for yourself.

Social setting

Shakespeare’s Macbeth is set in the Scottish upper class where almost everyone we meet are noblemen or royalty. Thus, the play focuses on the power struggles and codes of conduct of the medieval upper class - and how disturbances here affect every class in society. The only characters that do not belong to the upper class are the witches and minor characters like the porter (Act 2, Scene 3), the old man (Act 2, scene 4), or the doctor and the gentlewoman (Act 5, Scene 1), and their status is typically mirrored by their language. Read more about this in our Language section.

It is a setting highly influenced by Christian values and beliefs. This is partly because religion was an essential part of the Elizabethan context in which Macbeth was written, and partly because the biblical theme of good and evil goes nicely with a story of a man who kills his king. A specific example is Macbeth’s fear of the consequences of killing Duncan; as a Christian, he knows that he will be punished as a sinner at Judgement Day. Another example is when Lady Macbeth encourages her husband to “look like the innocent flower,/ But be the serpent under’t” (1.5.73-74). The image of the snake is a symbol of treacherous danger because it is a reference to the biblical Fall of Man, where innocent obedience to God was replaced with guilty disobedience through the trickery of the serpent in the Garden of Eden. A third example is how the dead Duncan’s body is compared to “The Lord’s anointed temple” (2.3.80). A king was believed to have been placed on the throne by God, so killing him was a serious sin.

To a minor extent, gender roles are also relevant to the social setting of Macbeth. In the first part of the play, Lady Macbeth’s behaviour does not match that of a medieval or Elizabethan woman who both ought to be pious, feminine, and subjected to her husband. Instead, Lady Macbeth invokes evil spirits to give her more masculine characteristics so that she may assist in the murder of the King, and she bosses around her weak husband. Eventually her husband takes charge, shuts her out, and she kills herself - another sin, according to the Christian world picture. Read more about how Shakespeare plays with ideas of masculinity and femininity in our Themes section.
