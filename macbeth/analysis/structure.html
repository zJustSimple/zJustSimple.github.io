General structure of Macbeth

Shakespeare’s Macbeth consists of five acts, in keeping with the structure of most like classical dramas. However, the number of scenes per act in this play vary quite a lot, as does the length of each scene. Macbeth is one of Shakespeare’s shortest plays and it is very fast-paced, which can partly be explained by the fact that there is no subplot: there is only the main plot about Macbeth’s desire to be king. This structure is typical of classical tragedies, too.

Macbeth has a two-part structure: the first part is about crime, the second is about consequence. What Macbeth does in the first part of the play returns to haunt him in the second. This may be linked to one of the key elements of the play: the world is turned upside down and “foul” has become “fair” (and vice versa). Life is not black and white, and what may seem desirable like becoming king, may turn out to have catastrophic consequences.

Dramatic structure of Macbeth

The play can be analysed according to what is called Freytag's pyramid. This diagram divides a plot into five parts: exposition, rising action, climax, falling action, and resolution.

 

Act 1: Exposition

Macbeth starts in medias res in Act 1, Scene 1 when we meet the three witches who are in the middle of a conversation about a battle. The strange words and appearance of the witches, combined with the violent storm which accompanies them, catch our attention from the start and create a sense of suspense. What are these creatures up to? What is the battle they talk of? And who is Macbeth?

As we move into the following scenes of Act 1, we are introduced to the central characters, time, place, and key conflicts of Macbeth. We learn that Macbeth is a brave warrior and Duncan a gracious king. We also hear the witches’ prophecies for Macbeth and Banquo and realise that Macbeth and his encouraging wife are plotting to kill the King, despite Macbeth’s inner conflict. This, too, builds tension. King Duncan’s comment on never having suspected Cawdor of treason foreshadows how Macbeth will soon exploit that naive trust, just like Cawdor did.

Act 2: Rising action

We sense the action and tension of the play rising as the trusting King Duncan is brutally murdered in his sleep, his two sons flee under suspicious circumstances, and nature itself seems to rebel against the horror of the murder of a king. Macbeth is crowned as king, just like the prophecy promised, and he now has ultimate power. The murder of the King is Macbeth’s point of no return, and from now on he has to go on killing to avoid the consequences of this one murder.

In addition, we get to know the central characters better. For example, we see the moral side of Macbeth. This side of him imagined a bloody dagger floating in the air before Duncan’s murder and makes him horrified at what he has done after the murder. The vision of the dagger also foreshadows the murder of the King, as well as Macbeth’s later psychological breakdown.

Act 3: Climax

Tension culminates as Macbeth decides to have Banquo killed to prevent Banquo’s part of the prophecy from coming true. However, Banquo’s ghost comes back to haunt him on the night of the banquet when Macbeth and his wife intended to celebrate their reign. The other thanes (noblemen) have so far accepted Macbeth’s ascent to the throne, but now they see a different and more vulnerable side of him. The banquet scene becomes a turning point in the play and marks Macbeth’s fall from well-respected, loyal thane to hated tyrant king. Ironically, it is when Macbeth is at his lowest morally that he becomes his greatest by becoming king.

Act 4: Falling action

The consequences of Macbeth’s actions now play out: He consults the witches for the second time, he has Macduff’s family murdered, and he prepares for war. Also, Macbeth and his wife seem to be drifting apart.

We sense the action and tension of the play falling, especially when the second round of prophecies enigmatically predict Macbeth’s defeat. Macbeth himself does not realise yet; as the tragic hero, he is flawed and blinded by hubris, which means that he is overly confident and arrogant. We realise that Macbeth is probably doomed to fail, however, which is one of many examples of dramatic irony in the play. Although tension is slowing, we are still kept in suspense because we do not know how Macbeth will meet his tragic end.

Act 5: Resolution

This part is also referred to as denouement.

The final act takes us through the moral and mental breakdown of Macbeth and his wife. Lady Macbeth commits suicide, as her conscience finally seems to have caught up with her. Macbeth loses hope of survival when Malcolm’s army attacks under the disguise of branches from Birnam Wood, and he learns that Macduff is the man who was “not of woman born”, whom he discovers is destined to kill him. However, Macbeth decides to die fighting. Our feeling of suspense gradually shrinks as the apparitions’ predictions come true one after another.

How do we interpret the ending?

Order in Scotland is restored as Macduff kills Macbeth and Malcolm heads for Scone to be crowned as king. Traditionally, this has been read as a closed and relatively happy ending. However, you might argue that we leave the play with an ominous feeling that we have been here before and that history may repeat itself. Part of the reason is that the ending has many parallels to the beginning: Malcolm relies on Macduff to kill the treacherous Macbeth, just as Duncan relied on Macbeth to kill Cawdor. Macduff kills a properly crowned king when he kills Macbeth, just as Macbeth did when he killed Duncan. Macbeth’s head is fixed on a pike, just as Cawdor’s was fixed on the battlements. Also, when the thanes hail Malcolm’s future reign as king (Act 5, Scene 9), it reminds us of how the witches hailed Macbeth as future thane of Cawdor and king (Act 1, Scene 3).

On top of this, you may ask, where on earth is Fleance, Banquo’s son who escaped? His absence in the final scene seems to indicate that the witches’ prophecy of Banquo fathering a line of kings has not yet come true. For now, Malcolm is king, but the resolution of the prophecy about Banquo seems unfinished.

Our feeling of unease in the ending may also have to do with the fact that the characters in Macbeth struggle to get closure. For instance, Macbeth and his wife cannot finish Duncan’s murder; Lady Macbeth needs to go back with the daggers, and the thought of Duncan’s blood on their hands continues to haunt them. Banquo defies death; he returns as a ghost, and he (supposedly) lives on in a line of future kings. Both Macbeth and his wife struggle with sleep; Macbeth does not sleep at all, and Lady Macbeth sleepwalks and cannot stop her activity even in sleep.

The lack of closure is mirrored in the frequent use of the word “done” in the play. When considering killing Duncan, Macbeth wishes that “it were done when ‘tis done” (1.7.1), meaning that he is aware that it will not end with the murder of Duncan. There will be consequences. This is a play where things seem to go on and on in a sort of circular structure, which supports the feeling that the ending of Macbeth may be an ominously open ending.

You might also question where our sympathy lies in the final scenes. We have suffered together with Macbeth throughout five acts and cannot help but respect him and feel sorry for his tragic end, despite his flaws. When witnessing his downfall, we go through a whole range of emotions which results in a sense of relief for us as order appears to be restored in the story. This is also what is called “catharsis” in classical drama.

When the new king, Malcolm, merely refers to Macbeth as “this dead butcher” (5.9.41), it is hard not to object to this description. Macbeth was so much more than that. And yet at the same time, the play’s end comes with the understanding that Macbeth became deluded and was willing to commit murder to secure his aims, killing his king and even another man’s wife and children.

You might also ask yourself how you feel about the ending? You might also want to watch various film or theatre adaptations of Macbeth to see how different directors have portrayed the ending.
