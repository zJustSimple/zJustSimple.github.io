Banquo

We hear of Banquo before we meet him in Shakespeare’s Macbeth. In Act 1, Scene 2, a wounded sergeant describes how Macbeth and his fellow general Banquo fought bravely for King Duncan “as cannons overcharged with double cracks” (1.2.41), meaning they fought twice as hard as the enemy. This tells us that Banquo’s main qualities are his bravery and loyalty to king and country. This mirrors the description we get of Macbeth in the first scenes. Both Macbeth and Banquo are Duncan’s generals, brave soldiers, and seemingly friends with one another.

However, in Act 1, Scene 3 we start seeing the differences between Banquo and Macbeth. While Macbeth reacts with shock and conviction upon hearing the witches’ predictions, Banquo is much more practical and sceptical. He scolds the witches when they first only deliver a prophecy for his friend, which shows that he is not afraid of supernatural creatures. This fits the idea of a soldier being a rational, down-to-earth type of person. When Banquo has heard his own prophecy (that he will father kings), he is wary: “And oftentimes, to win us to our harm,/ The instruments of darkness tell us truths,/ Win us with honest trifles, to betray’s/ In deepest consequence.” (1.3.132-135). In these lines, he is warning Macbeth about trusting supernatural creatures since they might trick you, and his words therefore foreshadow Macbeth’s tragic end. Banquo’s words here also tell us that unlike Macbeth, Banquo does not let his imagination run away with him.

However, despite his scepticism towards the witches, you could argue that he is too trusting towards Macbeth. Banquo does not seem to realise that if both his own and Macbeth’s prophecies come true, Banquo and his heirs will be in danger. Macbeth has been promised that he will become king, and Banquo that he will father future kings. This would make it logical for Macbeth to want to remove Banquo and his line of heirs so that Macbeth will be the one fathering future kings. You could argue that this is the downside of not being imaginative: at this point, Banquo simply cannot imagine that his friend would turn on him.

Banquo remains loyal to King Duncan throughout. In Act 2, Scene 1, right before the murder of Duncan, Macbeth hints that Banquo would do well to support him in the future. Still, Banquo does not seem to suspect any foul play and answers: “So I lose none/ In seeking to augment it, but still keep/ My bosom franchis’d and allegiance clear,/ I shall be counsell’d.” (2.1.33-36). He is saying that he will support Macbeth as long as it does not clash with his sworn loyalty towards King Duncan.

However, once Duncan has been murdered and Macbeth crowned as king, Banquo becomes nervous, which is clear from his soliloquy: “Thou hast it now: king, Cawdor, Glamis, all./ As the weird women promised, and I fear,/ Thou play’dst most foully for’t.” (3.1.1-3). However, he does seem to be optimistically aware that his own part of the prophecy may come true too as he says of the witches: “May they not be my oracles as well”? (3.1.9). He seems to decide to give his loyalty to the new king, Macbeth, in the hope that all will end well. But Macbeth has him murdered before long. Banquo’s son Fleance escapes, though, which indicates that Banquo may indeed father a line of kings.

Banquo has two main functions in the play. First, he serves as a contrast to Macbeth, particularly in the sense that he is more loyal than his usurping friend. Second, he serves as Shakespeare’s flattery of King James. King James I had ultimate power and was also the financial supporter of Shakespeare’s acting company. As mentioned in our Background information, Shakespeare would have been very interested in pleasing King James - particularly because Macbeth is a play about a Scottish nobleman usurping the throne, and James I was a Scottish king who had just become King of England.

In Shakespeare’s day, Banquo was believed to have been a real, historical person who founded the Scottish Stuart dynasty of kings. James I believed himself to be a descendant of Banquo, which would help legitimise his claim to the throne. Shakespeare has his play support this theory when he makes Macbeth see Banquo’s ghost holding a mirror portraying many future kings to come in Act 4, Scene 1. Another sign of flattery of the King is that Shakespeare has turned Banquo into a good and loyal person - despite the fact that historically Banquo was believed to have been Macbeth’s accomplice.

So, the character of Banquo both has an internal, dramatic function within the play itself and an external function of pleasing Shakespeare’s financial supporter and king.
