Learning to speak Shakespeare

Shakespeare is renowned for his language. It may seem difficult at first, but once you crack the code, you will discover the richness of it. Shakespeare is particularly fond of images and double-meanings, which demands that his audience or readers stay on their toes.

Another characteristic of the language in Shakespeare’s works is that it is used as a marker of both status and state of mind for the characters. This means that you will be able to make some important analytical points about the play by understanding how each character speaks. In the following, we take you through the metre, tone, and use of pronouns and verbs in Macbeth.

Metre: prose and verse

The style of language in Shakespeare’s plays is a mixture of prose and verse. Prose is what we would call ordinary language, whereas verse is characterised by a specific metrical rhythm (although you should note that the metre in Macbeth is often fairly irregular). Sometimes verse includes rhyme. It is important to understand the importance of language in Shakespeare: how a character speaks reflects the character’s place in the social hierarchy as well as the character’s current state of mind.

Prose is generally a sign of low social status, which is why we see non-nobles or commoners speak this way. It may also be used for comical purposes. For example, in Act 2, Scene 3, the hung-over working-class porter speaks using prose and dark humour. We also typically find prose in letters: Macbeth’s letter to his wife in Act 1, Scene 5 is one example. Finally, prose may be a sign of madness, which is the reason we see Lady Macbeth using fragmented and incoherent prose when her guilty conscience makes her sleepwalk (Act 5, Scene 1). Up until now, she has been speaking beautifully in upper-class blank verse (defined below), so this change is important: her mental breakdown is mirrored in the breakdown of her language.

Verse signals high social status. Macbeth is a play about the Scottish upper class and most of the characters are noblemen or royalty, which is why almost all the characters speak in verse. Verse is also employed for more sophisticated purposes like emotional, lyrical passages. There are many kinds of verse, but here we focus on three types: iambic pentameter (or blank verse), trochaic tetrameter, and rhymed couplets.

Iambic pentameter is a line of verse written with a five-beat rhythm where an unstressed syllable is followed by a stressed syllable. It is also called blank verse and echoes the natural metrical pattern, or sound, of English. All upper-class characters speak this way. For instance, Banquo speaks in iambic pentameter when he describes the witches: “The earth has bubbles, as the water has.” (1.3.81). As you can see, this line has five iambs, as they are called, and the rhythm moves from unstressed to stressed. Banquo’s use of iambic pentameter informs us of his position in the social hierarchy: he is a nobleman.

Most of the scenes in Macbeth are written in iambic pentameter, which again indicates that the characters generally belong to the upper class. Note that you will sometimes see characters completing each other’s verse to make up a unit of, say, five feet. Most book versions of the play today indicate this via the placement of lines on the page.

Tetrameter, or trochaic tetrameter, is a four-beat rhythm where a stressed syllable is followed by an unstressed one. It is mainly supernatural creatures that speak this way, and in Macbeth we see the witches sometimes using tetrameter: “When the battle’s lost and won.” (1.1.1-2). As you can see, there are four trochees in this line, and the rhythm moves from stressed to unstressed - the opposite of iambic pentameter. This rhythm does not generally echo the sound of ordinary English, which makes it seem more dramatic and strange.

Often tetrameter is accompanied by rhyme. Rhyme in general has many functions and may be used by very different types of characters. Most importantly, it has the function of bringing our attention to what is being said. Often, we find rhyme in important passages in a play. Iambic pentameter does not normally rhyme, but when Macbeth decides to go out and die fighting towards the end of the play, he starts using end rhyme while still speaking in iambic pentameter:

I gin to be aweary of the sun,
And wish the estate o’ the world were now undone.
Ring the alarum-bell! Blow, wind! come, wrack!
At least we’ll die with harness on our back.” (5.5.54-57)

This is a man who has lost all hope, and yet he decides to fight valiantly and die like a man. His sudden use of rhyme shows us his current mental state and makes his speech more dramatic and emotional, which stirs our emotions.

Rhyme may also have the more practical function of marking the exit of an important character or the end of a scene. This has to do with the fact that there was no curtain that could be drawn in Elizabethan theatre, so rhyme became a way of signalling a change of scene to the audience.

Rhyme may also be used to indicate something supernatural, which is why we see the witches frequently speaking in rhyme in Macbeth. Often, their use of end rhyme is coupled with alliteration (the use of adjacent words beginning with the same letter; another type of rhyme), as well as tetrameter, like in these lines: “Fair is foul, and foul is fair./ Hover through the fog and filthy air.” (1.1.12-13). The witches’ language varies a lot throughout the play since they both use prose, tetrameter, and sometimes even something called iambic tetrameter, which we will not go into. They tend to rhyme, except when speaking in prose. Thus, their language is unpredictable - just like they are. It shows us how they do not obey the norms of the Chain of Being.

Finally, Shakespeare also uses couplets. A couplet (pronounced “cup-let” in English) is a unit of two lines, which typically use the same type of metre and rhyme. Sometimes a couplet is part of a larger group of lines, and sometimes the couplet is separated from the other lines. In the examples above, Macbeth and the witches are actually speaking in rhymed couplets. You may find couplets in iambic pentameter as well as in tetrameter, and sometimes they rhyme.

Confused? It is all a matter of practice. Whether you speak of “rhymed couplets” or just “rhyme” when analysing Macbeth does not matter much; the important thing is that you can spot the use of rhyme (end rhyme) and explain the effect of it. The same goes for iambic pentameter and tetrameter; you should be able to tell them apart and remember which type of characters typically speak that way.

The sinister tone of the play

Macbeth is one of Shakespeare’s darkest plays, and an atmosphere of looming danger and evil runs throughout most of the scenes. This tone is struck already in Act 1, Scene 1 when the play opens with a violent storm and three witches that speak of a battle. Later we learn that the country has been attacked by a rebel army as well as the King of Norway, which tells us that this is a dangerous time in Scotland because order is threatened.

As we read on and learn about the prophecy and Duncan’s murder, our feeling of unease grows into a sense of foreboding; this simply cannot end well. Towards the end of the play we feel the same resigned pessimism as Macbeth when he compares a man’s life to a briefly burning candle (9.5.21-30) shortly before his death.

Several elements in the play contribute to the gloomy tone. The story opens and ends with a violent battle (the fight against the rebels and the attack on Macbeth’s castle by the English army) and in between we have a series of gruesome murders. We also have elements such as sleepwalking, night scenes, supernatural creatures, ghosts, and predictions. The sense of danger and darkness is even visible in language itself via the frequent use of words like “darkness”, “night”, “death”, and “blood”.

Personal pronouns and verb forms

You have probably noticed how Macbeth includes strange-looking pronouns like “thou”, “thee”, “thine”, and “thy”. While this is partly due to the age of the language, the characters’ use of pronouns also holds vital information about how close the characters are or about their social status. In this paragraph, we break it down to you.

The personal pronoun “you” is used by inferiors to superiors, such as children to parents or servants to masters, to show respect. Upper-class people typically use this pronoun with one another, even when they are closely related. As a contrast, the personal pronoun “thou” is used between working-class people and is also the way superiors address them. Thus, “thou” either reflects intimacy or difference in social status between the characters, but it may also be used to show a lack of respect. Here are some examples for you:

In Act 1, Scene 4, Macbeth respectfully addresses King Duncan with “you” whereas Duncan replies with “thou”. Since he is the King, he does not need to demonstrate humility, unlike Macbeth.

In Macbeth’s letter for his wife he addresses her thus: “This have I thought good to deliver/ thee, my dearest partner of greatness, that thou/ mightst not lose the dues of rejoicing.” (1.5.10-12). His use of “thou”, combined with the fact that he chooses to confide in her in the first place, show how close they are. He loves her and trusts her judgment. Lady Macbeth mirrors this intimacy in her soliloquy on Macbeth’s gentle nature in the following lines of the scene where she also uses “thou”.

Later, when Macbeth gets cold feet about killing Duncan, his wife mocks him for his weakness. She starts off using “thou” but soon switches to “you”: “When you durst do it, then you were a man.” (1.7.54). This may be a sign that she is distancing herself from him; she is disappointed in him. Thus, the change in pronouns here reflects a change in their relationship.

Also, kings make use of what is called “the royal plural”. Duncan continually refers to himself as “we”, “our”, and “us”, and Macbeth and Malcolm start doing the same when they in turn become king. The use of the plural pronoun signals the high position of a ruler.

Finally, you have probably also noticed that Macbeth often includes unfamiliar verb forms. In the second person singular “-(e)st” is added to the stem of the verb, although it becomes “art” when the verb is “to be”: “Thou wouldst be great;/ Art not without ambition.” (1.5.18-19) In the third person singular “-(e)th” is added: “He can report,/ As seemeth by his plight, of the revolt/ The newest state.” (1.2.1-2). This reflects the grammar rules of the Elizabethans.
