What we may learn from a tragedy

We can extract several messages from Shakespeare's Macbeth. The play is a tragedy, and therefore we know from the start that this will end badly. In this way, the play is instructive about how we should go about certain things in life, such as our own ambition or ability to recognise our own faults. In the paragraphs below, we outline some of these instructions, or messages.

It should perhaps be added that Shakespeare obviously sought to entertain as well as educate his audience. The fact that his plays do both at the same time adds to their popularity.

Power corrupts

You may know the saying that “power corrupts, but absolute power corrupts absolutely”. This fits well with Shakespeare’s Macbeth. Macbeth starts out as a loyal thane, but this position is not enough for him. On the contrary, it only seems to have sparked his ambition for more. When he gets the chance, he seizes absolute power and becomes King of Scotland. The problem is that he did not earn the crown the right way, and this starts eating away at his conscience until he imagines seeing floating daggers and the ghost of Banquo.

After the murder of loyal Banquo, people around Macbeth start questioning his right as well as ability to rule, which provokes him into further paranoia and a spiral of violence and tyranny. Towards the end of the play, he is hated by everyone and has lost his wife and the will to live.

The last description that others make of Macbeth in the play is “this dead butcher” (5.9.41); when we first heard of him, he was “brave Macbeth” (1.2.18). His downfall is complete and absolute, and we get the feeling that Shakespeare is warning his audience of the dangers of being overly ambitious.

No action without consequence

The play may roughly be divided into two parts: the first part is about the Macbeths’ decision and action of murdering the King, and the second part is about their increasingly desperate attempts to avoid the consequences of that monstrous murder. The tragedy comes from the fact that Macbeth knew this would happen. Already before the murder, he speaks of “bloody instructions, which, being taught, return/ To plague the inventor.” (1.7.9-19). What he is talking about here is basically karma (to use a modern phrase unknown by Shakespeare!). He knows you cannot kill your king without consequence, either in this life or the next.

Also, after the murders of Duncan and Banquo, Macbeth concludes that “blood will have blood” (3.4.144); he is realising how one murder has led to another.

Once this spiral of death has begun, Macbeth seems unable to stop. All actions have consequences, but some actions - such as regicide - have much greater consequences than others. This message goes nicely with the fact that King James I was the financial supporter of Shakespeare’s acting company: James would of course fully agree with Shakespeare that regicide is a horrible act.

Our faults make us blind

As with any tragedy, we follow the hero through various struggles until his tragic downfall, which is brought on partly by fate and partly by a tragic flaw within his character. Macbeth’s flaw is ambition, and he tragically fails to see it because of hubris - human arrogance which angers God. Whereas he is aware of the fact that the regicide will have consequences, he fails to recognise how arrogant he is to think that he can control fate and go against the natural order of things. He constantly demonstrates that he distrusts fate and takes matters into his own hands, such as killing Duncan himself.

You might argue that this behaviour is wrong of Macbeth in two ways: first, he has no right to kill his king, and second, he could have just waited for fate to deliver the crown to him, for instance through Duncan and his heirs dying by someone else’s hand. Instead, Macbeth becomes overly confident.

Yet, we pity him. Macbeth is not an out-and-out villain; Shakespeare has given him a moral sensitivity that makes it easy to relate to him and his dreams and fears. In many ways, Macbeth brought on his downfall himself, but he was influenced by his wife as well as strong supernatural powers, and his end is almost too humiliating for a great man like him. Therefore, we are able to feel some sympathy for him. We might not have considered killing a king ourselves, but anyone can relate to wanting something that we know we should not have.
