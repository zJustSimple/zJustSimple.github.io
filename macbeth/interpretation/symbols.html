Blood

One of the key symbols in Shakespeare's Macbeth is blood. Indeed, one of the very first characters we meet in the play is a bleeding sergeant (1.2.1-9). The words “blood”, “bloody”, and “bloodier” are used several times throughout the play. Most people associate blood with death or danger, and this is true of Macbeth as well. However, more than anything blood symbolises guilt in Shakespeare’s play.

Right after Duncan’s murder, the hands of both Macbeth and his wife are stained with Duncan’s blood. While Macbeth is horrified and feels like no amount of water will be able to clean his hands (and soul), Lady Macbeth is less troubled and taunts his lack of courage: “My hands are of your colour; but I shame/ To wear a heart so white. [...] A little water clears us of this deed.” (2.2.79-83).

Ironically, Lady Macbeth ends up echoing Macbeth’s words about blood in her sleepwalking scene when guilt seems to have caught up with her: “Here’s the smell of blood still: all the/ perfumes of Arabia will not sweeten this little/ hand. Oh, oh, oh!” (5.1.45-47). No amount of perfume will remove her sin. Shakespeare underlines how you cannot escape your guilt or the consequences of your actions when he has Lady Macbeth repeat Macbeth’s words here.

After the murder of Banquo, Macbeth concludes two important things about blood: First, that “blood will have blood” (3.4.144), meaning that one murder will lead to another. Second, that “I am in blood/ Stepp’d so far that, should I wade no more,/ Returning were as tedious as go o’er.” (3.4.159-161), meaning that he has already come too far, so there is no turning back. These two conclusions can be related to the play as a whole. Macbeth is the story of a man who goes too far by killing his king and who has to keep killing - thereby adding to his guilt - to try to avoid the consequences of that murder.

To a lesser extent, blood also symbolises the right of inheritance or royal blood. The play deals with the right of kinghood and usurpers. In connection with this, you may want to check out our background information on King James I's claim to the English throne or our characterisation of Duncan which discusses who has the biggest claim to the throne in Macbeth.

Clothing

Another central image is the symbol of clothing. The play has several references to ill-fitting clothing, which symbolises Macbeth’s unsuitability as a king. When Macbeth is greeted as thane of Cawdor, he does not know that the previous Cawdor has been stripped of his title and therefore reacts with surprise: “The thane of Cawdor lives: why do you dress me/ In borrow’d robes?” (1.3.113-114). Macbeth deserves the title of Cawdor because he has fought valiantly for his king, but his action of killing King Duncan to get the royal title is not well-deserved, and this is shown by the many symbols of ill-fitting garments.

As Macbeth’s power declines towards the end of the play, Angus remarks that “now does he feel his title/ Hang loose about him, like a giant’s robe/ Upon a dwarfish thief.” (5.2.23-25). The image here is that Macbeth trying to rule instead of Duncan is like a dwarf wearing a giant’s clothes; he looks ridiculous and as if he stole the clothes.

The recurring symbol of clothing tells us two things: that others believe Macbeth is unsuited to be king, and that Macbeth feels uneasy being king. You might add that, being a soldier, it is really in his armour he feels most comfortable. This is seen in the final scenes where he decides to enter the battlefield and die like a man; this is really when he finds himself again.

Sleep

Several scenes in Macbeth take place at night and some of the major events are connected to sleep: Duncan is murdered in his sleep (Act 2, Scene 2), which makes his death an even greater crime, and Lady Macbeth can only admit her guilt when she is sleepwalking (Act 5, Scene 1).

Sleep has two meanings in the play. First, it symbolises the loss of innocence because of guilt. Macbeth fears that he will never be able to sleep again after having committed the crime of regicide. Right after the murder, he thinks he hears a voice in the night saying: “ ‘Sleep no more!/ Macbeth does murder sleep’.” (2.2.46-47). Whereas Macbeth has trouble sleeping because of a guilty conscience, his wife has no problems of the kind - until Act 5 when we learn that she has started sleepwalking and is afraid of the dark.

Second, sleep symbolises naturalness, which means that sleepwalking or being unable to sleep is unnatural. This can be linked to the Elizabethans’ love of order; anything unnatural was closely related to chaos and therefore bad. Thus, Macbeth and his wife’s inability to sleep reflect their personal state of chaos as well as the universal state of chaos that Scotland is now in.

The weather

Macbeth is full of bad weather like storms that blow down chimneys, and the weather generally symbolises Scotland’s state of chaos after Duncan’s murder. This is explained in full in our section on setting.
