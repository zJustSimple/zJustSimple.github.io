Victor Frankenstein

Table of contents
  • Outer characterization

Outer characterization

Victor Frankenstein is the main character in Mary Shelley’s Frankenstein. The novel is even named after him. When readers first meet him, he has just been rescued from the Arctic sea ice. Captain Robert Walton describes how “his limbs were nearly frozen, and his body dreadfully emaciated by fatigue and suffering” (p.26). Walton later states: 

I never saw a more interesting creature; his eyes have generally an expression of wildness, and even madness, but there are moments when, if any one performs an act of kindness towards him […], his whole countenance is lighted up, as it were, with a beam of benevolence and sweetness that I never saw equaled. (p. 27)

This immediately suggests Frankenstein’s sympathetic and unsympathetic qualities. It also tells us that he has gone through many difficulties. 

When Frankenstein begins his narrative, he quickly explains that he is from Geneva, Switzerland, and that he was born to a wealthy and loving family. He is 17 when his mother dies and he goes to university in Ingolstadt. Readers do not learn much about Frankenstein’s physical appearance, but we learn that he is constantly falling ill. He explains early on that his obsession with creating life causes him bodily illness: “My cheek had grown pale with study, and my person had become emaciated with confinement.” (p. 55)

Inner characterization

Two of Victor Frankenstein’s key characteristics are his desire for knowledge and his ambition. He discovers a love for learning at an early age: 

The world was to me a secret which I desired to divine. Curiosity, earnest research to learn the hidden laws of nature, gladness akin to rapture, as they were unfolded to me, are among the earliest sensations I can remember. (p. 38)

Here, Frankenstein reveals his fundamental flaw – an obsessive wish for knowledge that will eventually cause his downfall. Almost as soon as he arrives at university at the age of 17, his ambition becomes apparent and he is determined to achieve something extraordinary in his scientific studies: 

So much has been done, exclaimed the soul of Frankenstein, - more, far more, will I achieve: treading in the steps already marked, I will pioneer a new way, explore unknown powers, and unfold to the world the deepest mysteries of creation. (p. 49)

Ironically, even though he wishes to reveal the “mysteries of creation” to the whole world, in the end he will be too ashamed or afraid to tell anyone what he has done. His refusal to admit to creating the creature results in the unjust conviction of Justine, as well as the deaths of everyone he loves.

His interest in discovering the source of life quickly becomes an obsession. This obsession causes him to ignore everything else in his life – most notably, his relationships with family and friends, and his physical health. While looking for the origin of life and then while creating the creature, he seems to be driven by both scientific interest and by “an almost supernatural enthusiasm” (p. 52). 

Frankenstein is controlled by reason and passion

In many ways, the main character is characterized by the opposing forces of both reason and passion. He repeatedly swings between thinking logically and being overcome by his emotions. For example, after he sees the creature near the spot where his brother was murdered, he claims “no one can conceive the anguish I suffered during the remainder of the night” (p. 78). 

However, the next morning, he rationally decides that it would be pointless to explain about the creature, because no one would believe him: “I well knew that if any other had communicated such a relation to me, I should have looked upon it as the ravings of insanity.” (p. 79). His failure to try to save Justine from being unfairly convicted of the creature’s crime is one of several moments where readers might lose sympathy with Frankenstein.  

His aloneness is another characteristic

Frankenstein is also characterized by his aloneness. His obsession with his scientific research almost immediately “caused me also to forget those friends who were so many miles absent, and whom I had not seen for so long a time” (p. 56). During his attempt to build the creature, Frankenstein “shunned my fellow-creatures as if I had been guilty of a crime” (p. 57). This ironically foreshadows the crimes committed by the creature, for which he is indirectly guilty. 

Rejected by Frankenstein, the creature takes revenge on Frankenstein’s friends and family. After each crime, Frankenstein repeatedly seeks to be alone, refusing to reveal the creature’s existence or to explain his extreme behavior: “I shunned the face of man; all sound of joy or complacency was torture to me; solitude was my only consolation – deep, dark, deathlike solitude.” (p. 94) 

Even in the final chapter of the frame story, when everyone he loves has been killed by the creature, he rejects Captain Walton’s offer of friendship: “ ‘when you speak of new ties, and fresh affections, think you that any can replace those who are gone? Can any man be to me as Clerval was; or any woman another Elizabeth?’ ” (pp. 214-215)

Does Frankenstein develop during the novel?

Frankenstein can in some ways be considered a developing character – for example, in terms of his increasing grief and his eventual desire for revenge on the creature (whereas earlier in the story he simply wants to forget about the creature and protect his family). 

However, he is not a developing character when it comes to his continued determination to be alone and to reject the social and emotional ties that might have saved him. This results in his own grief and eventual death. It also results in the grief and death of the creature, who (the story implies) might not have become a monster if Frankenstein had shown him friendship.
