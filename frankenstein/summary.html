Summary

Mary Shelley’s novel Frankenstein begins with a series of letters from Captain Robert Walton to his sister. Walton explains that he is an explorer who has hired a ship to explore the waters around the North Pole. He and his crew spot a gigantic figure traveling across the ice, and later rescue a nearly frozen man called Victor Frankenstein. Frankenstein sees himself in Walton’s determination to risk everything for his expedition – and decides to tell his own story as a warning to Walton. This provides the frame narrative for Frankenstein to take over as narrator. 

Frankenstein starts by describing his childhood in Switzerland, with his parents, brothers, and an adopted sister, Elizabeth – with whom Frankenstein falls in love. He gains an early love of natural history. Just before he goes to university, his mother dies. 

At university, he immerses himself in science and eventually works out the formula for giving life to dead flesh. He creates an oversized figure of a man from parts of dead bodies and brings it to life. However, he is repulsed by the ugliness of the creature and runs away. Meeting his friend Henry Clerval on the street, he brings him back to his apartment – but the creature is gone. 

Frankenstein falls ill for several months but Clerval helps him recover. When he improves, he receives a letter from his father telling him his young brother William has been murdered. On his return to Geneva, he realizes that the creature is responsible. A much-loved family servant, Justine, is accused of the crime and hanged, but Frankenstein does not reveal the creature’s existence. Instead, the creature finds him and insists on telling his own story. 

The creature takes over the role of narrator, and recounts his experiences, beginning with his first days of life, when he discovers that people fear and hate him because of the way he looks. He finds a home in an abandoned shack attached to a cottage and becomes fond of the poor family living there. He begins to help them by collecting wood for them and learns to speak by listening to them. 

He also learns to read after he finds some lost books, educating himself about the world. He hopes that, despite his terrifying appearance, the family will accept him. He initially manages to speak to the father, who is blind. However, the other family members drive him away in fear, before abandoning their home. The creature is disappointed and decides to seek revenge on his maker, Frankenstein. 

The creature asks Frankenstein to make him a female partner, arguing that as he has life, he also has a right to happiness. He also argues that Frankenstein has a moral obligation to help since Frankenstein created him. The creature promises to disappear if Frankenstein does what he asks, but also threatens to kill Frankenstein’s friends and family if he doesn’t.

At this point, Frankenstein takes over the narrative again, and he and his friend Clerval travel to England before parting ways. Alone in the Orkney Islands, Frankenstein begins to work on a companion for the creature. However, he is full of doubt, and when he sees the creature watching him through the window, he destroys the body he has created. 

The creature angrily tells Frankenstein, “I will be with you on your wedding night”, which Frankenstein believes means the creature will kill him just as he achieves happiness. Frankenstein is swept across to Ireland in a boat, where he is arrested for murder – the creature has killed Clerval and framed Frankenstein. 

Frankenstein falls ill again, but is eventually cleared of murder and returns to Switzerland. He marries Elizabeth and arms himself against the creature on their wedding night. However, while Frankenstein is searching for him, the creature strangles Elizabeth. Frankenstein’s father soon dies of grief. Now seeking revenge himself, Frankenstein follows the creature into the Arctic, where he is picked up by Captain Walton’s ship nearly dead.

Walton now resumes his narrative in the form of a final letter to his sister. His ship becomes stuck in the ice, and when it melts, he decides he must head back south, as his crew refuse to continue north. Frankenstein refuses to go south and claims he will continue north alone, but he dies before he can set off. Walton finds the creature on the ship, mourning over Frankenstein’s body and that fact that he is now totally alone. The creature vows to kill himself and floats away on an ice raft into the darkness.
