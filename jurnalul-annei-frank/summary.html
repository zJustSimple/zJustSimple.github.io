Summary

The diary Anne Frank wrote between June 12, 1942, and August 1, 1944, is a remarkable document about the suffering during the Holocaust and the Second World War. Anne begins her notes on her thirteenth birthday and when she writes her last entry - number 195 in the diary - she is fifteen years old. 

On July 6, 1942, when Anne and her family are forced to go into hiding, she suddenly leaves behind her life as a relatively carefree schoolgirl. From that moment , she is forced to grow up more quickly than she would have ever thought possible because of the confines and inconveniences of the hiding place. In the 195 entries, Anne recounts her thoughts and most intimate feelings, the various experiences of everyday life in the secret annex, and the development of the war. During the 25 months, not only the world changes, but also Anne personally, and the teenage girl also discovers sexuality and love.

Anne's life before hiding

Anne was born in Frankfurt am Main in 1929 to Jewish parents but emigrated with her family to the Netherlands at the age of four to escape Nazi persecution in Germany. Until 1940, the family led a normal, middle-class, and carefree life in Amsterdam.

In 1940, the German army occupies the Netherlands, making life considerably more difficult for the Jewish family because of  many anti-Jewish laws. Although faced daily with the countless restrictions imposed on Jewish citizens, Anne still leads a relatively carefree life. In her diary entries up to the day she went into hiding, she focuses mainly on her everyday school life, her relationship with her numerous girlfriends, and her interactions with several admirers.

Only one thing really depresses Anne these days: she longs for a confidential and truly intimate friendship. All her friendships and admirers cannot hide the fact that deep down she is lonely. For this reason, she begins writing a diary addressed to her imaginary friend Kitty.

The move into the secret annex

When Anne's three years older sister Margot receives a summons to report to the SS, the Frank family moves the following day  to a hiding place carefully prepared for their arrival. Anne's father Otto Frank has arranged for the family to find refuge for several months  in the back building of his company. 

Together with the van Daans - a Jewish couple they befriended and their son Peter - who arrive shortly afterwards, they move into the two-story building, which is very small for two families. Four months later, both families decide to take in one more person, a Jewish dentist named Albert Dussel.

After being informed about the coming of the Jewish families, the company's employees actively help them move into the secret annex situated in the back house. In the next two years, their helpers will not only provide essential food supplies for those in hiding but will also comfort and encourage them.

Life in the secret annex

According to Anne's account, numerous disputes and conflicts arise after only a few weeks, despite the fact that the different parties actually appreciate each other. The constant fear of discovery and arrest, the cramped quarters, the shootings coming from outside, the scarce food, and the boredom overshadowing everything cause the mood to be almost always tense. 

The longer the war goes on, the more hopeless and pessimistic the dwellers of the secret annex become. They follow the development of the world conflict on the radio every day.

Anne is constantly longing to be truly understood and to be able to confide in someone without reservation. Since the teenager cannot find a reliable person in the tightly closed circle of the secret annex, she withdraws further and further into herself for a while. She often gets into quarrels with the adults. She adores her father but has a particularly tense relationship with her mother.

In early 1944, Anne’s interest in the three years older Peter van Daan  awakens. However, after a brief period of intimacy and mutual trust, which she describes as her best time in the secret annex, the hesitant young man disappoints her. Anne feels she can`t share all her feelings with him after all.

Anne reads a lot during the two years in the secret annex and among other things learns shorthand and foreign languages. She wants to become a writer after the war and live in Holland. In her later diary entries, Anne not only reflects objectively on the everyday life, hopes, and quarrels of the secret annex residents, but also contemplates God, the nature of humanity, feminism, and the fate of the world.

Events after the last diary entry

On August 4, 1944, three days after Anne's 195th and last entry, the hiding place is discovered. After spending 761 days in the secret annex, all eight inhabitants are arrested and transported to various concentration camps.

Anne, together with her sister Margot, is deported first to Auschwitz and then to the Bergen-Belsen concentration camp, where the sisters must endure hunger, cold, and appalling hygienic conditions. Margot and Anne die there at the end of February 1945, within a few weeks of each other, presumably of typhus. Only their father, Otto Frank survives the Holocaust and has Anne's diary published in 1947.
