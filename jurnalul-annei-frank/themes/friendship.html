The search for a true friend

Even before going into hiding, Anne Frank writes in her diary that she  longs for a friendship in which she can reveal her true self. The thirteen-year-old decides to confide in her fictional friend Kitty by writing a diary in order to come to terms with her loneliness: “I hope I will be able to confide everything to you, as I have never been able to confide in anyone, and I hope you will be a great source of comfort and support.” (0%)

In the cramped environment of the secret annex, Anne finds no one with whom she can have a deep friendship. She longs for a kind of tenderness that seemingly none of the secret annex residents, not even her beloved father, can give her: “I’m no longer satisfied with the meaningless affection or the supposedly serious talks. I long for something from Father that he’s incapable of giving.” (41%).

The loneliness and self-doubt generate a feeling of great emptiness in Anne. Because she cannot distract herself with the usual outdoor pleasures, she is constantly contemplating herself. Anne has the feeling that she does not fit in with her family and that none of the others are interested in her opinion.

Friendship and love

After Anne dreams of her childhood love, Peter Schiff, her desire for affectionate intimacy increases beyond measure. Anne is convinced that she will never be able to love anyone again as much as she loves Peter Schiff: 

Oh, Petel, Petel, how am I ever going to free myself from your image? Wouldn’t anyone who took your place be a poor substitute? I love you, with a love so great that it simply couldn’t keep growing inside my heart, but had to leap out and reveal itself in all its magnitude. (49%). 

Although Anne is convinced that she will never again be able to love anyone as she loves Peter Schiff, the fact that she tries to get more intimate with Peter van Daan immediately after her romantic dream is significant.

Previously, she had called him a boring “dope” (11%) and complained that he sometimes stroked her cheek. However, after her dream, her “longing for someone to talk to” (48%) is so great that she decides to get to know Peter better. 

After only a few weeks, Anne feels a sense of “fellowship” (56%) in Peter`s presence and is sure that something “beautiful is going to develop between Peter and [her], a kind of friendship and a feeling of trust" (57%). Although Anne still denies being in love with him at this point, her feelings quickly change.

Anne is pleased with herself only when she gets closer to Peter van Daan and is in the middle of the first phase of being in love: 

Oh, when I think back to Saturday night, to our words, our voices, I feel satisfied with myself for the very first time; what I mean is, I’d still say the same and wouldn’t want to change a thing, the way I usually do. (68%). 

But when it turns out that she can't trust Peter with everything after all, Anne is disappointed: “Yet why does he hide his innermost self and never allow me access?” (96%).

The more Anne pays attention to Peter`s character flaws, the stronger she feels that she has created a dream image of him and that she was only interested in him mainly because she longed for intimacy. She regrets using intimacy to get closer to Peter instead of simply being friends with him and otherwise keeping her distance.

Fear of revealing her own character

While outwardly always dismissing criticism casually, Anne is her own harshest critic and always strives to improve her character. She is well aware of her “faults and shortcomings” (94%) and reprimands herself for her faulty behavior: “What’s so difficult about my personality is that I scold and curse myself much more than anyone else does (…)” (94%). What Anne would really like is a person who would clear away her self-doubts and self-accusations and not aggravate them with their own criticism.

Anne feels that she is made of two different sides, a cheerful and carefree one that she presents to the outside world and a more thoughtful and reflective one that nobody but herself knows. She has the feeling that her side which the others see, amuses people briefly, but is not enough for the others to really like her in the long run. Nevertheless, she does not dare to show others her inner side: “I’m afraid they’ll mock me, think I’m ridiculous and sentimental and not take me seriously.” (100%).
