The future of Brexit

Cuprins
  • Introduction
  • Four main possibilities
  • Which possibility is more likely?

Introduction

The UK was originally scheduled to leave the EU on 29th of March, 2019. However, on the 15th of March, 2019, the British Parliament voted to seek an extension of the deadline, to have more time to come up with a deal that can be accepted by both Parliament and the EU. The extension was initially scheduled to last until the April 12, but a few days before the deadline the EU granted a further extension lasting until the October 31, 2019. As the UK failed to meet that deadline too, EU finally accepted a so-called 'flextension', which set a new deadline on January 31, 2020, but also included the option of leaving earlier if an acceptable agreement can be found before then.

Four main possibilities

Since the referendum, there have been a few different possibilities for Brexit.

One option is a hard Brexit. This would mean that the UK would refuse to compromise on certain aspects with the EU – such as the free movement of people. In turn this would lead to the UK leaving the Single Market and giving up free trade agreements. In other words, the relationship between the EU and the UK would be very different from the one they have today.

The second option would be a soft Brexit. This would mean that many of the current relationships between the two sides would be maintained. For example, the UK might continue to be part of the single market and have free trade deals, but it would probably have to agree to the free movement of people as well.

The third option would be no deal Brexit. This is a risk if no agreement can be reached before the deadline, and could have very negative consequences. Specifically, no deal also means no transition period for leaving the EU, which forces businesses, laws, and public institutions to adapt to the UK leaving the EU immediately – which might cause some economic and bureaucratic challenges on both sides.  

Some have also hinted at a fourth option – a second referendum, which might lead to the reversal of Brexit and Britain retaining its full membership of the EU. This seems less likely than any of the other options and would probably cause much political criticism given that it would invalidate the decision of the first referendum – but some have expressed hopes that it might still happen, especially given the current chaos of the Brexit process.

Which possibility is more likely?

It is currently very difficult to say which of the four scenarios is more likely.

Theresa May's original deal was a compromise between soft and hard Brexit, breaking off with the EU in some respects, but leaving certain trade agreements in place temporarily, and ensuring that the Irish border would not become an issue (through a backstop solution). However, May's deal was massively unpopular across the political spectrum, and on January 15, 2019, it was voted down in one of the greatest political defeats in the history of British democracy. When she put a slightly revised deal to a vote again roughly two months later, on March 12, it was once again overwhelmingly defeated - the relatively minor changes May had secured from the EU failed to satisfy Parliament. After even more failed attempts to get a deal passed, May was finally forced to announce her resignation, and she left her post on June 7th 2019.

When Boris Johnson (a strong supporter of hard Brexit) was chosen as Theresa May's successor in July 2019, no-deal became even more likely, as Johnson is known for his uncompromising views on Brexit and has frequently stated that he is prepared to leave without a deal if negotiations with the EU prove unsuccessful. However, because Parliament introduced the so-called Benn Act, Johnson was ultimately forced to ask the EU for a further extension after being unable to meet the October 31 deadline, thus breaking his promise of leaving in October regardless of whether a deal had been made.  

Talk of a second referendum has also increased following the defeat of May's plans and the multiple deadline extensions. Supporters of a second referendum tend to assume that people will choose to vote Remain this time around, especially if faced with the possibility of crashing out with no deal as the alternative. On February 25th, 2019, Jeremy Corbyn announced that Labour will support a second referendum if the government does not accept Labour's suggested modifications for the Brexit deal. For a long time, Corbyn had been unwilling to voice support for a second referendum, so this new announcement marked a major shift in Labour politics (probably as a response to much internal division in the party).
