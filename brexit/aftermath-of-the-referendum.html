The aftermath of the 2016 referendum

After the results of the referendum came out, there were both immediate reactions and important political changes in the country.

Initially, there were voices in the UK that contested the results. For example, the referendum was criticised for not allowing people that were aged between 16 and 18 to vote, whereas they had been allowed to vote in the Scottish independence referendum. Those who used this criticism argued that the effects of the referendum will have a life-long impact on the lives of young people, compared to the shorter impact it will have on the lives of older people.

Another immediate reaction was the creation of a petition to organise a second referendum in case the percentage of wining votes was not at least 60% on a 75% turnout. Although the petition gathered thousands of signatures, no new referendum was organised.

The day after the referendum, PM David Cameron announced his resignation by October, because the Leave campaign won. As a supporter of the Remain campaign, he considered the referendum a political defeat. After only three weeks, Conservative Theresa May became the new Prime Minister.

The results of the referendum also triggered criticism of the Labour Party Leader, Jeremy Corbyn. Most of the Labour Party supported the Remain campaign. Corbyn was considered partially responsible for poor campaigning among Labour voters. Nevertheless, he refused to resign as party leader.

Shortly after the referendum, the leader of UKIP, Nigel Farage, also renounced his leadership. He did not do this because of failure, but because he believed that he had achieved his political goal of taking the UK out of the EU. In the time since the referendum, UKIP has lost much of its popularity. This is not very surprising considering that the main platform of the party was campaigning against the EU.

Given that the majority of Scottish people voted to remain in the EU, the Scottish Government suggested that Scotland might organise a new independence referendum, which would allow Scotland to continue as a part of the EU

Another immediate effect of the referendum was a sharp drop in the value of the British pound, one of the biggest drops in UK’s history. Nevertheless, the currency regained stability in the following months.

In March 2017, the British PM Theresa May triggered Article 50 of the European Union Treaty and initiated the two-year withdrawal procedure. After that, she called for a snap election – which she saw as a way to consolidate her power in the Brexit negotiations. However, the elections did not consolidate her government’s power: the elections lead to the Conservative Party losing seats in the Parliament, instead of increasing their majority. This created what is called a hung parliament, meaning no party or coalition has the absolute majority.

The snap election was a huge political failure for Theresa May, who had been hoping to increase her political majority for the negotiations to come, but instead reduced her own influence.
