The 2016 referendum

Cuprins
  • Overview
  • The Remain campaign
  • The Leave campaign

Overview

Amid growing Euro-scepticism and pressure from anti-EU parties like UKIP, the Conservative Prime Minister David Cameron and his party promised voters in the 2015 general elections that if their party won, then a referendum would be organised regarding UK’s membership in the EU.

In 2013, Cameron first promised British voters that he would renegotiate the terms of EU membership. These renegotiated terms were reached in early 2016. However, there were no significant changes in the EU-UK relationship apart from the already established opt-outs and the UK rebate. At the same time, Parliament had already approved the referendum and campaigning had already started.

The official group which was approved to organise the Remain campaign was Britain Stronger in Europe, while Vote Leave was the group which organised the Leave campaign.

The Remain campaign was mostly supported by liberals and members of the Labour Party, while the Conservative Party was split on the issue. The Leave campaign was supported by Euro-sceptics and some Conservatives, including MP Boris Johnson who became a frontman for Vote Leave.

The Conservative Prime Minister supported the Remain campaign, but the members of his party were free to endorse whichever campaign they agreed with. In general, the Conservative Party as a whole adopted a neutral position, avoiding getting too involved in any of the campaigns. 

Apart from the two official campaigns, UKIP organised a separate campaign aimed at persuading voters to opt for leaving the EU. Their campaign was titled Leave EU.

Each campaign was further endorsed by different business, media, and trade unions, leading to both campaigns having support across all segments of society.

The referendum was organised for 23rd of June, 2016. The results of the polls revealed that the Leave campaign won with 51.9% of the votes. In total, it is estimated that about 30 million people voted in the referendum, making up 71.8% of the total British voters.

However, the results were different from country to country. A majority of people in England and Wales voted for Leave: 53.4% in England and 52.5% in Wales. But in Northern Ireland and Scotland, a majority of voters sided with the Remain campaign: 62% in Scotland and 55.8% in Northern Ireland. It is also notable that the majority of people in London – a highly globalised city with many connections to other nations – voted to Remain in the EU.

There were also significant differences in voters’ preference according to age and education. According to a YouGov survey, most Remain voters were young people holding a higher education degree. By contrast, most people who voted Leave were over 50 years old or did not hold a higher education degree.

The referendum was not legally binding, meaning that the government was not legally obliged to act according to the results of the popular vote. However, after PM David Cameron resigned, his successor, Theresa May started the withdrawal process on 29th of March, 2017. According to EU rules, this gave the UK two years to negotiate the terms of leaving the European Union.

The Remain campaign

During the campaign for the Brexit referendum, the Remain campaign argued in favour of the UK staying in the EU by focusing on the economic and political advantages of EU membership.

One argument was that leaving the EU would mean that the UK would lose many trade benefits that it has with neighbouring countries, thanks to the Single Market. In turn, this would also affect the country’s negotiation advantages with other parts of the world. 

Another argument put forward by the Remain side was that UK might lose its status as a financial centre of the world because it will no longer have ‘entrance to the EU’, something which attracted financial investors and banks from US and other non-EU countries.

The campaigners also argued that trading some sovereignty with the EU was a win-win situation, because it also allowed the UK to be able to have a voice at negotiations both in the EU and outside the EU. At the same time, they pointed out that the UK leaving the EU does not mean more sovereignty as the country is also bound to other international organisations such as the UN, NATO or WTO.

The Remain Campaign also tried to focus on the positive aspects of immigration. Campaigners agreed that immigration does raise challenges but also pointed out that it can have positive social and economic outcomes. Furthermore, they argued that leaving the EU would not stop global immigration trends from affecting the UK.  Additionally, leaving the EU would lead to a shortage of workers for jobs that are now typically performed by immigrants, such as essential roles in the National Health Service.

Another Remain argument was that leaving the EU would lead to people losing jobs related to trade with the EU. Furthermore, they suggested that more jobs could be lost if trade and investments shrink after Brexit.

Yet another argument related to the issue of the border between Northern Ireland and Ireland. This has historically been a ‘soft border’ with little control and bureaucracy, thanks to the Good Friday peace agreement that put an end to the Troubles. In the event of Brexit, however, it would be hard to maintain such a soft border, as people and goods would now be travelling in and out of the EU when crossing the border, which would require much more strict control. The Irish border issue would later turn out to be one of the main difficulties when trying to create an acceptable Brexit agreement.  

Finally, campaigners argued that leaving the EU would result in losing political influence in Europe, and affect the UK as a global political power.

As you can see, most of the arguments put forward by the Remain campaign focused on the negative impact that Brexit would have on the UK. Because of this, some sections of the media called the campaign “Project Fear”.

The Leave campaign

The Leave campaign convinced voters to opt for leaving the EU using arguments related to immigration, the economy, bureaucracy and British independence.

For example, campaigners argued that leaving the EU would save the UK money because it would no longer contribute to the EU budget. According to 2016 data, the UK paid £13.1bn pounds towards the EU, that campaigners argued could be used internally. Specifically, they used the argument that the money could be spent on the National Health Service (NHS). Nevertheless many of the figures they put forward were fact checked and proved inaccurate. For example, in 2016, the UK also received £4.5bn from the EU. Consequently, the real contribution to the EU budget was smaller – £8.5bn. The Leave campaign later had to withdraw their claims about the huge amount of money that could potentially be spent on the NHS upon leaving the EU.

At the same time, campaigners overlooked the fact that leaving the EU would also have a lot of immediate costs which were estimated at about 40bn euros.

The Leave Campaign also argued that while UK might lose the trade framework of the Single Market, this would also mean that the country is free to establish its own trade deals which could be more advantageous.

Similarly, the campaigners responded to concerns about financial and economic aspects by arguing that the UK will be free from EU regulations and bureaucracy, and thus might re-invent itself as an independent, strong economy such as Singapore.

A key argument for leaving the EU put forward by campaigners was related to sovereignty. In their view, giving up some sovereignty to the EU is never worth it. Campaigners argues that after leaving the EU, there would be no interfering in domestic and foreign policies established by the UK.

Many campaigners claimed that leaving the EU would lead to a dramatic drop in immigration and allow the UK to have control over its borders. This argument was often rejected by the Remain campaign, so some of Leave campaigners simply argued that even if immigration were not reduced, the UK would be free to set its own rules regarding the movement of people.

Although the UK is not a member of the Eurozone, campaigners still used the argument of the euro being an economic disaster to suggest that the EU is inefficient and does not create economic progress.

Finally, Leavers also argued that by leaving the EU, the UK could become an independent, powerful political voice in the world again.
