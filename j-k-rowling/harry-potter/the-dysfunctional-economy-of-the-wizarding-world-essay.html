Harry Potter and the dysfunctional economy of the Wizarding World | Essay

In 1997, the British writer Joanne Rowling released a book called Harry Potter and the Philosopher’s Stone. The book is a marvelous piece of literature about a young and orphaned boy trying to find his place, as he got introduced to the great world of magic and magical beasts alike. The book series ended up being one of the most well written and recognizable book series to date. J. K. Rowling absolutely knew how to write about a great magical universe, but there was one part of the Wizarding World that was shrug off before it received a real explanation. The economy of the Potterverse. What actually happens in the banks? Is the economy in this universe important for the story? Did she mean for the economy to be an important part, or just a loose thread she forgot to tie up? 

The currency of the Potterverse 

The currency of the Potterverse is essential to acknowledge if someone would want to get a better understanding of what Rowling thought when making the fictional economy, if she put any thought into the economy at all. The currency in Harry Potter is divided into three different categories, Knut, Sickle, and Galleon. They represent the three different precious metals bronze, silver and gold from our world respectively. In the sequel book “Fantastic beasts and where to find them” set in the same universe there is given an example of exchange, as it explains that 250 million USD is the equivalent to 34 million Galleons. Some believe that we can pinpoint the exact value of the Potterverse-currency compared to the different currencies in our world. With help from excel-charts and currency-calculators one can confidently say that 1 Galleon equals 7.35 USD/53.81 SEK. Furthermore, the value of the Wizarding Currency would be dramatically low if this was the actual value, as it would be nowhere near valuable enough to have any significance. This would indicate that she did not have much knowledge about currency, or at least did not invest much thought when mentioning it the way she did.

The making and usage of “Wizarding Currency” 

With the knowledge of what the currency is and its value, it is possible to evaluate how they obtain and use the currency. This is a very vague area because it is never explained how they make the coins, if they even do. With a little bit of speculation and small jumps in conclusions we can determine that the way the coins are obtained is by mining. In ancient times, they used leaves with copper remnants until they changed to mining and forging the currency now used. The currency is however not used for buying most general goods directly but is used to trade with “Muggles”, except for magical artifacts and creatures like brooms, wands, owls, frogs etc. This information is proof that Rowling did not put much thought into the process of currency exchange either, and this already gives a reason to believe that she did not put much time at all into creating a functional economic system. 

The Weasleys/the Malfoys 

To get a better view of the differences and similarities between differently situated families based on wealth, and try to see if the economic system can be explained from a different perspective, one could try and compare two families who are somehow important to the story, and see if there are any significant differences.

The Weasleys, a family who is presented in the series as a loving and caring family of seven (counting the most central to the plot) is also presented as a family with little money. They all live in a “shack-like cottage”, and it is mentioned more than once that he lives in poor conditions. Draco Malfoy is often shown using the Weasley’s family-name as a representation of being poor in multiple occasions. Ron has a complication throughout the books as his wand is in a broken state as he can’t afford to repair it. There are a few instances of the wand backfiring and reflecting the spells he casts because of this. The family is overall depicted as a family with poor living conditions based on their clothing, living conditions and social status. 

The Malfoys, a family of only three, are presented as a very wealthy family with a habit of spending. We later get descriptions of the Malfoy Manor , depicting it as a great mansion.

An example of a paid service they approve of, is the usage of house elves. House elves are strong magical creatures who are unfortunately abused as poorly paid slaves who do typical house chores like cleaning and receiving guests. The Malfoys are depicted as having a shared “snobby” personality, and the clothes they wear, the wands they have, and the lifestyle can tell us that they are quite wealthy opposed to the Weasleys. 

With knowing this, one could understand that she at least tried to make some sort of system when it comes to personal wealth, as it is mentioned quite often throughout the books. 

Job employment in the wizarding world 

Something we also should account for is the fact that jobs exist in the Potterverse. We can take examples from the scenery and settings in both the books and movies to confirm a few different jobs. Some of the more obvious are the positions as bankers (commonly employed by goblins), farmers (proven by the different farmland we encounter throughout the series, for example Hagrid’s pumpkin farm and mentions of farms with livestock like eels), magical goods-salesmen (wands, brooms, potions) mining (suggested by the existence of Wizarding Currency and other artifacts made from precious metals), teachers, and positions like spokesmen and counselors in the “Ministry of Magic”. There is no useable information about how much they earn at all. But knowing that jobs exist gives us an insight into the working class. 

Is the economy stable and fair? 

All the previous information that has been gathered, suggests that the economy is failing to hold up, and would probably collapse fairly quick compared to the different real-life economic systems that we can compare it to. All of this suggests that the wizarding world actually suffers from some kind of “lesser poverty” as families like the Weasleys are just a middle-class family (gets profiled in a fairly poor state), Harry would actually be poor as he got older as the money he inherited from his parents was never actually worth a lot (proven by a fan who managed to figure out and calculate how much money was in the Potter-vault) and families like the Malfoys will keep on inheriting money that has been passed down through generations, and have no real need to do any significant work to earn money, but rather depend on the family’s previous wealth. The nature of this economic system is cruel to someone like Harry and Ron and very beneficial for someone like Draco Malfoy, as they would be presented with either the low-class, middle-class, or high-class even before they are born. 

To summarize the topic, one could describe the economy of the Potterverse as a poorly explained part of the Harry Potter franchise, as it is never properly explained, and does not seem to have any real consistency about what it is, how it operates, and how it compares to the real world. Even though Rowling was a brilliant fantasy-writer, the same could not be said about her knowledge of substantial economics, as proven by this analysis.
