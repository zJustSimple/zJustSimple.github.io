Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone by J.K. Rowling - Book review

Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone by J.K. Rowling - a typical fantasy

This novel emerged from the creative mind of J.K. Rowling that is the first of seven following novels in the Harry Potter series. Fantasy is truly characterized throughout the book by the several factors there is. 

It is about a boy in the age of eleven who seems to be as unexceptional as any, when one day this statement comes to its trail. He is a wizard. Though he is not alone and that he get knowledge of after he receives a letter from the school Hogwarts of Witchcraft and Wizardry. During the year he experiences great adventures, confrontation with a few wicked teachers and some students, and faces all kinds of trails. By this Harry grows more and more mature and gradually transforms into to the the novels powerful hero from the weakling he were before, by facing the greatest trail of them all, by meeting his parents murderer the so called lord Voldemort. 

Fantasy is the genre where the plot takes place in a divergent reality, from the perspective of our own. In this novel there is various factors like wizards, magic spells and unnatural creatures like trolls, werewolves and dragons that clearly indicates the genre of fantasy, since these elements is typical in other similar stories. 

Something I find interesting in the genre of fantasy is that those stories often got based on similar stories from the epoch of the ancient Greeks. Nowadays novels overall structures on common symbols the ancient Greeks constructed, like the “hero image”, that from the beginning comes from this far away era. Harry’s scar is one of those symbols. 

Harry receives a lightning-shaped scar from Voldemort before the plot even takes place and this symbolizes everything unique and amazing about him. Odysseus, from the famous poem of the ancient Greeks, also owns a similar scar which, as for Harry, is a badge of honor, a token of having survived a great battle and to wage the future battles to come. It also continuously connects Harry to his past, to his loving parents who tried to protect him with their lives. The scar also indicate Harry’s emotional sensitivity because of the pain it causes him when hatred is directed at him, as for example when Quirrell tries to grab him. 

Another example of how the fantasy genre has developed from the ancient Greeks is the desire that grows within the characters. In one of their tragedies a kings heir sleeps with his mother and kills his father without knowing that they are his parents, because of his desire of the throne.

 Voldemort’s desire of the Philosopher’s Stone for eternal life and wealth, Harry’s desire to meet his dead parents, Ron Weasleys desire of winning the Quidditch cup and for once be noticed, to make his great family proud. The Mirror of Erised reveals that learning what to want is an important part of one’s development by its “desirereflection”. There is a great number of different desires and for some, those with too much desire and less humility, is often condemned like Dudley was from the beginning with his outrageous demands for multiple television, games and toys, to appear foolish and obnoxious. The uncontrollable greed and desires that Voldemort, that though appears in much more evil, and Dudley share is what repels Harry which makes him to the enemy of them both. 

A part in the ending shows that the author also got its concept from the Christian belief (where one can describe the Bible as a fantasy) as Professor Dumbledore tells Harry about his old friend called Flamel who sacrificed himself by letting them destroying the stone, after Voldemort's attempt to get his hands on it, which would lead to Flamel’s own death as Jesus sacrificed himself for the humankind. His decision reveals his wisdom as you can compare with other great philosophers like the Greek Socrates and the Indian Buddha. 

These are all examples of themes and symbols that is been used in this novel and in many ways indicates the fantasy genre because of the comparison of both older but also more recent novels respectively epos and similar stories.
