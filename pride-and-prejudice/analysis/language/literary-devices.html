Literary devices

Imagery

Jane Austen uses only little imagery in Pride and Prejudice, instead focusing on realism, precision, and dialogue. One example of a simile is when Elizabeth talks about Wickham with Jane: "… ‘Your profusion makes me saving; and if you lament over him much longer, my heart will be as light as a feather.’ '' (p. 157)

In the phrase “Elizabeth made no answer, and walked on, her heart swelling with indignation” (p. 130), the author constructs a metaphor to suggest Elizabeth’s heart was hurt. 

Personification also make up the imagery of the novel. For example: “ ‘His pride,’ said Miss Lucas, ‘does not offend me so much as pride often does’ ” (p. 13). In this case, pride is given human attributes, suggesting the significance of pride in the novel.

Hyperbole

Hyperbole means to exaggerate a situation, character trait etc. A number of characters frequently use hyperbole to express themselves, with Mrs Bennet being the queen of hyperbole: " ‘I am sure,’ she added, ‘if it was not for such good friends I do not know what would become of her, for she is very ill indeed, and suffers a vast deal, though with the greatest patience in the world’" (p. 29). Here, Mrs Bennet is exaggerating Jane's illness, making it seem like Jane is dying. Mrs Bennet also frequently exaggerates her own suffering.

In another example, the hyperbole even includes tricolon (repeating things in threes): “Her manners were pronounced to be very bad indeed, a mixture of pride and impertinence; she had no conversation, no style, no beauty” (p. 24). In this case, it emphasises the unpleasant manners of Miss Bingley and her strong dislike of Elizabeth.

Antithesis

Antithesis refers to creating opposition between people or situations to emphasise an idea.

One example is: “She sat down again and tried to conceal, by incessant employment the feelings which were divided between distress and diversion” (p. 74). Here, the antithesis between her ‘distress’ and ‘diversion’ helps to emphasise Elizabeth’s conflicting feelings.

A more extensive example of antithesis is between how Mr and Mrs Bennet receive their daughters home from Netherfield:

They were not welcomed home very cordially by their mother. Mrs. Bennet wondered at their coming, and thought them very wrong to give so much trouble, and was sure Jane would have caught cold again.--But their father, though very laconic in his expressions of pleasure, was really glad to see them; he had felt their importance in the family circle. (p. 42)

For the mother, getting her daughters married was more important than them being at home. By contrast, the father values the opposite. Thus, the use of antithesis helps us see the personalities of Mr and Mrs Bennet.

Humour, irony, and sarcasm

Irony and humour play a key role in the novel and is used by both Austen and the characters. For example, when Darcy enters the ball room: “the report which was in general circulation within five minutes after his entrance, of his having ten thousand a year” (p. 6). Here, Austen makes fun of the gossipy nature of people and suggests Darcy might be justified in feeling uncomfortable.

The author is both ironical and humorous when describing certain characters, often in contrast. One relevant example can be found in the description of Mr and Mrs Bennet:

Mr. Bennet was so odd a mixture of quick parts, sarcastic humour, reserve, and caprice, that the experience of three-and-twenty years had been insufficient to make his wife understand his character. Her mind was the less difficult to develop. She was a woman of mean understanding, little information, and uncertain temper. (p. 3)

Here, irony and humour help suggests a poor relationship between spouses and the fact that they each have flaws. Particularly Mrs Bennet is spoken ironically of by the narrator.

The author also uses extensive ironical dialogue which helps to reveal the silliness of characters such as Mrs Bennet and Mr Collins. Verbal irony is noticeable in most characters’ comments, including Elizabeth’s:

‘Yes; these four evenings have enabled them to ascertain that they both like Vingt-un better than Commerce; but with respect to any other leading characteristic, I do not imagine that much has been unfolded’ (p. 15).

Here, Elizabeth is talking to Charlotte about Bingley and Jane, suggesting that even if they have spent some time together, they have not had an opportunity to discuss anything serious.

Sometimes the characters' use of irony turns into sarcasm, which refers to the use of irony to convey contempt. This technique is often used when the characters interact with each other. For example, one of the most sarcastic characters is Mr Bennet who often mocks his wife, but also Mr Collins, Wickham, and others. Here is one line in which Mr Bennet is sarcastic: “ ‘If your daughter should have a dangerous fit of illness, if she should die, it would be a comfort to know that it was all in pursuit of Mr. Bingley, and under your orders’ ” (p. 21). Some may see this is just great fun, others as quite mean of him.

Satire 

Pride and Prejudice as a whole is often considered a satire of upper-class society and norms during Regency England – a narrative that seeks to expose both individual and social flaws. This is why many characters may seem like caricatures, moved by one dominant trait. For instance, Lydia’s dominant trait is vanity while Wickham’s is dishonesty. Also, both Mrs Bennet and Mr Collins are ridiculous, but in different ways. 

The satirical nature of the novel is even suggested from the very first lines of the book: “It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife” (p. 1). These lines are dripping with irony.
