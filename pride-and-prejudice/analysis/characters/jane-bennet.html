Jane Bennet

Cuprins
  • Outer characterisation
  • Inner characterisation

Outer characterisation

Jane Bennet is one of the more important characters in Pride and Prejudice because of the focus on her relationship with both Bingley and her sister Lizzy. She is a static, or flat, character as she does not change much throughout the novel.

Her outer characterisation reveals that she is “almost three-and-twenty” (p. 154) years old and the eldest of the Bennet sisters (p. 7). We can assume her future dowry at marriage is £1,000 as this is what Elizabeth is also going to receive. By the end of the novel, she marries the wealthy Charles Bingley. She is considered the most beautiful of the Bennet sisters, especially by her doting mother (p. 30).

Inner characterisation

Jane’s inner characterisation is conveyed both directly by the narrator and other characters, and indirectly, through her actions and attitudes.

Overall, Jane is beautiful and good-natured. Her refusal to see people’s flaws and think badly of them is both a virtue and a weakness. On the one hand, she manages to see the positive of every situation and in every person and defends them which makes Elizabeth say:

‘Oh! you are a great deal too apt, you know, to like people in general. You never see a fault in anybody. All the world are good and agreeable in your eyes. I never heard you speak ill of a human being in your life.’ (p. 9)

Elizabeth also teasingly says that Jane has the ability “to take the good of everybody’s character and make it still better, and say nothing of the bad” (p. 9)

However, this also makes Jane come across as naïve, such as when it comes to Wickham’s true character. When Elizabeth tells her that Wickham probably lied about his past with Darcy, Jane finds it hard to believe it: “It was not in her nature to question the veracity of a young man of such amiable appearance as Wickham” (p. 60).

But Jane can also be firm and determined in certain matters. One example is when she decides to leave Netherfield early after her cold, despite the fact that Bingley “repeatedly tried to persuade Miss Bennet that it would not be safe for her—that she was not enough recovered; but Jane was firm where she felt herself to be right” (p. 41).

Because she is so sweet-natured, Jane is also more likely to be hurt and disappointed by others, such as when Caroline Bingley visits her in London and offends her (p. 104). Jane is again disappointed to find out that Lydia and Wickham ran away but continues to believe and hope that it will end well and that Wickham truly loves Lydia. Eventually, however, she realises that Elizabeth was right about Wickham.

Jane is also supportive of her mother during the Wickham-Lydia incident. She does everything to comfort Mrs Bennet during the search for Lydia: “My mother was in hysterics, and though I endeavoured to give her every assistance in my power, I am afraid I did not do so much as I might have done!” (p. 202 ).

Jane’s relationship with Elizabeth is very close. They are not only sisters but also close confidants. For this reason, Jane is almost shocked to find out that her sister loves Darcy at the end of the novel, because Elizabeth has never spoken to her about it, and as such, she believes it not to be true: “Oh, Lizzy!  do anything rather than marry without affection.” (p. 262).

A significant part of Jane’s inner characterisation is conveyed in relation to Charles Bingley, with whom she falls in love quite fast. However, her passiveness and shyness prevents her from showing her affection to Bingley directly. This is why it is easy for Darcy and the Bingley sisters to convince Charles that Jane is not in love with him. Darcy later explains his actions in his letter by describing Jane’s appearance:

‘I shall not scruple to assert, that the serenity of your sister’s countenance and air was such as might have given the most acute observer a conviction that, however amiable her temper, her heart was not likely to be easily touched.’ (p. 138)

However, Jane confesses she is willing to accept Bingley despite his sisters’ disapproving of her, suggesting her love for him: “You must know that though I should be exceedingly grieved at their disapprobation, I could not hesitate” (p. 85). Despite her desperate wish to be liked by everyone, Jane is ready to follow her heart.

However, for most of the novel, Jane is rather unhappy because she loses contact with Bingley after he leaves Netherfield and goes to London. As her mother (rather dramatically) puts it, Jane “is very ill indeed, and suffers a vast deal, though with the greatest patience in the world, which is always the way with her, for she has, without exception, the sweetest temper I ever met with” (p. 29).

When she goes to stay with her aunt and uncle in London, Jane hopes to see Bingley, which suggests that she is still in love with him. However, his sisters and Mr Darcy don't inform him Jane is in London, and they never meet there.

She is happy when Mr Bingley and Mr Darcy return to Netherfield, and she gladly accepts when Bingley asks her hand in marriage. Unselfish as always, she is glad that her marriage will bring happiness to others: “Oh! Lizzy, to know that what I have to relate will give such pleasure to all my dear family! how shall I bear so much happiness!” (p. 242). This poses a clear contrast to Elizabeth who refuses to marry Mr Collins although it would please her mother enormously.

According to gender role ideals of the time, Jane is the ideal woman of the gentry: beautiful, gentle, and compliant. To modern readers, however, she may come across as naïve and passive. Whereas Elizabeth and Darcy are each other's opposites and help each other develop into better people, Jane and Bingley are more similar to each other. They are both gentle, kind, and fairly naïve, and although you may argue that this brings no development to their personalities, it does make them well-suited.
