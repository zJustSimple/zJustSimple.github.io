Mr. Bingley

Cuprins
  • Outer characterisation
  • Inner characterisation

Outer characterisation

Mr Charles Bingley is a static character who does not change during the course of Jane Austen's novel, Pride and Prejudice. For example, he likes Jane from the beginning and continues to like and love her throughout the novel.

His outer characterisation reveals that he is 22 years old, is Darcy's friend, and has two sisters, Louisa and Caroline. He is rich because he inherited £100,000 upon his father’s death, and he is able to rent the grand estate Netherfield Park (p. 6). He is described as being attractive in every way: “Mr. Bingley was good-looking and gentlemanlike; he had a pleasant countenance, and easy, unaffected manners” (p. 6).

Inner characterisation

Charles Bingley’s inner characterisation is initially conveyed in contrast to that of Mr Darcy: “Bingley was sure of being liked wherever he appeared; Darcy was continually giving offence” (pp. 10-11). Unlike Darcy, Bingley is characterised by “easiness, openness, and ductility of his temper” (p. 10). You may say that just as Elizabeth and Jane, who are equally close, are each other's opposites, so are Bingley and Darcy.

Bingley is a sociable and well-intended person, who does not pay too much attention to class differences and enjoys the company of people from different social classes: “ ‘When I am in the country’, he replied, ‘I never wish to leave it; and when I am in town it is pretty much the same. They each have their advantages, and I can be equally happy in either.’ ” (p. 30).

Jane is attracted to him and finds him a good match: “ ‘He is just what a young man ought to be,’ said she, ‘sensible, good-humoured, lively; and I never saw such happy manners!–so much ease, with such perfect good breeding.’ ” (p. 9). Elizabeth agrees and teasingly says: “Well, he certainly is very agreeable, and I give you leave to like him.  You have liked many a stupider person.” (p. 9).

He is also “sensible” (p. 9) and dislikes arguments and tries to avoid them (p. 35), which shows that he is eager to be liked by everyone - just like Jane.

However, Bingley also has his own type of pride as he often talks about his loyalty to friends (p. 10), about being spontaneous, and about possessing a natural modesty (p. 139). Bingley also takes pride in being easy-going and sometimes careless, according to his friend Darcy:  “for you are really proud of your defects in writing, because you consider them as proceeding from a rapidity of thought and carelessness of execution, which, if not estimable, you think at least highly interesting” (p. 33).

At the same time, he is able to joke about his friend Darcy, suggesting that Bingley is aware of others’ flaws and is not always as naïve as other think him to be:

‘I assure you that if Darcy were not such a great tall fellow, in comparison with myself, I should not pay him half so much deference. I declare I do not know a more awful object than Darcy, on particular occasions, and in particular places; at his own house especially, and of a Sunday evening when he has nothing to do.’ (p. 35)

A trait that is both a virtue and a flaw is that he is easily influenced by others. The most important example of this is when Darcy and Bingley’s sisters convince him to leave Jane and go to London. Mr Darcy, in particular, convinces Bingley that Jane is not really in love with him. However, Bingley's affection for Jane doesn't change during his stay in London. He is constant in his emotions - unlike a character like Wickham.

After Darcy and Elizabeth talk about Jane and Bingley (in connection to Darcy’s first marriage proposal), Darcy changes his attitude and convinces Bingley that Jane loves him and that he should return to Netherfield. Bingley does return and starts to court Jane again until he eventually proposes.

The end of the story shows that, although Bingley is easy-going, he and Jane both recognise that it is not good for them to be so near the Bennets, and he buys an estate near Pemberley. This shows they can be wise when protecting their relationship: “Mr. Bingley and Jane remained at Netherfield only a twelvemonth. So near a vicinity to her mother and Meryton relations was not desirable even to his easy temper, or her affectionate heart.” (p. 270). You may say that this decision is the closest the two of them come to developing as characters in this story.
