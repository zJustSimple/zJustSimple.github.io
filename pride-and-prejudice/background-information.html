Background information

Published in 1813, Jane Austen’s novel, Pride and Prejudice is both written and set in Regency England. The Regency era refers to the time between 1811 and 1820 when King George III was considered unfit to rule, and his son ruled for him as Prince Regent. British society at that time is often described as interested in art but also by political and economic change, which was particularly triggered by the upheaval caused by the Napoleonic Wars (1803–1815). The wars are hinted at in Austen's novel through the presence of the militia officers, whom Lydia and Kitty are so crazy about.

This era saw a rise in the popularity of novels. This popularity was fuelled by increasing ease of printing and distribution, as well as increasingly widespread literacy, particularly among women. However, novels during this time were generally focused on the upper classes of society. We see this in Austen too; we never encounter the working class in her novels.

Regency era society was very stratified, meaning that there was a large gap between the very poor and the very rich. The issue of class is key to Pride and Prejudice, as it is to Austen’s other novels. The nuances of the class system in Regency England can sometimes be difficult for us to understand today.

Austen's novel gives a realistic depiction of rural upper-class life in the early 19th century. All of Austen’s novels focus on the gentry, meaning the land-owning class who did not work for a living but instead relied on income from their property. However, they were generally not members of the aristocracy, the social class who had titles and land that had been owned by their families for many generations. The Bennets belong to the gentry whereas Bingley and particularly Darcy represent the upper class. 

By describing the relationship which the characters of Pride and Prejudice have with one another, Austen shows us that there were many different levels of the gentry and that members of the gentry also sometimes interacted with people from other social classes. We see this in the relationships the Bennet family has with Mr Darcy, Mr Bingley, Sir William Lucas, and Mrs Bennet’s siblings, who all have different social statuses.

Regency society was also very different from today in terms of gender roles. For example, women rarely owned land as it was the custom for land to be passed through the male line only. This is why Mr Collins is supposed to inherit the Bennet estate in the novel, rather than Mr Bennet’s wife and daughters. Gentry women were also not expected to work, but they were definitely expected to marry. In many cases, as the novel suggests, marriage was the only way for an adult woman to secure a home and means of support for herself. Otherwise she would end up as a burden to her male relatives.

Jane Austen herself suffered from the restrictive role given to women. She was unable to publish under her own name and therefore published her works anonymously. Furthermore, to publish, she needed the consent of a male relative.

However, it is notable that Jane Austen herself, despite recognising the unfair position of many women in society, had relatively conservative political views. Although she highlights the difficulties of women in her novels, and despite never marrying herself, all of her novels end with the marriage of her central characters, rather than offering an alternative way of life for her female protagonists. Furthermore, although some of her characters, like Elizabeth Bennet, push at the boundaries of their class, the distinction between the nobility, the gentry, and the lower classes remains fixed and the significance of her characters’ class is confirmed throughout her writings.

This can partly be explained by the idea that Jane Austen was part of a conservative movement which indirectly responded to the recent class upheaval that had happened in France during the French Revolution (1789). In this revolution, class distinctions were destroyed as members of the aristocracy were brutally decapitated by lower class rebels. The response in Britain was generally to reinforce ideas of class to discourage the lower classes from rising up. Preserving order was important.
