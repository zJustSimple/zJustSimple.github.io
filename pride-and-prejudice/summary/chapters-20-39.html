Chapter 20

Mrs Bennet finds out about Elizabeth’s refusal to marry Mr Collins and angrily asks her husband to convince Elizabeth to accept. However, Mr Bennet tells his daughter that he does not want her to marry someone like Collins. Mrs Bennet continues to pressure Elizabeth to say yes. In the midst of all of the confusion, Charlotte Lucas comes to visit. Mr Collins finally accepts Elizabeth’s refusal, but is extremely offended.

Chapter 21

Mr Collins starts ignoring Elizabeth and paying attention to Charlotte. The Bennet sisters walk to Meryton where Elizabeth meets Wickham and speaks to him. At home, Jane finds a letter from Caroline Bingley saying that Mr Bingley will be in London indefinitely and suggesting he might marry Georgiana Darcy. Elizabeth tells Jane that the jealous Caroline is probably trying to manipulate her brother, but that he will surely return because he is in love with Jane.

Chapter 22

Mr Collins proposes to Charlotte, who accepts his offer. He leaves without telling the Bennets about his engagement. However, Charlotte soon tells Elizabeth that she has agreed to marry Mr Collins because she will have a good home with him. Elizabeth is shocked and disappointed that her best friend has chosen to marry someone as silly as Mr Collins and only for material reasons. However, she hides her thoughts from Charlotte.

Chapter 23

When Sir William Lucas announces Charlotte and Collins' engagement to the Bennet family, Mrs Bennet is surprised and refuses to believe it. She also gets angry with Elizabeth again for refusing Collins’ proposal and generally feels very sorry for herself. At the same time, Jane and Elizabeth are worried because Mr Bingley has not written to Jane yet. Mr Collins returns to the estate, but spends most of his time at the Lucas household, courting his future wife. Elizabeth also feels that she and Charlotte will never be close friends again.

Chapter 24

Caroline Bingley sends a new letter to Jane, announcing that they will be in London for the winter. The gentle Jane admits to Elizabeth that their mother’s continued mentions of Mr Bingley are very painful to her, now that Bingley's interest in her seems to have gone. Elizabeth continues to comfort her. Wickham, who comes more often to visit the Bennet family, ends up telling all of them – and their acquaintances – his version of his conflict with Darcy.

Chapter 25

Mr Collins finally leaves Longbourn. The Bennets receive a visit from the Gardiners – Mrs Bennet’s brother and his wife – who propose that Jane go back to London with them once they learn about her heartbreak. Elizabeth hopes Jane might meet Bingley there. The Gardiners also notice that Elizabeth is attracted to Wickham.

Chapter 26

Mrs Gardiner warns Elizabeth about becoming attracted to a man with as low financial prospects as Wickham. Elizabeth promises to be cautious, but does not admit to either liking or disliking Wickham. Jane leaves for London with the Gardiners, and Collins returns for his wedding with Charlotte. Charlotte makes Elizabeth promise she will visit her at her new home with Mr Collins in Hunsford. 

Elizabeth finds out from a letter from Jane that Caroline Bingley treated Jane quite badly when they finally met in London. Elizabeth writes to Mrs Gardiner telling her that Wickham is now attached to a wealthy heiress, concluding that she does not love him because she feels no resentment.

Chapter 27

After a long winter, Elizabeth and Wickham part ways in a friendly manner. She then goes to visit Mr and Mrs Collins, together with Sir William Lucas and Charlotte’s sister Maria. On their way, they visit Jane in London. Elizabeth finds out from Mrs Gardiner that Jane is frequently unhappy due her broken heart. They also discuss the economic reasons behind Mr Wickham’s new choice. The Gardiners invite Elizabeth to join them in a country trip during the summer.

Chapter 28

When the party arrives at Hunsford, Mr Collins greets them in his usual overly formal way. Charlotte enjoys managing the house, and Elizabeth finds that her friend is doing quite well as Mrs Collins.

Lady Catherine’s daughter drives past the Collins’ house and invites them to dinner at the manor of Rosings Park. Elizabeth notices how sickly the daughter looks and thinks about Darcy’s future with her.

Chapter 29

Mr Collins tells his guests about how wonderful Rosings is, making them nervous about the posh dinner. They finally go to dinner and meet Lady Catherine, who comes across as imposing and condescending. After dinner, Lady Catherine shares her opinion on many topics and constantly advises Charlotte on how to manage her household. She then rudely asks Elizabeth a series of rather private questions about her family, education, and social status.

Chapter 30

Sir William Lucas leaves Hunsford, but Elizabeth stays on to keep her friend Charlotte company. The Collins family and Elizabeth dine at Rosings regularly, and all the dinners follow the same boring pattern. Elizabeth finds out that Darcy is to visit his aunt and looks forward to seeing him again, especially because she is curious about how he will interact with Miss de Bourgh, his fiancee, according to Lady Catherine.

Darcy arrives, bringing his cousin, Colonel Fitzwilliam. The two men immediately visit Elizabeth and Charlotte. Elizabeth asks Darcy about seeing Jane in London, but he does not seem to have known that Jane was in London.

Chapter 31

When Elizabeth and Mrs and Mr Collins next go for dinner at Rosings, Elizabeth enjoys talking with Colonel Fitzwilliam, who is a pleasant and entertaining sort of man, but Lady Catherine interrupts them because she feels left out. Darcy seems ashamed of his aunt’s rude behaviour. When Elizabeth plays the piano, Darcy approaches her, and she teases him for not practising his social skills. Lady Catherine intervenes again, and she ends up criticising Elizabeth’s piano skills.

Chapter 32

The next day, Darcy pays a visit to the Collins’ home and finds Elizabeth alone. Their conversation is awkward, and he leaves shortly after Charlotte and her sister return to the house. Charlotte begins to think that Darcy is in love with Elizabeth, but Elizabeth rejects this theory as ridiculous.

Darcy and Fitzwilliam visit the Collins household frequently after this, which Elizabeth does not understand. Elizabeth compares Colonel Fitzwilliam to Wickham, and Charlotte speculates about Elizabeth marrying Colonel Fitzwilliam.

Chapter 33

Darcy and Elizabeth frequently meet unintentionally in the park, where Darcy always offers to accompany her home, despite saying very little. Darcy suggests that she might one day return to Rosings as a guest, and Elizabeth assumes that he might believe that Colonel Fitzwilliam wants to marry her.

When Elizabeth meets Colonel Fitzwilliam and starts talking about needing to marry a woman with a good income, she takes this as an indirect apology for not making an offer of marriage to her. He also reveals to her that Darcy has boasted about saving a friend from marrying an unsuitable woman, and both Elizabeth and the Colonel assume it to be Bingley. Distressed for her sister to the point of crying, Elizabeth develops a headache and refuses to go to Rosings for dinner that night.

Chapter 34

Darcy comes to asks Elizabeth about her health, acting strangely. Suddenly, he blurts out that he loves her, but then moves on to point out in detail the difference in their social status and the objectionable qualities of Elizabeth’s family since this has kept him silent on the topic until now. Offended, Elizabeth refuses his marriage proposal. She tells him that she cannot marry someone who is so arrogant, who separated her sister from Bingley, and who treated Wickham so badly. 

Darcy, who has been expecting her to accept, gets angry and argues that Elizabeth seems mostly offended by the honest way of his proposal, indicating that she is shallow. Elizabeth denies it and accuses him of being ungentlemanly, and he leaves. Elizabeth starts crying. Although she is flattered by his proposal, she is also unable to forgive his pride and his actions towards Wickham and Jane.

Chapter 35

Walking in the park the next morning, Elizabeth comes across Darcy, who hands her a letter. In the letter, he addresses the accusations Elizabeth made. He explains that he separated Bingley from Jane because he thought Jane’s feelings were not sincere, just as he stresses the tactless behaviour of Jane's family in general. He also tells her the truth about Wickham: Wickham refused the clergy position and quickly spent his inheritance on other things. After Darcy refused to give him more money, Wickham tried to elope with Darcy's wealthy little sister Georgiana, but Darcy caught them in time.

Chapter 36

After reading the letter several times, Elizabeth is sceptical about Mr Darcy’s account. However, thinking more carefully about it and reanalysing Wickham’s attitude, she starts to think that Darcy might be telling the truth. She becomes ashamed of her prejudices and vanity. She also realises that Jane might have seemed aloof to those who do not know her and that Darcy is to some extent right in his accusations about her family’s poor behaviour. When she arrives at Charlotte’s house, she discovers that Darcy and Colonel Fitzwilliam have left Rosings Park.

Chapter 37

When Lady Catherine invites Elizabeth and the Collins family to dinner, Elizabeth amuses herself thinking she could have become the Lady’s niece through marriage. Lady Catherine presses her to stay longer, but Elizabeth is determined to return home with Charlotte’s sister Maria. She spends a few days thinking about Darcy’s letter and behaviour. She feels bad about misjudging him but not about rejecting him. She thinks about how her parents allow Lydia and Kitty to act almost frivolously and how they might thereby have ruined Jane’s chance at a happy marriage.

Chapter 38

Before Elizabeth leaves, Mr Collins triumphantly speaks to her about how happy Charlotte is to be his wife and the good fortune of their situation. One the way home, Elizabeth and Charlotte’s sister stop in London and take Jane home with them. They are also met by Lydia and Kitty. Elizabeth wants to tell Jane what happened with Darcy, but decides to postpone the news until they are alone.

Chapter 39

At home, Jane and Elizabeth find out that the officers are leaving, to Kitty and Lydia’s disappointment. Lydia hopes to convince her parents to let her go to Brighton where the officers will be stationed next.

Additionally, Lydia tells Elizabeth that Wickham is no longer going to marry the rich girl he was courting. Lydia wants to walk to Meryton, but Elizabeth refuses to join her because she does not want to see Wickham again and because she wants to end her family’s association with the men of the militia. Darcy's letter has made Elizabeth painfully conscious of the importance of a good reputation.
