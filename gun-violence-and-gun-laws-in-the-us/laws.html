Gun laws in the US

Cuprins
  • General aspects
  • Federal laws
  • State laws
  • Conflict between state and federal laws

General aspects

The United States have a federal system of government . This means that governing powers are divided between national and state governments. Each level of government has full autonomy to pass laws and rules in certain areas while in other areas they share power. For instance, only the federal government can declare war, but both levels have authority regarding tax law.

Between the 1980 and 2001, the government in the US was characterized by what is called “New Federalism” . This type of federalism is about gradually giving states more autonomy and power.

Judicial courts play an important role in the US federal system because court rulings become law. The highest judiciary body in the US is the Supreme Court  which functions at a federal level. The Supreme Court is not only the highest court of appeal but also the main authority in interpreting the US Constitution. 

Gun legislation  is an area in which state and national governments share power. Gun laws are concerned with the production, trade, ownership, transfer, registration, and destruction of firearms, as well as regulation regarding ammunition and weapon accessories. They are enforced by both the federal Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives and local state agencies.

Federal laws

There are over a dozen federal laws that include references to gun laws in the US.

For example, the 1934 National Firearms Act established the taxation of production and transfer of weapons such as machine guns, heavy weapons, and certain types of rifles.

Another example is the Federal Firearms Act of 1938 which, among others, requires a firearm license and bans the transfer of weapons to convicted felons.

A more recent federal law is the 1990 Gun Free School Zones Act that bans individuals from carrying a gun into a school area. Also, the Brady Handgun Violence Prevention Act from 1993 requires background checks for those who want to purchase firearms.  

A 1994 law – the Federal Assault Weapons Ban – blocked the sales of semiautomatic guns that looked like assault weapons. However, the law expired in 2004.

Also, in 2005, a law was passed which relieved gun sellers of any responsibility if the guns they sold were used to commits crimes.

As you can see, federal laws vary in their aims: some of them aim at introducing gun control measures, while others protect certain gun rights. 

Because of the way the US government works -  and the general disagreements between Democrats and Republicans regarding gun laws  - it is often difficult to pass new federal laws about guns . For example, if the country has a Democratic President, but the Republicans happen to be in control of the Senate and/or the House of Representatives, it is unlikely that any of the President's proposed laws will actually be approved by the Senate and the House. This is one of the main problems that Barack Obama faced during his presidency. 

State laws

States also have the right to regulate certain aspects regarding gun ownership and use. In many instances, state gun laws are less restrictive than federal ones. Here are some examples of common gun ownership aspects that are regulated at a state level:

  • Obtaining a license or permit to buy or own firearms.
  • Registering firearms with the police.
  • Laws regarding open carry and concealed carry.
  • Restrictions regarding different types of weapons.
  • Stand-your-ground laws, which allow shooting someone in self-defense, even if it would have been possible to escape the situation instead.
  • Requiring background checks for buying a weapon.
  • Red-flag laws which allow judges to temporarily confiscate arms from people that are deemed a threat to others.

Around 12 states require purchase permits for handguns. Other states only require an ownership permit, while others have no permit requirements for handguns.

States also have laws regarding what kind of weapons are allowed to be carried in public, with many states banning concealed carry, which is associated with criminals. Some exceptions are Alaska, Arizona, Kansas, New Hampshire, and Maine which have recently allowed unrestricted concealed carry for residents. States can also establish gun free zones, where it is illegal to carry concealed weapons.

Some of the states with the most restrictive gun laws are California , New Jersey , Massachusetts , New York , and Connecticut . At the opposite end, some of the states with the most relaxed gun laws are Utah , Alaska , Arizona , Oklahoma , and North Dakota .

Conflict between state and federal laws

Because the power to pass gun laws is shared between federal and state governments, sometimes conflict arises between different types of laws.

The right to keep and bear arms is guaranteed by the  Second Amendment of the US Constitution, but because of the language used in the text, there is a considerable legal debate about how the amendment should be interpreted.

For example, it was not until the District of Columbia v. Heller decision in 2008  that an individual right to keep and bear arms was clearly established. The Supreme Court ruled against Washington D.C.'s handgun ban and requirement to keep guns unloaded or blocked by trigger lock in the house. The Court decided that this ban violated people’s right to keep and bear arms and decided that this right is not connected to being part of a militia.

A similar case that showed the conflict between state and federal laws was the 2010 McDonald v. Chicago trial . The Supreme Court ruled against a Chicago ordinance prohibiting the ownership of handguns. The Court established that both the  Second and the Fourth Amendments guarantee that people’s right to keep arms is protected against the states.

Other similar trials continue to show the conflict between state and federal laws regarding guns, leading to an ongoing debate about who should regulate gun ownership. Some argue that states should be able to legislate the matter while others argue that it is the federal government that has this right because gun ownership is part of the US Constitution.
