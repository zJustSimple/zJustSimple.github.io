The apartheid regime in South Africa

The apartheid regime in South Africa was a legally implemented system of racial segregation, discriminating against the native black African population. The system was implemented from 1948 to 1990 and was very similar to the Jim Crow laws in the United States.

Under the apartheid law, black and mixed-race South Africans had to use separate public facilities from the white (European-descended) population. Like in the US, black South Africans would have to attend separate schools, shop separately, use different toilets and hotels, sit on designated benches, and be treated at separate hospitals. As in the US, these separate facilities were often in poorer conditions than those designated for the white population.

In the long run, segregation policies led to black South Africans being discriminated against on the job market, receiving worse education, and being generally poorer compared to the white population. What is more, unlike in the US, the black population was also compelled to learn Afrikaans in schools, a language derived from Dutch which was foreign to many of them. Also, black South Africans were forcibly removed from their residential areas and moved to live in areas established by the authorities.

Inspired by the civil rights movement in the US, and especially by Martin Luther King, black and mixed-race South Africans tried to fight the apartheid system in the 1960s and 1970s. One of the of the early leaders of the anti-apartheid groups was Nelson Mandela, who was sentenced to life in prison for treason.

Unlike in the US, the militant actions of anti-apartheid activists were not always primarily focused on non-violence. As the African National Congress (ANC) party failed to secure any rights for black South Africans via negotiations and non-violent actions, a military faction was created to fight for black South Africans’ rights. However, these militant actions were typically crushed violently by the government.

It was not until 1990s that the apartheid regime ended after numerous negotiations between black leaders and the white authorities. During this time, Nelson Mandela was freed, and he went to become the first democratic president of South Africa in 1994.

The Stolen Generation in Australia

The Stolen Generation in Australia refers to children of Aboriginal descent being taken from their families by the Australian Federal and State government agencies and placed to be raised in state institutions and religious missions. These removals of mixed race children were enforced between 1905 and 1967.

The policy was implemented because European-origin Australians believed themselves to be superior to aboriginals and thought that it would be best if mixed-race children were raised in a ‘civilized manner’ that would preserve Western civilization. In other words, the policy was a result of racial discrimination and prejudice.

In practice, the policy rarely achieved its goals of integrating mixed-race children with white Australian society. Often, these children were later rejected by white people and could not return to their aboriginal families because they had no information about them.

The policy formally ended in 1967 following a national referendum to modify the Constitution to create laws for Aboriginal people. It was not until 2007 that the Australian Government offered an official apology for the child removal practice. Today, the subject is still highly debated in Australian society and its effects are still visible.

At the same time, it was not until 1962 that Aboriginals were explicitly given the right to vote. Until then, Aboriginals were theoretically allowed to vote, but they rarely did in practice as they tended to live separately from mainstream society.
