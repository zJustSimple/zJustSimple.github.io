Other struggles for civil rights

Women’s rights

The women’s rights movement in the US began in the second half of the 19thcentury when activists systematically began to advocate and lobby for women’s suffrage (right to vote).

Women’s suffrage associations formed starting 1869 and merged into a single big association in 1890. During this time, women’s voting rights were typically advocated through state and national conventions, but also through peaceful marches and protests.

In 1869, activists lobbied for the Fifteen Amendment of the US Constitution, granting African Americans voting rights to also include women, but without success. However, in the following years, suffrage activists managed to obtain voting and civil rights for women in several states. The movement for women’s suffrage was met with opposition from many politicians and even from some women.

By 1916, women’s suffrage became a national issue thanks to strong activism. Following World War I, all women were granted voting rights in 1920 through the Nineteenth Amendment to the US Constitution.

The women’s rights movement was also supported by the civil rights movement as the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights Act of 1965 not only banned discrimination against African Americans but also discrimination based on gender, religion, or ethnic background.

 

Native American rights

Native Americans in the US are considered both citizens of the US and of their tribal nations, a status which has created tension in the past and continues to be challenging today.

Like the African-American community, Native Americans did not enjoy equal civil rights officially in the US until after the civil rights movement. It was in 1968 that the US passed the Indian Civil Rights Act (ICRA), granting Native Americans rights like freedom of speech and assembly, right to an attorney if accused of a crime, and equal protection in the justice system.

Until then, Native Americans had to face a series of deprivations of their rights. Starting with the 1830s, tribes were forcibly removed from their lands. By 1871, they were forced to live in reservations, and their tribes were no longer recognized as nations. It was only in 1924 that Native Americans were declared US citizens and were given the right to vote, but it took until 1948 for every state to implement the measure.

Unlike African Americans who created a nationwide movement to demand and defend their civil rights, Native Americans were a much smaller minority and could not advocate for their rights in the same way as African Americans did.

 

LGBTQ rights

The LGBTQ community in the US discretely began fighting for civil rights in the 1950s with the formation of the first gay associations, at a time when homosexuality was still a criminal offence in all US states. The first state to decriminalize homosexuality was Illinois in 1962.

The first gay riots took place in 1969 in New York, giving the gay rights movement visibility.

During the 1970s, gay rights gained prominence through the political activity of Harvey Milk, a gay official. Open activism culminated with the 1979 National March on Washington for Lesbian and Gay Rights, which was probably inspired by the 1963 March on Washington during the civil rights movement.

Throughout the 1980s and 1990s, more states decriminalized homosexuality and some also outlawed discrimination based on sexual orientation.

In 1993, another March on Washington was organized for gay rights, gathering over 800,000 people. By the 2000s, US states begin to recognize civil unions between gay people, culminating with a 2015 Supreme Court decision which extended the right for same-sex couples to marry nationwide.
