Glossary

On this page, you can find words and phrases that are particularly useful to know when studying the civil rights movement. Click each word for a full definition in English, including information about the way the word is specifically relevant in the context of civil rights.

Abolition

The act of ending a practice, system or institution. The word is often used specifically in connection with ending slavery in countries like the US, but it also has a more general meaning.

Boycott 

Refusal to buy a product or use a service. During the civil rights movement, activists boycotted buses (which had segregated seating) and discriminatory businesses to advance their cause.

Black Codes 

A series of state laws implemented after the American Civil War which restricted African Americans’ rights to vote, to own property, to gain employment, or to improve their economic status.

Black Nationalism 

An ideology advocating a separate national status for black people in the US. Black nationalists encouraged African Americans to take pride in their cultural heritage and refuse to integrate into mainstream white American society.

Black Power 

A US movement which developed in the 1960s and 1970s, advocating for rights and political power for African Americans. The Black Power movement often disagreed with the civil rights movement on the proper methods of protesting, as many of its members advocated using militant methods in the struggle against discrimination. 

Black Panther Party

A political organization founded in the US in the 1960s with the purpose of defending African-American rights and patrolling neighborhoods to reduce police brutality (which sometimes resulted in violent clashes with the police). The group also promoted social community programs. 

Civil disobedience 

A non-violent form of political protest usually manifested by refusing to respect certain laws or refusing to pay taxes and fines. Along with other non-violent means, civil disobedience was promoted and encouraged by civil rights leaders in the US.

Civil rights 

Citizens' rights to freedom and equality in society and politics. After slavery was abolished and amendments were made to the US Constitution to grant African Americans civil rights, many state governments gradually took these rights away and instituted a system of racial segregation. This led to the African-American civil rights movement from the 1950s and 1960s.

Constitution 

The US Constitution is the foundation of US law. The original Constitution from when the nation was formed is in many ways still in effect, though a number of Amendments have been added to update it over time. Sometimes the laws of individual states are challenged with reference to the Constitution - the Supreme Court then decides whether the law in question is constitutional or unconstitutional. These cases tend to have dramatic effect on future policies as well - they are usually referred to with names such as 'Browder v. Gayle' or 'Brown v. Board of Education' that describe both parties of the case.

CORE 

A shortened form of the Congress of Racial Equality, a civil rights organization founded in 1942 whose goals were to promote equality regardless of ethnicity, religion, gender, age, disability or sexual orientation.

Disenfranchisement 

Being deprived of a right or privilege. The term refers to African Americans being deprived of their right to vote and other civil rights after three amendments to the US Constitution granted them those rights. The Disenfranchisement period started after 1877 and culminated with a 1896 Supreme Court decision which established segregation.

Freedom Rides

Refers to civil rights activists ('Freedom Riders') riding interstate buses in racially mixed groups to show that segregation was still practiced in these means of transport although it had been declared unconstitutional.

Jim Crow laws 

Term used to describe the laws that dictated segregation and discrimination of African Americans in the US between 1896 and 1965. 'Jim Crow' was a negative term used to describe an African American person, supposedly inspired by a song called ‘Jump Jim Crow’, which mocked a disabled African slave. 

Ku Klux Klan

The KKK was an extremist secret society in the US, advocating white supremacy and conducting violent attacks on non-white Americans. The society was formed as a response to the abolition of slavery and emancipation laws. Various groups which lay claim to the KKK name still exist in the US today.

Lynching 

The act of a group or mob killing someone in public without a trial, usually by hanging. Lynching events were common prior to and during the civil rights movement. Typically, white crowds would lynch African Americans suspected of committing crimes against white people.

March (s)

In the context of civil rights, a march is a walk undertaken by a large group of people, used as a means of protest. The term is used to describe several protests during the civil rights movement, when activists would walk to a significant place to demonstrate against injustice. Famous civil rights marches include the March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom and the Selma to Montgomery marches. 

NAACP Acronym for the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People. This political organization was formed in 1908, one of the first to advocate for civil rights for African Americans in the US. The NAACP used lawsuits as a main way to fight discrimination and racial segregation. They played a major role in the civil rights movement. 

Non-violence 

Using peaceful means to advocate political or social change. Non-violent protesters typically reject violence even when aggression is used against them. Numerous civil rights activists encouraged protesters to answer nonviolently to authorities.

Riot 

A violent public disturbance usually involving a group of people, who typically damage property or fight with authorities. The civil rights movement included various riots triggered by frustrations among African American communities because of economic inequalities and the way they were treated by the authorities. Riots were also caused by white Americans as a reaction to the civil rights movement.

SCLC

Acronym for the Southern Christian Leadership Conference, an organization set up by Martin Luther King to promote the civil rights movement by gaining the support of churches and religious organizations across the US.

Segregation

A system that provides separate facilities or residential areas for separate groups; often involving enforced separation between them. The term is often used in the context of racial segregation whereby certain racial groups are not allowed to share the same facilities as white people, a discriminatory practice.

Sit-in 

A type of protest in which people occupy a place and refuse to leave until their demands are met. The practice was used by black students who would sit at "white-only" lunch counters and other segregated public places until they were served.

SNCC 

Acronym for the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee, an American civil-rights organization formed by students, whose objective was to promote political and economic equality for African Americans. In spoken language, the acronym was often pronounced 'SNIK'.

'Separate but equal'

A doctrine enforced by a 1896 US Supreme Court decision, which claimed that separate facilities for African Americans did not violate the constitution and were not a form of discrimination.
