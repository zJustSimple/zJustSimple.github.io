Achievements of the civil rights movement

Introduction

The main achievements of the civil rights movement are the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and the Voting Rights act of 1965. However, many of the actions and events during the civil rights movement led to a number of minor victories which paved the way for the two legislative acts that secured the civil rights of African Americans.
For example, the Montgomery Bus Boycott eventually led to the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People (NAACP) winning a Supreme Court Decision in 1956 to desegregate the bus system in 1956.
In 1957, a Civil Rights Act was passed making it possible for the Attorney General to sue on behalf of persons whose voting rights were not respected. The act also created the Commission on Civil Rights which was meant to investigate voting rights violations.
In 1960, a Civil Rights Act allowed federal courts to appoint referees to supervize voter registration in places where voter discrimination was practiced.
Freedom Rides lead to desegregating the interstate buses and facilities. The 1963 Birmingham Campaign managed to end segregation in the city of Birmingham. The 1963 March on Washington raised global awareness about the civil rights movement in the US and inspired similar actions in other countries.

The Civil Rights Act of 1964

The Civil Rights Act of 1964 officially bans discrimination based on race, color, religion, gender, or national origin in the US. The act outlaws racial segregation in schools, workplaces, and public facilities, as well as the unequal application of voter rights.

The legislation was initially proposed by President John F. Kennedy in 1963 but was rejected in the Senate. John F. Kennedy was persuaded to propose the legislation by the Birmingham Campaign and the growing number of protests across the US. 

After the Kennedy was assassinated, his successor President Lyndon B. Johnson pushed for the bill. It was eventually approved by the US Congress, through numerous lobbying actions and legislative tactics.

The act was received as the greatest achievement of the movement by civil rights leaders. However, the way the act was phrased also secured equal rights for women and other minorities. Finally,  the act also paved the way for subsequent civil rights legislative changes that would benefit many other minorities as well as African Americans, including the Voting Rights Act of 1965 and the Civil Rights Act of 1968.

The Voting Rights Act of 1965

The Voting Rights Act of 1965 is a federal act that bans racial discrimination when voting. The purpose of the act was to secure the voting rights that were already established by the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments of the US Constitution, which were often violated in practice in many of the Southern states.

The act did not only include African Americans but all minorities. It also contains numerous specifications such as banning any law that results in the discrimination of minorities or requires passing literacy tests to be able to register as a voter.

The act was a result of pressure from civil rights organizations like the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC) and the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC), following Lyndon B. Johnson’s election as President of the US.

Because African-American voters in Selma, Alabama were forcibly prevented by the police from exercising their rights, the SCLC and other civil rights organizations organised a series of protests that were met with police violence. The media coverage, along with a speech by Malcolm X inciting activists to violence and an open letter from Martin Luther King Jr., who was jailed in Selma, pressured President Lyndon B. Johnson to push for the Voting Rights Act in the Congress.

The law had immediate positive effects, decreasing voter discrimination and increasing the number of minority voters. However, in the following years, the act had to be amended several times because it was observed that it could be used to reduce the power of the minority vote through different changes such as manipulation of voter districts (a practice known as gerrymandering). 

Another long-term effect of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 (which was enacted by a Democratic president) is believed to be the shifting of party alliances. Whereas until this point the Republican Party typically attracted African American voters, this began to change as African American citizens began voting for liberals in the Democratic Party and white voters in the South began favouring the Republican Party. 

The Civil Rights Act of 1968

Less well-known compared to the two acts mentioned above, the Civil Rights Act of 1968 was designed to ensure equal housing opportunities for all Americans regardless of ethnicity, religion, or national origin.

The act was signed by President Lyndon B. Johnson during the riots following Martin Luther King’s assassination.

Also known as the Fair Housing Act, the document allowed victims of discrimination related to renting, buying, or financing the purchase of a property to seek justice in court.
