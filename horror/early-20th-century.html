Horror in the early 20th century

Cuprins
  • Early horror movies
  • Pulp magazines
    • H.P. Lovecraft

Early horror movies

In the first decades of the 20th century, movies gradually became a more mainstream medium, and some of the most famous early cinematic movies were based on the iconic gothic horror stories of the previous century. Thus movies like Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde (1908) and Frankenstein (1910) suddenly brought a new kind of life and fame to these stories. Later on, the Hollywood-based Universal Pictures studies began to systematically adapt classic horror stories from the previous century, resulting in works such as Dracula (1931) and The Invisible Man (1933).

German cinema also had strong influence on the horror genre. A particular highlight here was Nosferatu (1922) – also a (loose) Dracula adaptation – which helped introduce many of the horror devices we still see in movies today, such as long shadows and atmospheric backgrounds. You can watch an English version of Nosferatu here.

It is important to note that these early horror movies typically focused on gradually building suspense, as well as the creation of a scary atmosphere - they did not typically feature the gore and jump scares that many associate with modern horror movies. The earliest horror movies also faced the limitation of being silent movies - with sound movies not becoming widespread until the early 1930s. Even so, the horror movies of the early 20th century still shocked contemporary audiences, and were often marketed in a way that suggested terror was the main appeal. 

Pulp magazines

Another medium which breathed new life into old gothic horror stories - and gave rise to some notable new ones - were the American pulp magazines, which started to emerge in the 1920s. The most famous of these was Weird Tales, whose first issue was released in 1923. These magazines meant that a broad audience could get cheap and regular access to horror stories. Their name derives from the fact that they were printed on cheap wood pulp paper.

The magazines typically contained a mix of retold classic tales - often with scary or gory imagery alongside them - and all-new stories in the horror, fantasy or science fiction genres.

Pulp magazines started to decline in popularity towards the end of the 1930s, and in the years of WW2 there was little literary interest in the horror genre - perhaps because of the real-life horrors of war and genocide that the world was facing at the time.

H.P. Lovecraft

Perhaps the most famous horror writer to emerge in the era of pulp magazines was H.P. Lovecraft. Though the fact that his work first appeared in pulp magazines - which had a reputation for not being serious literature - meant that he was not taken very seriously as a writer in his own time, he later went on to gain more professional recognition. Today he is regarded as hugely influential writer of horror, who has been an enormous inspiration for later horror writers - and whose iconic characters (and monsters) often show up in popular culture as well.

Lovecraft’s stories typically take place in a shared fictional universe known as the Cthulhu Mythos. The main idea of this setting is that our world is full of terrible beings beyond human understanding - and sometimes even to learn of such beings can drive a person insane. Lovecraft’s protagonists are often seekers of forbidden knowledge, who meet terrible fates as they learn more and more about the dark truths of reality.

This type of story and setting became so popular that Lovecraft pioneered the genre known as cosmic horror (also known as Lovecraftian horror).

Lovecraft’s most famous story is “The Call of Cthulhu” (first published in Weird Tales, 1928), the tale of a terrible, ancient being which sleeps deep beneath the ocean and touches the dreams of madmen with prophetic signs that it will someday rise again and consume everything.
