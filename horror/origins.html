The origins of horror stories

Cuprins
  • Early human history
  • The Middle Ages and Early Modernity
  • Elizabethan England and Shakespeare

Early human history

It is impossible to point to a specific point in time when human beings started telling frightening stories to each other. Horror elements have likely been part of human folklore for thousands of years, probably even before the invention of writing. The Epic of Gilgamesh, one of the first preserved written stories dating back to around 2100 BC, contains descriptions of violent encounters with terrifying monsters, suggesting that people in the ancient world also enjoyed the thrill of terror.

Ancient writers from Greece and Rome also produced numerous horror stories, which often featured elements we still associate with horror to this day - such as haunted houses, werewolves, monsters and visits to the Underworld.

The Middle Ages and Early Modernity

During the Middle Ages in Europe, many of the popular horror stories were linked to Christianity in some way. The Bible describes devils and demons who seek to tempt or corrupt humanity, as well as the horrors of Hell and Purgatory.

Famous poets such as Dante Alighieri (1265-1321) and John Milton (1608-1674) expanded upon the ideas found in the Bible to create even more gruesome depictions of Satan and Hell, which were enormously influential on popular ideas about these horrors.

Because these stories were tied to religion, the line between fact and fiction was often blurred, and many people believed that the monsters and horrors described by Christian stories were real and had the power to hurt them. These fears were also made evident in a widespread fear of witches, for example, which would eventually lead to the infamous waves of witch trials in the Early Modern period, during which many innocent people were executed - most of them women.

Many classic European folk tales also contain strong elements of horror and can be surprisingly graphic, even though some of them have since been modified to become milder in tone. A good example of a dark folk tale is “The Tale of The Juniper Tree”, collected by the Brothers Grimm. The story features an evil stepmother who brutally murders her stepson, makes his sister believe she is guilty of the murder, and then bakes the dead boy into a pudding and serves it to the boy’s father, who proceeds to eat his own son. You can read an English translation of the story here.

Elizabethan England and Shakespeare

Horror elements were common in Elizabethan theatre, and the works of William Shakespeare were no exception. Many of Shakespeare’s most famous plays contain elements that would not seem out of place in modern horror stories.

Ghostly beings are featured in both Hamlet and Macbeth, for example, and the latter also famously includes a trio of witches that drive Macbeth towards evil deeds. The Tempest features a sorcerer who controls spirits, as well as the deformed monster Caliban. Though missing any supernatural elements, Titus Andronicus might also be regarded as a horror story because of its extreme focus on violence, and its graphic depictions of rape, mutilation, murder and cannibalism.

Shakespeare was not the only Elizabethan playwright who dealt in dark themes, however. Another famous play from the period was Doctor Faustus by Christopher Marlowe, the story of a man who makes a deal with the devil in exchange for magical powers, but damns his own soul in the process and meets a horrible end. The story of Faustus has had an immense impact on popular culture and is still frequently referenced in both literature and other media (note that Marlowe did not come up with the story of Doctor Faustus, but he did help bring it to a much wider audience).
