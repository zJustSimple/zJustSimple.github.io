Definition of gothic horror

Gothic horror is a particular type of horror literature which was especially popular in the 18th and 19th centuries. Though there were many different gothic writers, the stories typically have some characteristics in common.

First of all, the stories often feature a dark and mysterious setting, which is typically described in detail to draw the reader into the strange universe of the story. Typical settings are ancient, gloomy castles full of dark corridors and secret passageways. In fact, this is how the genre got its name - gothic architecture is typical of medieval castles in Europe.

Secondly, the stories often rely on mixing the emotions of wonder and terror, making them pleasant to read despite the darkness they contain. For example, the setting might be described as strangely fascinating despite its dark elements, or there might be something oddly attractive – or even romantic - about the monstrous beings lurking in the shadows.  

Thirdly, the stories often feature supernatural elements, and things that turn out to be different from what they seem. Often these supernatural elements are only gradually revealed to the reader, just as the main character of the story gradually becomes aware of them. In later gothic stories - especially the works of Edgar Allan Poe - it is sometimes an open question whether the supernatural elements are real, or whether they are simply a product of the main character’s growing madness.

The first gothic horror stories

Though stories with gothic elements started to appear in the first half of the 18th century, the first true gothic novel is thought to be The Castle of Otranto (1764) by Horace Walpole (which was even subtitled “A Gothic Story” in some editions). As the title suggests, the main setting of the novel is a gothic castle, which even appears to be haunted.

Walpole’s work inspired writers such as Clara Reeve, famous for The Old English Baron (1778), and Ann Radcliffe, famous for The Mysteries of Udolpho (1794). Radcliffe is also known for introducing an early feminist element to her gothic stories, as her female protagonists would often pretend to fit stereotypes of weak women, only to reveal their true strength as the story progressed.

Early reactions to these gothic stories were mixed. Though they achieved great popularity among the general public, many scholars looked down upon this new literary genre as silly or even immoral. Furthermore, while there were a number of talented writers of gothic fiction, they also inspired a number of copycat writers whose work had less substance and style. These writers are mocked and satirised by Jane Austen in Northanger Abbey (1803).

Gothic horror in the 19th century

The 19th century produced a large number of gothic stories, many of which are still highly influential today. The first major gothic novel of the 19th century was Frankenstein (1818) by Mary Shelley, which describes a scientist’s ill-fated experiment to create life, and the monstrous being produced as a result. Note that the title refers to the scientist, and not to the monster as is commonly believed today.

A few decades later, the American author Edgar Allan Poe wrote a number of short stories and poems which contained many gothic elements, such as “The Fall of the House of Usher” (1839) and “The Raven” (1845). Poe is still considered one of the greatest horror writers in American history and you can read much more about him here.

The latter half of the 19th century continued the trend of influential gothic horror stories, and many of the most famous authors of the time wrote some novels within the genre, even if they did not dedicate their career to it. This included authors such as Robert Louis Stevenson (The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde, 1886), Oscar Wilde (The Picture of Dorian Gray, 1891), and Henry James (The Turn of the Screw, 1898).  

At the very end of the century, Bram Stoker wrote Dracula (1897), which may be the most famous example of gothic horror in history - and a story that has inspired an enormous quantity of other horror stories featuring its iconic villain, the vampire Count Dracula. Note that Stoker did not invent the idea of vampires (it most likely dates back thousands of years), but he strongly influenced the way they were later portrayed in horror fiction.
