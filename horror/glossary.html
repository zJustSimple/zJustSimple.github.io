Glossary

On this page, you can find words that are particularly useful to know when studying the horror genre. Click each word for a full definition in English, including information about how the word is specifically relevant in the context of the topic.

Body horror (s)
 

A type of horror that relies specifically on presenting distorted versions of the human body, for example through deformity, mutilation or unnatural movement, which often produces a strong sense of unease or disgust in the audience. A famous example of body horror is the scene from The Exorcist (1973) where the possessed girl’s head turns 180 degrees - because we know that normal human heads cannot move like that, this can be very disturbing to watch.

Catharsis (s)
 

A psychological term that describes a pleasant feeling of release that may follow a frightening or stressful experience, because it helps bring repressed emotions or desires to the surface. Some researchers have used the concept of catharsis to explain why the horror genre is appealing to people.

Cosmic horror (s)
 

A subgenre of horror that focuses on the idea that terrible beings lurk at the edge of human reality - or in the outer reaches of the cosmos (universe). People who gain knowledge of these creatures are often driven insane simply by the impossible truths they represent. Cosmic horror typically does not rely heavily on direct gore and violence, but focuses more on presenting creatures that are so alien and bizarre that they defy the normal rules of reality.

Cosmic horror is also known as Lovecraftian horror, because H.P. Lovecraft pioneered the genre.

Folklore (s)
 

Fictional stories that have become an integrated part of a culture through constant telling and retelling. The stories are often presented as truth, and may gain a widespread following. Many classic horror monsters (such as ghosts and vampires) originate in folklore.

Found footage (s, adj)
 

A specific subgenre of horror film. Found footage movies are designed to seem more realistic than other horror movies, as the movie is meant to look and feel like authentic footage that someone stumbled upon in real life, rather than a work of fiction. The Blair Witch Project (1999) was the first famous example of this genre, and more recently the Paranormal Activity movies (2007-2015) have also become very popular. 

Gore (s)
 

The noun 'gore' literally refers to blood that has been shed. It is also used as a more general term to describe graphic displays of blood, bodies and internal organs in both horror literature and horror movies. Splatter movies (see below) in particular are defined by extreme amounts of on-screen gore.   

Gothic horror (s)
 

A type of horror that emerged in the mid-1700s and gained widespread popularity in the 1800s. Gothic horror stories are often characterised by an atmospheric setting and use of supernatural (or apparently supernatural) elements. Two famous examples of gothic horror are Frankenstein (1818) by Mary Shelley and Dracula (1897) by Bram Stoker.

Jump scare (s)
 

A jump scare is an effect in a horror movie designed to produce a direct shock in the viewers, ideally making them jump in their seats (explaining the name). Typical jump scares might include a monster suddenly turning up behind the main character, a seemingly dead creature coming alive when the main character touches it, or a twisted face which suddenly appears in a mirror (never trust a mirror in a horror movie!) - often with scary sound effects added in.

Sometimes modern horror movies are criticised for an over-reliance on jump scares, which can seem like cheap tricks, and may get in the way of a more gradual build-up of a horror atmosphere.   

Penny dreadful (s)
 

A British precursor to the pulp magazine (see below) in the 19th century. Penny dreadfuls were inexpensive story serials that were published in weekly installments and were accessible to a wide audience. They were often horror stories - sometimes reprints of gothic classics - but could also cover other genres intended for entertainment, such as romance or crime fiction.

Though few penny dreadful stories remain famous today, the medium has spawned some still-known characters, such the murderous barber Sweeney Todd.

Pulp magazine (s)
 

A type of literary magazine which was particularly popular in the US in the 1920s and 1930s, which often featured horror stories combined with scary illustrations. The magazines were named after the inexpensive wood pulp paper used to print them. Just like the British penny dreadfuls (see above), the inexpensive pulp magazines helped bring horror stories to mainstream audiences.

Furthermore, they were often considerably darker and more gory than the horror movies which were produced in the same era. H.P. Lovecraft notably published much of his early work through pulp magazines.

Slasher film (s)
 

A subgenre of horror films focused on a killer who chases a group of people (often teenagers) and usually kills them off one by one, before finally being confronted and defeated by the last surviving main character (though often a twist ending implies that the killer is not truly gone). Halloween (1978) is a famous early slasher movie which spawned many sequels and remakes. 

Splatter film (s)
 

A subgenre of horror films characterised by extreme levels of blood and gore, which started to emerge in the 1960s and 1970s. A variation on the genre, which has been nicknamed ‘torture porn’ because of its extreme focus on graphic scenes of torture and suffering, gained popularity in the 2000s - most famously through the Saw franchise.

Suspense (s)
 

A technique of gradually building up tension which is commonly used in the horror genre across all types of media. In modern horror movies, scenes of drawn-out suspense will often lead to a jump scare (see above).

Uncanny (adj)
 

A term originating in Freudian psychoanalysis, which describes something that is familiar and strange at the same time. Seeing something uncanny can be deeply disturbing, because it closely resembles something we are very familiar with, but at the same time something about it seems wrong. Many horror stories draw upon uncanny elements - such as monsters that resemble human beings in almost all ways, but with one detail being wrong.  

Urban legend (s)
 

A modern form of folklore (see above). Urban legends are fictional stories presented as truth that gain attention within popular culture. Often urban legends are focused around particular areas, though the internet also means that some of the most famous legends receive global attention. Many (but not all) urban legends feature horror elements, and many horror books and movies have been inspired by these urban legends. The influence goes both ways, as some horror stories have in turn sparked new urban legends.
