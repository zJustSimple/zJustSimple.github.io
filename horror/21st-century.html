Horror in the 21st century

Cuprins

Horror literature in the 21st century

Horror literature moved in many different directions in the early 21st century, often mixing with other genres and literary styles, or using experimental or unusual literary devices to heighten the feeling of horror and unease.

A famous example of postmodern horror is House of Leaves (2000) by Mark Z. Danielewsky. This incredibly strange novel is a confusing mix of journal entries, letters and footnotes that tells the story of a house that somehow defies the rules of physics. The novel frequently shifts between narrators and employs many other postmodern devices that create a unique kind of horror, which attempts to make readers question their own sanity while reading the book.  

Though they are more romance than horror, the incredibly successful Twilight books (2005-2008) by Stephenie Meyer are notable because of their reinvention of vampires, an iconic monster from the horror tradition. Meyer’s vampires are usually characterised as mysterious, romantic outcasts rather than terrible monsters.

Pride and Prejudice and Zombies (2009) started a trend of genre mashups within the field of horror literature. It contains the entire text of Jane Austen’s original romantic novel Pride and Prejudice (1813), but then includes extra text that adds a zombie apocalypse to the plot and casts main characters Elizabeth and Mr. Darcy as professional zombie hunters. Despite this rather silly premise, the novel gained widespread popularity and paved the way for similar horror/comedy mashups of other out-of-copyright literary works.

Horror movies and tv shows in the 21st century

A number of new horror movie franchises became popular in the early 21st century - some inspired by earlier styles, with others breaking new ground.

Some of these new horror franchises built upon the splatter genre and featured extreme focus on graphic scenes of torture and suffering, with the main characters being exposed to nightmarish death traps and torture scenarios with little possibility of escape. The most famous examples of this style are the Saw movies (2004-2017) and the Hostel movies (2005-2011). Critics of these films dubbed the genre ‘torture porn’, suggesting that the movies have little artistic value but mainly try to shock and horrify.

Other franchises went in entirely different directions, moving away from explicit gore and shock effects and focusing on more subtle kinds of terror. An early example of this trend was The Ring (2002), a remake of a Japanese horror movie from 1998. It became a huge success and found audiences across the world, and it helped bring international attention to horror films from other nations.

The Spanish El Orfanato (‘The Orphanage’) (2007) is another example of a movie from a niche market that received international success - and just like The Ring it relies on subtle horrors instead of graphic violence. El Orfanato also includes elements of gothic horror, being set in an old, labyrinthine building in a remote location and delving into classic gothic themes such as loss, despair and madness.

Another recent trend is a blend of horror and satire. For example, the critically acclaimed Get Out (2017) is both a horror movie and a satirical exploration of racism against African Americans in US society. Some movies have also directly satirised the horror genre - Shaun of the Dead (2004) parodies many zombie movie cliches, while Cabin in the Woods (2012) provides a satirical twist on the classic slasher movies of the 1970s and 1980s.

A number of horror-themed TV shows became very popular alongside the emergence of TV streaming services such as HBO and Netflix. The zombie apocalypse show The Walking Dead (2010-ongoing) is a viewer success that has helped bring new attention to the zombie genre, while the more recent Stranger Things (2016-ongoing) was inspired by Lovecraftian ideas of horrors lurking just beyond our reality.

Apart from all of these new franchises, the early 21st century also saw many remakes of - or sequels to - earlier horror classics, such as Dawn of the Dead (2004), Friday the 13th (2009), It (2017) and Halloween (2018).

Horror video games in the 21st century

The development of horror games continued in the 21st century, with many sequels to earlier popular franchises, but also new titles.

The independently developed and low-budget Amnesia: The Dark Descent (2010) became a cult classic because of its carefully developed atmosphere, and its focus on terrifying monsters that were impossible to fight and dangerous to even look at - making evasion or escape the frightened player’s only options. Both the themes and visuals of the game were heavily inspired by the works of H.P. Lovecraft. Amnesia was also notable because it was released at a time when many other popular horror franchises were increasingly drifting away from horror, becoming more like pure action games.

The increasing popularity of virtual reality has created a market for new kinds of intense horror experiences. When wearing VR goggles, the horrors can now emerge from all sides, and the experience can thus reach new levels of intensity. Some have even voiced concerns that this kind of medium might call for more restraint in horror game developers, as it might be possible to create experiences that are too scary to be entertaining - and that might even have lasting negative effects on the player.
