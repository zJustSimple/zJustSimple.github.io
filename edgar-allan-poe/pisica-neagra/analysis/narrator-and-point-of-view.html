Narrator and point of view

The short story “The Black Cat” by Edgar Allan Poe is a first-person narrative.

The narrator of the story is unreliable. In the first lines, he tries to explain to the readers that he is not mad, even though what he is about to narrate might make him seem insane: “Mad indeed would I be to expect it, in a case where my very senses reject their own evidence. Yet, mad am I not -- and very surely do I not dream” (ll. 2-3). However, the fact that he claims sanity but then he admits that alcohol has clouded his judgment and that he started acting violently (ll. 36-37) is contradictory. Furthermore, when he tries to explain his deeds resorting to rationality, he fails to offer a logical explanation:

…hung it because I knew that it had loved me, and because I felt it had given me no reason of offence; — hung it because I knew that in so doing I was committing a sin — a deadly sin that would so jeopardize my immortal soul as to place it — if such a thing were possible — even beyond the reach of the infinite mercy of the Most Merciful and Most Terrible God (ll. 74-77).

Furthermore, his tone through the story shifts to become increasingly tense and nonsensical: “…the image of a hideous -- of a ghastly thing -- of the GALLOWS! -- oh, mournful and terrible engine of Horror and of Crime -- of Agony and of Death !” (ll. 159-160). This image can also imply that the narrator suffers from hallucinations, or chimaeras, as he calls them (l. 151). These hallucinations affect the narrator’s state of mind, enforcing his unreliability.

Also, the narrator claims to narrate everything just as it happened: “in the circumstances I detail with awe, nothing more than an ordinary succession of very natural causes and effects” (ll. 10-11). However, since the story is told in retrospect, the narrator has the advantage of being able to select what he deems relevant, leaving out details or perhaps even altering the truth of the story. Moreover, notice that the narrator tries to attract the reader’s sympathy by posing as a victim of alcohol and of the cat. But while he admits feeling some remorse after he tortures and then kills Pluto, he shows no remorse immediately after killing his wife.

Having limited knowledge, he presents the events only from his perspective and focuses on his own reactions to what he has experienced: “One night, returning home, much intoxicated, from one of my haunts about town, I fancied that the cat avoided my presence” (ll. 45-46). Therefore, the narrator is also subjective. Since the story follows his point of view, the actions and feelings of other characters are subject to his interpretation and the reader cannot know for certain if this is an objective observation or the narrator’s subjective opinion on the events. The narrative is unusually closely focused on the narrator’s point of view. He hints at other things happening but is only concerned with that which directly affects him: “The guilt of my dark deed disturbed me but little. Some few inquiries had been made, but these had been readily answered. Even a search had been instituted—but of course nothing was to be discovered. I looked upon my future felicity as secured” (ll. 217-220)

The narrator is mostly explicit because he openly presents the events of the narrative without hiding the meaning. The action is directly described as it happens: “I aimed a blow at the animal which, of course, would have proved instantly fatal had it descended as I wished. But this blow was arrested by the hand of my wife” (ll. 177-179). The narration also offers direct insight into the narrator’s thoughts: “I blush, I burn, I shudder, while I pen the damnable atrocity” (ll. 51-52). Therefore, the reader does not have to interpret the meaning behind the narrator’s actions to understand his feelings.

However, in some instances, the narrator is also implicit. He does not explicitly state that he continues to be violent towards his wife, but this is implied by his remark: “my uncomplaining wife, alas! was the most usual and the most patient of sufferers” (ll. 172-173). He is also not explicit about what happens to him after the police discover the body. The reader is informed only that the narrator has been arrested (l. 149) and that he is going to die (l. 3).
