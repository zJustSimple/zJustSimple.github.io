Title

The title of the short story “The Black Cat” by Edgar Allan Poe has both a literal and a symbolic meaning.

In the literal sense, the title is a direct reference to the two black cats belonging to the narrator. The narrator’s first pet, Pluto, is described as “a remarkably large and beautiful animal, entirely black, and sagacious to an astonishing degree” (ll. 25-26). The second cat is also “a black cat–a very large one–fully as large as Pluto, and closely resembling him in every respect but one” (ll. 116-117).

The title can also be connected to the comment about black cats made by the narrator’s wife: “In speaking of his intelligence, my wife, who at heart was not a little tinctured with superstition, made frequent allusion to the ancient popular notion, which regarded all black cats as witches in disguise” (ll. 26-28). This connection creates reader expectation, as it implies that the story may deal with the supernatural and the mysterious. The mysterious nature of the narrator’s cats is also emphasised by the striking similarity between the two animals, their odd behaviour, the strange shape-shifting mark bore by the second cat, the fire that destroyed the narrator’s house after he killed the first cat, and the image of a hanged cat embedded in the fresh plaster of a ruined wall in his house.

In a symbolic sense, the title reflects the “blackness” of the narrator’s heart. He transforms from a kind and loving person (ll. 13-14) into a cruel and hateful man (l. 39). The first black cat, Pluto, represents the morality of the narrator. He was an affectionate and friendly cat (ll. 31-32), yet the narrator states that Pluto “was now becoming old, and consequently somewhat peevish” (ll. 43-44). This change of behaviour is also reflected in the narrator, who becomes more and more evil. Moreover, the cat becomes more distant towards the narrator as he, in turn, becomes more cruel. When he kills Pluto, the narrator also severs his ties to his old self, an action which is also reflected by the house burning down.

The second black cat represents the narrator’s guilt. The narrator tries to appease his conscience by replacing Pluto, however, the second cat turns into a reminder of his cruelty. As a result, the narrator becomes afraid of confronting the cat (ll. 130-131), because he is also afraid of facing his own guilt. He can only get some rest when the cat disappears (ll. 212-214), an act that signifies the loss of his conscience. Therefore, the two black cats symbolise the deep psychological turmoil of the narrator, around which the story is centred.

Beginning

The exposition presents the narrator directly addressing the reader. The narrator argues that he is not mad, although what he is about to narrate might seem strange and it might be hard to believe. “For the most wild, yet most homely narrative which I am about to pen, I neither expect nor solicit belief. Mad indeed would I be to expect it…” (ll. 1-2). This acts as a narrative hook, as it catches the readers’ attention and creates reader expectation. It also confers a confessional nature to the story. His mention that he is going to die (l. 3) foreshadows the crime he commits. Furthermore, by hinting that the cat could be a witch in disguise (l. 18), the narrator foreshadows the unexplainable influence the cats have over his temperament.

Through a backstory from his childhood, the narrator introduces himself as a person with “tenderness of heart” (ll. 12-13) and a fondness for animals. He also introduces his wife and his pets, among which the black cat Pluto is listed as his favourite (l. 31).

Middle

In the rising action, the narrator talks about developing a drinking problem and the way this made him become violent towards his wife and pets. Through a backstory, the narrator recounts how he, in a fit of rage, wounded his cat by cutting out its eye. The narrator states: “My original soul seemed, at once, to take its flight from my body; and a more than fiendish malevolence, gin-nurtured, thrilled every fibre of my frame” (ll. 48-49). By hurting the cat, the narrator alters his morality, inflicting a wound upon his own sense of goodness. He seems to be aware of this, since when he kills the cat, the narrator cries knowing that his soul has been jeopardised and placed “beyond the reach of the infinite mercy of the Most Merciful and Most Terrible God” (l. 77).

A tension point is created when the narrator hangs Pluto. This event is immediately followed by another tension point, as the narrator’s house catches fire. This event suggests the loss of his morality. The image of a cat embedded in the fresh plaster of a ruined wall in his house foreshadows the end of the story where the second black cat had been walled in together with the corpse of the dead wife, in a cellar that has similarly been recently plastered.

Through a backstory, the narrator tells how he came upon the second black cat and brought it home as a replacement for Pluto. However, the narrator soon tires of the cat: “I soon found a dislike to it arising within me. This was just the reverse of what I had anticipated” (ll. 127-128). This statement shows that the narrator has reached a point of no return: he can no longer feel love or recover his goodness. As the representation of his guilt and shame, the cat becomes a terrifying creature for the narrator (l. 133). The white patch of fur slowly taking the shape of gallows (l. 159) foreshadows the narrator’s possible death sentence and stands as a reminder of his cruelty.

The climax of the story happens when the narrator kills his wife in a fit of rage because she stops him from hurting the cat:

I aimed a blow at the animal (…) But this blow was arrested by the hand of my wife. Goaded, by the interference, into a rage more than demoniacal, I withdrew my arm from her grasp and buried the axe in her brain. She fell dead upon the spot, without a groan (ll. 177-181).

Ending

The falling action presents the narrator trying to hide the body of his wife. The narrator is remorseless and decides to hide her inside the wall of the cellar. He details the process of opening the wall, hiding her inside, and concealing the act. As the cat is nowhere to be seen, the narrator finds temporary peace: “for one night at least, since its introduction into the house, I soundly and tranquilly slept; aye, slept even with the burden of murder upon my soul!” (ll. 213-214). This statement suggests that, with his sense of guilt no longer there, the narrator’s conscience falls silent.

In the resolution, the police come to investigate the disappearance of the narrator’s wife. The resolution holds a sense of irony, as the narrator incriminates himself by trying to prove that he is not guilty: “and here, through the mere phrenzy of bravado, I rapped heavily, with a cane which I held in my hand, upon that very portion of the brick-work behind which stood the corpse” (ll. 235-237)

The ending is closed. It is revealed that the cat – which has been walled up with the dead wife - brings about the downfall of the narrator, as its cries alert the police. This represents a twist because it leads to an unexpected outcome. However, the reader knows from the beginning of the story that the narrator has been arrested and is going to die, and it is, therefore, implied that he is awaiting execution.
