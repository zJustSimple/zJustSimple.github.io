Setting

Cuprins

  • Social setting

Time and physical setting

Both the exact time and the setting of the story “The Black Cat” by Edgar Allan Poe are omitted. We do not know in which city, year, or month the events narrated happened. However, we can assume the author intended the story to be read as a contemporary one, taking place sometime in the 1840s. The fact that the story is not very exact regarding time and physical setting contributes to creating a sense of mystery fitting for a horror story.

The main events span over a few months, since the night the narrator first tortured Pluto, taking the animal’s eye out, until the police find his wife’s body together with the new black cat. 

Specific elements of the setting include the narrator’s initial house, which is burnt down, and the cellar of his second house. The first house the narrator lived in is depicted as having been filled with various types of animals, collected because both him and his wife are animal lovers: “We had birds, gold-fish, a fine dog, rabbits, a small monkey, and a cat.” (p. 1, l. 24). The house also has a garden with a tree which the narrator uses to hang Pluto (ll. 97-99). Particular attention is given to one of the walls of the house after it burns down, where an impression of the hanged black cat was preserved.

In the second house where the narrator lived, the focus is put on the cellar:

For a purpose such as this the cellar was well adapted. Its walls were loosely constructed, and had lately been plastered throughout with a rough plaster, which the dampness of the atmosphere had prevented from hardening. Moreover, in one of the walls was a projection, caused by a false chimney, or fireplace, that had been filled up, and made to resemble the rest of the cellar. (ll. 192-196)

Social setting

The narrator denounces alcoholism, referring to it as “the Fiend Intemperance” (l. 35) and exclaiming that “for what disease is like Alcohol!” (ll. 42-43). The narrator blames alcoholism for his violent behaviour, however, alcoholism could function only as a trigger for his violent instincts. The narrator also talks about being possessed by the “spirit of PERVERSNESS” (l. 62), which is describes as a primitive impulse of human nature. Therefore, he suggests he is naturally predisposed towards violence. The narrator also seems to have an unstable state of mind: he is suspicious of the cat (ll. 133-134), he has irrational thoughts (ll. 158-160), he tends to be superstitious (ll. 25-30), and he experiences hallucinations (ll. 150-151).

The story also deals with domestic violence and the situation of women and men in the 19th century. During this time, temperance, modesty, and humility were seen as social virtues, especially for women. The narrator’s wife seems to correspond with this description. She is briefly portrayed and does not seem to possess many distinguishing characteristics: she loves animals (ll. 22-24), and she is knowledgeable about superstitions, although she does not believe in them (ll. 26-28). Throughout the story, she is only found inside the house and, through a comparison with the narrator, she is implied to enjoy simple and pure pleasures (l. 139). In contrast, the narrator goes out into town, returning home at night intoxicated and having to sleep off “the fumes of the night’s debauch” (ll. 53-54). He also goes to “a den of more than infamy” (l. 111), which most likely refers to a brothel or opium den. This situation reflects the role imposed on women during this period of time. Women were expected to take up domestic roles: taking care of the household and having a nurturing and kind temperament. On the other hand, men could take more liberties than women, and belonged to the public sphere, moving around freely and engaging in social life.

During this time, a man would assume the role of “head of the family,” and his wife or female relatives were dependent on him and expected to show obedience. In the story, the family’s wealth seems to have belonged to the narrator (l. 81). Moreover, as he becomes abusive towards his wife, she remains uncomplaining, being “the most patient of sufferers” (ll. 172-173). When she finally opposes him by trying to stop him from harming the cat, the narrator kills her in a fit of rage. The narrator notes that she “fell dead upon the spot, without a groan” (ll. 180-181), therefore being “uncomplaining” even as she dies.

The story also discusses superstition and the relationship between humans and animals. In common beliefs, humans associated black cats with evil forces of magic. For this reason, many animals were brutally killed, since animal abuse was not illegal. This aspect is also mentioned by the narrator’s wife who “made frequent allusion to the ancient popular notion, which regarded all black cats as witches in disguise” (ll. 27-28). The narrator seems to share this superstition. Although he tries to deny it by saying that superstitions are not serious (l. 28) and that he is above “the weakness of seeking to establish a sequence of cause and effect” (l. 83) between the cat’s death and his arrest, the narrator begins to fear his pets. After he kills his first cat, the second cat becomes a source of terror for the narrator who sees the animal as an evil force: “This dread was not exactly a dread of physical evil -- and yet I should be at a loss how otherwise to define it” (ll. 148-149). For this reason, he decides to kill the cat in another act of animal cruelty, however, he fails and kills his wife instead.
