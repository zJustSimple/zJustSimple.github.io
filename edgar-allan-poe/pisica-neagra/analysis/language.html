Style of writing

The language used in the short story “The Black Cat” by Edgar Allan Poe is slightly old-fashioned English, given that the text is more than 150 years old. The story is also written in a very fragmented way. Comments, explanations, and repetitions are sometimes inserted in the middle of a sentence: “When reason returned with the morning — when I had slept off the fumes of the night’s debauch — I experienced a sentiment half of horror” (ll. 53-54). This is done to draw attention to the line, bring clarifications, or strengthen the message of the text. The fragmentation also echoes the narrator’s fragmented and unstable state of mind.

Interestingly, some words are written with capital letters, suggesting the narrator’s emphasis and desperation: “…the image of a hideous -- of a ghastly thing -- of the GALLOWS ! -- oh, mournful and terrible engine of Horror and of Crime -- of Agony and of Death !” (ll. 159-160). Similarly, the narration uses an unusual number of exclamation marks, suggesting the narrator’s agitation and the psychological strain of the story.

The story features little dialogue and is written as a confession made in retrospect. Notice that the only voice we can hear is that of the first-person narrator, even when direct speech is used. We never hear the wife or the policemen: “ ‘I may say an excellently well constructed house. These walls — are you going, gentlemen? — these walls are solidly put together’ ” (ll. 234-235).

The style of writing is typical of thriller stories, with the narrator keeping readers in suspense and increasing tension gradually up to the breaking point.

Symbols

The eye is a symbol of self-reflection. In popular notion, the eyes are thought to be the mirrors of the soul. By cutting Pluto’s eye out of the socket (l. 51), the narrator removes a part of his sense of self. Also, he describes how when hurting Pluto, he feels his soul “take its flight from my body; and a more than fiendish malevolence, gin-nurtured, thrilled every fibre of my frame” (ll. 49-48). Symbolically, the narrator turns a blind eye on his morality. The loss of the eye can also represent a shift in vision. By renouncing his old self, the narrator gains a new perspective on the world: “the feeble remnant of the good within me succumbed. Evil thoughts became my sole intimates” (ll. 168-169). His perspective is a negative one, as he feels himself turning into a person who is willing to commit evil deeds.

The house represents the narrator’s decay. After he kills Pluto, the narrator destroys his morality. The house burns down in the same night, signifying the moral decay of the narrator. It also represents a physical decay, as alcohol has negative effects on both mind and body. It can also reflect how the narrator squanders his money away on alcohol and debauchery. The narrator states “my entire worldly wealth was swallowed up, and I resigned myself thenceforward to despair” (ll. 81-82). After this, he has to live in poverty (l. 175).

The walls reflect the narrator’s denial. The narrator refuses to face the consequences of his actions and rejects his sense of guilt and remorse. After his house burns down, a single wall is left standing. On it, the narrator sees the figure of a cat. However, the narrator refuses to see it as a warning sign, trying to come up with an explanation: “The falling of other walls had compressed the victim of my cruelty into the substance of the freshly-spread plaster; the lime of which, had then with the flames, and the ammonia from the carcass, accomplished the portraiture as I saw it” (ll. 100-103). Similarly, he places the body of his dead wife behind the wall of the cellar and refuses to take responsibility for his actions, acting as if nothing has happened.

The black cat(s) play a symbolic role in the story and can be seen as two faces of the same symbolic character. The first cat, Pluto, is depicted physically in a way that shows the narrator’s affection for the animal (ll. 25-26), however, when the narrator cuts out one of its eyes, the cat starts avoiding him (l. 59). Therefore, Pluto is the symbol of the victim, an animal which becomes the target of the narrator and, therefore, represents the narrator’s discarded morality. However, upon the cat’s murder, the animal gains another symbolic role: it represents the narrator’s conscience and his remorse. The cat becomes like a ghost haunting the narrator through the impression on the wall and in his dreams.

This symbolic role is also taken up by the second cat, which strongly resembles Pluto because it also misses an eye, but has a white spot on its chest. The cat soon becomes a constant reminder of the gruesome way the narrator had treated Pluto. The white spot slowly taking the form of gallows symbolises the narrator’s guilty conscience.. In the end, the second cat becomes a symbol of justice, for both the murder of Pluto and the wife, since it is the animal who alerts the police of the corpse behind the wall and causes the narrator to be punished for his crime.
