Literary context

Edgar Allan Poe’s short stories are often seen as gothic stories. This genre frequently resorts to ambiguity and puzzling characters. Furthermore, the setting is dark, and the aim is to instil feelings of horror and suspense in the readers. These elements can also be encountered in “The Black Cat,” as the setting of the story is ambiguous, and the narrator has an unstable state of mind that drives him to commit murder.

The gothic genre also frequently deals with issues of the supernatural, but also with psychological breakdown. In “The Black Cat”, the writer suggests that the cats may be supernatural (the reference to witches and the changing shape of the second cat’s patch of white fur). However, the events in the story may also be entirely down to the narrator’s psychological transformation – he may be imagining that the cat’s pattern changes shape, for example, because of his feeling of guilt. It is very typical of Poe's stories that there are elements that could be supernatural, but could also be products of the narrator's madness. 

Gothic characters are often enigmatic and exhibit contradicting behaviours. The narrator of “The Black Cat” fits this description, since he starts off as a loving character and ends up a murderer. His violence and his moral and psychological transformation represent the main themes of the story. These themes fit the gothic genre because they produce feelings of mystery and horror. The social setting of the story also fits the gothic genre because it deals with human nature and the supernatural.

Texts by the same author

"The Tell-Tale Heart" is very similar to "The Black Cat". Both short stories are first-person narratives of unreliable narrators and both narrators obsess over something that finally pushes them to kill a person. In “The Tell-Tale Heart”, the narrator believes that the eye of his future victim is evil, while in “The Black Cat”, the narrator is highly suspicious of his cats. The two stories also have very similar endings.

In “The Facts in the Case of M. Valdemar”, a mesmerist keeps a gravely ill man alive by mesmerising him right before he dies. The story, like “The Black Cat”, deals with human nature and the supernatural. By asking the mesmerist to keep him alive long after he dies, the man breaks the laws of nature, therefore, acting to the spirit of perverseness described in “The Black Cat”. The supernatural is encountered in the mesmerist’s ability to keep the man alive.

In “The Cask of Amontillado”, the narrator of the story, Montresor, plans to kill Fortunato because he offended the narrator’s family. Montresor chooses a similar plan as the narrator in “The Black Cat”, although he traps his victim alive behind a wall. However, Montresor’s crime is not discovered and so he remains unpunished.

Movie and TV adaptations

“The Black Cat” is one of Poe’s most famous short stories, and it has been adapted into films and multiple times from 1910 onwards. 

A notable adaptation of the story is in the movie “Two Evil Eyes” (1990) starring Harvey Keitel in the lead role. The movie blends this short story with another one by the same author: “The Facts in the Case of M. Valdemar”.

Also, “The Black Cat” is the title of an episode of the second season of the series Masters of Horror (2007). In that episode, the protagonist is the character of Edgar Allan Poe, who undergoes some events related to a black cat that prompt him to write the short story.
