Themes and message

Racism and prejudice

One of the main themes of the novel To Kill a Mockingbird by Harper Lee is racism. The theme is explored by looking at a small town in Southern US in the 1930s and at the trial of a Black man falsely accused of raping a white woman. 

The town of Maycomb appears to be a place where the majority of inhabitants have racist beliefs. While Black people are no longer enslaved, the town follows the racial segregation typical of that time, and many characters express racist views. For example, the Black people in town go to a separate church, called the First Purchase African Church as it was paid for from the first earnings of the freed slaves. Aunt Alexandra is concerned that the children went to a Black church and does not wish them to further associate with Black people, which shows she holds racist beliefs and agrees with racial segregation.

The theme is perhaps best illustrated by the fact that Tom Robinson, a Black man, is falsely accused of rape without any evidence against him, and he still loses his trial because the jury chooses to believe white people instead of a Black man. This shows a case of systemic racism, where an entire institution – in this case the judicial system – is shown to unfairly favor white people to the disadvantage of racial minorities. 

It is also important to note that the theme is explored from the perspective of a white child in the 1930s, who often presents an innocent yet simplistic view of racism and sometimes fails to notice the subtler racist acts happening around her. 

Childhood Innocence and Growing up

The novel explores the theme of childhood innocence and growing up by looking at Scout and Jem Finch and the ways they mature throughout the novel. At the beginning of the story, Scout and Jem are both young children who are innocent and have not experienced the harsh realities of adult life. This is shown by the child-like worldview they have of many of the adults around them. For example, they believe Boo Radley is a frightening and mean man even though Atticus and Maudie tell them this is not true. 

Moreover, because of their innocent perspective, Scout and Jem are sometimes unable to fully understand what they are experiencing or what adults around them are talking about. For example, during Jem’s reading sessions for Mrs. Dubose, the children do not understand that the woman is having fits because she is going through withdrawal. 

Throughout the novel, however, as they grow up and have more significant experiences, the children begin to mature. The injustice of Tom Robinson’s trial is an especially important experience for them as it reveals to them that the world is often an unjust place where good and innocent people do not always win. Jem seems particularly upset and marked by the trial, and the moment marks a turning point in his development and the loss of his innocence. 

Morals and Courage

Another theme explored in the novel is the theme of morals. This is mainly shown through Atticus Finch and the morals he has and wishes to pass on to his children. 

For example, Atticus aims to show his children that courage does not involve doing dangerous activities, but it means doing difficult things even when you know you are likely to fail because it is the right thing to do. This is also illustrated by the way Atticus does not take pride in being skilled at shooting, but he is unashamed and proud of defending an innocent Black man in court, even if the town resents him for it. 

Message

The message of the novel To Kill a Mockingbird by Harper Lee is that people deserve to be treated with respect and empathy regardless of their background or race. Atticus Finch is shown as the character who embodies this message and tries to pass it on to his children too. The mantra he repeats to his children throughout the novel, “ ‘You never really understand a person until you consider things from his point of view’ ” (p. 33), highlights this message. 

Title

The title of the story, To Kill a Mockingbird, refers mainly to the unfairness of Tom Robinson’s persecution and death. At one point, Atticus tells Scout it would be wrong to kill mockingbirds, as they do not harm anyone and actually bring people joy. The same way, Tom Robinson only wanted to help Mayella, which makes his conviction and death wrong and unfair.

At the end of the novel, Scout compares the idea of putting Boo Radley in the spotlight for killing Bob Ewell with shooting a mockingbird. This shows that she understands that Boo is a kind person who does not deserve the persecution he would most likely suffer.
