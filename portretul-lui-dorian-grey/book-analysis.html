Book analysis

While reading Oscar Wilde’s book The Picture of Dorian Gray, I cannot help fantasizing about living during that time. That time when the ‘Decadent artistic movement of the late nineteenth century’ ruled and the difference between the dull middle-class and the higher society was nothing you can disregard. 

The Decadent movement was an artistic and literary movement, which grew strong in primarily France and England, in the late nineteenth century during the period of the Victorian era (1837-1901). Many writers throughout this time took the name Decadence writers and they were influenced by the Gothic novels and authors like Edgar Allan Poe. Oscar Wilde was one of these Decadence writers and his work The Picture of Dorian Gray counts as a decadent book, due to its urban gothic style, which combines horror and romanticism.

This is one of the main themes of the book; the higher society with their immoral behavior and the gloomy middle-class. Lord Henry Wotton, one of the more significant character of the story, symbolizes the immorality and he speculates in an exceptionally shallow, though to this era very common way, about the only things that are important in life; youth and appearance. It is obvious that he included Dorian Gray, Basil Hallward and their friends and acquaintances belong to the higher class as he mentions the ‘middle-class life’ and how ‘the middle classes are not modern’.

The story takes place in London in both the west and the east end, of course these parts of London are as like as chalk and cheese in some ways. In the west end the substantial people, like Gray and Lord Henry live, for instance Gray speaks of ‘Lord Henry’s house in Mayfair’ and Mayfair is still a neighborhood of the Westminster area. Here they also visits operas, theatres and other cultural thing, which are only available to the wealthy and noble citizens. On the other side of London the east end is situated, where it is densely populated and the district where the non-socialists live. 

Vehicles, as an example are objects which is mentioned in Wilde’s book and reveals what kind of society and time the story takes place in even further. On page 81 the main character Dorian Gray is spotted ‘in an open carriage with two ladies’, though on page 49 the characters mentions ‘cars provided by the directors’ as if cars is a new and exclusive object to be in possession of. The car was an object that was accessible to the higher class in the late nineteenth century and the most established brand amongst those who could afford to own a car was Benz. Benz started to sell its cars 1886 and because of the discussion about the car being very exclusive in Wilde’s book, this is a solid reason to think Dorian Gray and his companions lived during this time. This also support the argument about Dorian Gray, Lord Henry Wotton and Basil Hallward belonging to the higher society. 

Whilst Dorian Gray was wealthy and had a fair amount of free time in his hands he was inclined to different movements for his own pleasure, such as ‘the materialistic doctrines of the Darwinismus movement in Germany’, which also took place in the late nineteenth century and was a movement started by Ernst Haeckel after a lecture that he held before important German scientists and doctors 1863.

The above presented example concludes this analysis about where and when the story of The Picture of Dorian Gray by Oscar Wilde takes place. There are several additional examples which could be analyzed and brought up further to advocate the conclusion of this paper, however it would only be superfluous.

The conclusion as such, is well processed due to all instances that have been displayed through this analysis and the story evidently takes place during the late nineteenth century during the Victorian era amongst the higher society in London.
